# Anatomy and Physiology

## Renal Anatomy

The urinary system consists of the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra. The primary function of this system is to filter the blood and produce urine as a waste byproduct.

### Kidney Structure

The kidney is divided into two main regions:

- **Renal cortex**: The outer region of the kidney
- **Renal medulla**: The inner region of the kidney

The **renal columns** are connective tissue extensions that project from the cortex into the medulla, separating the renal pyramids and papillae. The **papillae** are bundles of collecting ducts that transport urine from the nephrons to the calyces for excretion. The **renal hilum** serves as the entry and exit point for blood vessels, nerves, lymphatics, and ureters.

## Renal Physiology

The **nephron** is the functional unit of the kidney. It processes filtrate through four primary mechanisms: filtration, reabsorption, secretion, and excretion.

### Filtration

Blood flows through Bowman's capsule, where fluid and solutes are filtered out to form the glomerular filtrate.

### Reabsorption Sites

The following segments of the nephron are responsible for reabsorption of water and ions:

- Proximal convoluted tubule
- Loop of Henle
- Distal convoluted tubule
- Collecting ducts

### Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR)

The glomerular filtration rate can be measured or estimated using several methods:

- **Measured with exogenous substances**: Inulin or radioisotopes
- **Estimated with endogenous substances**: Creatinine or cystatin C

In clinical practice, the **estimated GFR (eGFR)** is calculated using the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration (CKD-EPI) formula, which has the best accuracy in patients with normal eGFR. The Modification of Diet in Renal Disease (MDRD) equation is also used. These equations have largely replaced the Cockcroft-Gault equation.

#### Clinical Pearls for Interpreting eGFR

- eGFR is most accurate when a patient is in a steady state with stable serum creatinine.
- When interpreting eGFR, consider factors that may affect accuracy:
  - Muscle mass variations
  - Medication effects (e.g., trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole)
  - Dynamic states such as early acute kidney injury
- If eGFR accuracy is critical, consider calculating eGFR with cystatin C, a freely filtered molecule that is not affected by muscle mass.
