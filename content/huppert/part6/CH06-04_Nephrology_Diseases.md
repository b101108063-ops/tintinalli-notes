# Diseases

## Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

### Definition

AKI is defined by any of the following criteria:

- Serum creatinine increase of 0.3 mg/dL or greater within 48 hours
- Serum creatinine increased to 1.5 times baseline or greater within the prior 7 days
- Urine volume less than 0.5 mL/kg/h for 6 hours or more

### Prerenal AKI (Decreased Renal Perfusion)

**Etiologies** include:

- Hypovolemia (poor oral intake, diuresis, hemorrhage)
- Vasodilation (sepsis, anaphylaxis)
- Poor effective circulating volume (heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome)
- Contrast exposure
- Medications (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, NSAIDs)
- Abdominal compartment syndrome

**Clinical features** include:

- BUN/Cr ratio greater than 20:1
- Fractional excretion of sodium (FENa) less than 1%
- Urine osmolality greater than 500 mOsm/kg H₂O
- Urine Na⁺ less than 20 mEq/L
- Benign sediment with few hyaline casts and no cellular casts

### Intrarenal AKI (Direct Injury to Renal Tissue)

#### Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

**Etiologies** include:

- **Ischemia**: Prolonged or severe hypoperfusion (e.g., from hemorrhage or sepsis)
- **Toxic injury**: Pigment (rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis), paraproteins (multiple myeloma), medications (aminoglycosides, cisplatin, foscarnet, pentamidine, tenofovir), IV contrast

**Clinical features** include "muddy brown" granular casts and usually FENa greater than 2%.

> **Pearl**: Ischemic ATN lies on a spectrum of decreased renal perfusion along with "prerenal" AKI. A single patient—or even a single kidney—may exhibit both prerenal and ATN pathophysiology simultaneously.

#### Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)

**Etiologies** include:

- **Medications**: Antibiotics (especially beta-lactams), NSAIDs, PPIs
- **Infections**: Streptococcal infection, diphtheria, CMV, EBV, tuberculosis, hantavirus, leptospirosis
- **Systemic autoimmune disease**: Sjögren's syndrome, sarcoidosis, SLE, IgG4 disease

**Clinical features** include rash, eosinophilia, and WBC casts. Eosinophils in the urine are not specific or sensitive for diagnosis. Instead of ordering urine eosinophils, if there is high suspicion for AIN, stop the culprit medication and consider a kidney biopsy.

#### Acute Glomerulonephritis

**Etiologies** include several causes listed under nephrotic and nephritic syndromes.

**Clinical features** include proteinuria, hypertension, hematuria, and RBC casts.

### Postrenal AKI (Obstruction of Urinary Tract)

**Etiology** includes benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), malignancy, strictures, stones, functional urinary retention, and congenital abnormalities. Typically, AKI is caused by bilateral obstruction.

**Clinical features** include increased post-void residual volume on ultrasound and hydronephrosis on ultrasound or CT.

### General Approach to Diagnosis and Management of AKI

1. **Address urgent management needs**: Treat severe hyperkalemia, hypoxemia due to pulmonary edema, or other immediately life-threatening issues.
2. **Confirm baseline creatinine and diagnose the etiology**:
   - Review the differential diagnosis above.
   - Volume exam suggesting hypovolemia may indicate a prerenal cause.
   - Obtain a bladder scan to rule out postrenal causes.
   - Get a UA with microscopy.
   - Consider checking the fractional excretion of sodium (FENa), which can help differentiate prerenal AKI from ATN.
   - Most AKI in the hospital will be prerenal (due to hypovolemia), intrarenal (ATN), or postrenal (obstruction). A majority of inpatient AKI can be resolved with fluids and/or a Foley catheter.
3. **Treat the underlying cause** (e.g., give fluids if prerenal; place Foley if postrenal).
4. **Monitor for complications**: Check electrolytes, evaluate volume status, reassess for uremia regularly. AKI can cause CKD progression in patients with baseline kidney dysfunction.
5. **Avoid factors that may worsen AKI**: Avoid nephrotoxic medications and adjust dosing of renally excreted medications.

## Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD)

### Definitions

**CKD** is defined as any of the following signs of kidney disease lasting more than 3 months:

- GFR less than 60 mL/min/1.73 m²
- Albuminuria greater than 30 mg/g or 30 mg/24 hr
- Urine sediment suggesting kidney damage (e.g., tubular casts, hematuria with dysmorphic RBCs)
- Electrolyte abnormalities attributed to tubular disease
- Abnormalities on kidney biopsy histology
- Structural kidney disease on imaging
- History of kidney transplantation

**ESRD** typically refers to patients with CKD who have either:

- GFR less than 15 mL/min/m²
- Requirement for long-term dialysis or history of kidney transplant

### CKD Stages

**GFR Categories**:

| Stage | eGFR (mL/min/1.73 m²) |
|---|---|
| G1 | Greater than 90 |
| G2 | 60–90 |
| G3a | 45–59 |
| G3b | 30–44 |
| G4 | 15–29 |
| G5 | Less than 15 |

**Albuminuria Categories**:

| Stage | UACR (mg/g) |
|---|---|
| A1 | Less than 30 |
| A2 | 30–300 |
| A3 | Greater than 300 |

### Etiologies

**Systemic disease**: Diabetes, hypertension, glomerulonephritis due to autoimmune or infectious or inflammatory disease, polycystic kidney disease, malignancy, nephrotoxin exposure.

**Primary kidney disease**: Primary glomerulonephritis, renal artery stenosis, chronic urinary obstruction, congenital kidney disease.

### Treatment

- Treat the underlying cause of kidney disease.
- Prevent progression of kidney damage.
- Avoid nephrotoxic medications and adjust dosing of renally excreted medications. NSAIDs are a particularly common nephrotoxic medication. Most antibiotics need dose adjustment for decreased renal function.
- Manage complications (see below).
- Prepare for renal replacement when GFR is severely decreased (see dialysis section).
- Consider using a tool to predict the probability of dialysis or transplant for patients with CKD stage G3 to G5.

- KDIGO Work Group. KDIGO 2012 clinical practice guideline for the evaluation and management of chronic kidney disease. Kidney Int Suppl. 2013;3(1):1–150.

## Dialysis

### Types of Dialysis

**Hemodialysis (HD)**: Blood is pumped along a semipermeable membrane across which electrolytes, water, and toxins are removed by diffusion into dialysate on the other side of the membrane. Water is filtered across the membrane by pressure. Usually performed over 2–4 hours, three times per week in a dialysis center.

- **Pros**: Frequent contact with providers; can be initiated emergently.
- **Cons**: Hypotension; circuit needs anticoagulation; requires vascular access.

**Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT)**: Continuous venovenous hemodialysis (CVVHD) is performed continuously. Indicated for hemodynamically unstable patients requiring dialysis (e.g., patients requiring pressors). Only performed in the ICU.

**Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)**: The peritoneum is used as a semipermeable membrane for removal of water and toxins from the blood via diffusion into intraperitoneal dialysate.

- **Pros**: Performed at home; "gentler" fluid and electrolyte shifts.
- **Cons**: Increased risk of hyperglycemia, peritonitis, and catheter-associated infections; several contraindications related to abdominal anatomy.

### Indications for Dialysis

**Acute indications** ("AEIOU"):

- **A**cidosis
- **E**lectrolytes (e.g., hyperkalemia)
- **I**ntoxication (e.g., methanol, lithium, aspirin, ethylene glycol)
- **V**olume Overload
- **U**remia

**Indications for dialysis in CKD**: Development of signs and symptoms of uremia (especially malnutrition) and complications of CKD that become refractory to medical management.

### Dialysis Access

- **Arteriovenous fistula (AVF)** (preferred): Needs 3–4 months to mature after placement; lowest infectious risk.
- **Arteriovenous graft (AVG)**: Typically needs 1 month to mature (newer grafts can be accessed in 2–3 days); moderate infectious risk.
- **Central venous catheter**: Can access immediately after placement; may be a temporary catheter or a permanent tunneled dialysis catheter placed by interventional radiology; highest infectious risk.
- **Peritoneal dialysis catheters**: Placed by general surgery, interventional radiology, or interventional nephrology laparoscopically. Ideally, allow 2 weeks for surgical wound healing to avoid dialysate leaks; however, "urgent start peritoneal dialysis" can be initiated with low volume, supine exchanges to avoid temporary venous catheter placement.

### Renal Transplant

- **Referral timing**: Usually when eGFR is between 20 and 30 mL/min/1.73 m².
- **Evaluation**: Includes months-long extensive biopsychosocial assessment.
- **Contraindications**: Generally contraindicated if there is concern for limited medical prognosis or ability to adhere to medical treatment.
- **Waiting time**: For living and deceased donor transplants, ranges from 1–5 years depending on the region.

## Management of Complications of Advanced CKD/ESRD

### Blood Pressure and Volume

**Pathophysiology of hypertension in CKD** is complex and multifactorial, attributed to reduced nephron mass, increased Na⁺ retention, extracellular volume expansion, activation of RAAS and the sympathetic nervous system, and endothelial dysfunction.

**Goal blood pressure**: Generally less than 130/80 mmHg. This may be more liberal when accounting for certain factors such as degree of kidney disease, dialysis tolerance, polypharmacy, adverse effects of medication, and history of transplant.

**Treatment**:

- **Sodium restriction** is a key element of blood pressure control.
- **Fluid restriction** becomes more important when GFR is severely reduced. Patients should monitor oral intake and weight.
- **Dry weight** is estimated by exam and maintained through diuresis or ultrafiltration during dialysis.

**Pharmacotherapy**:

- **Diuretics**: If a patient is still urinating, diuretics can assist with volume management. Diuretics depend on reaching active concentrations in the tubule lumen; higher doses are required with worsening renal function as urea competitively binds to Organic Anion Transporter 1 (OAT1). Loop diuretics are often necessary if eGFR is less than 30 mL/min/1.73 m² (efficacy order: bumetanide > furosemide > metolazone > chlorthalidone > hydrochlorothiazide).
- **ACE inhibitors/ARBs**: First-line antihypertensives in CKD, especially with proteinuria and relatively preserved native kidney function. Can cause hyperkalemia. An increase in creatinine after starting these agents is not necessarily indicative of kidney injury; generally should not be discontinued unless eGFR decreases by more than 30%. Combination ACE/ARB is associated with increased renal decline and generally contraindicated (ONTARGET study, Lancet 2008).
- **Calcium channel blockers (CCBs)**: Do not require dose modification for renal clearance; relatively few adverse effects; can cause lower extremity edema. Preferred in CKD after ACE/ARB and diuretics; can reduce proteinuria.
- **Beta-blockers**: Typically used if secondary prevention of cardiac events is warranted. Agents with combined alpha/beta antagonism are preferred (e.g., carvedilol, labetalol). Non-renal clearance is preferred.
- **Clonidine**: No renal dose adjustment; often used for hypertension on days between dialysis sessions. Associated with rebound hypertension when discontinued or if doses are missed.

### Anemia

**Pathophysiology**: Results from decreased erythropoietin (EPO) production, EPO resistance, and decreased RBC lifespan.

**Treatment**:

- **Goal hemoglobin**: 10–11.5 g/dL.
- **Iron studies**: Check iron studies in CKD patients with anemia. Guidelines recommend transferrin saturation greater than 30% and ferritin greater than 500 ng/mL, though practice patterns vary and less stringent targets may be used (e.g., transferrin saturation greater than 20% and ferritin greater than 100 ng/mL). Ferritin can be elevated with inflammatory states as well. Rule out GI bleeding if iron deficiency is detected.
- **Iron supplementation**: IV iron is preferred in patients on hemodialysis. Oral supplementation causes constipation, adds to pill burden, and is minimally absorbed compared to IV formulations. Iron sucrose (Venofer) and ferric gluconate (Ferrlecit) are the most commonly used formulations. IV iron can cause anaphylaxis when first given; provide a test dose and administer slowly for patients who are IV iron-naive.
- **Erythropoietin-stimulating agents (ESAs)**: Give if patient is iron replete but remains below hemoglobin goal. Three formulations:
  - Epoetin alfa (Epogen): 3 times per week during HD or weekly in non-HD CKD
  - Darbepoetin alfa (Aranesp): Weekly during HD or monthly in non-HD CKD
  - Methoxy polyethylene glycol-epoetin beta (Mircera): Every 2 weeks
- **RBC transfusion**: Consider transfusion if ESAs are contraindicated or unsuccessful. Administer pRBCs if anemia is symptomatic or severe (hemoglobin less than 7 g/dL).

### Bone Mineral Metabolism

**Pathophysiology**: Mineral bone disorder is due to secondary hyperparathyroidism, with two main contributing factors:

1. Decreased 1-alpha-hydroxylase (made by the kidney) leads to decreased activation of 25-hydroxy vitamin D to 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D, leading to decreased Ca²⁺ absorption, decreased serum Ca²⁺ level, and increased PTH.
2. Poor phosphate excretion leads to elevated phosphate, increased PTH, and decreased 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D.

**Manifestations of CKD mineral bone disorder**:

- **Mineral and hormone abnormalities**: Increased phosphate, increased PTH, decreased 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D
- **Structural bone abnormalities**: Renal osteodystrophy (includes osteomalacia, adynamic bone disease, and osteitis fibrosa cystica)
- **Increased alkaline phosphatase**: Reflects osteoblast activity and bone turnover
- **Increased FGF23**: Secreted by osteocytes, involved in phosphate regulation
- **Calcification** of blood vessels and soft tissue

**Management of CKD mineral bone disorder**:

#### Phosphate

Phosphate levels should ideally be within the normal range, though levels less than 5.5 mg/dL are often tolerated.

**Phosphate binders** are used to lower phosphate by preventing absorption of dietary phosphate:

| Binder Type | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium-based | Calcium acetate (Phoslo), Calcium carbonate (Tums) | Calcium acetate 667 mg/tablet is strong and cheap |
| Non-calcium-based | Sevelamer (Renvela), Lanthanum carbonate (Carbrenol) | Sevelamer preferred by some due to absence of calcium |
| Iron-based | Ferric citrate, Sucroferric oxyhydroxide | May be preferred if iron supplementation is needed |

#### Calcium

Keep corrected Ca²⁺ within the normal range. Dialysate can be adjusted to address low or high Ca²⁺ levels. Consider measuring ionized calcium (iCa) in patients with significant hypoalbuminemia, acidemia, or hyperphosphatemia.

#### Vitamin D

All CKD patients should be screened and treated for vitamin D deficiency or insufficiency (check 25-hydroxy vitamin D level). The 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D is not routinely measured in practice. Treat with cholecalciferol or ergocalciferol unless hypercalcemia or significant hyperphosphatemia is present.

#### PTH

Guidelines recommend that PTH remain within 2–9 times the upper limit of normal (approximately 150–600 pg/mL) in hemodialysis patients. Practice varies and treatment in non-dialysis patients is controversial.

**Treatment** for severely elevated or rising PTH with replete 25-hydroxy vitamin D includes:

- **Active vitamin D analogs**: Calcitriol (Rocaltrol) for non-dialysis patients; Paricalcitol (Zemplar) and Doxicalciferol (Hectorol) administered IV, usually with hemodialysis.
- **Calcimimetics**: Competitive binders of the Ca²⁺-sensing receptor, which regulates PTH release. Usually used when active vitamin D analogs are inadequate or contraindicated due to hypercalcemia. Side effects include hypocalcemia and nausea/vomiting. Examples include Cinacalcet (Sensipar, oral) and Etelcalcetide (Parsabiv, IV, given with hemodialysis).
- **Parathyroidectomy**: Reserved for severe and refractory hyperparathyroidism in CKD.

## Nephrotic Syndrome

### Overview

Glomerular diseases are divided into two patterns (nephrotic and nephritic) based on clinical features including urine sediment and degree of proteinuria. Either syndrome can be caused by diseases that only affect the kidney (primary) or systemic diseases (secondary).

### Pathophysiology

Nephrotic syndrome is a syndrome of abnormal glomerular permeability and consequent proteinuria with associated symptoms and metabolic abnormalities.

### Clinical Features

- **Nephrotic-range proteinuria**: Greater than 3 g/day or UPCR greater than 3 g/g, which leads to negative nitrogen balance and muscle wasting (may be obscured by edema)
- **Edema**: Due to decreased oncotic pressure (hypoalbuminemia) and increased Na⁺ absorption in the distal nephron; often includes morning periorbital edema; treated with dietary sodium restriction and loop diuretics
- **Hypercoagulability**: Due to many causes including protein C and S deficiency and compensatory increase in procoagulant factor production by the liver; most commonly manifests as lower extremity DVT; PE and renal vein thrombosis are other important manifestations; prophylactic anticoagulation is considered when serum albumin is less than 2 g/dL
- **Hyperlipidemia**: Clinical significance is controversial; patients with nephrotic syndrome have significantly increased cardiovascular events; statin medications may be given
- **Increased infection risk**: Patients with nephrotic syndrome should receive the pneumococcal vaccine

### Glomerular Pathologies Associated with Nephrotic-Range Proteinuria

#### Minimal Change Glomerulopathy (MCG)

- **Epidemiology**: Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children; less common in adults
- **Pathophysiology**: Defined by nephrotic-range proteinuria and kidney biopsy with little or no change to glomerular structure on light microscopy. Primary MCD is idiopathic. Secondary MCD may be associated with atopic disease, mononucleosis, Hodgkin's lymphoma, and medications (e.g., NSAIDs, interferon, lithium).
- **Treatment**: Steroids for 4–8 weeks; good prognosis.

#### Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

- **Epidemiology**: Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in Black adults
- **Pathophysiology**: Defined by proteinuria and scarring in scattered glomeruli on biopsy. Primary FSGS may result from genetic mutations in podocyte proteins. Secondary FSGS is associated with HIV ("collapsing" type), obesity, and heroin use.
- **Treatment**: Steroids or calcineurin inhibitors; 40–60% achieve remission; others have refractory disease and often advance to ESRD. Can recur in the transplanted kidney and is thought to be due to a soluble factor. Plasmapheresis can be utilized to remove this soluble factor to help prevent recurrence.

#### Membranous Glomerulopathy (MGN)

- **Epidemiology**: Second most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in U.S. adults (after diabetic nephropathy)
- **Pathophysiology**: Primary MGN is associated with anti-phospholipase A2 receptor (anti-PLA2R) antibody. Causes of secondary MGN include SLE, hepatitis B, solid tumor malignancy (lung, breast, and GI carcinomas), and medications (penicillamine, NSAIDs, mercury, captopril). Diagnosis requires kidney biopsy showing deposits of IgG and C3.
- **Clinical features**: Higher propensity for thromboembolic events (particularly renal vein thrombosis).
- **Treatment**: Up to one-third of patients experience spontaneous remission. Treatment with immunosuppression is considered for patients with a poor prognosis:
  - Less than 4 g protein/day: Good prognosis for remission
  - 4–8 g protein/day: Possible chance of remission
  - Greater than 8 g protein/day: Poor chance of remission; start treatment immediately

### Systemic Disorders Associated with Nephrotic-Range Proteinuria

**Diabetes**: Leading cause of glomerular disease and kidney failure in the United States. Hyperglycemia causes hyperfiltration through the glomeruli, leading to strain, scarring, proteinuria, and eventually decreased eGFR. Glycemic control and treatment of proteinuria may slow CKD progression.

**Amyloidosis**: Abnormally-folded proteins deposit in the glomeruli, resulting in impaired function and proteinuria. Dialysis-related amyloidosis is not typically a cause of nephrotic syndrome.

**HIV-Associated Nephropathy (HIVAN)**: Manifests as hematuria, proteinuria, edema with enlarged kidneys. Collapsing FSGS on pathology. Glucocorticoids for treatment are controversial.

**Lupus Nephritis**: Divided into six subclasses (Class I–VI) based on histological appearance. Nephrotic syndrome typically seen in Class III to VI.

| Class | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Minimal mesangial LN (no changes on light microscopy) |
| II | Mesangial proliferative LN (mesangial changes on light microscopy) |
| III | Focal LN (less than 50% of glomeruli involved) |
| IV | Diffuse LN (greater than 50% of glomeruli involved) |
| V | Lupus membranous nephropathy (diffuse capillary wall thickening) |
| VI | Advanced sclerosing LN (90% sclerosing glomeruli, often bland urine sediment) |

## Nephritic Syndrome

### Pathophysiology

Nephritic syndrome is a syndrome of symptoms and findings caused by inflammation of glomeruli.

### Clinical Features

- Oliguria/AKI
- Hematuria with RBC casts
- Subnephrotic range proteinuria (less than 3 g/24 hr)
- Edema
- Hypertension

### Glomerular Pathology Associated with Nephritic Syndrome

#### Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis

- **Epidemiology**: More common in children
- **Pathophysiology**: Glomerular damage is due to deposition of antistreptococcal antibodies in the glomeruli. Hematuria, hypertension, and edema begin 1–3 weeks after infection with Streptococcus bacteria.
- **Diagnosis**: Antistreptolysin O may be elevated; low complement.
- **Treatment**: Supportive with antihypertensives and loop diuretics; steroids if severe. Majority of cases self-resolve in weeks to months.

#### IgA Nephropathy (Berger Disease)

- **Pathophysiology**: Caused by deposition of dysregulated IgA immune complexes.
- **Clinical features**: Usually immediately preceded by or co-presenting with respiratory or GI infection ("synpharyngitic" hematuria as opposed to post-streptococcal GN, which occurs weeks after pharyngitis). Can also be "silent" and go undetected for years. Manifests as episodic gross hematuria (more common in patients less than 25 years old) or persistent microscopic hematuria (more common in patients greater than 25 years old). Can be primary or secondary to systemic IgA vasculitis.
- **Diagnosis**: Mesangial deposits of predominantly IgA immune complexes on biopsy. Complement levels are often normal.
- **Treatment**: No effective treatment; some advocate for steroids or fish oil. Most patients have good prognosis.

#### Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis

- **Pathophysiology**: Hypercellularity and thickening of glomerular basement membrane (GMB) on light microscopy.
  - **Type I (subendothelial GN)**: Immune complex-mediated; typically caused by infections (HBV, HCV, endocarditis, schistosomiasis)
  - **Type II (dense deposit disease)**: Complement-mediated; rare; typically in children but also occurs in adults with monoclonal gammopathy
- **Treatment**: Treat underlying cause; immunosuppression with steroids if idiopathic.

#### Rapidly Progressive (Crescentic) Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)

- **Clinical features**: Acute decline in renal function over days to weeks due to severe glomerular injury.
- **Diagnosis**: Light microscopy with crescents in greater than 50% of glomeruli due to fibrin formation and macrophages.

**Pathology and immunopathology** distinguish between three major etiologies:

| Type | Etiology | Immunofluorescence | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anti-GBM disease | Autoantibody to type IV collagen; called Goodpasture's syndrome when kidney and lung are involved | Linear | Cyclophosphamide, steroids, plasmapheresis |
| Immune complex disease | Systemic infections (poststreptococcal GN, infective endocarditis), systemic immune complex disease (IgA vasculitis, SLE), or primary GN (IgA nephropathy) | Granular | Treat underlying cause |
| Pauci-immune disease | Small vessel vasculitis (GPA, EGPA, MPA); can be drug-induced (penicillamine, hydralazine) | Absence or paucity of glomerular staining | Immunosuppression |

Approximately 80% of pauci-immune cases are ANCA-positive.

## Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA)

### RTA Type I (Distal)

- **Pathophysiology**: Defect in collecting tubule's ability to excrete H⁺ due to congenital causes, multiple myeloma, autoimmunity, or amphotericin B toxicity.
- **Labs**: Urine pH greater than 5.5; hypokalemia (secretes K⁺ instead of H⁺); non-anion gap metabolic acidosis.
- **Clinical features**: Increased risk of kidney stones (increased calcium and phosphate excretion into alkaline urine).
- **Treatment**: Sodium bicarbonate to correct acidosis; phosphate salts promote excretion of titratable acid.

### RTA Type II (Proximal)

- **Pathophysiology**: Carbonic anhydrase mutation leads to HCO₃⁻ not being reabsorbed. Associated with multiple myeloma (rule out), lead poisoning, amyloid, and Fanconi syndrome.
- **Labs**: Urine pH less than 5.5; hypokalemia; non-anion gap metabolic acidosis. Fanconi syndrome includes glycosuria.
- **Clinical features**: No risk of kidney stones like Type I; risk of hypophosphatemic rickets.
- **Treatment**: Sodium restriction increases Na⁺ resorption in proximal tubule with bicarbonate absorbed as well. Do not give bicarbonate because it will be excreted.

### RTA Type IV (Hyperkalemic)

- **Pathophysiology**: Hypoaldosteronism or lack of collecting tubule response to aldosterone; common with interstitial renal diagnosis or diabetic nephropathy.
- **Labs**: Acidic urine; hyperkalemia; non-anion gap metabolic acidosis. No stones.

## Other Diseases Affecting Renal Tubular Absorption

### Renal Papillary Necrosis

- **Etiology**: Poor medullary perfusion resulting in ischemia and necrosis. Most often bilateral. Caused by vascular disease (diabetes), vasoconstriction (NSAIDs), or vascular occlusion (sickle cell).
- **Treatment**: Stop offending agent; treat underlying disease.

### Hartnup Syndrome

- **Etiology**: Rare autosomal recessive inborn error of amino acid metabolism. Impaired amino acid absorption in the gut and proximal renal tubule causing variable losses of proteins (e.g., tryptophan).
- **Clinical features**: Variable; can be similar to pellagra: dermatitis, diarrhea, ataxia, psychiatric findings.
- **Diagnosis**: Elevated amino acids in the urine.
- **Treatment**: Supplemental nicotinamide if symptomatic.

### Fanconi Syndrome

- **Etiology**: Primary (idiopathic) or secondary proximal tubule dysfunction causing defective transport of glucose, amino acids, Na⁺, K⁺, phosphorus, uric acid, and bicarbonate. Causes include genetic (Wilson's disease, galactosemia), acquired damage from heavy metals, connective tissue disorders, hematologic malignancies (monoclonal gammopathy), and medications (tenofovir, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors).
- **Clinical features**: Glucosuria, phosphaturia (rickets, osteomalacia), proteinuria, polyuria, dehydration, hypercalciuria, hypokalemia.
- **Treatment**: Treat underlying cause if possible. Electrolyte and vitamin D supplementation may be required.

## Renal Cystic Diseases

### Classification

Renal cysts are categorized as simple or complex based on imaging appearance:

- **Simple cysts** (thin-walled, fluid-filled, no enhancement) are very common with advancing age. Typically benign, but may need intervention if size or location causes pain or impaired renal function.
- **Complex cysts** (enhancement, solid components) have a high risk of malignancy; may require partial or total nephrectomy for tissue examination.

### Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)

- **Etiology**: Mutation in PKD1 or PKD2 (chromosome 16); most common hereditary kidney disease in the United States.
- **Clinical features**: Onset of hypertension, hematuria, and nephrolithiasis in young adulthood (PKD1) or middle age (PKD2). Increased risk for extrarenal diagnoses: Circle of Willis berry aneurysm (risk factor for subarachnoid hemorrhage), hepatic cysts, diverticula, abdominal hernias, and consequences of hypertension.
- **Diagnosis**: Combination of family history, genetic testing, and ultrasound showing bilateral kidneys with cysts.
- **Treatment**: No curative treatment. Control hypertension; manage pain (from cyst rupture or pressure). The majority of patients will require dialysis or kidney transplant.

## Renal Vascular Diseases

### Renal Artery Stenosis

- **Etiology**: Majority of cases are due to atherosclerosis; most others are due to fibromuscular dysplasia (often in young women, 50% bilateral, can be hereditary).
- **Pathophysiology**: Renal artery narrowing and subsequent RAAS activation causes renovascular hypertension.
- **Clinical features**: Early or medication-refractory hypertension (uncontrolled on three antihypertensives including a diuretic); abdominal bruits on exam.
- **Diagnosis**: Duplex ultrasound, CTA, or MRA.
- **Treatment**: Treat hypertension with ACE inhibitor/ARB plus diuretic. Consider revascularization with stent or endarterectomy if there is high chance of benefit or high risk of complications (e.g., rapid progression, recurrent flash pulmonary edema, medical therapy not tolerated or ineffective, bilateral disease with declining kidney function).

### Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis

- **Etiology**: Sclerosis of the tubulointerstitial, glomerular, and vascular spaces due to genetic predisposition and chronic hypertension.
- **Clinical features**: Presents with two distinct clinical courses:
  - **Benign nephrosclerosis**: Most often seen in African Americans with high-risk APOL1 genotypes, preceding hypertension, thickening of glomerular afferent arterioles; presents as mild creatinine elevation, microscopic hematuria, and mild proteinuria.
  - **Malignant nephrosclerosis**: Rapid decrease in renal function with severe hypertension with markedly elevated BP (papilledema, cardiac decompensation, CNS findings), renal abnormalities (rapid creatinine increase, proteinuria, hematuria, RBC/WBC casts, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia).
- **Treatment**: Control blood pressure.

### Renal Vein Thrombosis

- **Etiology**: Can result from systemic inflammation or prothrombotic state (e.g., nephrotic syndrome, carcinoma, trauma, pregnancy/OCPs), extrinsic compression, or trauma.
- **Clinical features**: Evidence of decreased renal perfusion (rising creatinine, decreased urine output, hypertension) and renal injury (hematuria, proteinuria).
- **Diagnosis**: Duplex ultrasound, CTA, MRA.
- **Treatment**: Consider thrombolysis/thrombectomy or anticoagulation based on clinical severity.

### Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)

- **Etiology**: Syndrome of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, low platelets, and organ injury; most often due to TTP and atypical HUS; can also result from HUS, APLS, scleroderma renal crisis, malignant hypertension, or pregnancy (HELLP/preeclampsia).
- **Diagnosis**: Thrombi and fibrin accumulation in capillaries on glomerular biopsy.
- **Treatment**: Treat underlying cause. Plasma exchange if TTP.

## Stones and Obstructions

### Nephrolithiasis (Kidney Stones)

#### Types

| Type | Frequency | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Calcium stones** | 80% | Radiodense; calcium oxalate (90%, envelope shape, malabsorption) or calcium phosphate (10%, hyperparathyroidism, RTA) |
| **Uric acid stones** | 10% | Radiolucent; flat square plates; due to hyperuricemia (gout, tumor lysis syndrome) |
| **Struvite stones** | 5–10% | Radiodense; rectangular prisms, staghorn morphology; recurrent UTIs by protease-producing organisms (Proteus, Klebsiella, Serratia, Enterobacter convert urea to ammonia, which combines with magnesium or phosphorus to form struvite calculi) |
| **Cystine stones** | 1% | Poorly visualized by x-ray; hexagon-shaped crystals; screen with urinary cyanide nitroprusside |

#### Clinical Features

Acute renal colic (sudden onset, inability to sit still, cramping abdominal/flank pain in waves), nausea/vomiting, dysuria, with or without fever/chills.

#### Diagnosis

- **UA with microscopy**: Evaluate for microscopic/gross hematuria and crystals.
- **Imaging**: Non-contrast CT abdomen/pelvis if nephrolithiasis has not been previously established (superior sensitivity and specificity). Ultrasound is useful to avoid radiation in patients who may be pregnant.

#### Treatment

- **Acute treatment**: IV fluids, pain control (NSAIDs preferred over morphine), medical expulsive therapy with an alpha-blocker.
- **Urgent stone removal required if**: Stone unlikely to pass (greater than 10 mm), infection (UTI, sepsis), severe symptoms (intractable pain or nausea/vomiting).
- If stone is not removed urgently, consider follow-up imaging in 14 days to monitor stone position.

#### Prevention

- Adequate fluid intake (goal greater than 2 L urine output/24 hr).
- If high risk for stone recurrence (large stone burden, non-calcium stone, abnormal urological anatomy, history of GI disease or bariatric surgery, family history), refer for metabolic assessment.
- Almost all patients with nephrolithiasis should adhere to a low-sodium diet, which reduces calcium excretion.
- Pharmacologic treatments include thiazides for hypercalciuria, allopurinol for hyperuricemia, and alkali supplementation for hypocitraturia.

### Urinary Tract Obstruction

- **Etiology**:
  - **Lower tract**: Neurogenic bladder (e.g., due to diabetes), bladder cancer, BPH, prostate cancer, urethral stricture, blood, stone, trauma.
  - **Upper tract**: Intrinsic (kidney stones, blood clots, sloughed papillae, tumor, ureteral stricture) or extrinsic (pregnancy, tumor, abdominal aortic aneurysm, retroperitoneal fibrosis, endometriosis, hematomas, IBD, diverticulitis).
- **Clinical features**: Renal colic and pain (especially if acute), oliguria, recurrent UTIs, hematuria/proteinuria, renal failure.
- **Diagnosis**: Renal ultrasound. Advanced diagnostics include intravenous pyelography (IVP) for ureteral obstruction, voiding cystourethrography for lower tract obstruction, and cystoscopy for bladder.
- **Treatment**:
  - **Lower tract**: Foley catheter, BPH medications (e.g., alpha-blockers; 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor).
  - **Upper tract**: Ureteral stent via cystoscopy if possible, otherwise percutaneous nephrostomy tube.

## Sodium

### Overview

For each electrolyte, we discuss normal regulation followed by disorders of high and low levels.

**Sodium homeostasis** (Na⁺ 135–145 mmol/L): Extracellular Na⁺ concentration reflects water homeostasis, so hyponatremia and hypernatremia are caused by too much or too little water. Total Na⁺ content reflects Na⁺ homeostasis. Sodium is actively pumped out of cells; it is the main cation in extracellular fluid (ECF), and most sodium reabsorption occurs at the proximal tubule.

**RAAS in sodium homeostasis**: Decreased ECF volume leads to decreased renal perfusion, which increases renin release by juxtaglomerular (JGA) cells. ACE activates angiotensin II. Angiotensin II increases ADH and aldosterone secretion, which increases net Na⁺ reabsorption, and water is absorbed with Na⁺. Aldosterone is secreted by the adrenal gland: Na⁺/H₂O is reabsorbed, and H⁺/K⁺ is secreted.

**Water homeostasis (ADH)**: Increased plasma osmolarity stimulates osmoregulators in the hypothalamus to stimulate thirst and secretion of ADH. ADH increases H₂O permeability at principal cells, promoting water absorption and stimulating thirst.

## Hyponatremia (Na⁺ Less Than 135 mEq/L)

### Etiologies

*(See Figure 6.4 for a workup strategy.)*

### History

Key elements include:

- **Acuity**: Has hyponatremia occurred within the last 48 hours (e.g., postoperatively with normal preoperative baseline)?
- **Volume**: Fluid intake, diet, urinary changes, extrarenal losses (e.g., diarrhea, hemorrhage)
- **PMH**: Active renal, endocrine, pulmonary, or neurologic disease
- **Medications**: Especially diuretics and recent medication changes
- **Social history**: Use of alcohol, MDMA, other illicit substances

### Clinical Features

Signs and symptoms are predominantly neurologic: headache, delirium, hyperactive deep tendon reflexes, seizures, and coma.

### Diagnosis

*(See Figure 6.4 for a diagnostic flowchart.)*

**Step 1: Is sodium the problem (is blood hypotonic)?**

Check serum osmolality.

- **High serum osmolality** (>295 mOsm/kg): Hypertonic hyponatremia. Low sodium is an appropriate response to excess osmotic substrate (e.g., glucose, mannitol, maltose/sucrose in IVIG). Correct for hyperglycemia: for every 100 mg/dL increase in glucose above 100 mg/dL, add 1.6 mEq/L to Na⁺.
- **Normal serum osmolality** (280–295 mOsm/kg): Pseudohyponatremia (normal Na⁺ in water but higher-than-normal amount of another non-aqueous substance such as paraprotein, lipids, or bilirubin). May also be due to a mixed hypotonic and hypertonic process.
- **Low serum osmolality** (<280 mOsm/kg): True hyponatremia. Proceed to Step 2.

If measured serum osmolality is normal but calculated osmolality is low, a solute not included in the calculated estimate is present (e.g., ethanol or toxic alcohol).

**Step 2: Is the kidney responding appropriately (is ADH off)?**

Check urine osmolality. ADH causes increased reabsorption of free water at the collecting ducts. In hyponatremia, ADH should be repressed if kidneys are responding appropriately to eliminate excess free water.

- **If urine osmolality less than 100 mOsm/kg**: The problem is not in the kidney; it relates to water or salt consumption.
  - Too much water (primary polydipsia, 12–20 L/day): Psychosis, ecstasy, marathon running. Will produce copious urine in the absence of extrarenal fluid loss.
  - Too little solute ("beer potomania"/"tea and toast diet"): In this case, the kidney cannot produce as much dilute urine to remove excess free water as someone with normal solute intake. This is often a contributor in patients with concomitant hypovolemia.
- **If urine osmolality 100 mOsm/kg or greater**: Proceed to Step 3.

> **Pearl**: If intermediate urine osmolality—or even if low—consider tubular dysfunction due to diuretics, AKI, or CKD. In severe renal disease, the kidney cannot concentrate urine appropriately, which may cause hyponatremia. Isosthenuria (urine specific gravity similar to serum concentration of 1.006–1.012) may be an early clue.

**Step 3: Is ADH "on" for a hemodynamic reason?**

Check volume status and urine Na⁺.

- **Hypovolemia**: Leads to RAAS activation and ADH "on," increasing renal free H₂O absorption. Signs include orthostatic hypotension, dry mucous membranes, and decreased skin turgor. Typically urine Na⁺ less than 20 mEq/L if extrarenal losses (e.g., vomiting, diarrhea, third-spacing). Urine Na⁺ may be higher if patient is on diuretics or has tubular dysfunction (e.g., ATN, advanced CKD).
- **Hypervolemic**: ADH "on" due to perceived low volume at the JGA (e.g., heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome). Typically urine Na⁺ less than 20 mEq/L. Urine Na⁺ may be higher in advanced CKD or while taking diuretics.

**Step 4: Is ADH "on" for a non-hemodynamic reason?**

Consider euvolemic etiologies:

- **"Inappropriate" causes of ADH activity**: Syndrome of inappropriate ADH (SIADH)
- **Diseases**: CNS, pulmonary, malignancy (especially SCLC)
- **Medications**: SSRIs, antiepileptic drugs, chemotherapy
- **Cortical stimulation**: Pain, nausea, psychosis
- **Endocrine**: Hypothyroidism, mineralocorticoid deficiency, glucocorticoid deficiency

### Treatment of Hyponatremia

**General principles**: Do not correct too quickly unless acute (confirmed duration less than 48 hours, usually postoperatively) or presenting with severe symptoms (seizure, coma). Overcorrection can cause central pontine demyelination. The goal for correction is 6–8 mEq/24 hours. Do not correct sodium more than 8 mEq/24 hours if hyponatremia has been present for greater than 48 hours or for unknown duration.

- If Na⁺ less than 120 mEq/L: Consider nephrology consult; check Na⁺ every 2–4 hours when beginning correction.
- If severe symptoms or Na⁺ less than 115 mEq/L: Nephrology consult; check Na⁺ every 12 hours when beginning correction.

**Electrolyte-free water clearance (EFWC)** can estimate urinary free water excretion:

EFWC = Urine output × [1 − (Urine Na⁺ + Urine K⁺ / P) / Plasma Na⁺]

- If negative: Kidneys are concentrating urine and hypertonic saline will likely be needed.
- If positive: Kidney is correcting and hyponatremia may improve with isotonic fluids or salt tabs.
- Assumes consistent urine concentration (usually true in SIADH, usually not true in hypovolemia or heart failure).

> **Pearl**: Giving potassium will increase serum sodium because potassium is an exchangeable ion that will be transported intracellularly in exchange for sodium. Account for this when calculating fluids for sodium correction to avoid overcorrection.

**Treatment by volume status**:

- **Hypovolemia**: If mild, trial IV fluids and recheck. Monitor urine free water output and recheck serum sodium frequently to avoid overcorrection. May need to give DDAVP to slow correction after volume repletion if overcorrecting. If hyponatremia is severe (due to magnitude or symptoms) and hemodynamically stable, gentle volume resuscitation with hypertonic saline and DDAVP may be done, especially if SIADH is still on differential, but this should be done in consultation with nephrology.
- **Euvolemia** (often due to diuretics or SIADH): Address underlying cause. Free water restriction. Sodium tabs with loop diuretics or oral urea. Consider demeclocycline or vasopressin antagonists (tolvaptan, conivaptan) if refractory to conservative measures.
- **Hypervolemic**: Free water restriction. Diuresis with loop diuretics may be helpful and is often necessary. Vasopressin antagonists may be useful in heart failure but can cause liver injury in patients with cirrhosis.

### Complication of Rapid Sodium Correction

**Central pontine demyelination** is caused by osmotic shift due to sodium correction in the brain that has adapted to a hypotonic environment over 1–2 days. Clinical effects usually occur days after sodium overcorrection.

**High-risk populations**: Very low sodium less than 110 mEq/L, concurrent hypokalemia, liver disease, malnourished patients, and patients with heavy alcohol use.

## Hypernatremia (Na⁺ Greater Than 145 mEq/L)

### Etiologies

**Extrarenal water imbalance** (urine osmolality greater than 600 mOsm/kg):

- Inadequate free water intake (e.g., due to altered mental status, inadequate fluid replacement)
- Increased sodium intake (e.g., IV sodium bicarbonate, tube feeds, TPN)
- Extrarenal free water loss (e.g., vomiting, diarrhea)

**Renal free water loss**:

- Diuresis due to medications (usually loop diuretics) or osmotic diuresis (hyperglycemia, azotemia, mannitol)
- Diabetes insipidus (DI): Inadequate ADH activity; urine osmolality less than 600 mOsm/kg.
  - **Nephrogenic DI**: Kidney is ADH-resistant; urine osmolality less than 300 mOsm/kg; does not respond to water deprivation or desmopressin. Differential includes lithium, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, and tubulointerstitial disease.
  - **Central DI**: Pituitary is not secreting endogenous ADH; urine remains dilute with water deprivation and responds to desmopressin. Differential includes brain trauma, neurosurgery, tumor, and CNS infection.

### Diagnosis

**Water deprivation test**: Patient is deprived of water for 12–16 hours with serum sodium and serum osmolality ideally achieving the upper limit of normal. Normally, ADH should be maximally released and urine osmolality should rise above 600 mOsm/kg. If not increased, DI is present. Desmopressin can be administered to differentiate between nephrogenic and central DI; a greater than 50% increase in urine osmolality after desmopressin administration is consistent with central DI.

### Clinical Features

Neurologic symptoms: altered mental status, restlessness, weakness, confusion, seizures, and coma.

### Treatment

- Address the underlying cause.
- Replace free water deficit (most easily done with IV D5W). Goal correction rate is no more than 10 mEq/24 hours. Less data about overcorrection than in hyponatremia.
- Free water deficit can be estimated: TBW × [(serum Na⁺/goal Na⁺) − 1]. TBW is 0.6 × body weight in kg, varying by age, sex, and muscle mass.
- Accounting for urinary free water loss using electrolyte-free water clearance will help prevent under-replacement: EFWC = Urine output × [1 − (Urine Na⁺ + Urine K⁺) / Plasma Na⁺].
- Address volume depletion if present (with isotonic fluid or "mixing" isotonic fluid with free water, e.g., D5 ¼ normal saline is approximately 25% isotonic fluid and 75% electrolyte-free water).

## Calcium

### Calcium Regulation (Ca²⁺ 8.5–10.5 mg/dL)

**Physiology**: Most calcium is bound to albumin; free ionized calcium is the physiologically active form under control of PTH. Calcium levels are affected by albumin and pH.

- Hypoalbuminemia: Corrected Ca²⁺ = Measured Ca²⁺ + [(4 − albumin) × 0.8]
- High serum pH: Normal total Ca²⁺ but ionized Ca²⁺ is low

**Hormones and molecules regulating calcium**:

- **PTH**: ↓Ca²⁺ or ↓Mg²⁺ → ↑PTH (from chief cells) → Net ↑Ca²⁺, ↓PO₄³⁻
- **Bone**: PTH → Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻ release from bones
- **Kidney**: PTH → ↑Ca²⁺ reabsorption (distal tubule) + ↓PO₄³⁻ reabsorption (proximal tubule)
- **Gut**: PTH → ↑1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D by stimulating 1α-hydroxylase in kidney → ↑Gut absorption of Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻
- **Vitamin D**: D3 from sun, D2 ingested → 25-OH vitamin D in liver → 1,25-(OH)₂ vitamin D in kidney. ↑PTH, ↓PO₄³⁻, ↓Ca²⁺ → ↑Activated vitamin D → Net ↑Ca²⁺, ↑PO₄³⁻. In bone: activated vitamin D → bone resorption of Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻. In gut: activated vitamin D → absorption of Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻.
- **Calcitonin**: Parafollicular cells (C cells) of thyroid (relatively minor effect). ↑Ca²⁺ → ↑Calcitonin → ↓Bone resorption of Ca²⁺. Opposes PTH action.

## Hypercalcemia (Ca²⁺ Greater Than 10.5 mg/dL After Correcting for Albumin)

### Etiologies

*(See Table 6.3.)*

### Clinical Features

"Stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones":

- **Stones**: Nephrolithiasis, nephrogenic DI → polyuria
- **Bones**: Osteopenia, osteitis fibrosa cystica (occurs in severe hyperparathyroidism only)
- **Groans**: Abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting, constipation, pancreatitis
- **Psychiatric overtones**: Fatigue, depression, confusion, coma

### Diagnosis

*(See Table 6.3.)*

### Treatment

Address underlying etiology; withhold offending medications.

- **Calcium less than 12 mg/dL corrected**: If asymptomatic, often does not need immediate treatment but monitor closely. If symptoms of rising calcium, try fluids first. If etiology is hyperparathyroidism, parathyroidectomy is curative in most patients. Refer for surgery; if declined/deferred, offer cinacalcet or bisphosphonates.
- **Calcium greater than 12 mg/dL corrected**: Aggressive normal saline repletion (4–6 L/day). Increased urine output increases renal calcium secretion. Use furosemide cautiously, typically only if evidence of volume overload. Calcitonin acts rapidly (hours) but tachyphylaxis develops in 1–2 days. Bisphosphonates have slower onset (~2 days) and inhibit osteoclasts. Caution in renal failure. Consider hemodialysis for rapid correction if Ca²⁺ greater than 18 mg/dL.

**Special use therapies**: Phosphate (may increase risk of calciphylaxis), glucocorticoids (certain malignancies, vitamin D excess, sarcoidosis).

## Magnesium

### Magnesium Homeostasis (Mg²⁺ 1.8–2.5 mg/dL)

- **Distribution**: 99% intracellular (two-thirds of that in bones); 1% extracellular (30% bound to albumin)
- **Regulation**:
  - **Intake**: Passive intestinal absorption
  - **Storage**: Intracellular shift by insulin and glucocorticoids
  - **Excretion**: Inhibited by ADH, PTH, and hypomagnesemia

---

## Hypomagnesemia (Mg²⁺ Less Than 1.8 mg/dL)

### Clinical Features

- **Neurologic**: Tremors, hyperreflexia, seizures, altered mental status
- **ECG**: Prolonged QT, T-wave flattening, torsade de pointes
- Often also hypocalcemia (low PTH) and hypokalemia (in muscle/myocardium; Mg²⁺ and K⁺ move together)

### Etiologies

- **Poor intake**: Alcohol use, diarrhea, short bowel, fasting, malabsorption
- **Intracellular shift**: Insulin, hungry bone (post-parathyroidectomy), pancreatitis, catecholamine excess
- **Renal wasting**: Alcohol, diuretics, calcineurin inhibitors, aminoglycosides, cisplatin, amphotericin B, Gitelman syndrome

### Treatment

- Mild: Oral magnesium oxide.
- Severe: IV magnesium sulfate.

---

## Hypermagnesemia (Mg²⁺ Greater Than 2.5 mg/dL)

### Clinical Features

- Loss of deep tendon reflexes (earliest sign)
- Nausea, weakness
- ECG similar to hyperkalemia (increased PR interval, widened QRS, peaked T waves)

### Etiologies

- **Endogenous**: Renal failure, burns/trauma, adrenal insufficiency, rhabdomyolysis
- **Exogenous**: Magnesium enemas, excessive oral supplementation, IV magnesium for treatment of other pathology (e.g., preeclampsia)

### Treatment

- Stop exogenous magnesium.
- Give IV calcium gluconate for cardioprotection.
- Consider IVF/furosemide or dialysis if anuric.

## Potassium

### Potassium Regulation (K⁺ 3.5–5.0 mEq/L)

- **Distribution**: 98% intracellular; maintained by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase
- **Extracellular K⁺ shift**: Acidosis (especially non-anion gap), alpha adrenergic stimulation, hypertonicity, intracellular release (rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, TLS)
- **Intracellular K⁺ shift**: Insulin, alkalosis, beta-2 adrenergic agonists (via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase)
- **Secretion**: Via ROMK transporter, driven by voltage created from sodium reabsorption in the distal nephron
- **Aldosterone**: Increases sodium reabsorption via ENaC transporter in the distal nephon, increasing potassium excretion
- **Volume expansion**: Increases distal nephron sodium delivery, but usually compensated by decrease in aldosterone, which negates the effect on serum potassium
- **Magnesium**: Inhibits loss of potassium in the distal nephron in the absence of aldosterone

---

## Hypokalemia (K⁺ Less Than 3.5 mEq/L)

### Etiologies

- **Intracellular shift**: Insulin and beta-2 adrenergic stimulation are most common. Other causes include hypothermia, alkalemia, increased blood cell production, barium, chloroquine, cesium, and hypokalemic periodic paralysis.
- **Extrarenal losses** (transtubular potassium gradient [TTKG] less than 3 or urine K less than 20 mEq/day): Diarrhea or other fecal loss; less commonly profuse sweating.
- **Renal losses** (TTKG greater than 7 or urine K greater than 20 mEq/day):
  - **Excess mineralocorticoid activity**: Primary hyperaldosteronism, renin-secreting tumor, renovascular disease, Liddle's syndrome, licorice
  - **Increased distal tubule sodium delivery**: Alkalemia (emesis, nasogastric suction, diuretics, Bartter's, Gitelman's); acidemia (renal tubular acidosis, DKA after insulin treatment)
  - **Other**: Hypomagnesemia, amphotericin B

### Clinical Features

- **Arrhythmias**: Prolonged conduction, U waves, flattened T waves; can have normal ECG
- **Muscular**: Weakness, ileus, rhabdomyolysis

### Diagnosis

1. Repeat serum K⁺ and check Mg²⁺.
2. Assess volume and acid–base status.
3. Rule out common causes of cellular potassium shift (see potassium regulation section above) by history.
4. Obtain transtubular potassium gradient (TTKG) = [Urine K / Plasma K] × [Plasma Osm / Urine Osm] to help differentiate extrarenal vs. renal losses.

> **Pearl**: Normal urinary K⁺ excretion has significant circadian variation, limiting the utility of spot urine K⁺ measurement.

### Treatment

- **Potassium repletion**:
  - Mild: Oral potassium repletion.
  - Severe: IV potassium repletion. Do not give more than 20 mEq/hr via central line or 10 mEq/hr via peripheral line, as it is very irritating to blood vessels. Avoid dextrose-containing solutions, which cause intracellular K⁺ shift precipitated by insulin release.
  - Rule of thumb: 10 mEq KCl → increase of 0.1 mEq/L serum K⁺; replete with half as much potassium in the setting of renal dysfunction.
- Treat underlying cause; stop medications that exacerbate hypokalemia if possible (e.g., diuretics, amphotericin B).
- Must treat hypomagnesemia as well.

---

## Hyperkalemia (K⁺ Greater Than 5.0 mEq/L)

### Etiologies

- AKI or CKD (usually eGFR less than 20 mL/min/1.73 m²)
- **Spurious**: Hemolysis due to tourniquet, small-bore needles, or mechanical trauma
- **Extracellular shift**: Tissue injury (hemolysis, tumor lysis, rhabdomyolysis), acidosis (especially non-anion gap metabolic acidosis), alpha adrenergic stimulation, hypertonicity
- **Medications**: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, calcineurin inhibitors, trimethoprim, beta blockers
- **Hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism**: Diabetic nephropathy, interstitial renal disease
- **Tubular disease**: SLE, sickle cell, chronic urinary obstruction, amyloidosis, post-transplant
- Primary adrenal insufficiency

### Clinical Features

- Paresthesias, fasciculations, weakness, paralysis
- **EKG changes**: Evolution with increasing hyperkalemia (rate of K⁺ change more important than K⁺ concentration):
  - Peaked T wave → ST depression → PR prolongation and P wave flattening → QRS widening → "Sine wave" → V-fib or asystole

> **Warning**: An EKG can be normal minutes before a fatal arrhythmia. A normal EKG should not delay treatment.

### Treatment

**"C BIG K Drop"**:

| Letter | Intervention |
|---|---|
| **C** | **Calcium gluconate**: Stabilizes myocardial membrane (careful if patient on digoxin). Effect lasts up to 1 hour. |
| **B** | **Beta-agonist** (usually 10 mg of nebulized albuterol) |
| **I** | **Bicarbonate** (increases pH) – Shifts K⁺ into cells; quick fix |
| **G** | **Insulin / Glucose** – Requires frequent subsequent glucose measurement, especially in patients with impaired renal function |
| **K** | **Kayexalate** (sodium polystyrene sulfonate) – Binds potassium in the intestinal lumen; works over hours to days. Has risk of GI complications. If available, patiromer or zirconium cyclosilicate are safer alternatives. |
| **D** | **Diuretic** (loop diuretic, e.g., furosemide) – Causes urinary loss of potassium |
| **Drop** | **Dialysis** – Used in ESRD, severe AKI, or emergent cardiotoxicity |

## Phosphate

### Phosphate Regulation (PO₄³⁻ 3.0–4.5 mg/dL)

- **Distribution**: Most phosphorus is in bones (85%); remainder is in soft tissues (15%)
- **Absorption**: Vitamin D controls phosphate absorption in the GI tract
- **Excretion**: PTH inhibits phosphate absorption in the kidney, promoting excretion

---

## Hypophosphatemia (PO₄³⁻ Less Than 3.0 mg/dL)

### Etiologies

- **Decreased intestinal absorption**: Alcohol use, vitamin D deficiency, malabsorption, excessive antacid use, TPN
- **Increased renal excretion**: Excess PTH, hyperglycemia, ATN, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, Fanconi's syndrome
- **Other**: Refeeding syndrome, respiratory alkalosis, steroids, DKA, hungry bone syndrome

### Clinical Features

- **Mild**: Usually asymptomatic
- **Severe**: Can affect many organ systems:
  - Neurologic: Confusion, numbness
  - Musculoskeletal: Weakness, osteomalacia
  - Hematologic: Hemolysis
  - Cardiac: Cardiomyopathy, myocardial depression
  - Other: Rhabdomyolysis, anorexia

### Diagnosis

- Low serum phosphate.
- Also check creatinine.
- Consider checking PTH and vitamin D.

### Treatment

- Mild: Oral supplementation (milk, NeutraPhos, K-Phos).
- Severe: IV sodium phosphate or potassium phosphate.

---

## Hyperphosphatemia (PO₄³⁻ Greater Than 4.5 mg/dL)

### Etiologies

- **Decreased renal excretion** due to renal insufficiency (most common), bisphosphonates, hypoparathyroidism, vitamin D toxicity, tumor calcinosis
- Increased phosphate supplementation
- Rhabdomyolysis, cell lysis, acidosis

### Clinical Features

- Metastatic soft tissue calcification
- Hypocalcemia

### Diagnosis

- High serum phosphate.
- Also check creatinine.
- Consider checking PTH and vitamin D.

### Treatment

- Phosphate binders (bind phosphate in the bowel and prevent absorption).
- Hemodialysis if the patient is in renal failure or becoming severely hypocalcemic.

## Acid–Base Disorders

### Normal Values

- pH = 7.4 ± 0.05
- PaCO₂ = 40 ± 5 mmHg
- HCO₃⁻ = 24 mEq/L (typically normal range 22–30 mEq/L)
- Normal Anion Gap = Albumin × 2.5 (so typically 8–12 is normal)

### Approach to Acid–Base Disorders

**Step 1: Check pH**

- pH less than 7.4 = Acidemia
- pH greater than 7.4 = Alkalemia

**Step 2: Check PaCO₂**

- If the same direction as pH (both high or both low) → primary metabolic process
- If going in opposite directions → primary respiratory process

**Step 3: Check compensation**

Respiratory compensation occurs in hours; metabolic compensation takes days.

For primary metabolic acidosis, use **Winter's formula**:

Expected PaCO₂ = 1.5(HCO₃⁻) + 8 ± 2

- If actual PaCO₂ is higher than calculated → combined metabolic acidosis + respiratory acidosis
- If actual PaCO₂ is lower than calculated → metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation

**Step 4: Calculate the anion gap**

Anion gap = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)

Expected anion gap = 2.5 × albumin
