## 1. Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD)

**Definitions**: CKD is defined as any of the following signs of kidney disease lasting >3 months: GFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m², albuminuria >30 mg/g or 30 mg/24 hr, urine sediment suggesting kidney damage such as tubular casts or hematuria with dysmorphic RBCs, electrolyte abnormalities attributed to tubular disease, abnormalities on histology from a kidney biopsy, structural kidney disease on imaging, or history of kidney transplantation.

**Stages**: GFR categories are G1 with eGFR >90 mL/min/1.73 m², G2 with eGFR 60–90 mL/min/1.73 m², G3a with eGFR 45–59 mL/min/1.73 m², G3b with eGFR 30–44 mL/min/1.73 m², G4 with eGFR 15–29 mL/min/1.73 m², and G5 with eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m². Albuminuria categories are A1 with UACR <30 mg/g, A2 with UACR 30–300 mg/g, and A3 with UACR >300 mg/g. ESRD is a term that typically refers to patients with CKD who have either a GFR <15 mL/min/m² or a requirement for long-term dialysis or history of kidney transplant.

**Etiologies**: Systemic diseases include diabetes, hypertension, glomerulonephritis due to autoimmune, infectious or inflammatory disease, polycystic kidney disease, malignancy, and nephrotoxin exposure. Primary kidney diseases include primary glomerulonephritis, renal artery stenosis, chronic urinary obstruction, and congenital kidney disease.

**Treatment**: Treatment involves treating the underlying cause of kidney disease, preventing progression of kidney damage, and avoiding nephrotoxic medications while adjusting dosing of renally excreted medications. NSAIDs are a particularly common nephrotoxic medication, and most antibiotics need dose adjustment for decreased renal function. Complications must be managed, including a renal diet with Na⁺ 2–3 g/day, low K⁺, low phos, and fluid restriction if anuric generally limited to **1.5 L/day** max. Preparation for renal replacement should begin when GFR is severely decreased, including referring for transplant evaluation and preparing access for dialysis.

## 2. Dialysis and Access

**Types of Dialysis**: **Hemodialysis (HD)** involves pumping blood along a semipermeable membrane across which electrolytes, water, and toxins are removed by diffusion into another fluid being pumped along the other side of the membrane (dialysate). The composition of the dialysate solution and a number of dialysis specifications are adjusted to meet individual clearance needs. Water is filtered across the membrane by pressure. HD is usually performed over 2–4 hours three times per week in a dialysis center. Pros include frequent contact with providers and can be initiated emergently. Cons include hypotension, circuit needs anticoagulation, and need for vascular access.

**Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT)** involves continuous venovenous hemodialysis (CVVHD), which is dialysis performed continuously and is indicated for hemodynamically unstable patients requiring dialysis such as patients requiring pressors. It is only performed in the ICU.

**Peritoneal dialysis (PD)** uses the peritoneum as a semipermeable membrane for removal of water and toxins from the blood via diffusion into intraperitoneal dialysate. Pros include that it is performed at home and causes "gentler" fluid and electrolyte shifts. Cons include increased risk of hyperglycemia, peritonitis, and catheter-associated infections, as well as several contraindications related to abdominal anatomy.

**Indications for Dialysis**: Acute indications for dialysis can be remembered with "AEIOU": **A**cidosis, **E**lectrolytes such as hyperkalemia, **I**ntoxication such as methanol, lithium, ASA, or ethylene glycol, **V**olume Overload, and **U**remia. Indications for dialysis in CKD include development of signs and symptoms of uremia especially malnutrition and complications of CKD that become refractory to medical management.

**Dialysis Access**: An **arteriovenous fistula (AVF)** is preferred because it needs 3–4 months to mature after placement and has the lowest infectious risk. An **arteriovenous graft** typically needs 1 month to mature although newer grafts can be accessed in 2 to 3 days and has moderate infectious risk. A **central venous catheter** can access immediately after placement, may be a temporary catheter or a permanent tunneled dialysis catheter placed by IR, and has the highest infectious risk. **Peritoneal dialysis catheters** are placed by general surgery, IR, or interventional nephrology laparoscopically and ideally allow 2 weeks for surgical wound healing at the catheter placement site to avoid dialysate leaks from the peritoneal cavity. However, "urgent start peritoneal dialysis" can be initiated with low volume, supine exchanges for patients requiring dialysis sooner to avoid a temporary venous dialysis catheter placement. Patients need to start off with low volumes then increase slowly over 1–2 months to full prescription.

**Renal Transplant**: Transplant referral is usually considered when eGFR is between 20 and 30 mL/min/1.73 m². Evaluation includes a months-long extensive biopsychosocial assessment. Transplant is generally contraindicated if there is concern for limited medical prognosis or ability to adhere to medical treatment. Waiting time for living and deceased donor transplants ranges from 1–5 yr depending on the region.

## 3. Management of Complications of Advanced CKD/ESRD

**Blood Pressure/Volume**: The pathophysiology of hypertension in CKD is complex and multifactorial, attributed to reduced nephron mass, increased Na⁺ retention, extracellular volume expansion, activation of hormones (RAAS) and the sympathetic nervous system, and endothelial dysfunction. Goal blood pressure is generally <130/**80 mmHg**, though this may be more liberal when accounting for certain factors such as the degree of kidney disease, dialysis tolerance, polypharmacy, adverse effects of medication, and history of transplant.

**Treatment**: Sodium restriction is a key element of BP control, and fluid restriction becomes more important when GFR is severely reduced. Patients should monitor PO intake and weight. Dry weight is estimated by exam and maintained through diuresis or ultrafiltration during dialysis.

**Pharmacotherapy**: **Diuretics** can assist with volume management if a patient is still urinating, but they depend on reaching active concentrations in the tubule lumen, and higher doses are required with worsening renal function as urea competitively binds to the Organic Anion Transporter 1 (OAT1) which transports the diuretics into the tubular lumen. Most diuretics cause net fluid loss by preventing tubular sodium reabsorption. Loop diuretics will often be necessary if eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m², with efficacy in reduced eGFR being bumetanide > furosemide > metolazone > chlorthalidone > hydrochlorothiazide. **ACE inhibitors/ARBs** are first-line antihypertensives in CKD especially with proteinuria and relatively preserved native kidney function, can cause hyperkalemia, and an increase in creatinine after starting agents is not necessarily indicative of kidney injury. Generally, they should not be discontinued unless eGFR decreases by >**30%**. Combination ACE/ARB is associated with increased renal decline and generally contraindicated (ONTARGET study Lancet 2008). **Calcium channel blockers (CCBs)** do not need dose modification for renal clearance and have relatively few adverse effects but can cause lower extremity edema. They are the preferred class in CKD after ACE/ARB and diuretics and can reduce proteinuria. **Beta-blockers** are typically used if secondary prevention of cardiac events is warranted. Agents with combination alpha/beta antagonism such as carvedilol and labetalol are preferred to avoid metabolic effects of beta blockers, and nonrenal clearance is preferred such as carvedilol. **Clonidine** requires no renal dose adjustment and is often used for hypertension on days between dialysis sessions but is associated with rebound hypertension when discontinued or doses are missed.

**Anemia**: The pathophysiology results from decreased erythropoietin (EPO) production, EPO resistance, and decreased RBC lifespan. Treatment goal Hgb is 10–**11.5 g/dL**. Check iron studies in CKD patients with anemia. Guidelines recommend transferrin saturation >**30%** and ferritin >500 ng/mL, though practice patterns vary widely and less stringent targets may be used such as transferrin saturation >**20%** and ferritin >100 ng/mL. Ferritin can be increased with inflammatory states too. Rule out GI bleeding if iron deficiency is detected. **Iron supplementation** via IV iron is preferred in patients on hemodialysis because PO supplementation causes constipation, adds to pill burden, and is minimally absorbed compared to IV formulations. Iron sucrose (Venofer) and ferric gluconate (Ferrlecit) are the most commonly used formulations. IV iron can cause anaphylaxis when first given, so provide a test dose and administer slowly for patients who are IV iron naive. **Erythropoietin-stimulating agents (ESAs)** are given if the patient is iron replete but remains below Hgb goal. Three formulations exist: Epoetin alfa (Epogen) is given 3× per week during HD or weekly in non-HD CKD. Darbepoetin alfa (Aranesp) is given weekly during HD or monthly in non-HD CKD. Methoxy polyethylene glycol-epoetin beta (Mircera) is given every 2 weeks. Consider pRBC transfusion if ESAs are contraindicated or unsuccessful, and administer pRBCs if anemia is symptomatic or severe with Hgb <**7 g/dL**.

**Bone Mineral Metabolism**: The pathophysiology of mineral bone disorder is due to secondary hyperparathyroidism, which has two main contributing factors. Decreased 1-alpha-hydroxylase made by the kidney leads to decreased activation of 25-hydroxy vitamin D to 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D, leading to decreased Ca²⁺ absorption, decreased serum Ca²⁺ level, and increased PTH. Poor phosphate excretion leads to elevated phosphate, increased PTH, and decreased 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D. Manifestations of CKD mineral bone disorder include mineral and hormone abnormalities such as increased phosphate, increased PTH, and decreased 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D. Structural bone abnormalities include renal osteodystrophy, which ranges from abnormal bone pathology, bone turnover, and mineralization and includes osteomalacia, adynamic bone disease, and osteitis fibrosa cystica. Increased alkaline phosphatase reflects osteoblast activity and bone turnover. Increased FGF23 is secreted by osteocytes and involved in phosphate regulation. Calcification of blood vessels and soft tissue may also occur.

**Management of CKD Mineral Bone Disorder**: **Phosphate** levels should ideally be within the normal range though levels <**5.5 mg/dL** are often tolerated by providers. Phosphate binders are used to lower phosphate by preventing absorption of dietary phosphate. Calcium-based phosphate binders include calcium acetate (Phoslo) 667 mg/tablet, which is the binder used by **34%** of ESRD patients, is cheap ($678/user/yr), and is the strongest binder. Calcium carbonate (Tums) is cheap. Non-calcium-based phosphate binders include sevelamer (Renvela) 800 mg/tablet, which is preferred by some due to absence of calcium and is used by **54%** of ESRD patients but is expensive ($2000–$4000/user/yr). Lanthanum carbonate (Carbrenol) is used by **5%** of ESRD patients and is expensive ($5000/user/yr). Iron-based phosphate binders include ferric citrate and sucroferric oxyhydroxide, which may be preferred if iron supplementation is needed. **Calcium** should be kept with corrected Ca²⁺ within the normal range, and dialysate can be adjusted to address low or high Ca²⁺ levels. Consider measuring ionized calcium (iCa) in patients with significant hypoalbuminemia, acidemia, or hyperphosphatemia. **Vitamin D** screening and treatment for deficiency or insufficiency should be performed for all CKD patients by checking 25-hydroxy vitamin D level. 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D is not routinely measured in practice. Treat with cholecalciferol or ergocalciferol unless hypercalcemia or significant hyperphosphatemia is present. **PTH** guidelines recommend that PTH remain within 2–9× the upper limit of normal (~150–600 pg/mL) in HD patients, though practice varies and treatment in non-HD patients is controversial. Treatment with active vitamin D analogs or calcimimetics is prescribed for severely elevated or rising PTH with replete 25-hydroxy vitamin D. Active vitamin D analogs include calcitriol (Rocaltrol), which is usually used for non-HD patients; paricalcitol (Zemplar), which is administered IV and usually given with HD; and doxicalciferol (Hectorol), which is administered IV and usually given with HD. Calcimimetics are competitive binders of the Ca²⁺ sensing receptor that regulate PTH release and are usually used when active vitamin D analogues are inadequate or contraindicated due to hypercalcemia. They are more expensive than active vitamin D analogues and can cause hypocalcemia, nausea, and vomiting. Cinacalcet (Sensipar) is given PO and has been in use since 2004. Etelcalcetide (Parsabiv) is given IV and is given with HD. Parathyroidectomy is reserved for severe and refractory hyperparathyroidism in CKD.

## 4. Nephrotic Syndrome

**Pathophysiology**: Nephrotic syndrome is a syndrome of abnormal glomerular permeability and consequent proteinuria with associated symptoms and metabolic abnormalities.

**Clinical Features**: Nephrotic-range proteinuria is defined as >3 g/day or urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPCR) >3 g/g and leads to negative nitrogen balance and muscle wasting which may be obscured by edema. ACE inhibitors and ARBs are usually prescribed to reduce proteinuria. **Edema** is due to decreased oncotic pressure (hypoalbuminemia) and increased Na⁺ absorption in the distal nephron and often includes morning periorbital edema. It is treated with dietary sodium restriction and loop diuretics. **Hypercoagulability** is due to many causes including protein C and S deficiency and a compensatory increase in the production of procoagulant factors by the liver and most commonly manifests as lower extremity DVT. PE and renal vein thrombosis are other important manifestations. Prophylactic anticoagulation is considered when serum albumin is <2 g/L. **Hyperlipidemia** has controversial clinical significance, however patients with nephrotic syndrome have significantly increased rates of cardiovascular events and statin medications may be given. **Increased infection risk** means people with nephrotic syndrome should receive the pneumococcal vaccine.

**Glomerular Pathologies Commonly Associated with Nephrotic-Range Proteinuria**: **Minimal change glomerulopathy (MCG, "minimal change disease")** is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children and is less common in adults. It is defined by nephrotic-range proteinuria and kidney biopsy with little or no change to the glomerular structure on light microscopy. Primary MCD is idiopathic. Secondary MCD may be associated with atopic disease, mononucleosis, Hodgkin's lymphoma, and medications such as NSAIDs, interferon, or lithium. Treatment involves steroids for 4–8 weeks with a good prognosis.

**Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)** is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in Black adults. It is defined by proteinuria and scarring in scattered glomeruli on biopsy. Primary FSGS may result from genetic mutations in podocyte proteins. Secondary FSGS is associated with HIV ("collapsing" type), obesity, and heroin use. Treatment with steroids or calcineurin inhibitors achieves remission in 40–**60%** of patients, while others have refractory disease and often advance to ESRD. FSGS can recur in the transplanted kidney and is thought to be due to a soluble factor that has yet to be identified. Plasmapheresis can be utilized to remove this soluble factor to help prevent recurrence.

**Membranous glomerulopathy (MGN, "membranous nephropathy")** is the second most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in U.S. adults after diabetic nephropathy. Primary MGN is associated with the antiphospholipase A2 receptor (antiPLA2R) antibody and the majority of cases are primary. Causes of secondary MGN include SLE, hepatitis B, solid tumor malignancy such as lung, breast, and GI carcinomas, and medications such as penicillamine, NSAIDs, mercury, and captopril. Diagnosis requires a kidney biopsy which shows deposits of IgG and C3. Clinical features include a higher propensity for thromboembolic events particularly renal vein thrombosis. Treatment involves recognizing that up to one-third of patients will experience spontaneous remission and treatment with immunosuppression is considered for patients with a poor prognosis. A prognosis of <4 g protein/day suggests good chance of remission. A prognosis of 4–8 g protein/day suggests possible chance of remission. A prognosis of >8 g protein/day suggests poor chance of remission, so start treatment immediately.

**Other Systemic Disorders Associated with Nephrotic-Range Proteinuria**: **Diabetes** is the leading cause of glomerular disease and kidney failure in the United States. Hyperglycemia causes hyperfiltration through the glomeruli, leading to strain, scarring, proteinuria, and eventually a decrease in eGFR. Glycemic control and treatment of proteinuria may slow CKD progression. **Amyloidosis** involves abnormally-folded proteins depositing in the glomeruli resulting in impaired function and proteinuria. Dialysis-related amyloidosis is not typically a cause of nephrotic syndrome. **HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN)** manifests as hematuria, proteinuria, edema with enlarged kidneys and collapsing FSGS on pathology. Glucocorticoids for treatment are controversial. **Lupus nephritis** is divided into six subclasses (class I–VI) based on histological appearance. Nephrotic syndrome is typically seen in class III to VI. Class I involves minimal mesangial LN with no changes on light microscopy. Class II involves mesangial proliferative LN with mesangial changes on light microscopy. Class III involves focal LN with <**50%** of glomeruli involved. Class IV involves diffuse LN with >**50%** of glomeruli involved. Class V involves lupus membranous nephropathy with diffuse capillary wall thickening. Class VI involves advanced sclerosing LN with **90%** sclerosing glomeruli and often bland urine sediment.

## 5. Nephritic Syndrome

**Pathophysiology**: Nephritic syndrome is a syndrome of symptoms and findings caused by inflammation of glomeruli.

**Clinical Features**: Features include oliguria/AKI, hematuria with RBC casts, subnephrotic range proteinuria (<3 g/24 hr), edema, and hypertension.

**Glomerular Pathology Associated with Nephritic Syndrome**: **Poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis** is more common in children. Glomerular damage is due to deposition of antistreptococcal Ab in the glomeruli, so hematuria, hypertension, and edema begin 1–3 weeks after infection with Streptococcus bacteria. Diagnosis involves checking antistreptolysin O which may be elevated and low complement. Treatment is supportive with antihypertensives, loop diuretics, and steroids if severe. The majority of cases self-resolve in weeks to months.

**IgA nephropathy (Berger disease)** is caused by deposition of dysregulated IgA immune complexes. Clinical features include that it is usually immediately preceded by or copresenting with respiratory or GI infection ("synpharyngitic" hematuria as opposed to poststreptococcal GN which occurs weeks after pharyngitis). It can also be "silent" and go undetected for years. It manifests as episodic gross hematuria which is more common in patients <25 yr or persistent microscopic hematuria which is more common in patients >25 yr. It can be primary or secondary to systemic IgA vasculitis. Diagnosis involves mesangial deposits of predominantly IgA immune complexes on biopsy, and complement levels are often normal. Treatment involves recognizing there is no effective treatment, though some advocate for steroids or fish oil. Most patients have a good prognosis.

**Membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis** involves hypercellularity and thickening of glomerular basement membrane (GMB) on light microscopy. Type I (subendothelial GN) is immune complex mediated, typically caused by infections such as HBV, HCV, endocarditis, or schistosomiasis. Type II (dense deposit disease) is complement mediated, rare, typically in children but also occurs in adults with monoclonal gammopathy. Treatment involves treating the underlying cause and immunosuppression with steroids if idiopathic.

**Rapidly progressive (crescentic) glomerulonephritis (RPGN)** involves an acute decline in renal function over days to weeks due to severe glomerular injury. Diagnosis involves light microscopy with crescents in >**50%** of glomeruli due to fibrin formation and macrophages. Pathology and immunopathology distinguish between the three major etiologies. **AntiGBM disease** involves autoantibody to type IV collagen and is called Goodpasture's syndrome when kidney and lung are involved. It shows linear immunofluorescence. Treatment involves cyclophosphamide, steroids, and plasmapheresis. **Immune complex disease** can be caused by systemic infections such as poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis or infective endocarditis, systemic immune complex disease such as IgA vasculitis or SLE, or primary GN such as IgA nephropathy. It shows granular immunofluorescence. **Pauciimmune disease** is due to small-vessel vasculitis such as GPA, EGPA, or MPA, or can be drug-induced such as penicillamine or hydralazine. There is an absence or paucity of glomerular staining for immunoglobulins, and most are ANCA-positive (**80%**).

## 6. Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA)

**RTA Type I (Distal)**: The pathophysiology involves a defect in the collecting tubule's ability to excrete H⁺ due to congenital causes, multiple myeloma, autoimmunity, or amphotericin B toxicity. Labs show urine pH >5.5, hypokalemia (the kidney secretes K⁺ instead of H⁺), and nonanion gap metabolic acidosis. Clinical features include increased risk of kidney stones due to increased calcium and phosphate excretion into alkaline urine. Treatment involves sodium bicarb to correct acidosis and phosphate salts to promote excretion of titratable acid.

**RTA Type II (Proximal)**: The pathophysiology involves a carbonic anhydrase mutation leading to HCO₃⁻ not being reabsorbed. It is associated with multiple myeloma, which must be ruled out, as well as lead poisoning, amyloid, and Fanconi syndrome. Labs show urine pH <5.5, hypokalemia, and nonanion gap metabolic acidosis. Fanconi syndrome involves glycosuria. Clinical features include no risk of kidney stones like type I but risk of hypophosphatemic rickets. Treatment involves sodium restriction which increases Na⁺ resorption in the proximal tubule with bicarb absorbed as well. Do not give bicarb because it will be excreted.

**RTA Type IV (Hyperkalemic)**: The pathophysiology involves hypoaldosteronism or lack of collecting tubule response to aldosterone, which is common with interstitial renal diagnosis or diabetic nephropathy. Labs show acidic urine, hyperkalemia, and nonanion gap metabolic acidosis. There are no stones.

## 7. Other Diseases Affecting Renal Tubular Absorption

**Renal Papillary Necrosis**: Etiology involves poor medullary perfusion resulting in ischemia and necrosis, most often bilateral. It is caused by vascular disease such as diabetes, vasoconstriction such as from NSAIDs, and vascular occlusion such as from sickle cell. Treatment involves stopping the offending agent and treating the underlying disease.

**Hartnup Syndrome**: Etiology is a rare autosomal recessive inborn error of amino acid metabolism with impaired amino acid absorption in the gut and proximal renal tubule causing variable losses of proteins such as tryptophan. Clinical features are variable and can be similar to pellagra with dermatitis, diarrhea, ataxia, and psychiatric findings. Diagnosis involves elevated amino acids in the urine. Treatment involves supplemental nicotinamide if symptomatic.

**Fanconi Syndrome**: Etiology involves primary (idiopathic) or secondary proximal tubule dysfunction causing defective transport of glucose, amino acids, Na⁺, K⁺, phosphorus, uric acid, and bicarb. Causes include genetic causes such as Wilson's disease or galactosemia, acquired damage from heavy metals, connective tissue disorders, hematologic malignancies such as monoclonal gammopathy, and medications such as tenofovir or carbonic anhydrase inhibitors. Clinical features include glucosuria, phosphaturia with rickets or osteomalacia, proteinuria, polyuria, dehydration, hypercalciuria, and hypokalemia. Treatment involves treating the underlying cause if possible, and electrolyte and vitamin D supplementation may be required.

## 8. Renal Cystic Diseases

**Classification**: Renal cysts are categorized as simple or complex based on imaging appearance. Simple cysts (thin-walled, fluid-filled, no enhancement) are very common with advancing age and are typically benign but may need intervention if size or location causes pain or impaired renal function. Complex cysts (enhancement, solid components) have a high risk of malignancy and may require partial or total nephrectomy for tissue examination.

**Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)**: Etiology involves mutation in PKD1 or PKD2 on chromosome 16, and it is the most common hereditary kidney disease in the United States. Clinical features include onset of hypertension, hematuria, and nephrolithiasis in young adulthood for PKD1 or in middle age for PKD2. There is an increased risk for extrarenal diagnoses including circle of Willis berry aneurysm (risk factor for subarachnoid hemorrhage), hepatic cysts, diverticula, abdominal hernias, and consequences of HTN such as CVD. Diagnosis involves a combination of family history, genetic testing, and ultrasound showing bilateral kidneys with cysts. Treatment involves recognizing there is no curative treatment, controlling hypertension, and managing pain from cyst rupture or pressure. The majority of patients will require dialysis or a kidney transplant.

## 9. Renal Vascular Diseases

**Renal Artery Stenosis**: The majority of cases are due to atherosclerosis, and most others are due to fibromuscular dysplasia, which is often in young women, is **50%** bilateral, and can be hereditary. Renal artery narrowing and subsequent RAAS activation causes renovascular hypertension. Clinical features include early or medication-refractory hypertension and abdominal bruits on exam. Diagnosis involves duplex ultrasound, CTA, or MRA. Treatment includes treating hypertension with ACE inhibitor/ARB plus diuretic. Consider revascularization with stent or endarterectomy if there is a high chance of benefit or high risk of complications such as rapid progression of disease, recurrent flash pulmonary edema, medical therapy not tolerated or ineffective, or bilateral disease with declining kidney function.

**Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis**: Etiology involves sclerosis of the tubulointerstitial, glomerular, and vascular spaces due to genetic predisposition and chronic hypertension. Clinical features present with two distinct clinical courses defined as benign or malignant. **Benign nephrosclerosis** is most often seen in African Americans with high-risk APOL1 genotypes and preceding hypertension, with thickening of glomerular afferent arterioles. It presents as mild Cr elevation, microscopic hematuria, and mild proteinuria. **Malignant nephrosclerosis** involves a rapid decrease in renal function with severe hypertension leading to markedly elevated BP with papilledema, cardiac decompensation, and CNS findings, as well as renal abnormalities including rapid Cr elevation, proteinuria, hematuria, RBC/WBC casts, and microangiopathic hemolytic anemia. Treatment involves controlling blood pressure.

**Renal Vein Thrombosis**: Etiology can result from systemic inflammation or prothrombotic state such as nephrotic syndrome, carcinoma, trauma, pregnancy/OCPs, extrinsic compression, or trauma. Clinical features include evidence of decreased renal perfusion with rising Cr, decreased urine output, and hypertension, as well as renal injury with hematuria and proteinuria. Diagnosis involves duplex ultrasound, CTA, or MRA. Treatment includes considering thrombolysis/thrombectomy or anticoagulation based on clinical severity.

**Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)**: Etiology involves a syndrome of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, low platelets, and organ injury, most often due to TTP and atypical HUS, but can also result from HUS, APLS, scleroderma renal crisis, malignant hypertension, or pregnancy (HELLP/preeclampsia). Diagnosis involves thrombi and fibrin accumulation in capillaries on glomerular biopsy. Treatment involves treating the underlying cause and plasma exchange if TTP.

## 10. Stones and Obstructions

**Nephrolithiasis (Kidney Stones)**: **Types** include **calcium stones** (**80%**) which are radiodense and may be calcium oxalate (**90%**, envelope shape, malabsorption) or calcium phosphate (**10%**, hyperparathyroidism, RTA). **Uric acid stones** (**10%**) are radiolucent and appear as flat square plates, caused by hyperuricemia due to gout or tumor lysis syndrome. **Struvite stones** (5–**10%**) are radiodense with rectangular prisms and staghorn morphology, caused by recurrent UTIs by protease-producing organisms such as Proteus, Klebsiella, Serratia, or Enterobacter, which convert urea to ammonia that combines with magnesium or phosphorus to form struvite calculi. **Cystine stones** (**1%**) are poorly visualized by x-ray with hexagonal-shaped crystals and screened with urinary cyanide nitroprusside.

**Clinical Features**: Acute renal colic involves sudden onset, inability to sit still, and cramping abdominal/flank pain in waves. There may be nausea/vomiting, dysuria, and fever/chills.

**Diagnosis**: UA with microscopy evaluates for microscopic/gross hematuria and crystals. Imaging confirms diagnosis, stone size/location, and assesses for hydronephrosis. Noncontrast CT abd/pelvis is preferred if nephrolithiasis has not been previously established due to superior sensitivity and specificity. Ultrasound is useful to avoid radiation in patients who may be pregnant.

**Treatment**: Acute treatment involves IVF, pain control with NSAIDs > morphine, and medical expulsive therapy with an alpha blocker. Urgent stone removal is required if the stone is unlikely to pass at >**10 mm**, there is GU anatomical abnormality, infection such as UTI or sepsis, or severe symptoms with intractable pain or nausea/vomiting. If the stone is not removed urgently, consider followup imaging in 14 days to monitor stone position.

**Prevention**: Adequate fluid intake with a goal of >2 L urine output/24 hr. If high risk for stone recurrence, refer for metabolic assessment to tailor preventive measures. Almost all patients with nephrolithiasis should adhere to a low-sodium diet, which reduces calcium excretion. Other dietary changes may be beneficial based on urine lab abnormalities. Pharmacologic treatments include thiazides for hypercalciuria, allopurinol for hyperuricemia, and alkali supplementation for hypocitraturia.

**Urinary Tract Obstruction**: **Etiology** involves lower tract causes such as neurogenic bladder due to diabetes, bladder cancer, BPH, prostate cancer, urethral stricture, blood, stone, or trauma, and upper tract causes including intrinsic causes such as kidney stones, blood clots, sloughed papillary, tumor, or ureteral stricture, and extrinsic causes such as pregnancy, tumor, abdominal aortic aneurysm, retroperitoneal fibrosis, endometriosis, hematomas, IBD, or diverticulitis. **Clinical features** include renal colic and pain especially if acute, oliguria, recurrent UTIs, hematuria/proteinuria, and renal failure. **Diagnosis** involves renal ultrasound. Advanced diagnostics include intravenous pyelography (IVP) for ureteral obstruction, voiding cystourethrography for lower tract obstruction, and cystoscopy for bladder. **Treatment** for lower tract includes Foley catheter and BPH medications such as alpha-blockers or 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor. **Treatment** for upper tract includes ureteral stent via cystoscopy if possible, otherwise percutaneous nephrostomy tube.

## 11. Sodium

**Sodium Homeostasis (Na⁺ 135 to **145 mmol/L**)**: Extracellular Na⁺ concentration reflects water homeostasis, meaning hyponatremia and hypernatremia are caused by too much or too little water. Total Na⁺ content reflects Na⁺ homeostasis. Sodium is actively pumped out of cells. Na⁺ is the main cation in the extracellular fluid (ECF), and most sodium reabsorption occurs at the proximal tubule. Sodium homeostasis via RAAS involves decreased ECF volume leading to decreased renal perfusion, which increases renin by juxtaglomerular (JGA) cells. ACE activates angiotensin II, which then increases ADH and aldosterone secretion, which increases net Na⁺ reabsorption and water is absorbed with the Na⁺. Aldosterone is secreted by the adrenal gland, causing Na⁺/H₂O reabsorption and H⁺/K⁺ secretion. Water homeostasis via ADH involves increased plasma osmolarity stimulating thirst and secretion of ADH by osmoregulators in the hypothalamus. ADH increases H₂O permeability at the principal cells and promotes water absorption while also stimulating thirst.

**Hyponatremia (Na⁺ <**135 mEq/L**)**: **Etiologies** are summarized in Figure 6.4. History is key, including acuity (whether the hyponatremia occurred within the last 48 hours such as postoperatively with normal preoperative baseline), volume (fluid intake, diet, urinary changes, extrarenal losses such as diarrhea or hemorrhage), PMH (active renal, endocrine, pulmonary, or neurologic disease), medications especially diuretics and recent medication changes, and social history (use of alcohol, MDMA, other illicit substances). **Clinical features** predominantly neurologic and include headache, delirium, hyperactive deep tendon reflexes, seizures, and coma. **Diagnosis** involves the steps below. Step 1 checks if sodium is the problem by checking serum osms. Since sodium is the major solute in the blood, if sodium is low then the serum osms should also be low unless another process is contributing. High serum osms (>295 mOsm/kg) indicate hypertonic hyponatremia where low sodium is an appropriate response to excess osmotic substrate such as glucose, mannitol, or maltose/sucrose (IVIG). Correct for hyperglycemia: If glucose >**100 mg/dL**, for every additional **100 mg/dL**, add 1.6 to Na⁺. Normal serum osms (280–295 mOsm/kg) indicate pseudohyponatremia. Low serum osms (<280 mOsm/kg) indicate true hyponatremia and one proceeds to Step 2. Step 2 checks if the kidney is responding appropriately by checking urine osms. If urine osms <100 mOsm/kg, this indicates the problem is not in the kidney and relates to consumption. Too much water causes primary polydipsia, which can be caused by psychosis, ecstasy, or marathon running and will produce copious urine in the absence of extrarenal fluid loss. Too little solute causes "beer potomania"/"tea and toast diet" deficient in solute, where the kidney cannot produce as much dilute urine to remove excess free water as someone with normal solute intake. If urine osms ≥100 mOsm/kg, proceed to Step 3. A key pearl is that intermediate urine osms or even low urine osms should prompt consideration of tubular dysfunction due to diuretics, AKI, or CKD. Step 3 checks if ADH is "on" for a hemodynamic reason by checking volume status and UNa. Hypovolemia causes RAAS activation and ADH "on" with increased renal free H₂O absorption and signs of orthostatic hypotension, dry mucous membranes, and decreased skin turgor. Typically urine Na⁺ <**20 mEq/L** if extrarenal losses such as vomiting, diarrhea, or third-spacing, though urine Na⁺ may be higher if patient is on diuretics or if tubular dysfunction is present. Hypervolemic causes include heart failure, cirrhosis, and nephrotic syndrome where ADH is "on" due to perceived low volume at the JGA. Typically urine Na⁺ <**20 mEq/L** with these conditions. Step 4 checks if ADH is "on" for a nonhemodynamic reason with consideration of euvolemic etiologies including SIADH from CNS, pulmonary, or malignancy disease; SSRIs, antiepileptic drugs, or chemotherapy; cortical stimulation from pain, nausea, or psychosis; or endocrine causes including hypothyroidism, mineralocorticoid deficiency, or glucocorticoid deficiency. **Treatment** involves general principles of not correcting too quickly. Unless acute or presenting with severe symptoms, overcorrection can cause central pontine demyelination. Goal for correction is 6–**8 mEq**/24 hr, and do not correct sodium more than **8 mEq**/24 hr if hyponatremia has been present for >48 hours or unknown duration. If Na⁺ <**120 mEq/L**, consider nephrology consult and check Na⁺ q2–4 hours when beginning correction. If severe symptoms or Na⁺ <**115 mEq/L**, nephrology consult is needed and checks q12 hours when beginning correction. Electrolyte-free water clearance (EFWC) can estimate urinary free water excretion, calculated as EFWC = Urine output × [1 – (Urine Na⁺ + Urine K⁺ / P)/Plasma Na⁺]. This gives volume of water free of osmotically active electrolytes "cleared" from plasma. If negative, kidneys are concentrating urine and hypertonic saline will likely be needed. If positive, kidney is correcting and hyponatremia may improve with isotonic fluids or salt tabs. A key pearl is that giving potassium will increase serum sodium because the potassium is an exchangeable ion and will be transported intracellularly in exchange for sodium, so account for this when calculating fluids for sodium correction to avoid overcorrection. Treatment of nonacute hyponatremia by volume status involves **hypovolemia** where if mild, trial IV fluids and recheck. Monitor urine free water output and recheck serum sodium frequently to avoid sodium overcorrection. May need to give DDAVP to slow correction after volume repletion if patient is overcorrecting, and hypertonic saline will likely be needed for subsequent correction. If hyponatremia is severe and hemodynamics are stable, volume resuscitation can be done gently with hypertonic saline and DDAVP, especially if SIADH is still on differential diagnosis. **Euvolemia** often due to diuretics or SIADH involves addressing the underlying cause, free water restriction, sodium tabs with loop diuretics or oral urea, and consider demeclocycline or vasopressin antagonists if refractory. **Hypervolemic** involves free water restriction, diuresis with loop diuretics which may be helpful, and vasopressin antagonists which may be useful in heart failure but can cause liver injury in patients with cirrhosis. **Central pontine demyelination** is a complication of hyponatremia correction that is too rapid and is caused by osmotic shift due to sodium correction in the brain that has adapted to hypotonic environment over 1–2 days. Usually clinical effects occur days after sodium overcorrection. High-risk populations include very low sodium <**110 mEq/L**, concurrent hypokalemia, liver disease, malnourished patients, and patients with heavy alcohol use.

**Hypernatremia (Na⁺ >**145 mEq/L**)**: **Etiologies** include extrarenal water imbalance with μOsm >600 mOsm/kg from inadequate free water intake due to altered mental status or inadequate fluid replacement, increased sodium intake such as IV sodium bicarb, tube feeds, or TPN administration, and extrarenal free water loss such as vomiting or diarrhea. Renal free water loss includes diuresis due to medications usually loop diuretics or osmotic diuresis such as hyperglycemia, azotemia, or mannitol, and diabetes insipidus (DI) with inadequate ADH activity and μOsm <600 mOsm/kg. Two causes exist. **Nephrogenic DI** means the kidney is ADH-resistant with μOsm <300 mOsm/kg and does not respond to water deprivation and has decreased response to desmopressin. Differential diagnosis includes lithium, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, and tubulointerstitial disease. **Central DI** means the pituitary is not secreting endogenous ADH, urine remains dilute with water deprivation, and it responds to desmopressin. Differential diagnosis includes brain trauma, neurosurgery, tumor, and CNS infection. **Diagnosis** involves a water deprivation test where the patient is deprived of water for 12–16 hours with serum sodium and serum Osm ideally achieving upper limit of normal. Normally ADH should be maximally released and urine osmolality should rise above 600 mOsm/kg. If not increased, DI is present. Desmopressin can be administered to differentiate between nephrogenic and central DI, and >**50%** increase in urine osms after desmopressin administration is consistent with central DI. **Symptoms** are neurologic and include altered mental status, restlessness, weakness, confusion, seizures, and coma. **Treatment** involves addressing the underlying cause, replacing free water deficit most easily done with IV D5W. Goal correction rate is no more than **10 mEq**/24 hr. Free water deficit can be estimated by multiplying total body water (TBW) × [(serum Na⁺/goal Na⁺) – 1], where TBW is 0.6 × body weight in kg and varies depending on age, sex, and muscle mass. Accounting for urinary free water loss using electrolyte free water clearance will help prevent underreplacement. Address volume depletion if present.

## 12. Calcium

**Calcium Regulation (Ca²⁺ 8.5–**10.5 mg/dL**)**: Most calcium is bound to albumin, and free ionized calcium is the physiologic active form under control of PTH. Calcium levels are affected by albumin and pH. Hypoalbuminemia is corrected by adding [(4 – albumin) × 0.8] to total calcium. High serum pH causes normal total Ca²⁺ but low ionized Ca²⁺.

**Hormones/Molecules Regulating Calcium**: **PTH** is released when Ca²⁺ or Mg²⁺ decreases and causes net increase in Ca²⁺ and decrease in PO₄³⁻. In bone, PTH causes Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻ release. In the kidney, PTH increases Ca²⁺ reabsorption in the distal tubule and decreases PO₄³⁻ reabsorption in the proximal tubule. In the gut, PTH increases 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D by stimulating 1α-hydroxylase in the kidney, leading to increased gut absorption of Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻. **Vitamin D** from sun (D3) or ingested (D2) is converted to 25-OH vitamin D in the liver and then to 1,25-(OH)₂ vitamin D in the kidney. Increased PTH, decreased PO₄³⁻, and decreased Ca²⁺ lead to increased activated vitamin D, which causes net increase in Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻. In bone, activated vitamin D causes bone resorption of both Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻. In the gut, activated vitamin D causes absorption of both Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻. **Calcitonin** from parafollicular cells (C cells) of the thyroid has a relatively minor effect where increased Ca²⁺ leads to increased calcitonin, which decreases bone resorption of Ca²⁺ and opposes the action of PTH.

**Hypercalcemia (Ca²⁺ >**10.5 mg/dL** after correcting for serum albumin)**: **Etiologies** are listed in Table 6.3. **Clinical features** are remembered as "stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones." Stones include nephrolithiasis and nephrogenic DI with polyuria. Bones include osteopenia and osteitis fibrosa cystica, which occurs in severe hyperparathyroidism only. Groans include abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting, constipation, and pancreatitis. Psychiatric overtones include fatigue, depression, confusion, and coma. **Diagnosis** is shown in Table 6.3. **Treatment** involves addressing the underlying etiology and withholding offending medications. If Ca²⁺ <**12 mg/dL** corrected and patient is asymptomatic, often no immediate treatment is needed but monitor closely. If symptoms of rising calcium, try fluids first. If etiology is due to hyperparathyroidism, parathyroidectomy is curative in most patients. Refer for surgery, and if declined/deferred, can offer cinacalcet or bisphosphonates. If Ca²⁺ >**12 mg/dL** corrected, perform aggressive normal saline repletion at 4–**6 L/day**. Increased urine output equals increased renal calcium secretion. Use furosemide cautiously, typically only if evidence of volume overload. **Calcitonin** acts rapidly within hours but tachyphylaxis develops in 1–2 days so it is less effective long term. **Bisphosphonates** have a slower onset of action around 2 days and inhibit osteoclasts, but use caution in renal failure. Consider hemodialysis for rapid calcium correction if Ca²⁺ >**18 mg/dL**. Special use therapies include phosphate (may increase risk of calciphylaxis), glucocorticoids for certain malignancies, vitamin D excess, or sarcoidosis.

**Evaluation of Hypercalcemia** (Table 6.3): Step 1 involves corrections and considerations such as correcting for albumin if low and checking ionized calcium (iCa) in patients with conditions of increased protein binding. Step 2 involves checking PTH. If PTH is normal or high, consider primary hyperparathyroidism, which is the most common cause of asymptomatic hypercalcemia. This can be from a parathyroid adenoma (**80–90%**, usually solitary), parathyroid hyperplasia (**10–15%**, all four glands enlarged), or parathyroid cancer (<**1%**). Also consider familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH), tertiary hyperparathyroidism in ESRD, or lithium affecting PTH secretion. If PTH is normal or low, consider hypercalcemia of malignancy including PTHrP-mediated causes, osteolytic bone lesions, vitamin D-dependent hypercalcemia, or high bone turnover. Step 3 involves measuring fractional urinary calcium excretion (FECa) and sending additional tests.

**Hypocalcemia (Ca²⁺ <**7.0 mg/dL** after correcting for serum albumin)**: **Etiologies** are listed in Table 6.4. **Clinical features** include seizures, muscle spasms (tetany), Trousseau's sign (inflate BP cuff → carpal spasm), Chvostek's sign (tap facial nerve → contraction of facial muscles), hypotension, long QT, and renal osteodystrophy from secondary hyperparathyroidism. **Diagnosis** is shown in Table 6.4. **Treatment** involves correcting the underlying etiology, repleting calcium if severe/symptomatic or acute with IV calcium gluconate ± calcitriol and replete Mg²⁺ if needed, and if mild/asymptomatic using oral calcium and ensuring magnesium and vitamin D are repleted if needed. For chronic renal failure, use oral calcium, phosphate binder, and calcitriol.

**Hypocalcemia Workup** (Table 6.4): Step 1 involves corrections and considerations. Step 2 involves checking PTH. If PTH is normal or high, consider secondary hyperparathyroidism in chronic renal failure, vitamin D deficiency, hyperphosphatemia, destruction of parathyroid glands, abnormal parathyroid development, or hungry bone syndrome. If PTH is normal or low, consider destruction of parathyroid glands, altered calcium regulation, hypomagnesemia which causes functional reversible parathyroid hypofunction and must be corrected first.

## 13. Magnesium

**Magnesium Homeostasis (Mg²⁺ 1.8–**2.5 mg/dL**)**: Distribution is **99%** intracellular with two-thirds of that in bones, **1%** extracellular, and **30%** bound to albumin. Regulation involves intake with passive intestinal absorption, storage with intracellular shift by insulin and glucocorticoids, and excretion inhibited by ADH, PTH, and hypomagnesemia.

**Hypomagnesemia (Mg²⁺ <**1.8 mg/dL**)**: **Clinical features** include neurologic manifestations such as tremors, hyperreflexia, seizures, and altered mental status. ECG shows prolonged QT, T-wave flattening, and torsade de pointes. There is often also hypocalcemia with low PTH and hypokalemia as Mg²⁺ and K⁺ move together in muscle and myocardium. **Etiology** includes poor intake from alcohol use, diarrhea, short bowel, fasting, or malabsorption; intracellular shift from insulin, hungry bone postparathyroidectomy, pancreatitis, or catecholamine excess; and renal wasting from alcohol, diuretics, calcineurin inhibitors, aminoglycosides, cisplatin, amphotericin B, or Gitelman syndrome. **Treatment** involves mild cases with PO magnesium oxide and severe cases with IV magnesium sulfate.

**Hypermagnesemia (Mg²⁺ >**2.5 mg/dL**)**: **Clinical features** include loss of deep tendon reflexes (earliest sign), nausea, weakness, and ECG changes similar to hyperkalemia with increased PR interval, widened QRS, and peaked T. **Etiology** includes endogenous causes such as renal failure, burns/trauma, adrenal insufficiency, or rhabdomyolysis, and exogenous causes such as magnesium enemas, excessive PO supplementation, or IV magnesium for treatment of other pathology such as preeclampsia. **Treatment** involves stopping exogenous magnesium, giving IV calcium gluconate for cardioprotection, and considering IVF/furosemide or dialysis if anuric.

## 14. Potassium

**Potassium Regulation (K⁺ 3.5–**5.0 mEq/L**)**: Distribution is **98%** intracellular, maintained by Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase. Extracellular K⁺ shifts are caused by acidosis especially without anion gap, alpha adrenergic stimulation, hypertonicity, and intracellular release from rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, or TLS. Intracellular K⁺ shifts are caused by insulin, alkalosis, and beta-2 adrenergic agonists via Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase. Secretion of K⁺ occurs via the ROMK transporter, driven by voltage created from sodium reabsorption in the distal nephron. Aldosterone increases sodium reabsorption via ENaC transporter in the distal nephron, increasing potassium excretion. Volume expansion increases distal nephron sodium delivery but is usually compensated by decrease in aldosterone. Magnesium inhibits loss of potassium in the distal nephron in the absence of aldosterone.

**Hypokalemia (K⁺ <**3.5 mEq/L**)**: **Etiology** includes intracellular shift from insulin and beta-2 adrenergic stimulation, which are most common, and other causes including hypothermia, alkalemia, increased blood cell production, barium, chloroquine, cesium, and hypokalemic periodic paralysis. Extrarenal losses (transtubular potassium gradient [TTKG] <3 or urine K <**20 mEq**/day) include diarrhea or other fecal loss and less commonly profuse sweating. Renal losses (TTKG >7 or urine K >**20 mEq**/day) include excess mineralocorticoid activity from primary hyperaldosteronism, renin-secreting tumor, renovascular disease, Liddle's syndrome, or licorice; increased distal tubule sodium delivery from alkalemia such as emesis, nasogastric suction, diuretics, Bartter's, or Gitelman's, or acidemia such as renal tubular acidosis or diabetic ketoacidosis after treatment with insulin; hypomagnesemia; and amphotericin. **Clinical features** include arrhythmias with prolonged conduction, U waves, and flattened T waves, though ECG can be normal. Muscular features include weakness, ileus, and rhabdomyolysis. **Diagnosis** involves Step 1 repeating serum K⁺ and checking Mg²⁺, Step 2 assessing volume and acid–base status, Step 3 ruling out common causes of cellular potassium shift by history, and Step 4 obtaining the transtubular potassium gradient (TTKG) = [Urine K / Plasma K] × [Plasma Osm / Urine Osm] to help differentiate extrarenal versus renal losses. **Treatment** involves potassium repletion with mild cases using oral potassium and severe cases using IV potassium repletion. Do not give more than **20 mEq**/hr via central line or **10 mEq**/hr via peripheral line as it is very irritating to blood vessels. Avoid dextrose-containing solutions which cause intracellular K⁺ shift precipitated by insulin release. A rule of thumb is that **10 mEq** KCl increases serum K⁺ by **0.1 mEq/L**, and repletion should be done with half as much potassium in the setting of renal dysfunction. Treat the underlying cause and stop medications that exacerbate hypokalemia if possible. Must treat hypomagnesemia as well.

**Hyperkalemia (K⁺ >**5.0 mEq/L**)**: **Etiology** includes AKI or CKD usually eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73 m²; spurious causes from hemolysis due to tourniquet, small-bore needles, or mechanical trauma; extracellular shift from tissue injury such as hemolysis, tumor lysis, or rhabdomyolysis, acidosis especially nonanion gap metabolic acidosis, alpha adrenergic stimulation, or hypertonicity; medications such as ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, calcineurin inhibitors, trimethoprim, or beta blockers; hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism from diabetic nephropathy or interstitial renal disease; tubular disease from SLE, sickle cell, chronic urinary obstruction, amyloidosis, or posttransplant; and primary adrenal insufficiency. **Clinical features** include paresthesias, fasciculations, weakness, and paralysis. EKG changes evolve with increasing hyperkalemia, and the rate of K⁺ change is more important than K⁺ concentration. Changes progress from peaked T wave → ST depression → PR prolongation and P wave flattening → QRS widening → "sine wave" → V-fib or asystole. A normal EKG should not slow treatment. **Treatment** can be remembered as "C B I G K Drop." **Calcium gluconate** stabilizes the myocardial membrane but use careful if the patient is on digoxin, and effect lasts for up to 1 hour. **Beta-agonist** (usually 10 mg of nebulized albuterol) shifts K⁺ into cells. **Bicarbonate** increases pH and shifts K⁺ into cells for a quick fix. **Insulin/Glucose** shifts K⁺ into cells but requires frequent subsequent glucose measurement especially in patients with impaired renal function. **Kayexalate** (sodium polystyrene sulfonate) binds potassium in the intestinal lumen and usually works over hours to days but has risk of GI complications. If available, patiromer or zirconium cyclosilicate are safer alternatives. **Diuretic** (loop diuretic such as furosemide) causes urinary loss of potassium. **Dialysis** is used in ESRD, severe AKI, or emergent cardiotoxicity.

## 15. Phosphate

**Phosphate Regulation (PO₄³⁻ 3.0–**4.5 mg/dL**)**: Most phosphorus is in the bones (**85%**), with the remainder in soft tissues (**15%**). Phosphate absorption is controlled by vitamin D in the GI tract. Phosphate excretion is inhibited by PTH in the kidney, thus promoting excretion in the urine.

**Hypophosphatemia (PO₄³⁻ <**3.0 mg/dL**)**: **Etiology** includes decreased intestinal absorption from alcohol use, vitamin D deficiency, malabsorption, excessive antacid use, or TPN; increased renal excretion from excess PTH, hyperglycemia, ATN, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, or Fanconi's syndrome; and other causes including refeeding syndrome, respiratory alkalosis, steroids, DKA, or hungry bone syndrome. **Clinical features** in mild cases are usually asymptomatic. Severe cases can affect many organ systems including neurologic (confusion, numbness), MSK (weakness, osteomalacia), heme (hemolysis), cardiac (cardiomyopathy, myocardial depression), rhabdomyolysis, and anorexia. **Diagnosis** involves checking low serum phosphate and also checking Cr. Consider checking PTH and vitamin D. **Treatment** for mild cases involves oral supplementation with milk, NeutraPhos, or KPhos. For severe cases, use IV sodium phosphate or potassium phosphate.

**Hyperphosphatemia (PO₄³⁻ >**4.5 mg/dL**)**: **Etiology** includes decreased renal excretion due to renal insufficiency which is most common, bisphosphonates, hypoparathyroidism, vitamin D toxicity, or tumor calcinosis; and increased phosphate supplementation, rhabdomyolysis, cell lysis, or acidosis. **Clinical features** include metastatic soft tissue calcification and hypocalcemia. **Diagnosis** involves checking high serum phosphate and also checking Cr. Consider checking PTH and vitamin D. **Treatment** involves phosphate binders that bind phosphate in the bowel and prevent absorption, and hemodialysis if the patient is in renal failure or becoming severely hypocalcemic.

## 16. Acid–Base Disorders

**Normal Values**: pH = 7.4 ± 0.05, PaCO₂ = 40 ± **5 mmHg**, HCO₃⁻ = **24 mEq/L** (typically normal range 22–**30 mEq/L**), and normal anion gap = Albumin × 2.5 (so typically 8–12 is normal).

**Approach to Acid–Base Disorders**: Check pH first to determine acidemia if pH <7.4 or alkalemia if pH >7.4. Then check PaCO₂ to determine if the same direction as pH indicating primary metabolic process, or opposite directions indicating primary respiratory process. Then check compensation using Winter's formula for metabolic acidosis: Expected PaCO₂ = 1.5(HCO₃⁻) + 8 ± 2. If the actual PaCO₂ is higher than calculated PaCO₂, the patient has a combined metabolic acidosis plus respiratory acidosis. Then check the anion gap: Anion gap = Na⁺ – (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻), with expected anion gap = 2.5 × albumin.
