# Diagnostics

## Urinalysis

The urinalysis is a fundamental diagnostic tool in nephrology. The following table summarizes key urinalysis tests, their purposes, and the clinical significance of their results.

### Table 6.2: Urinalysis Tests and Interpretation

| Test | Purpose | Clinical Significance | False Negative Results | False Positive Results |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Leukocytes** | Detects presence of pyuria | Infection | Bladder tumor, SLE, medication effect, elevated urine glucose, protein, ketones, specific gravity | Certain oxidizing antibiotics (e.g., cephalexin, nitrofurantoin), contamination |
| **Nitrite** | Detects evidence of bacteriuria | Bacterial infection | Elevated specific gravity, pH <6.0, vitamin C | Contamination |
| **Urobilinogen** | Detects hepatic damage or obstruction; aids differentiation between obstructive and hemolytic jaundice | Low: May suggest biliary obstruction (reduced conjugated bilirubin reaching intestine for conversion to urobilinogen)<br>High: Hemolysis, hepatobiliary disease | Elevated nitrite levels, phenazopyridine | — |
| **Protein** | Detects glomerular disease or damage | Primary or secondary renal damage | Acidic or dilute urine; dipstick best detects albumin, so may miss non-albumin protein (e.g., abnormal antibodies in multiple myeloma) | Alkaline or concentrated urine, phenazopyridine |
| **pH** | Assesses renal function for acid excretion and acid–base balance maintenance | Acid–base disturbances, renal tubular acidosis (RTA) | — | — |
| **Blood** | Detects presence of free hemoglobin, myoglobin, or RBCs | **Hgb**: Extensive/rapid intravascular hemolysis<br>**Myoglobin**: Skeletal muscle trauma (e.g., rhabdomyolysis, seizure)<br>**RBC**: Trauma, infection, stone, malignancy, or glomerulonephritis | Elevated specific gravity, captopril, vitamin C, menstrual bleeding | — |
| **Specific gravity** | Assesses renal ability to concentrate and dilute urine | **Low**: Diabetes insipidus, renal disease, increased fluid intake, diuresis<br>**High**: Hypovolemia, low effective circulating volume (heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome), SIADH, contrast dye | — | Alkaline urine, IV contrast |
| **Ketone** | Assesses for evidence of stressors causing incomplete fat metabolism | Fever, starvation, diabetic ketoacidosis | Delay in urine examination | Elevated specific gravity, certain medications (e.g., mesna, levodopa, phenolphthalein) |
| **Bilirubin** | Detects hepatic damage or obstruction; aids differentiation between obstructive and hemolytic jaundice | Hepatobiliary disease, hyperthyroidism, septicemia | — | Chlorpromazine, selenium, phenazopyridine |
| **Glucose** | Detects evidence of hyperglycemia (often indicating diabetes) | Hyperglycemia (usually from diabetes), proximal tubule dysfunction (Fanconi syndrome), SGLT2 inhibitors | — | Elevated specific gravity, uric acid, vitamin C, ketones, levodopa |

> **Note on false results**: False positive results are caused by elevations in the given parameter from non-physiologic sources. False negative results are caused by depressions in the parameter from non-physiologic sources.

### Special Considerations

- **Multiple myeloma and light chain diseases** produce proteinuria without significant albuminuria, so UACR or dipstick may be "falsely" negative or unexpectedly mild.
- **Dilution and high pH** may also yield false-negative dipstick results. Specific gravity should be greater than 1.005 and pH less than 8 for accurate results.
- **Glucosuria with normal serum glucose** may indicate Fanconi syndrome.
