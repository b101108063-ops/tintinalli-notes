## 1. Proteinuria

**Definition**: Urine protein to creatinine ratio (UPCR) >150 mg/g or 24-hr protein >150 mg. Greater than 3 g/24 hr is nephrotic-range proteinuria. Urine albumin to creatinine ratio (UACR) >30 mg/g or 24-hr albumin excretion >30 mg. 30–300 mg/g is termed microalbuminuria. >300 mg/g is termed macroalbuminuria.

**Etiology**: Glomerular proteinuria is caused by increased glomerular permeability due to primary glomerulonephritis (GN) or GN secondary to systemic illness such as diabetes. Tubular proteinuria is caused by impaired tubular reabsorption due to tubular dysfunction, seen in tubular interstitial injury from systemic illness, congenital disease, endogenous toxins, or exogenous toxins such as beta-lactam antibiotics. Overflow proteinuria is caused by impaired resorptive ability of the proximal tubule due to excess filtration and/or production of proteins such as hemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, Bence-Jones protein in multiple myeloma, or amyloidosis. Other causes include fever, exercise, and orthostatic proteinuria (proteinuria only when standing, which is benign/self-limited and typically occurs in young people).

**Diagnosis**: Spot tests include UPCR, UACR, or dipstick. The dipstick detects albumin ranging from 1+ (30–**100 mg/dL**) to 4+ (>**1000 mg/dL**). A 24-hour urine is rarely needed but may be useful in establishing the accuracy of spot tests and quantifying a baseline level of proteinuria in a patient with persistent proteinuria, though it is cumbersome and susceptible to collection error.

**Pearls**: Multiple myeloma and other light-chain diseases will produce proteinuria without significant albuminuria, therefore UACR or dipstick will be "falsely" negative or unexpectedly mild. Dilution and high pH may also yield false-negative dipstick results. Specific gravity should be greater than 1.005 and pH <8 for accurate results. Persistent proteinuria is associated with progression of kidney disease and thus is often a treatment target and a surrogate outcome in the treatment of kidney disease. One needs to identify the underlying cause and treat it. ACEi/ARBs are often used to improve proteinuria.

## 2. Hematuria

**Types**: **Microscopic hematuria** is defined as normal-appearing urine with ≥ 2 RBCs/HPF detected in the UA, which should prompt consideration of glomerular disease. **Gross hematuria** is defined as visibly abnormal-appearing urine with RBCs on microscopy, which is more likely urologic disease such as trauma, infection, stones, malignancy, or PKD. Mimics of gross hematuria include menstruation or other uterine sources of bleeding, medications such as pyridium, phenytoin, or rifampin, foods such as beets, and metabolites such as bile, porphyrin, or methemoglobin. Causes of UA positive for blood without hematuria on microscopy include hemolysis and rhabdomyolysis.

**History**: The following clues may inform the differential diagnosis. Gross hematuria is more likely urologic but may also be caused by IgA nephropathy or renal vasculitis. Concurrent dysuria suggests UTI, malignancy, or stone. Recent URI suggests IgA nephropathy if synpharyngitic (i.e., at the same time as the infection) versus postinfectious GN (which usually occurs 2–3 weeks after). Family history may suggest polycystic kidney disease, Alport's syndrome, or benign familial hematuria. Flank pain suggests stones. Recent vigorous exercise suggests exercise-induced hematuria. Travel suggests urinary schistosomiasis or renal tuberculosis.

**Timing**: Blood at the beginning of urination suggests the urethra. Blood at the end of urination suggests the bladder or prostate. Blood throughout urination suggests the kidney or ureter. Transient or persistent hematuria suggests that transient asymptomatic hematuria occurs in young patients and can be benign.

**Workup**: Repeat UA and urine microscopy, then evaluate urine sediment. Glomerular hematuria is suggested by dysmorphic RBCs, acanthocytes ("Mickey Mouse ears"), brown urine, proteinuria, AKI, and systemic symptoms, which should prompt a renal ultrasound and referral to nephrology for evaluation and possible biopsy. Nonglomerular hematuria presents with bright red blood, blood clots, and isomorphic erythrocytes. If evidence of UTI, treat and repeat UA in 6 weeks. If the patient is complaining of acute flank pain, rule out nephrolithiasis or PKD with noncontrast CT abd/pelvis as an alternative to renal ultrasound. If gross hematuria is present in a patient age >40 yr for men or age >50 yr for women, or if there are risk factors for urological malignancy such as smoking, occupational dye exposure, chronic cystitis, pelvic irradiation, or cyclophosphamide use, get a CT urogram and refer to urology for evaluation and possible cystoscopy. One can start with renal ultrasound and cystoscopy if age <60 yr with <30 pack-yr smoking history and <25 RBCs/hpf on urine microscopy (intermediate risk). One can recheck UA within 6 months prior to imaging and cystoscopy if age <40 yr for men, age <50 yr for women, <10 pack-yr smoking history, and <10 RBCs/hpf on urine microscopy (low risk).

## 3. Uremia

**Definition**: Uremia is a syndrome of symptoms and metabolic derangements thought to be due to retained toxins in renal failure, meaning it is not attributable to abnormal fluid volume, electrolyte levels, or decreased renal synthetic function.

**Clinical Features**: Symptoms include fatigue, sleep disturbances, decreased mental acuity, nausea, decreased appetite, changes in smell and taste, pruritus, cramping, restless legs, serositis such as pericarditis, seizures, and coma. Metabolic changes include sexual dysfunction, reduced body temperature, insulin resistance, increased protein-muscle catabolism, platelet dysfunction, leukocyte dysfunction, and reduced muscle membrane potential.

**Diagnosis**: Diagnosis is primarily based on a combination of symptoms related to uremia, history of severe AKI or advanced CKD, and elimination of other potential causes of symptoms. Typically, elevated BUN and serum creatinine are present, however the degree of elevation is highly variable between different individuals and clinical scenarios.

**Treatment**: Long-term management of uremia requires dialysis or kidney transplantation. Dialysis is indicated for morbid and life-threatening uremic symptoms including malnutrition, pericarditis, bleeding, mental status changes, seizures, and refractory pruritus. Symptomatic management may be more appropriate depending on the patient's goals of care. For pain, synthetic opioids such as fentanyl, methadone, and hydromorphone are preferred to avoid neurotoxic metabolites produced by less synthetic opioids and prodrugs such as meperidine, codeine, tramadol, and morphine, which accumulate harmful metabolites in renal dysfunction. For nausea, haloperidol has more data for efficacy but must be dose-reduced, and ondansetron also has some data for efficacy. There is concern for limited efficacy and increased risk of EPS with metoclopramide. For pruritus, emollients are first-line treatment, and topical analgesics such as capsaicin may be trialed. Other treatments include antihistamines and gabapentin. For cramping, benzodiazepines or haloperidol may be used, but caution must be exercised with dosage.

## 4. Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

**Definition**: AKI is defined by a serum Cr increase ≥ **0.3 mg/dL** within 48 hours, serum Cr increased to ≥ 1.5× baseline within the prior 7 days, or urine volume <0.5 mL/kg/h for 6 hours.

**Prerenal (Decreased Renal Perfusion)**: Etiologies include hypovolemia such as poor PO intake, diuresis, or hemorrhage, vasodilation such as sepsis or anaphylaxis, poor effective circulating volume such as heart failure, cirrhosis, or nephrotic syndrome, contrast, and medications such as ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and NSAIDs, as well as abdominal compartment syndrome. Clinical features include BUN/Cr >20:1, fractional excretion of sodium (FENa) <**1%**, urine osmolality >500 mOsm/kg H₂O, urine Na⁺ <**20 mEq/L**, benign sediment, few hyaline casts, and no cellular casts.

**Intrarenal (Direct Injury to Renal Tissue)**: **Acute tubular necrosis (ATN)** is caused by ischemia from prolonged or severe hypoperfusion such as from hemorrhage or sepsis, or toxins including pigment such as rhabdomyolysis or hemolysis, paraproteins such as in multiple myeloma, medications such as aminoglycosides, cisplatin, foscarnet, pentamidine, or tenofovir, and IV contrast. Clinical features include "muddy brown" granular casts and usually FENa >**2%**. A key pearl is that ischemic ATN lies on a spectrum of decreased renal perfusion along with "prerenal" AKI, and a single patient or even a single kidney may exhibit both prerenal and ATN pathophysiology in tandem.

**Acute interstitial nephritis (AIN)** is caused by medications such as antibiotics especially beta-lactams, NSAIDs, and PPIs, infection such as streptococcal infection, diphtheria, CMV, EBV, tuberculosis, hantavirus, or leptospirosis, and systemic autoimmune disease such as Sjögren's, sarcoidosis, SLE, or IgG4 disease. Clinical features include rash, eosinophilia, and WBC casts. Eosinophils in the urine are not specific or sensitive and have low diagnostic value. Instead of ordering urine eos, if there is high suspicion for AIN, stop the culprit medication and consider a kidney biopsy.

**Acute glomerulonephritis** is caused by several causes listed under "Nephrotic Syndrome" and "Nephritic Syndrome." Clinical features include proteinuria, hypertension, hematuria, and RBC casts.

**Postrenal (Obstruction of Urinary Tract)**: Etiology includes benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), malignancy, strictures, stones, functional urinary retention, and congenital abnormalities. Typically, AKI is caused by obstruction of bilateral kidneys. Clinical features include increased PVR by ultrasound or Foley and hydronephrosis on ultrasound or CT.

**General Approach to the Diagnosis and Management of AKI**:

Step 1 involves addressing any urgent management needs such as treating severe hyperkalemia or hypoxemia due to pulmonary edema. Step 2 involves confirming baseline Cr and diagnosing the etiology, which includes seeing the differential diagnosis above. Volume exam suggesting hypovolemia may suggest a prerenal cause. Obtain a bladder scan to rule out postrenal causes and get a UA with microscopy. Consider checking the fractional excretion of sodium (FENa), which can help differentiate prerenal AKI from ATN. A key pearl is that most AKI in the hospital will be prerenal due to hypovolemia, intrarenal due to ATN, or postrenal due to obstruction. Thus, a majority of inpatient AKI can be resolved with fluids and/or a Foley catheter. Step 3 involves treating the underlying cause such as giving fluids if prerenal or placing a Foley if postrenal. Step 4 involves monitoring for complications, including checking electrolytes, evaluating volume status, and reassessing for uremia regularly. AKI can cause CKD progression in patients with baseline kidney dysfunction. Step 5 involves avoiding factors that may worsen AKI or complications, including avoiding nephrotoxic medications and adjusting dosing of renally excreted medications.
