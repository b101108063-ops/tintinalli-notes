# Approaches

## Proteinuria

### Definition

Proteinuria is defined as a urine protein to creatinine ratio (UPCR) greater than 150 mg/g, or 24-hour urine protein excretion exceeding 150 mg. A 24-hour urine protein exceeding 3 g is classified as nephrotic-range proteinuria.

The urine albumin to creatinine ratio (UACR) is also used:

- **Microalbuminuria**: UACR of 30–300 mg/g (or 24-hour albumin excretion of 30–300 mg)
- **Macroalbuminuria**: UACR greater than 300 mg/g

### Etiology

**Glomerular proteinuria** results from increased glomerular permeability due to primary glomerulonephritis or glomerulonephritis secondary to systemic illness (e.g., diabetes).

**Tubular proteinuria** occurs when tubular reabsorption is impaired due to tubular dysfunction, seen in tubular interstitial injury from systemic illness, congenital disease, endogenous toxins, or exogenous toxins (e.g., beta-lactam antibiotics).

**Overflow proteinuria** results from impaired resorptive ability of the proximal tubule due to excess filtration or production of proteins (e.g., hemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, Bence-Jones protein in multiple myeloma, amyloidosis).

**Other causes** include fever, exercise, and orthostatic proteinuria (proteinuria only when standing; benign and self-limited, typically in young people).

### Diagnosis

- **Spot tests**: UPCR, UACR, or dipstick. The dipstick detects albumin in ranges from 1+ (30–100 mg/dL) to 4+ (>1,000 mg/dL).
- **24-hour urine collection** is rarely needed but may be useful for establishing the accuracy of spot tests and quantifying baseline proteinuria in patients with persistent proteinuria. However, it is cumbersome and susceptible to collection error.

### Clinical Pearls

- Multiple myeloma and other light chain diseases produce proteinuria without significant albuminuria, so UACR or dipstick will be "falsely" negative or unexpectedly mild.
- Dilution and high pH may yield false-negative dipstick results. Specific gravity should be greater than 1.005 and pH less than 8 for accurate results.
- Persistent proteinuria is associated with progression of kidney disease and is often a treatment target and surrogate outcome.
- ACE inhibitors and ARBs are frequently used to reduce proteinuria.

## Hematuria

### Types

**Microscopic hematuria** refers to urine that appears normal but has 3 or more RBCs per high-power field on microscopy. This finding suggests glomerular disease.

**Gross hematuria** refers to urine that appears visibly abnormal with RBCs on microscopy. This finding is more likely urologic in origin (e.g., trauma, infection, stones, malignancy, polycystic kidney disease).

### Mimics of Gross Hematuria

- Menstruation or other uterine sources of bleeding
- Medications (e.g., pyridium, phenytoin, rifampin)
- Foods (e.g., beets)
- Metabolites (e.g., bile, porphyrin, methemoglobin)

### Causes of UA Positive for Blood Without Hematuria on Microscopy

- Hemolysis
- Rhabdomyolysis

### History

The following clues may help narrow the differential diagnosis:

- **Gross hematuria**: More likely urologic but may also be caused by IgA nephropathy or renal vasculitis
- **Concurrent dysuria**: Urinary tract infection, malignancy, or stone
- **Recent upper respiratory infection**: IgA nephropathy if synpharyngitic (concurrent with infection) versus post-infectious glomerulonephritis (typically occurs 1–3 weeks after)
- **Family history**: Polycystic kidney disease, Alport syndrome, benign familial hematuria
- **Flank pain**: Stones
- **Recent vigorous exercise**: Exercise-induced hematuria
- **Travel history**: Urinary schistosomiasis, renal tuberculosis

### Timing of Hematuria in Urination Stream

- **Beginning of urination**: Urethra
- **End of urination**: Bladder or prostate
- **Throughout urination**: Kidney or ureter

### Hematuria Pattern

- **Transient**: Often benign in young patients
- **Persistent**: Requires further workup

### Workup

1. **Repeat UA and urine microscopy**, then evaluate urine sediment.
2. **Glomerular hematuria** is suggested by dysmorphic RBCs, acanthocytes ("Mickey Mouse ears"), brown urine, proteinuria, AKI, or systemic symptoms. These patients should undergo renal ultrasound and referral to nephrology for evaluation and possible biopsy.
3. **Non-glomerular hematuria** is suggested by bright red blood, blood clots, and isomorphic erythrocytes.
4. If there is evidence of UTI, treat and repeat UA in 6 weeks.
5. If the patient has acute flank pain, rule out nephrolithiasis or polycystic kidney disease with non-contrast CT abdomen/pelvis (alternative: renal ultrasound).
6. **Gross hematuria with risk factors** (age greater than 40 years for men or greater than 50 years for women, or risk factors for urologic malignancy such as smoking, occupational dye exposure, chronic cystitis, pelvic irradiation, cyclophosphamide use) warrants CT urogram and referral to urology for evaluation and possible cystoscopy.
7. **Intermediate-risk patients** (age less than 60 years, less than 30 pack-year smoking history, less than 30 RBCs/hpf on urine microscopy) may start with renal ultrasound and cystoscopy.
8. **Low-risk patients** (age less than 40 years for men, less than 50 years for women, less than 10 pack-year smoking history, less than 10 RBCs/hpf) may have repeat UA within 6 months prior to imaging and cystoscopy.

## Uremia

### Definition

Uremia is a syndrome of symptoms and metabolic derangements thought to be due to retained toxins in renal failure. It is not attributable to abnormal fluid volume, electrolyte levels, or decreased renal synthetic function.

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms** include fatigue, sleep disturbances, decreased mental acuity, nausea, decreased appetite, changes in smell and taste, pruritis, cramping, restless legs, serositis (e.g., pericarditis), seizures, and coma.

**Metabolic changes** include sexual dysfunction, reduced body temperature, insulin resistance, increased protein-muscle catabolism, platelet dysfunction, leukocyte dysfunction, and reduced muscle membrane potential.

### Diagnosis

Diagnosis is based on a combination of symptoms related to uremia, history of severe AKI or advanced CKD, and elimination of other potential causes. Elevated BUN and serum creatinine are typically present, but the degree of elevation varies widely between individuals.

### Treatment

Long-term management requires dialysis or kidney transplantation. Dialysis is indicated for morbid and life-threatening uremic symptoms including malnutrition, pericarditis, bleeding, mental status changes, seizures, and refractory pruritis.

**Symptomatic management** may be more appropriate depending on the patient's goals of care:

- **Pain**: Synthetic opioids (e.g., fentanyl, methadone, hydromorphone) are preferred to avoid neurotoxic metabolites produced by less synthetic opioids and prodrugs (e.g., meperidine, codeine, tramadol, morphine), which accumulate harmful metabolites in renal dysfunction.
- **Nausea**: Haloperidol has more data for efficacy but must be dose-reduced. Ondansetron also has data for efficacy. There is concern for limited efficacy and increased risk of EPS with metoclopramide.
- **Pruritis**: Emollients are first-line treatment. Topical analgesics (e.g., capsaicin) may be trialed. Other treatments include antihistamines and gabapentin.
- **Cramping**: May be treated with benzodiazepines or haloperidol, but caution must be exercised with dosage.
