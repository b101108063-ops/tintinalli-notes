## 1. Approach to Diagnosing and Treating Infectious Diseases

The approach to diagnosing and treating infectious diseases involves several key phases of clinical decision-making that help guide appropriate management.

### Define the Illness

The first step in evaluating a suspected infection is to define the illness based on three key factors. **Host factors** are critical because different individuals, depending on their age, exposure history, prior infections, and immune status, are at risk for different types of infections. Understanding the host allows clinicians to better predict which organisms the patient is most susceptible to. **Tempo** is another important consideration because different pathogens cause different tempos of illness, and labeling the illness tempo helps determine which infections are more or less likely. The time course can generally be labeled as acute (hours to days up to 1 week), subacute (weeks to months), or chronic (months to years). **Clinical syndrome** involves defining the specific syndrome (such as pneumonia or cholangitis), and the final step is to integrate these three features. For example, an individual with HIV and a CD4 count of 112 cells/μL who presents with subacute onset of fever and cough represents a very different clinical scenario than an elderly person with hypertension who presents with acute onset of fever and cough. While both patients present with a similar clinical syndrome, the potential organisms that cause this syndrome vary dramatically based on the host and the tempo of illness.

### Select Empiric Therapy

Many infections have a high morbidity and mortality early in the course of illness when there is diagnostic uncertainty. Therefore, antimicrobial therapy is often started before identifying the definitive pathogen or pathogens. Most suspected infections that require hospitalization warrant empiric antibiotic therapy. Based on the host, tempo, and clinical syndrome, clinicians should generate a list of possible and probable pathogens to guide empiric antibiotic therapy.

### Finalize the Management Plan

Targeted antibiotics and the duration of therapy depend on the antimicrobial susceptibilities of the identified organism or organisms. **Source control** is a key step in controlling any infection, and niduses for ongoing infection include indwelling catheters, infected fluid pockets (such as an abscess or effusion), and obstructed organs (such as pyelonephritis with an obstructing stone or cholangitis).

## 3. Principles of Antibiotic Selection and Infection Management

Several key principles guide appropriate antibiotic selection and infection management. **Start antibiotics early** because early antibiotics in sepsis decrease mortality. **Obtain cultures before antibiotics** whenever possible, including blood cultures and easily accessible organ-specific cultures. **Administer antibiotics before invasive procedures** in acutely ill patients without delaying antibiotics for time-intensive culture sampling. **Use prior microbiology data** to understand the organisms with which a patient has previously been infected and their antibiotic susceptibilities. **Consider a broader differential diagnosis and broader empiric coverage** for immunocompromised patients. **Obtain source control as soon as possible** by considering removal and drainage of potential contained sources of infection. **Use cultures to guide antibiotic choice** as culture data and susceptibilities return. **Keep local antibiotic resistance patterns in mind** when selecting empiric therapy.

## 4. Primary Fever Syndromes

### Fever

**Fever** is defined as a regulated elevation in core body temperature, usually defined as 38.3°C or higher, as part of the body's inflammatory response. The differential diagnosis for fever includes infectious, autoimmune or inflammatory conditions, malignancy, drug reactions, transfusion reactions, and deep venous thrombosis (DVT).

### Evaluation of Fever

The evaluation of fever involves assessing for signs of sepsis. The **SIRS criteria** require two or more of the following: temperature greater than 38.0°C, heart rate greater than 90 beats per minute, respiratory rate greater than 20 breaths per minute or PaCO2 less than **32 mmHg**, and white blood cell count greater than 12,000 cells/μL or less than 4,000 cells/μL. The **qSOFA score** assigns one point each for respiratory rate of 22 breaths per minute or greater, altered mental status, and systolic blood pressure of **100 mmHg** or less. A score of 2 or more raises concern for an infection causing life-threatening organ dysfunction. It is important to note that the absence of fever does not rule out infection because many patients, such as the elderly or immunocompromised, may not have the immune response necessary to mount a fever. Furthermore, interventions in the intensive care unit such as CRRT or ECMO lower core body temperature and may mask the development of a fever.

A thorough history and physical exam can help identify a possible infectious source. Cough or sputum production may suggest a respiratory tract infection. Dysuria, urinary urgency, or flank pain may suggest a urinary tract infection. An erythematous or painful lower extremity may suggest a skin or soft tissue infection. Diarrhea or abdominal pain may suggest a gastrointestinal infection. Rigors may suggest bacteremia.

For diagnostic evaluation, two sets of blood cultures should be sent in any patient with a suspected severe infection causing sepsis. In patients with a clear localizing source of infection, the workup should focus on the affected organ system. In patients with no clear localizing symptoms, studies that can help localize a potential infection include urinalysis and urine culture, chest X-ray, and CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis.

Core principles of sepsis management include volume resuscitation with at least 30 mL/kg of fluids within the first 3 hours, supporting a mean arterial blood pressure of greater than **65 mmHg** using fluids and vasopressors when necessary, and administering empiric antibiotic therapy within one hour of identifying sepsis.

If the initial infectious workup is unrevealing, other sources of infection and noninfectious causes of fever should be considered. Possible causes include an overlooked source of infection in the primary evaluation, antibiotic administration that interfered with the accuracy of certain tests, an indolent or difficult-to-diagnose infection, or a noninfectious cause of fever such as rheumatologic, oncologic, drug fever, or VTE.

### Fever of Unknown Origin (FUO)

**Classic FUO** is defined as fever greater than 38.3°C continuing for 3 weeks or more with no diagnosis despite a thorough evaluation, which includes 3 days of inpatient evaluation or three outpatient visits. **Nosocomial FUO** is fever greater than 38.3°C starting after hospitalization, lasting for 3 or more days, with an unclear source despite thorough evaluation. **Neutropenic FUO** is fever greater than 38.3°C on multiple occasions in a patient with an absolute neutrophil count of greater than 500 cells/μL or expected to fall to that level within 1–2 days. **HIV-associated FUO** is fever greater than 38.3°C on multiple occasions over more than 4 weeks, or more than 3 days for hospitalized patients with HIV.

Common etiologies of FUO include infections such as tuberculosis (often extrapulmonary or disseminated), cytomegalovirus, Epstein-Barr virus, endocarditis (often culture-negative), occult abscesses, osteomyelitis, sinusitis, and zoonotic infections. Systemic inflammatory or rheumatologic causes include systemic lupus erythematosus, adult Still's disease, giant cell arteritis, other vasculitides, rheumatoid arthritis, sarcoidosis, inflammatory bowel disease, and thyroiditis. Neoplasms include lymphomas, leukemias, multiple myeloma, and solid tumors. Other causes include drug fevers, thromboembolic disease, hematomas, and hereditary fever disorders such as familial Mediterranean fever.

The workup for FUO should include a detailed history covering localizing symptoms, sick contacts, travel history, exposures, and systemic symptoms, followed by a focused physical exam and targeted labs and imaging. The initial workup should focus on key features identified in the history and physical. Advanced diagnostics such as FDG-PET scanning (now preferred over indium-111 labeled leukocyte scanning) should be considered if the above workup is unrevealing.

### Febrile Neutropenia (FN)

**Febrile neutropenia** is defined by the presence of fever (a single oral temperature of 38.3°C or higher, or a temperature of 38.0°C for over 1 hour) combined with neutropenia (defined variably as ANC less than 1,000/μL or sometimes less than 500/μL). The pathogenesis involves cytotoxic chemotherapy nonspecifically targeting rapidly proliferating cells, including myelopoietic stem cells, resulting in decreased cell counts including decreased absolute neutrophil count. Cytotoxic chemotherapy can also destroy rapidly proliferating gastrointestinal mucosal cells, resulting in decreased integrity of the GI mucosa. This results in an increased frequency of bacterial translocation across the gut barrier and bacteremia, which is the most common cause of infection in patients with febrile neutropenia.

Bacterial infections are the most common cause of febrile neutropenia. Fungal infections are predominantly due to *Candida* species (which develop after translocation across the gut barrier) and *Aspergillus* species (which develop after inhalation of spores). Viral infections most commonly involve human herpesviruses, including HSV-1 and HSV-2, herpes zoster, EBV, and CMV.

For outpatient management of febrile neutropenia in low-risk patients, oral antibiotic regimens should include antipseudomonal coverage with a fluoroquinolone. For inpatient management, IV antibiotics that include antipseudomonal coverage should be administered immediately. Gram-positive coverage (such as vancomycin) should be considered for shock, pulmonary infection, indwelling central venous catheters, and skin and soft tissue infections. Fungal coverage (such as an echinocandin or liposomal amphotericin B) should be considered for shock or persistent fevers for 4 or more days with no source identified.

### Fever in a Returning Traveler

When considering travel-related fevers, geography and incubation period should be used to narrow the differential diagnosis. It is important to note that a patient can have a fever due to their travel or independent of it, so alongside travel-dependent fevers, it is important to consider the usual travel-independent categories of fever including infectious causes such as CAP and UTI, and noninfectious causes such as malignancy, rheumatologic conditions, and medications or toxins.

The history should cover travel destination (urban or rural), vaccinations and malaria prophylaxis, exposures to animals, bites, water, diet, tattoos, and sexual contact, and timing in terms of the incubation period. Pathogens to consider by geography include the Caribbean, Central America, and South America, where Chikungunya, dengue, malaria, Zika, typhoid, and paratyphoid fever (in South America) should be considered. South-central Asia and Southeast Asia should prompt consideration of dengue, malaria, and typhoid and paratyphoid fever (in South-central Asia). Sub-Saharan Africa should prompt consideration of dengue, malaria, tickborne rickettsial infections, and schistosomiasis.

Infections with an incubation period of less than 21 days include bacterial causes such as typhoid, nontyphoidal salmonellosis, leptospirosis, meningococcemia, and rickettsial diseases; viral causes such as Zika, dengue, chikungunya, EBV, West Nile virus, Japanese encephalitis, and yellow fever; and parasitic causes such as malaria. Infections with an incubation period greater than 21 days include bacterial causes such as Q fever, mycobacterial causes such as tuberculosis, viral causes such as viral hepatitis, acute HIV, and rabies, fungal causes such as endemic fungi, and parasitic causes such as leishmaniasis.
