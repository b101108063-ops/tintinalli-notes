# Approaches

## Introduction

This chapter covers approaches and chief complaints in infectious diseases.

## An Approach to Diagnosing and Treating Infectious Diseases

| Phase of Clinical Decision-Making | Approach |
|----------------------------------|----------|
| **Define the illness** | **Host:** Age, exposure history, prior infections, and immune status determine susceptibility to different infections. <br><br> **Tempo:** Different pathogens cause different tempos of illness. Label the time course as acute (hours/days to 1 week), subacute (weeks to months), or chronic (months to years). <br><br> **Clinical syndrome:** Define the clinical syndrome (e.g., pneumonia or cholangitis). <br><br> **Putting it together:** Frame the patient based on these three features. For example: An HIV patient with CD4 112 cells/μL presents with subacute onset of fever and cough versus an elderly person with hypertension presenting with acute onset fever and cough. |
| **Select an empiric therapy** | Many infections have high morbidity and mortality early in the course when diagnostic ambiguity exists. Start antimicrobial therapy prior to identifying the definitive pathogen(s). Most suspected infections requiring hospitalization warrant empiric antibiotic therapy. Based on host, tempo, and clinical syndrome, generate a list of possible and probable pathogens to guide empiric therapy. |
| **Finalize the management plan** | **Targeted antibiotics and duration:** Depends on antimicrobial susceptibilities of identified organism(s) and type of infection. <br><br> **Source control:** Key step in controlling infection. Niduses include indwelling catheters, infected fluid pockets (abscess or effusion), and obstructed organs (pyelonephritis with obstructing stone, cholangitis). |

### Empiric Antibiotic Regimens for Hospitalized Immunocompetent Patients

| Clinical Syndrome | Common Pathogen(s) | Possible Empiric Antibiotic Regimen* | Comments |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------------------------|----------|
| **Meningitis (community onset, age <50 yr, no immunocompromise)** | Bacteria: S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, Group B Strep, H. influenzae <br> Viruses: HSV-1, VZV | Vancomycin + ceftriaxone (2 g IV q12h) ± acyclovir ± adjunctive corticosteroids | Perform LP to confirm diagnosis. Do not delay empiric antibiotics if LP is delayed. Vancomycin covers ceftriaxone-resistant S. pneumoniae. Add ampicillin for patients >50 yr or immunocompromised to cover L. monocytogenes. If S. pneumoniae suspected, give dexamethasone prior to antibiotics. |
| **Meningitis (age >50 yr or immunocompromising condition)** | Above plus L. monocytogenes, Gram-negative rods (E. coli) | Vancomycin + ceftriaxone + ampicillin + acyclovir ± corticosteroids | Same comments as above. |
| **Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)** | S. pneumoniae, M. pneumoniae, C. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Legionella, Klebsiella <br> Viruses: Influenza, RSV, Rhinovirus, SARS-CoV-2 | **Medical ward:** Ceftriaxone (1 g IV daily) ± cefepime + doxycycline (100 mg PO/IV q12h) OR azithromycin (500 mg daily) <br><br> **ICU:** Vancomycin + ceftriaxone (1 g daily) | Empirically treat or broaden to vancomycin (MRSA) and cefepime (pseudomonal) if risk factors or clinically worsening after 48–72 hours. Get influenza testing and give oseltamivir during flu season. Consider viral panel for critically ill or immunocompromised. Anaerobic coverage only needed for pulmonary abscesses, necrotizing pneumonia, empyema. |
| **Hospital-Acquired/Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia** | P. aeruginosa, Klebsiella, E. coli, Enterobacter, MDR Gram-negative rods, S. aureus | Vancomycin + cefepime OR piperacillin-tazobactam ± 2nd Gram-negative agent | Double cover Gram-negatives in critically ill with risk factors for MDR organisms (septic shock, pneumonia >5 days after admission, ARDS, renal replacement therapy). |
| **Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)** | E. coli, Klebsiella, Streptococci | Ceftriaxone (1 g IV daily) | Sample peritoneal fluid and obtain gram stain. |
| **Secondary Bacterial Peritonitis** | E. coli, Klebsiella, B. fragilis, Streptococcus | Ertapenem (1 g IV daily) OR piperacillin-tazobactam ± vancomycin | Often polymicrobial. Prompt evaluation for bowel perforation. |
| **Urinary Tract Infections** | E. coli (most common) | **Pyelonephritis:** Ceftriaxone (1 g IV daily) OR ertapenem (if ESBL risk) | Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria (except pregnant women, kidney transplant <1 month, urologic procedures causing mucosal bleeding). Check prior cultures for MDR organisms. |
| **Endocarditis** | S. aureus, Streptococcus spp. | Vancomycin + ceftriaxone (2 g IV daily) | Get TTE; if inconclusive, get TEE. Obtain 3 sets of blood cultures. |
| **Bloodstream Infections** | S. aureus, Streptococcus, E. coli, Klebsiella, Candida | **Gram-positive cocci:** Vancomycin <br> **Gram-negative rods:** Ceftriaxone OR piperacillin-tazobactam OR cefepime OR carbapenem <br> **Yeast:** Echinocandin | Consult ID for all cases of S. aureus bacteremia. Treat S. lugdunensis like S. aureus. Candidemia warrants ID and ophthalmology consults. |
| **Cellulitis (Nonpurulent)** | S. aureus, Streptococcus | Cefazolin (1–2 g IV q8h) | Consider imaging if no improvement after 72h to rule out abscess. |
| **Cellulitis (Purulent)** | S. aureus (often MRSA) | Vancomycin | If drainable abscess present, perform I&D. |
| **Necrotizing Fasciitis** | S. aureus, Group A strep, Anaerobes, Gram-negative rods | Vancomycin + ertapenem OR piperacillin-tazobactam + clindamycin | Emergent surgical and ID consult. Clindamycin for antitoxin effect. |
| **Septic Arthritis** | S. aureus, Streptococci, N. gonorrhoeae, Enterobacteriaceae | Vancomycin + ceftriaxone (2 g IV daily) OR cefepime (2 g IV q8h if P. aeruginosa risk) | Sample joint fluid for Gram stain and culture. |
| **Osteomyelitis** | S. aureus (hematogenous) | Vancomycin + ceftriaxone (2 g IV q12h) | Consider bone biopsy if clinically stable. If diabetic foot ulcer, add Gram-negative coverage. If vascular insufficiency, consider P. aeruginosa and anaerobic coverage. |
| **Severe Sepsis with Unclear Source** | Not yet determined | Vancomycin + ertapenem OR piperacillin-tazobactam OR cefepime | Attempt to identify source with history, physical, labs, and imaging. |

*Each antibiotic listed with a separate bullet is recommended in combination unless stated with "OR" or "+/−".

**Color coding for antibiotics:** Red = mainly Gram-positive coverage, Blue = mainly Gram-negative coverage, Green = atypical or anaerobic coverage, Brown = anaerobic coverage, Purple = antiviral coverage, Orange = antifungal coverage.

## Principles of Antibiotic Selection and Infection Management

- **Start antibiotics early:** Early antibiotics in sepsis decrease mortality.
- **Cultures before antibiotics:** Obtain blood cultures and easily-accessible organ-specific cultures prior to starting antibiotics when possible.
- **Antibiotics before invasive procedures:** Do not delay antibiotics for time-intensive culture sampling (LP, paracentesis, thoracentesis, abscess drainage).
- **Use prior microbiology data:** Prior data helps understand previous organisms and antibiotic susceptibilities.
- **Immunocompromised patients:** Consider broader differential and broader empiric coverage.
- **Obtain source control ASAP:** Consider removing/draining catheters, hardware, effusions, and abscesses.
- **Use cultures to guide therapy:** Narrow antibiotics based on culture data and susceptibilities.
- **Keep local antibiotic resistance patterns in mind.**

## Primary Fever Syndromes

### Fever

**Definition:** A regulated elevation in core body temperature, usually ≥38.3°C, as part of the body's inflammatory response.

**Differential diagnosis:**
- Infectious
- Autoimmune/inflammatory conditions
- Malignancy
- Drug reactions
- Transfusion reactions
- Deep venous thrombosis (DVT)

### Steps in Evaluation of a Fever

| Step | Action | Details |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | Evaluate for signs of sepsis | **SIRS Criteria (≥2 = SIRS):** Temperature >38.0°C, HR >90 beats/min, RR >20 breaths/min or PaCO₂ <32 mmHg, WBC >12,000 or <4,000 cells/μL. <br><br> **qSOFA Score (>2 raises concern):** RR ≥22, altered mental status, SBP ≤100 mmHg. <br><br> Note: Absence of fever does not rule out infection. Many patients (elderly, immunocompromised) may not mount a fever. ICU interventions (CRRT, ECMO) lower core temperature and may mask fever. |
| 2 | Review localizing signs and symptoms | Cough/sputum → respiratory tract infection. Dysuria, flank pain → UTI. Erythematous/painful lower extremity → SSTI. Diarrhea, abdominal pain → GI infection. Rigors → bacteremia. |
| 3 | Perform diagnostic evaluation | Send two sets of blood cultures in any patient with suspected severe infection causing sepsis. Focus workup on affected organ system if localizing source identified. If no clear source: Urinalysis and urine culture, CXR, CT abdomen/pelvis. |
| 4 | Treat sepsis or suspected infection | Volume resuscitation (≥30 mL/kg within first 3 hours). Support MAP >65 mmHg with fluids and vasopressors as needed. Administer empiric antibiotics within 1 hour of identifying sepsis. |
| 5 | If initial workup unrevealing, consider other sources | Overlooked infection source, antibiotic interference with test accuracy, indolent infection, or noninfectious cause (rheumatologic, oncologic, drug fever, VTE). Reevaluate for common infections, consider noninfectious causes, indolent infections, viral/atypical pathogens. |

### Fever of Unknown Origin (FUO)

**Definitions:**
- **Classic FUO:** Fever >38.3°C continuing 3 weeks, no diagnosis despite thorough evaluation (3 days inpatient or 3 outpatient visits).
- **Nosocomial FUO:** Fever >38.3°C starting after hospitalization, lasting ≥3 days, unclear source despite evaluation.
- **Neutropenic FUO:** Fever >38.3°C on multiple occasions in a patient with ANC >500 cells/µL.
- **HIV-associated FUO:** Fever >38.3°C on multiple occasions over >4 weeks (or >3 days for hospitalized patients with HIV).

### Common Etiologies of FUO in Adults

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Infections** | Tuberculosis (often extrapulmonary or disseminated), CMV, EBV, Endocarditis (often culture-negative), Occult abscesses, Osteomyelitis, Sinusitis, Zoonotic infections (Q-Fever, bartonellosis, brucellosis) |
| **Systemic Inflammatory/Rheumatologic Diseases** | SLE, Adult Still's Disease, Giant cell arteritis, Other vasculitides (Takayasu's, GPA), Rheumatoid arthritis, Sarcoidosis, Inflammatory bowel disease, Thyroiditis |
| **Neoplasms** | Lymphomas, Leukemias, Multiple myeloma, Solid tumors (breast, lung, colorectal, kidney, liver), Castleman's disease |
| **Other** | Drug fevers, Thromboembolic disease, Hematomas, Hereditary fever disorders (e.g., familial Mediterranean fever) |

**Workup:** History (localizing symptoms, sick contacts, travel, exposures, systemic symptoms), Physical exam, Labs and imaging focused on key features from H&P. Consider: CBC with diff, CMP, urinalysis and urine culture, blood cultures (3 sets), HIV Ag/Ab, ESR, CRP, latent TB testing, ANA, RF, SPEP, CXR and/or CT chest, CT abdomen/pelvis. Consider testing for viral (EBV, CMV) and atypical pathogens (Q fever, Bartonella, Brucella) if risk factors present. If unrevealing, consider FDG-PET.

### Febrile Neutropenia (FN)

**Definition:**
- **Fever:** Single oral temperature ≥38.3°C or temperature 38.0°C for >1 hour.
- **Neutropenia:** ANC <1000/μL (or sometimes <500/μL).

**Pathogenesis:** Cytotoxic chemotherapy targets rapidly proliferating cells including myelopoietic stem cells, causing decreased neutrophil counts. Chemotherapy also destroys GI mucosal cells, increasing bacterial translocation and bacteremia.

**Pathogens:**
- **Bacterial infections:** Most common cause of FN.
- **Fungal infections:** Most invasive fungal infections are Candida (translocation) and Aspergillus (inhalation).
- **Viral:** Human herpesviruses (HSV 1&2, herpes zoster, EBV, CMV).

**Diagnostics:** History, physical, labs, panculture.

**Treatment:**

*Outpatient management (if deemed safe):*
- Oral antibiotic regimens with antipseudomonal coverage:
  - Levofloxacin 750 mg PO daily, OR
  - Ciprofloxacin 500 mg PO BID + amoxicillin-clavulanate (500/125 mg PO TID or 1000/250 mg PO BID)

*Inpatient management:*
- IV antibiotics with antipseudomonal coverage:
  - Cefepime 2 g IV q8h, OR
  - Meropenem 1 g IV q8h, OR
  - Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6h
- Consider adding Gram-positive coverage (e.g., vancomycin) if shock, pulmonary infection, indwelling catheters, or SSTIs.
- Consider adding fungal coverage (echinocandin or liposomal amphotericin B) if shock or persistent fevers for 4+ days with no source.

### Fever in a Returning Traveler

**Approach:** Consider geography and incubation period to narrow differential. A patient can have fever due to travel OR independent of it. Consider travel-dependent AND travel-independent causes of fever.

**History:** Travel destination (urban/rural), vaccinations and malaria prophylaxis, exposures (animals, bites, water, diet, tattoos, sexual contact), timing (incubation period).

**Pathogens to Consider by Geography:**

| Region | Pathogens |
|--------|-----------|
| Caribbean, Central America, South America | Chikungunya, dengue, malaria, zika, typhoid and paratyphoid fever (South America) |
| South-Central Asia, Southeast Asia | Dengue, malaria, typhoid and paratyphoid fever (South-Central Asia) |
| Sub-Saharan Africa | Dengue, malaria, tickborne rickettsial infections, schistosomiasis |

**Infections with Incubation Period <21 Days:**
- **Bacterial:** Typhoid, nontyphoidal salmonellosis, leptospirosis, meningococcemia, rickettsial diseases
- **Viral:** Zika, dengue, chikungunya, EBV (mononucleosis), West Nile virus, Japanese encephalitis, yellow fever
- **Parasitic:** Malaria

**Infections with Incubation Period >21 Days:**
- **Bacterial:** Q-fever
- **Mycobacterial:** Tuberculosis
- **Viral:** Viral hepatitis (A, E), acute HIV, rabies
- **Fungal:** Endemic fungi (check prevalence by region)
- **Parasitic:** Leishmaniasis
