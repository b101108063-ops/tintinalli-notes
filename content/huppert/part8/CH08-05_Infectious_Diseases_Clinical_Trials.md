# Clinical Trials

This chapter covers key clinical trials and publications in infectious diseases.

---

## Fever of Unknown Origin

### Fever and Fever of Unknown Origin: Review, Recent Advances, and Lingering Dogma

**Reference:** Open Forum Infect Dis. 2020;7(5):ofaa132.

This review article integrates new data on fever of unknown origin (FUO) into the clinical approach and diagnosis, and considers how this new data should influence clinical decision making and definitions of FUO.

---

## Neutropenic Fever

### Clinical Practice Guideline for the Use of Antimicrobial Agents in Neutropenic Patients with Cancer: 2010 Update

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2011;52(4):e56–e93.

This IDSA clinical practice guideline is intended to guide the use of antimicrobial agents in patients with cancer who develop fever in the setting of chemotherapy-induced neutropenia. It includes the risk stratification algorithm to determine which patients are at high- and low-risk of complications.

---

### Outpatient Management of Fever and Neutropenia in Adults Treated for Malignancy: ASCO and IDSA Clinical Practice Guideline Update Summary

**Reference:** J Oncol Pract. 2018;14(4):250–255.

This guideline seeks to answer questions around which patients with fever and neutropenia can be managed as outpatients. It also includes recommendations on the basic workup of febrile neutropenia.

---

### Antimicrobial-Resistant Gram-Negative Bacteria in Febrile Neutropenic Patients with Cancer: Current Epidemiology and Clinical Impact

**Reference:** Curr Opin Infect Dis. 2014;27(2):200–210.

This review examines recent trends in epidemiology and antimicrobial resistance in Gram-negative infections in neutropenic cancer patients, with particular attention to the impact of antimicrobial resistance on outcomes of severe infections.

---

## Fever in a Returning Traveler

### Approach to Fever in the Returning Traveler

**Reference:** N Engl J Med. 2017;376(6):548–560.

This review focuses on epidemiologic features of infections in traveling patients and offers an approach to life-threatening and highly transmissible infectious diseases that commonly impact travelers. It includes key information on geography, incubation period, and transmission of clinically relevant infections in travelers.

## CNS Infections

### Corticosteroids for Acute Bacterial Meningitis

**Reference:** Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2015;2015(9):CD004405.

A systematic review of 25 randomized trials including 4,121 patients examining outcomes and adverse effects of corticosteroid use for acute bacterial meningitis. Corticosteroids significantly reduced hearing loss and neurological sequelae but did not reduce overall mortality. This review supports the use of corticosteroids in patients with bacterial meningitis.

---

### Computed Tomography of the Head Before Lumbar Puncture in Adults with Suspected Meningitis

**Reference:** N Engl J Med. 2001;345(24):1727–1733.

A prospective cohort study of 301 adults with suspected meningitis. Clinical features associated with abnormal findings on CT of the head were age ≥60 years, immunocompromise, history of CNS disease, and seizure within a week of presentation. Neurologic abnormalities associated with abnormal imaging included: altered level of consciousness, inability to answer two consecutive questions correctly or follow two consecutive commands, gaze palsy, abnormal visual fields, focal deficits, and abnormal language. This study supports using clinical features to identify those who need CT imaging before lumbar puncture.

---

### How Do I Perform a Lumbar Puncture and Analyze the Results to Diagnose Bacterial Meningitis?

**Reference:** JAMA. 2006;296(16):2012–2022.

A systematic review including evidence about test accuracy of CSF analysis in adult patients with suspected bacterial meningitis. It demonstrated that a CSF:blood glucose ratio of 0.4 or less and a CSF WBC count of 500 cells/µL or higher significantly increase the likelihood of bacterial meningitis.

## Pneumonia

### Diagnosis and Treatment of Adults with Community-Acquired Pneumonia: ATS and IDSA Clinical Practice Guideline

**Reference:** Am J Respir Crit Care Med. 2019;200(7):e45–e67.

This guideline provides evidence-based clinical practice guidelines on the management of adult patients with community-acquired pneumonia. Key items include antimicrobial regimens based on risk factors for certain pathogens and site of care, as well as updates on the use of specific diagnostic tests (blood cultures, sputum cultures, procalcitonin levels).

---

### Management of Adults with Hospital-Acquired and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia: 2016 Clinical Practice Guidelines

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2016;63(5):e61–e111.

This guideline discusses the diagnosis and management of patients with hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP) and ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP). It includes the rationale behind the removal of the previously used category of healthcare-associated pneumonia (HCAP).

---

### Short-Course Versus Prolonged-Course Antibiotic Therapy for Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia in Critically Ill Adults

**Reference:** Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2011;(10):CD007577.

A systematic review of eight studies including 1,703 patients that concluded a short course of antibiotics (7–8 days) may be more appropriate than a prolonged course (10–15 days) for HAP/VAP given decreased rate of recurrence with no difference in other negative outcomes.

## Influenza

### Diagnosis of Influenza in Intensive Care Units: Lower Respiratory Tract Samples Are Better Than Nose-Throat Swabs

**Reference:** Am J Respir Crit Care Med. 2012;186(9):929–930.

This brief communication includes important data on the frequency of discordant upper and lower respiratory tract specimens tested for influenza via PCR methods. This supports testing lower respiratory tract specimens (e.g., endotracheal aspirate) in critically ill patients with negative upper respiratory tract specimens.

## Tuberculosis

### One Month of Rifapentine plus Isoniazid to Prevent HIV-Related Tuberculosis

**Reference:** N Engl J Med. 2019;380(11):1001–1011.

This randomized, open-label, non-inferiority trial evaluated the efficacy and safety of a 1-month regimen of rifapentine plus isoniazid compared with 9 months of isoniazid alone in HIV-infected individuals. The 1-month regimen was not inferior to 9 months of isoniazid alone and was associated with high treatment completion rates. This study has led to the use of a new, short treatment regimen for latent tuberculosis infection in individuals with HIV.

### International Clinical Practice Guidelines for the Treatment of Acute Uncomplicated Cystitis and Pyelonephritis in Women: 2010 Update

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2011;52(5):e103–120.

This clinical guideline update focuses on the treatment of urinary tract infections in nonpregnant women and includes recommendations on empiric antimicrobial regimens based on antimicrobial resistance patterns.

---

### Diagnosis, Prevention, and Treatment of Catheter-Associated Urinary Tract Infection in Adults: 2009 International Clinical Practice Guidelines

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2010;50(5):625–663.

This clinical guideline update reflects the most recent recommendations from the IDSA on the diagnosis, prevention, and management of individuals with catheter-associated urinary tract infections (CAUTI).

---

### Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Asymptomatic Bacteriuria: 2019 Update by IDSA

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2019;68(10):e83–e110.

This clinical guideline update reflects the most recent recommendations from the IDSA on the management of asymptomatic bacteriuria, including adult populations not previously addressed, such as solid organ transplant recipients. It also reviews the interpretation of nonlocalizing symptoms (e.g., confusion) in patients with otherwise asymptomatic bacteriuria.

## Endocarditis

### Infective Endocarditis in Adults: Diagnosis, Antimicrobial Therapy, and Management of Complications: AHA Scientific Statement

**Reference:** Circulation. 2015;132(15):1435–1486.

This clinical guideline update on infective endocarditis includes key changes to diagnosis and management, including the addition of specific criteria for Coxiella burnetii testing as a major criterion for diagnosis of infective endocarditis and the removal of minor echocardiographic criteria.

---

### Comprehensive Diagnostic Strategy for Blood Culture-Negative Endocarditis: A Prospective Study of 819 New Cases

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2010;51(2):131–140.

This prospective, multimodal study incorporated serological, molecular, and histopathological assays to investigate 819 cases of culture-negative endocarditis (CNE). This study highlighted the major role of zoonotic agents (Bartonella and Coxiella spp.) in cases of CNE, as well as the previously underappreciated prevalence of noninfectious diseases. This study contributed to evidence that shifted the evaluation of CNE from HACEK organisms toward zoonotic organisms and noninfectious processes.

---

## S. aureus Bacteremia

### Impact of Routine Infectious Diseases Service Consultation on the Evaluation, Management, and Outcomes of Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2008;46(7):1000–1008.

This study compared evaluation, management, and outcomes of cases of S. aureus bacteremia (SAB) before and after an institution-mandated consultation with infectious diseases for all cases of SAB. It demonstrated that ID consultation decreases rates of treatment failure, increases adherence to guideline-directed care, and improves the diagnosis of endocarditis and metastatic foci of infection.

---

### Impact of Infectious Disease Consultation on Quality of Care, Mortality, and Length of Stay in Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia: Results from a Large Multicenter Cohort Study

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2015;60(10):1451–1461.

A retrospective cohort study examining consecutive cases of SAB from six academic and community hospitals, comparing those with and without ID consultation. Outcomes included quality measures of management (echocardiography, repeat blood cultures, removal of infectious foci, antibiotic therapy), in-hospital mortality, and length of stay. ID consultation was associated with better quality measures, reduced in-hospital mortality, and decreased length of stay.

---

### Clinical Practice Guidelines for the Treatment of MRSA Infections in Adults and Children

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2011;52(3):e18–55.

This comprehensive clinical practice guideline includes recommendations from the IDSA on the management of multiple types of MRSA infections, including skin and soft tissue infection, endocarditis, bacteremia, CNS infections, and bone and joint infections. It also discusses treatment failure and emerging resistance patterns in MRSA.

## Gram-Negative Bacteremia

### Seven Versus 14 Days of Antibiotic Therapy for Uncomplicated Gram-Negative Bacteremia: A Non-Inferiority Randomized Controlled Trial

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2019;9(7):1091–1098.

A randomized, multicenter, open-label, non-inferiority trial evaluating 7 versus 14 days of antibiotics for Gram-negative bacteremia in patients who were afebrile and hemodynamically stable for 48 hours without an uncontrolled focus of infection. Seven days was not inferior to 14 days based on all-cause mortality. This study supports shorter courses of antibiotics for uncomplicated Gram-negative bacteremia.

---

### Association of 30-Day Mortality with Oral Step-Down vs. Continued Intravenous Therapy in Patients Hospitalized with Enterobacteriaceae Bacteremia

**Reference:** JAMA Intern Med. 2019;179(3):316–323.

This retrospective multicenter cohort study included 4,967 unique propensity score-matched patients hospitalized with monomicrobial Enterobacteriaceae bacteremia. It compared patients treated with oral step-down therapy within the first 5 days with those treated with continuous intravenous antibiotics. Thirty-day mortality was not different among groups, suggesting oral step-down therapy may be effective with adequate source control and clinical response. Most patients received a quinolone or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole; oral beta-lactams were not thoroughly evaluated.

## Invasive Candidiasis

### Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Candidiasis: 2016 Update

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2016;62(4):e1–50.

This clinical practice guideline includes recommendations on the diagnosis, management, and prevention of invasive candidiasis. It covers treatment considerations in neutropenic patients, management of central venous catheters in patients with candidemia, empiric therapy for suspected invasive candidiasis, and best practices for management of Candida spp. isolated from the respiratory and urinary tract.

## Skin and Soft Tissue Infections

### Practice Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Management of Skin and Soft Tissue Infections: 2014 Update

**Reference:** Clin Infect Dis. 2014;59(2):147–159.

This clinical practice guideline includes recommendations from the IDSA on the management of various skin and soft tissue infections, including those in immunocompromised hosts. It was updated to align with recommendations for MRSA infections.

---

### A Placebo-Controlled Trial of Antibiotics for Smaller Skin Abscesses

**Reference:** N Engl J Med. 2017;376(26):2545–2555.

This multicenter, prospective, double-blind, randomized controlled trial evaluated outcomes of patients who did and did not receive antibiotics after incision and drainage of skin abscesses ≤5 cm. Administration of antibiotics (TMP-SMX or clindamycin) led to higher cure rates compared to placebo. Many patients in the placebo group also recovered, suggesting antibiotics after I&D improves short-term outcomes, though benefits must be weighed against risks.

### Sexually Transmitted Diseases Treatment Guidelines, 2015

**Reference:** MMWR Recomm Rep. 2015;64(RR03):1–137.

This comprehensive clinical practice guideline covers the diagnosis and management of nearly all sexually transmitted infections and reflects the CDC's most recent recommendations.

## HIV/AIDS

### Panel on Antiretroviral Guidelines for Adults and Adolescents: Guidelines for the Use of Antiretroviral Agents in Adults and Adolescents with HIV

**Reference:** Department of Health and Human Services.

These guidelines reflect current knowledge regarding the use of ARV drugs for the treatment of HIV. It includes recommended regimens for starting antiretroviral therapy, managing treatment failure, considerations for coinfections, and drug-drug interactions and safety considerations.

---

### Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Opportunistic Infections in HIV-Infected Adults and Adolescents

**Reference:** CDC, NIH, HIV Medicine Association of the Infectious Diseases Society of America.

This clinical practice guideline covers the treatment and prevention of nearly all opportunistic infections for which individuals with HIV are at risk. It is the most comprehensive resource for opportunistic infections in those living with HIV in the United States.
