# Medications

This chapter covers key medications and interventions in infectious diseases.

---

## Antibiotics

Antibiotics are organized by their coverage spectrum. Generally, antibiotics positioned more to the left have stronger Gram-positive coverage, while those positioned more to the right have stronger Gram-negative coverage. Atypical coverage is at the bottom, and anaerobic coverage is in a separate category.

**Key abbreviations:**
- **(P)** indicates antibiotics that cover Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
- **+MRSA** indicates antibiotics that cover methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus.
- **+VRE** indicates antibiotics that cover vancomycin-resistant enterococcus.
- **+ESBL** indicates antibiotics that cover extended-spectrum beta-lactamase producing bacteria.
- **IV** = intravenous; **PO** = per os (oral).

## Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

### Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NRTIs)

| Drug | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| **Abacavir** | — | Risk of hypersensitivity; must rule out HLA-B*5701 mutation first. |
| **Tenofovir** | — | Renal toxicity (decreases GFR; risk of Fanconi's syndrome); decreased bone density and osteoporosis risk. TAF has lower bone and renal toxicity than TDF. TDF associated with lower lipid levels. |
| **Lamivudine** | Well-tolerated | Higher risk of resistance. |
| **Emtricitabine** | Very similar to lamivudine | Similar resistance profile to lamivudine. |

**Note:** Abacavir/lamivudine and tenofovir/emtricitabine are common pairings.

---

### Nonnucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NNRTIs)

| Drug | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| **Efavirenz** | — | Can cause vivid dreams, worsening of underlying psychiatric disorders, suicidality. |
| **Rilpivirine** | Better tolerated | Higher likelihood of resistance; requires food and gastric acid for absorption (cannot use with PPI). |

---

### Protease Inhibitors (PIs)

| Drug | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| **Darunavir** | Potent (high barrier to resistance) | Nausea, diarrhea, hyperlipidemia. |
| **Atazanavir** | — | Causes indirect hyperbilirubinemia due to Gilbert's syndrome; not harmful; can indicate medication compliance. |

**Boosters:** PIs are often given with ritonavir or cobicistat to improve potency and allow less frequent, lower dosing. Both are CYP450 inhibitors.

**Drug-drug interactions:** Many drug-drug interactions. When patients take a PI, discuss dosing modifications for any new drug. Common interacting drug classes include:
- Acid reducers (PPIs, H2 blockers, antacids)
- Alpha-blockers
- Antimycobacterials, macrolides, antifungals, antimalarials
- Anticoagulants (including DOACs, dabigatran, warfarin), antiplatelets
- Anticonvulsants
- Antidepressants, anxiolytics, antipsychotics
- Antiarrhythmics, beta-blockers
- HCV treatment
- Hormonal agents, including oral contraceptives
- Statins
- Opioids and medications for opioid use disorder

---

### Integrase Inhibitors

| Drug | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| **Elvitegravir** | — | Lower barrier to resistance than dolutegravir. |
| **Dolutegravir** | Difficult to become resistant | Small bump in creatinine, but no change in GFR. |
| **Raltegravir** | — | BID dosing makes compliance more challenging. |
| **Bictegravir** | High barrier to resistance, fewer side effects than dolutegravir | Small bump in creatinine. |

---

### Pharmacokinetic Boosters

| Drug | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| **Ritonavir** | Boosts protease inhibitors; increases trough for daily dosing | Nausea, diarrhea, hyperlipidemia. |
| **Cobicistat** | Similar to ritonavir | Decreases tubular secretion; should not be used in patients with CKD. |

---

### CCR5 Receptor Antagonist

| Drug | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| **Maraviroc** | — | Need to test tropism assay prior to use; can cause hypersensitivity, hepatitis. Overall a less potent medication. |

---

### Fusion Inhibitors (Not First-Line)

| Drug | Cons |
|------|------|
| **Enfuvirtide** | Injectable; very common injection site reactions. |

---

### Common Starting ARV Regimens

| Regimen | Components |
|---------|------------|
| **Biktarvy** | Bictegravir/tenofovir alafenamide/emtricitabine |
| **Descovy + Tivicay** | Tenofovir alafenamide/emtricitabine + dolutegravir (TAF/FTC + DTG) |
| **Truvada + Tivicay** | Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate/emtricitabine + dolutegravir (TDF/FTC + DTG) |
| **Raltegravir + (emtricitabine or lamivudine) + (TAF or TDF)** | Three-drug regimen |
| **Triumeq** | Dolutegravir/abacavir/lamivudine (DTG/ABC/3TC); only use in HLA-B*5701 negative patients. |
| **Dovato** | Dolutegravir/lamivudine (DTG/3TC); cannot use if HIV RNA >500,000 copies/mL, HBV coinfection, or ART starting before HIV resistance or HBV testing available. |

## Antifungal Therapies

### First-Line Treatments for Invasive Fungal Infections

- **Invasive candidiasis (e.g., candidemia):** Echinocandins are first-line. Can transition to fluconazole for some species (e.g., C. albicans). C. krusei is intrinsically resistant to fluconazole. If Candida from bloodstream, consult ID and ophthalmology.
- **Angioinvasive fungal infection:**
  - Suspect mucormycosis: Empiric amphotericin B.
  - Suspect invasive Aspergillus: Empiric voriconazole.

### Spectrum of Coverage for Antifungal Agents

| Antifungal | Dimorphic Fungi | C. albicans | C. glabrata | C. parapsilosis | C. krusei | C. neoformans | A. fumigatus | Fusarium | Scedosporium | Mucormycosis |
|------------|----------------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|-----------|---------------|---------------|----------|--------------|--------------|
| **Fluconazole** | Histoplasmosis, Blastomycosis, Coccidioidomycosis | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| **Itraconazole** | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| **Isavuconazole** | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| **Voriconazole** | ✓ | ✓ | Limited | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ |
| **Posaconazole** | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Similar to voriconazole |
| **Amphotericin B** | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Similar to voriconazole | ✗ | ✗ | First-line if Aspergillus suspected |
| **Echinocandin** | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | Broadest spectrum; use for empiric coverage of invasive candidiasis prior to speciation |

**Notes:**
- Isavuconazole has increased activity against Mucor compared to voriconazole.
- Amphotericin B has the broadest coverage including Aspergillus and Mucor.
- Echinocandins are useful for empiric broad-spectrum coverage of invasive candidiasis prior to speciation.
