## 1. Antibiotics

Antibiotics can be organized based on their spectrum of activity, with some agents having stronger gram-positive coverage while others have stronger gram-negative coverage. Understanding the relative coverage of different antibiotics helps guide appropriate empiric selection.

## 2. Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

Antiretroviral therapy consists of several drug classes that target different stages of the HIV replication cycle.

### Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NRTIs)

**Abacavir** carries a risk of hypersensitivity, and the HLA-B*5701 mutation must be ruled out before use. **Tenofovir** (available as TAF and TDF forms) carries risks of renal toxicity including decreased GFR and risk of Fanconi's syndrome, as well as decreased bone density and risk of osteoporosis. TAF has a lower risk of bone and renal toxicities compared to TDF, while TDF is associated with lower lipid levels. **Lamivudine** is well-tolerated but has a higher risk of resistance. **Emtricitabine** is very similar to lamivudine and can be considered equivalent. Common NRTI pairings include abacavir with lamivudine and tenofovir with emtricitabine.

### Non-Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NNRTIs)

**Efavirenz** can cause adverse psychiatric effects including bad dreams, worsening of underlying psychiatric disorders, and suicidality. **Rilpivirine** is better tolerated but has a higher likelihood of resistance and requires food and gastric acid for absorption, so it cannot be used with a PPI.

### Protease Inhibitors (PIs)

**Darunavir** is potent with a high barrier to resistance but can cause nausea, diarrhea, and hyperlipidemia. **Atazanavir** causes an indirect hyperbilirubinemia due to Gilbert's syndrome, which is not harmful and can actually be an indication of medication compliance. PIs are often given with a booster such as ritonavir or cobicistat to improve potency and allow for less frequent and lower dosing. Both ritonavir and cobicistat are CYP450 inhibitors. PIs have many drug-drug interactions with acid reducers, alpha-blockers, antimycobacterials, macrolides, antifungals, antimalarials, anticoagulants, antiplatelets, anticonvulsants, antidepressants, antiarrhythmics, HCV treatment, hormonal agents including oral contraceptives, statins, and opioids.

### Integrase Inhibitors

**Elvitegravir** has a lower barrier to resistance than dolutegravir. **Dolutegravir** is difficult for the virus to develop resistance against but causes a small bump in creatinine without a change in GFR. **Raltegravir** requires BID dosing, which can make compliance more challenging. **Bictegravir** has a high barrier to resistance and fewer side effects than dolutegravir but also causes a small bump in creatinine.

### Pharmacokinetic Boosters

**Ritonavir** boosts protease inhibitors, increases trough so it can be dosed daily, but causes nausea, diarrhea, and hyperlipidemia. **Cobicistat** is similar but decreases tubular secretion and should not be used in patients with CKD.

### CCR5 Receptor Antagonist

**Maraviroc** requires a tropism assay prior to use and can cause hypersensitivity and hepatitis. It is overall a less potent medication than others.

### Fusion Inhibitors

**Enfuvirtide** is an injectable medication with very common injection site reactions. It is not a first-line agent.

### Common Starting ARV Regimens

**Biktarvy** is bictegravir plus tenofovir alafenamide plus emtricitabine. **Descovy plus Tivicay** is tenofovir alafenamide plus emtricitabine plus dolutegravir. **Truvada plus Tivicay** is tenofovir disoproxil fumarate plus emtricitabine plus dolutegravir. **Triumeq** is dolutegravir plus abacavir plus lamivudine and should only be used in patients confirmed to be HLA-B*5701 negative. **Dovato** is dolutegravir plus lamivudine and cannot be used when HIV RNA exceeds 500,000 copies/mL, there is HBV coinfection, or ART is going to be started before HIV resistance testing for reverse transcriptase or HBV testing are available.

## 3. Antifungal Therapies

### First-Line Treatments for Invasive Fungal Infections

**Invasive candidiasis** (including candidemia) is treated with echinocandins as first-line therapy. Patients can transition to fluconazole for some species such as *C. albicans*, though *C. krusei* is intrinsically resistant to fluconazole. If *Candida* species is cultured from the bloodstream, ID consultation is indicated, along with ophthalmology consultation to evaluate for endophthalmitis.

For **angioinvasive fungal infection**, if mucormycosis is suspected, empiric amphotericin B should be initiated. If invasive aspergillosis is suspected, empiric voriconazole should be initiated.
