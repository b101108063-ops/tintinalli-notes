# Diseases

This chapter covers diseases and pathophysiology in infectious diseases. Because diagnostic and therapeutic decisions are heavily informed by a patient's clinical syndrome, they are included with each specific disease.

---

## Central Nervous System Infections

### Meningitis

**Note:** Meningitis and encephalitis are two distinct clinical entities, each with their own list of causative organisms. However, in practice the distinction may be blurred as these syndromes can share clinical features.

**Clinical Features:**
- **Classic triad:** Fever, nuchal rigidity, altered mental status (though less than half of patients have all three).
- **Physical exam findings** (diagnostic utility is poor with limited negative predictive values):
  - **Kernig's sign:** Inability to fully extend knees when patient is supine with hips flexed.
  - **Brudzinski's sign:** Flexion of legs/thighs brought on by passive flexion of neck.
  - **Jolt test:** Painful to turn head side-to-side.
- **Rashes:**
  - N. meningitidis: Maculopapular rash with petechiae/purpura (about half of patients will have a rash on presentation).
  - HSV: Vesicular lesions (may be absent in encephalitis).
  - VZV: Vesicular lesions (encephalitis may develop weeks before or after rash onset).

**Pathogens:**
- **Bacteria:**
  - Neonates: Group B strep, E. coli, L. monocytogenes
  - Children >3 months: N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae
  - Adults: S. pneumoniae (>half of cases), N. meningitidis, Group B strep, H. influenzae
  - Elderly/immunocompromised: L. monocytogenes
- **Viruses:** Enterovirus, HSV-2, VZV, HIV, mumps, arbovirus (West Nile virus, St. Louis encephalitis virus)
- **Other:** Mycobacterium tuberculosis, syphilis, Cryptococcus spp., Coccidioides spp.

**Diagnosis:**
- **Lumbar puncture (LP):** Ensure no contraindications (deteriorating consciousness, anticoagulation, epidural abscess).
- **Non-contrast head CT prior to LP** if: New focal neurologic deficits, altered mental status, age >60, immunocompromised, concern for increased ICP (papilledema, vomiting), seizures, known brain metastases.
- Obtain blood cultures prior to antibiotics but do not delay empiric antibiotics for imaging or LP.
- **CSF studies:** Cell count and differential, Gram stain and bacterial culture, glucose (check simultaneous serum glucose for CSF:serum ratio), protein concentration. Additional tests based on clinical concern (viral PCR, fungal testing, metagenomic NGS, universal PCR).

**Typical CSF Profiles:**

| Condition | Cell Count (cells/µL) | Glucose (mg/dL) | Protein (mg/dL) |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Normal | 0–5 | 45–80 | 18–58 |
| Bacterial meningitis | >500 (PMN predominant) | <40 | >200 |
| TB meningitis | 5–1000 (lymph predominant) | <10 | >400 |
| Viral meningitis | 100–1000 (lymph predominant) | 45–80 | 50–200 |
| Guillain-Barré | 0–5 | 45–80 | 45–1000 |

**Treatment:**

| Host Characteristics | Empiric Regimen |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| Adult <50 yr, no immunocompromise | Vancomycin + ceftriaxone (2 g IV q12h) ± acyclovir ± dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg q6h ×4 days |
| Adult >50 yr or immunocompromised | Vancomycin + ceftriaxone + ampicillin (2 g IV q4h) ± acyclovir ± dexamethasone |

**Prevention:** Vaccinate all individuals ≥65 yr and immunocompromised patients for S. pneumoniae. Vaccinate asplenic patients for S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, and H. influenzae.

### Encephalitis

**Clinical features:** Encephalopathy, seizures, altered mental status. HSV-1 infects the temporal lobe, causing aphasia, olfactory hallucinations, personality changes.

**Pathogens:**
- **Viral:** HSV-1, HSV-2, VZV, arbovirus (Eastern equine encephalitis, West Nile virus, St. Louis encephalitis), enterovirus, measles, mumps, EBV, CMV
- **Parasitic:** Toxoplasmosis
- **Noninfectious:** Autoimmune encephalitis (e.g., anti-NMDA receptor antibody often associated with ovarian teratomas), T-cell lymphoma

**Diagnosis:** Non-contrast head CT then LP. MRI brain shows increased T2 FLAIR in frontotemporal region if HSV-1.

**Treatment:** Often supportive. For HSV encephalitis: IV acyclovir. For CMV: IV ganciclovir.

---

### Brain Abscess

**Pathophysiology:** Complication of otitis media, sinusitis, odontogenic infection, or post-surgical infection.

**Clinical features:** Fever, headache, focal neurologic deficits (classic triad present in only about 1 in 4 cases).

**Pathogens:** Streptococcus spp. and Staphylococcus spp. are most frequent. Often polymicrobial with both aerobic and anaerobic organisms.

**Diagnosis:** Brain MRI. Needle aspiration and surgical excision for microbiologic diagnosis.

**Treatment:** Empiric antibiotics: Vancomycin + ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h + metronidazole 500 mg IV/PO q8h. Immediate neurosurgical evaluation for drainage. Consider glucocorticoids if mass effect.

---

### Spinal Epidural Abscess

**Pathophysiology:** Bacteria spread from infected vertebrae or nearby soft tissues, or via hematogenous spread.

**Clinical features:** Often slow and insidious (weeks to months), but can be more acute with aggressive organisms like S. aureus. Back pain is most common symptom. Fever variably present. About 4 in 5 patients have neurologic symptoms (radiculopathy, motor or sensory dysfunction) developing later.

**Diagnosis:** Spinal MRI.

**Treatment:** Empiric antibiotics: Vancomycin IV + ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h (or cefepime 2 g IV q8h for pseudomonal coverage). Immediate neurosurgical evaluation for drainage.

## Pulmonary Infections

### Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

**Definition:** Acute pulmonary infection developing outside the hospital or within 48 hours of admission.

**Pathophysiology:** Organisms colonizing the nasopharynx reach the lungs via microaspiration. Poor mucociliary clearance and virulence factors allow replication. The immune response creates clinical features.

**Clinical features:** Cough, shortness of breath, fever.

**Criteria for Severe CAP:**

| Criteria | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Major Criteria** | Septic shock requiring vasopressors; Respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation |
| **Minor Criteria** | RR ≥30 breaths/min; PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤250; Multilobar infiltrates; Confusion/encephalopathy; BUN ≥20 mg/dL; Leukopenia (WBC <4000 cells/µL); Thrombocytopenia (Plt <100K); Hypothermia (<36°C); Hypotension requiring fluid resuscitation |

**Pathogens:**
- **Typical bacteria:** S. pneumoniae (most common, decreasing due to vaccines), H. influenzae (especially with underlying lung disease), M. catarrhalis
- **Atypical bacteria:** M. pneumoniae (transmitted via close contact), C. pneumoniae, C. psittaci, Legionella spp.
- **Other bacteria:** S. aureus (including MRSA; preceding influenza is a risk factor), Gram-negative rods (P. aeruginosa with specific risk factors), K. pneumoniae (underlying lung disease, alcohol use)
- **Viruses:** Influenza A/B, RSV, parainfluenza, rhinovirus, human metapneumovirus, Coronavirus (including SARS-CoV-2)

**Diagnosis:** CXR and/or chest CT showing focal consolidation with compatible clinical syndrome. Obtain blood cultures, sputum Gram stain and culture in severe CAP, immunocompromised hosts, or when empirically treating for MRSA or P. aeruginosa. Obtain nasopharyngeal swab for influenza PCR during flu season.

**Treatment:**
- **Outpatient CAP (no recent antibiotics/comorbidities):** PO amoxicillin, doxycycline, or azithromycin.
- **Outpatient CAP (recent antibiotics, more severe, notable comorbidities):** [Amoxicillin/clavulanate or cefpodoxime or cefuroxime] AND [doxycycline or azithromycin] OR a respiratory fluoroquinolone.
- **Hospital admission (general medical floor):** Ceftriaxone + [azithromycin or clarithromycin] + oseltamivir during flu season.
- **ICU admission:** Vancomycin + [ceftriaxone or cefepime if pseudomonal coverage needed] + azithromycin + oseltamivir during flu season.
- **Duration:** 5 days for most patients; extended for delayed response or S. aureus/Pseudomonas infections.

---

### Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia (HAP) and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)

**Definitions:**
- **HAP:** Pneumonia diagnosed >48 hours after hospital admission.
- **VAP:** Pneumonia diagnosed >48 hours after intubation.

**Pathogens:** Gram-negative rods (P. aeruginosa, Klebsiella, E. coli, Enterobacter with increased MDR risk), Gram-positive cocci (S. aureus).

**Treatment:** Empiric therapy should include coverage of MRSA (vancomycin) and P. aeruginosa/Gram-negative rods (piperacillin-tazobactam or cefepime). Consider ESBL coverage and/or second Gram-negative agent for critically ill patients. Duration: 7 days for most patients.

---

### Parapneumonic Effusions

**Definition:** Pleural effusions developing in the setting of pneumonia adjacent to the infection.

**Pathogens:** Pyogenic bacterial infections with Gram-positive organisms most common: S. pneumoniae, Viridans streptococci, S. aureus (most common cause of hospital-acquired), Anaerobic oral flora.

**Diagnosis:** Imaging (CXR, CT, bedside ultrasound). Pleural fluid analysis is critical.

| Type | Features | Management |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Uncomplicated** | Free-flowing, no loculations; exudative by Light's criteria, sterile; normal pH and glucose | Antibiotics alone; drainage not indicated |
| **Complicated** | Loculations often present; exudative; pH <7.2, glucose <40 mg/dL, or positive Gram stain/culture | Antibiotics with anaerobic coverage; drainage with chest tube ASAP |
| **Empyema** | Characteristics of complicated parapneumonic effusion + frank pus | Drainage with chest tube; antibiotics 4–6 weeks |

---

### Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia (PJP)

**Risk factors:** HIV (CD4 <200 cells/µL), cancer, solid organ transplant, rheumatologic disease (especially GPA), chronic steroids (prednisone ≥20 mg for ≥20 days).

**Clinical features:** Gradual onset of fever, cough, dyspnea over days to weeks in HIV; more aggressive in non-HIV patients.

**Diagnosis:** Bronchoscopy is gold standard. Check beta-D glucan, LDH. Can often diagnose via induced sputum.

**Treatment:** High-dose trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (15–20 mg/kg trimethoprim divided 3–4 daily doses for 21 days). Add glucocorticoids if A-a gradient >35 mmHg or PaO₂ <70 mmHg.

**Prophylaxis:** Bactrim (alternatives: dapsone, atovaquone, inhaled pentamidine) for HIV CD4 <200, immunocompromised conditions with prednisone ≥20 mg for ≥20 days, acute lymphocytic leukemia, stem cell transplant, solid organ transplant.

---

### Influenza

**Clinical features:** Spread by respiratory droplets. Symptoms include fevers, chills, cough, malaise. Can be asymptomatic.

**Diagnosis:** CXR (normal or alveolar pattern). Check influenza PCR.

**Treatment:** Oseltamivir, supportive care.

---

### SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19)

**Clinical features:** Spread mainly by respiratory droplets. Causes cough, fever, shortness of breath, muscle aches, GI symptoms, loss of smell, leukopenia. Can be asymptomatic in many cases.

**Diagnosis:** Molecular tests (primary method); rapid antigen tests may be used in certain situations.

**Treatment:** Supportive care is mainstay. Remdesivir for hospitalized patients with lower respiratory tract disease (200 mg IV ×1, then 100 mg IV daily for 4 additional days; may extend to 10 days). Dexamethasone 6 mg IV or PO for up to 10 days for patients on mechanical ventilation, noninvasive ventilation, high-flow nasal cannula, or supplemental oxygen.

---

### Mycobacterium tuberculosis (MTB)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Screening for latent TB (LTBI):** PPD (positive if ≥5 mm with no risk factors, ≥10 mm with medium risk, ≥15 mm with high risk) or IGRA.
- **Active TB evaluation:** CXR or CT chest for pulmonary TB; CT of suspected area for extrapulmonary TB; core biopsy for TB lymphadenitis; sputum AFB cultures and TB PCR.

**Treatment:**
- **Latent TB:** First rule out active TB, then: Rifampin ×4 months OR isoniazid/rifapentine ×3 months OR isoniazid ×9 months.
- **Active TB:** RIPE therapy (rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol). Pyridoxine for all patients on isoniazid.

**Subtypes of Tuberculosis:**

| Subtype | Clinical Features |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Primary TB** | Bacilli inhaled; some bacteria ingested by alveolar macrophages but survivors form granulomas. Asymptomatic unless weak immune system. CXR: Ghon's complex (calcified primary focus with lymph nodes); Ranke's complex if fibrosis present. |
| **Secondary (Reactivation) TB** | If host immunity weak, TB reactivates at lung apex. Symptoms: Fever, night sweats, weight loss, malaise, cough. CXR: Upper lobe or superior segment of lower lobe infiltrates with cavitation, centrilobular nodules. |
| **Extrapulmonary TB** | Hematogenous or lymphatic spread to almost any organ. Lymph nodes most common. Adrenal glands, GU tract, and bones also common. Pleural effusions are extrapulmonary TB. |
| **Miliary TB** | Hematogenous spread. CXR: Diffuse reticulonodular infiltrate. CT: Innumerable randomly distributed nodules. |

---

### Nontuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM)

**Pathogens:**
- **M. avium (MAC):** Most common NTM infection; middle-aged men with underlying lung disease OR female nonsmokers; subacute-chronic cough ± weight loss.
- **M. kansasii:** Second most common NTM; COPD, HIV, hematologic malignancy risk factors; similar to TB symptoms.
- **M. abscessus, M. fortuitum, M. chelonae:** Underlying lung disease and/or immunosuppression.

**Diagnosis:** CXR or chest CT, AFB cultures, PCRs.

**Treatment:** Regimens vary; see IDSA guidelines.

## Intraabdominal Infections

### Peritonitis/Intraabdominal Abscess

**Pathogenesis:** Disruption or inflammation of GI tract allows normal bowel flora to enter the abdominal cavity, causing frank peritonitis and/or abscess formation.

**Pathogens:** Almost always polymicrobial. Colonic organisms predominate: E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterobacter, Enterococcus, anaerobic organisms (especially B. fragilis).

**Clinical features:** Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, bloating ± fever, tachycardia, hypotension. Some patients may appear stable while others have florid shock. Elderly, immunocompromised, or those on chronic steroids may have occult presentations.

**Diagnosis:** If unstable, consult general surgery. CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast.

**Treatment:**
- **Procedural:** Surgical intervention or percutaneous drainage. Consult surgery and/or IR. Obtain cultures from drained material.
- **Antibiotic therapy:** Start ASAP to cover enteric Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms.
- **High-risk patients** (age >70, diffuse peritonitis, severe sepsis, immunocompromised, healthcare-associated): Cover MDR organisms with piperacillin-tazobactam, meropenem, or cefepime + metronidazole.
- **Low-risk community-acquired:** Ertapenem or ciprofloxacin/ceftriaxone + metronidazole.

---

### Clostridium difficile

**Pathophysiology:** C. difficile colitis from community-acquired or nosocomial infections. Only toxin-producing strains cause disease.

**Risk factors:** Recent antibiotic use (clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins), age ≥65, PPIs or H2 blockers.

**Clinical features:** Range from mild diarrheal illness to severe, fulminant, potentially fatal colitis. Severe disease criteria: More frequent diarrhea, severe abdominal pain, very high leukocytosis (often ≥15K), hypotension and lactic acidosis, AKI, or toxic megacolon.

**Diagnosis:** C. difficile stool testing in patients with ≥3 watery stools daily and no alternative explanation. Consider CT abdomen/pelvis if concern for severe/fulminant disease.

**Treatment:**
- **First-line:** Vancomycin 125 mg PO QID ×10 days or fidaxomicin 200 mg PO BID ×10 days. Add metronidazole 500 mg IV TID only if fulminant.
- **Recurrent disease:** Longer course of antibiotics.

## Genitourinary Infections

### Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

**Definition:** Bacterial growth in properly collected urine culture from a patient without symptoms of GU inflammation.

**Management:** Does not warrant treatment in most individuals. Three populations who warrant treatment:
1. **Pregnant individuals:** Asymptomatic bacteriuria confers worse outcomes (preterm birth, perinatal mortality, low fetal birth weight).
2. **Renal transplant recipients:** Within one month of transplant due to higher risk of symptomatic UTI and acute graft rejection.
3. **Patients undergoing urologic procedures expected to cause mucosal bleeding:** Untreated bacteriuria increases infectious complications.

---

### Simple Cystitis

**Definition:** Infection of the urinary tract without signs/symptoms suggesting extension beyond the bladder:
- Temperature ≥100.0°F
- Systemic illness signs (rigors, chills, significant malaise)
- Flank pain or CVA tenderness
- Perineal or pelvic pain in men (suggests prostatitis)

**Pathophysiology:** Ascent of bacteria (usually intestinal flora) accessing the urinary tract.

**Pathogens:** E. coli (most common), S. saprophyticus, Klebsiella, Enterococcus, Proteus, P. aeruginosa, Enterobacter, Group B strep.

**Clinical features:** Urinary frequency/urgency, dysuria, suprapubic pain.

**Diagnosis:**
- **Urinalysis:** Demonstrates pyuria (required but not sufficient). Absence of pyuria strongly suggests against UTI. May show leukocyte esterase, nitrites, WBCs (>10/hpf = pyuria).
- **Urine culture:** Identifies causative organism for tailored therapy.

**Treatment:** First-line options:
- Nitrofurantoin 100 mg BID ×5 days
- Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 1 DS tablet BID ×3 days
- Alternatives: Amoxicillin-clavulanate 500 mg BID ×5–7 days, cefpodoxime 100 mg BID ×5–7 days, cephalexin 500 mg PO QID ×5–7 days

---

### Pyelonephritis

**Pathophysiology:** Ascending infections extend beyond the bladder to the upper urinary tract (ureters, kidneys).

**Microbiology:** Gram-negative (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, P. aeruginosa), Gram-positive (Enterococcus faecalis, S. aureus suggesting descending UTI).

**Clinical features:** Flank pain, CVA tenderness, systemic illness signs (fevers, chills, rigors).

**Diagnosis:** Clinical features + pyuria and/or bacteriuria. Urinalysis and urine culture (false negatives possible with obstructing stones). Blood cultures in severe sepsis, septic shock, or bacteremia symptoms. Imaging (ultrasound or CT) for severely ill, persistent illness despite 48–72 hours of antibiotics, or suspected obstruction.

**Treatment:**
- **Outpatients:** PO ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, cefpodoxime, or cephalexin.
- **Inpatients:** Ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily (if low risk for MDR). Review prior urine cultures for P. aeruginosa or ESBL history. Broader empiric coverage if critically ill.

---

### Catheter-Associated UTI (CAUTI)

**Epidemiology:** Most common healthcare-acquired infection.

**Microbiology:** Similar to other UTIs plus Candida spp.

**Diagnosis:** Pyuria and bacteriuria (≥10⁵ CFU/mL) or funguria in a patient with indwelling urinary catheter AND systemic infection findings OR urinary-localizing findings AND exclusion of alternative infection source.

**Treatment:** Antibiotics, remove urinary catheter as soon as possible.

---

### Prostatitis

**Pathogens:** Gram-negative rods (E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus, P. aeruginosa, Enterobacter).

**Acute bacterial prostatitis:**
- **Pathophysiology:** Pathogens gain entrance to the prostate via the urethra.
- **Clinical features:** Acutely ill with high fevers, chills, dysuria, urgency, frequency, pelvic or perineal pain.
- **Diagnosis:** Digital rectal exam reveals boggy, tender prostate. Urinalysis shows profound pyuria. Urine culture almost always positive.
- **Treatment:** Mild illness: Outpatient with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 1 DS BID or ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID. Severe illness: Admit, treat with IV ciprofloxacin or ceftriaxone. Carbapenems if MDR risk. Duration: 2–6 weeks.

**Chronic bacterial prostatitis:**
- **Pathophysiology:** Similar to acute, arising from incomplete treatment.
- **Clinical features:** More common than acute; men age 40–70; often asymptomatic or mildly ill. Recurrent UTIs with same organism. Dull back, scrotal, perineal, or suprapubic pain.
- **Diagnosis:** Finding bacteria at higher concentration in prostatic fluid vs. urinary tract specimens (gold standard); insensitive, so empiric treatment acceptable.
- **Treatment:** Prolonged antibiotics (4 weeks). Fluoroquinolones or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole achieve excellent prostatic concentrations.

## Infective Endocarditis

### Pathogens

- **Culture-positive:** Gram-positive cocci most common: S. aureus (most common), coagulase-negative staphylococci, Streptococcus spp. (viridans streptococci), Enterococcus spp.
- **Culture-negative:** HACEK organisms (Hemophilus, Aggregatibacter, Cardiobacterium hominis, Eikenella, Kingella). Coxiella burnetii and Bartonella are now most common causes of true culture-negative endocarditis.
- **Prosthetic valve:**
  - 0–1 year after surgery: S. aureus and coagulase-negative staphylococci most common.
  - ≥1 year after surgery: Similar to native valve.

### Risk Factors

- Age >60
- **Injection drug use (IDU):** High risk for bacteremia; seeds right side of heart first (tricuspid valve); particulate matter in injected material causes direct valve damage.
- Poor dentition/dental infection
- Structural heart disease

### Clinical Features

- **Fever:** Most common symptom.
- **New cardiac murmur:** Present in most cases.
- **Left-sided endocarditis:** Embolic phenomena to systemic circulation (brain, spleen, kidneys, bone/joints). Splinter hemorrhages, Janeway lesions (painless erythematous macules on palms/soles), Osler nodes (painful nodules on finger pads).
- **Right-sided endocarditis:** Cough, pleuritic pain, shortness of breath. Septic pulmonary emboli (diffuse, peripheral, randomly distributed pulmonary nodules that may cavitate).

### Diagnosis

- Collect at least three sets of blood cultures prior to antibiotics.
- Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) in all patients; if negative, obtain TEE (more sensitive, preferred for prosthetic valves and perivalvular abscess concern).
- Consider ECG, CXR/CT chest, CT abdomen/pelvis.
- Modified Duke criteria (see table below).

### Modified Duke's Criteria for Diagnosing Native-Valve Infective Endocarditis

**Pathologic Criteria:**
- Histologic evidence of active endocarditis on vegetation or endocardial abscess sampling.
- Culture or histology of vegetation or endocardial abscess showing microorganisms.

**Major Criteria:**
- Positive blood cultures meeting one of the following:
  - Two cultures positive with organisms typically causing endocarditis (viridans streptococci, S. aureus, S. bovis, HACEK organisms, enterococci without a primary source).
  - Persistently positive cultures (2 cultures >12 hours apart with typical organisms OR ≥3 of ≥4 sets positive with common skin contaminants).
- Single positive culture for Coxiella burnetii or phase I IgG titer >1:800.
- Echocardiographic findings: Vegetation, abscess, dehisced prosthetic valve, new valvular regurgitation.

**Minor Criteria:**
- Fever
- Risk factors (injection drug use, valvular heart disease)
- Embolic phenomena
- Immunologic phenomena
- Positive blood cultures not meeting major criteria.

### Treatment

| Type | Empiric Regimen | Duration |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Native valve** | Vancomycin IV + ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily, then narrow based on culture results | 4–6 weeks total |
| **Prosthetic valve** | Vancomycin IV + antipseudomonal cephalosporin (e.g., cefepime) ± gentamicin and rifampin, narrow based on results | 6 weeks |

**Surgical Indications:** Heart failure, fungal or MDR organism infection, persistent infection despite therapy, complicated infections (paravalvular abscess, heart block, fistula), recurrent emboli or enlarging vegetation, large vegetations (>10 mm), severe valvular regurgitation. Consult cardiothoracic surgery.

## Bloodstream Infections

### Pathophysiology

Bloodstream infection can arise from:
- Endovascular source (endocarditis, septic thrombophlebitis)
- Complication of another organ infection (e.g., E. coli bacteremia from pyelonephritis)
- Direct bloodstream inoculation (central line infection, IV drug use)

### Risk Factors

Any risk factor for severe infection increases bacteremia risk. Indwelling devices (central venous catheters, hemodialysis catheters) increase risk. Certain infections carry increased bacteremia risk (pyelonephritis, cholangitis, pyogenic liver abscess, severe HAP/VAP, severe SSTI).

### Pathogens

- **Gram-positive:** S. aureus, S. pneumoniae (often from S. pneumoniae pneumonia), viridans streptococci, Enterococcus, S. lugdunensis.
- **Gram-negative:** E. coli, K. pneumoniae, P. aeruginosa, Enterobacter.
- **Fungi:** Most often Candida spp. (immunocompromised, critically ill).

### Clinical Features

Rigors suggest bloodstream infection. Other features nonspecific (fevers, malaise).

### Diagnosis

Blood cultures ×2. Note: Positive blood culture does not always mean clinically significant bloodstream infection.

### Interpretation of Positive Blood Cultures

| Significance | Organisms |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Always clinically significant** | S. aureus, S. lugdunensis, S. pneumoniae, Group A streptococcus, P. aeruginosa, E. coli, K. pneumoniae, Candida spp., Enterococcus spp. |
| **Often clinically significant, occasionally contaminant** | Viridans streptococci |
| **Often contaminants** | Coagulase-negative staphylococci (except S. lugdunensis), Corynebacterium spp. |

### Treatment

- **Source control:** Remove central venous catheters if possible, drain fluid collections, obtain TTE for S. aureus bacteremia or suspicion for endocarditis.
- **Empiric antibiotic therapy:**
  - Gram-positive cocci: Vancomycin.
  - Gram-negative rods: Ceftriaxone IV 2 g daily (or broader coverage if P. aeruginosa or ESBL risk factors). Ensure anaerobic coverage for intraabdominal infection, empyema, or necrotizing SSTI.
  - Candida spp.: An echinocandin.
- **Targeted therapy and duration:** Depends on antimicrobial susceptibilities and source of infection. Gram-positive: Endocarditis and S. aureus 4–6 weeks; others 10–14 days. Gram-negative: 7–14 days; can transition to oral after clinical improvement if susceptibilities allow (fluoroquinolone preferred).
- **Special considerations:**
  - S. aureus: Consult ID (improved mortality). Obtain TTE for all cases.
  - S. lugdunensis: TTE and ID consult (aggressive infection with metastatic foci; treat like S. aureus).
  - Candida: ID and ophthalmology consult (endophthalmitis). Remove central venous catheters.

## Skin and Soft Tissue Infections (SSTIs)

### Nonpurulent and Purulent Cellulitis

**Pathophysiology:** Infection of deeper layers of dermis. Can be nonpurulent or purulent (associated with pustules or abscess).

**Pathogens:**
- Nonpurulent: Beta-hemolytic streptococci, especially Group A (S. pyogenes). MRSA rarely implicated.
- Purulent/abscess: S. aureus, including MRSA.

**Clinical features:** Unilateral warmth, tenderness, and erythema of a distal extremity. Bilateral involvement is rare. Fever, chills, or systemic manifestations can be present.

**Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis! Typically no diagnostic tests needed. Consider imaging if necrotizing SSTI or abscess suspected; blood cultures if notable systemic symptoms; Gram stain/culture of purulent fluid after I&D.

**Treatment:**

| Type | Outpatient | Inpatient |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Nonpurulent cellulitis** | Cephalexin 500 mg PO QID (preferred) or clindamycin 300 mg PO TID (alternative). Duration: 5 days. | Cefazolin 1 g IV q8h. If not responding after 72h, obtain imaging, expand to MRSA coverage, consider alternative diagnoses. |
| **Purulent cellulitis/abscess** | If drainable abscess present: I&D ± antibiotics (TMP-SMX, doxycycline, or clindamycin). If no abscess: MRSA coverage with TMP-SMX 1 DS BID or doxycycline 100 mg BID or clindamycin 300 mg PO TID. Duration: 5 days. | IV vancomycin (preferred). Alternatives: IV daptomycin or linezolid. If responding, switch to PO. Duration: 5 days from vancomycin start. |

---

### Necrotizing Fasciitis

**Pathophysiology:** Aggressive, morbid, limb- and life-threatening infection within deep skin layers.
- **Type 1:** Polymicrobial (aerobic + anaerobic organisms).
- **Type 2:** Monomicrobial (Group A strep most common, less often other beta-hemolytic streptococci).

**Clinical features:** Physical exam shows erythema (often more purple), pain out of proportion to skin findings, crepitus (subcutaneous gas). Labs nonspecific (leukocytosis, hyponatremia, elevated inflammatory markers).

**Diagnosis:** Clinical. Consult general surgery immediately. Imaging can help risk-stratify (soft tissue gas) but should not delay surgical consultation.

**Treatment:** Empiric antibiotics (vancomycin + ertapenem or piperacillin-tazobactam + clindamycin for antitoxin effect). **Emergent surgical debridement is critical.** Mortality approaches high rates without surgery.

---

### Bite-Related Infections

**Pathogens:** Consider pathogens in the animal's mouth and on the patient's skin:
- **Canine/feline oral flora:** Pasteurella spp., Capnocytophaga canimorsus, Staphylococcus spp., Streptococcus spp. Cat bites are more morbid (>half become infected vs. ~1 in 5 dog bites) due to deeper penetration.
- **Human oral flora:** Staphylococcus spp., Streptococcus spp., anaerobes (Eikenella corrodens, Peptostreptococcus, Fusobacterium, Prevotella).
- Most bite infections are polymicrobial.

**Treatment:** Antibiotic prophylaxis with amoxicillin-clavulanate 875 mg PO BID for 3–5 days for: Lacerations requiring stitches, bite wounds on hand/face/genitals, immunocompromise or advanced liver disease, edema, deep puncture, or vascular graft near bite site. Consider surgical consultation for deep space infection, hand/face infection, immunocompromise, or crepitus. Ensure tetanus vaccination current. Consider rabies post-exposure prophylaxis.

## Bone and Joint Infections

### Osteomyelitis

**Types and Pathogenesis:**
- **Hematogenous osteomyelitis:** Develops with bacteremia (endocarditis or other endovascular infection). S. aureus most common.
- **Vertebral osteomyelitis:** Hematogenous spread, local tissue invasion, or direct inoculation after procedure. S. aureus most common, plus Streptococcus, Gram-negative rods, Mycobacterium (Pott disease = TB spine).
- **Nonhematogenous osteomyelitis:** Poor wound healing (diabetic foot ulcers, decubitus ulcers). Common skin flora and Gram-negative organisms most common.

**Symptoms:** Dull pain over infected bone (gradual onset), local infection signs (erythema, warmth, swelling, tenderness), systemic infection signs (fevers, rigors).

**Diagnostic tests:** Labs often nonspecific (leukocytosis, elevated ESR/CRP). Blood cultures obtain in all patients before antibiotics (most often positive in hematogenous osteomyelitis). Imaging: MRI most sensitive; X-ray useful for longstanding symptoms.

**Diagnosis:** Definitive diagnosis with bone biopsy (open biopsy higher yield than needle). Inferred diagnosis with combination of clinical features and imaging.

**Treatment:** Antibiotics: Empiric vancomycin + ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily (or cefepime 2 g IV q8h for P. aeruginosa coverage). Narrow based on cultures. Duration: Typically 6 weeks. Surgical: Bone debridement sometimes needed if necrosis present. Consult orthopedic surgeon.

### Septic Arthritis

**Pathogenesis:**
- **Hematogenous seeding:** Most common mechanism; hematogenous spread to synovial membrane extending into joint space.
- **Direct inoculation:** Less common; local spread from contiguous tissues (bite wound, joint space procedure).

**Risk factors:** Rheumatoid arthritis or other joint pathology.

**Pathogens:**
- S. aureus: Most common cause, especially with prosthetic joint or RA.
- N. gonorrhoeae: Most common in young, previously healthy adults.
- Streptococcus spp.: Increased risk with functional or true asplenia.
- Gram-negative organisms (e.g., P. aeruginosa): Immunocompromised patients.
- Mycobacterium spp.: Underlying immunocompromise or TB risk factors.

**Clinical features:** Red, hot, swollen, tender joint with extreme pain with passive or active range of motion. Joint effusion often present. Fever may be absent (especially older adults).

**Diagnosis:** Arthrocentesis and synovial fluid analysis:
- **WBC count:** >50K cells/µL suggests septic arthritis (especially >100K); differential often neutrophil-predominant. Crystal arthritis can have similarly high counts.
- **Gram stain:** Positive in most cases; negative does not rule out.
- **Culture:** Positive in most cases of non-gonococcal septic arthritis.
- **Crystals:** Presence suggests crystal arthritis but does not exclude concurrent septic arthritis.

**Treatment:**
- **Empiric antibiotics:** Vancomycin + ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily (without P. aeruginosa coverage) OR vancomycin + cefepime 2 g IV q8h (with P. aeruginosa coverage). Narrow based on culture results. Duration: 2–4 weeks.
- **Joint drainage:** Surgical joint washout is cornerstone. Consult orthopedic surgery.

## Systemic Fungal Infections

### Diagnostic Tests for Fungal Infections

| Test | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **1,3-Beta-D-glucan (BDG)** | Tests for cell wall polysaccharide present in most fungi (not mucormycosis, Cryptococcus, Blastomyces). Sensitivity/specificity ~75%. False positives with IVIG, albumin, hemodialysis filters. |
| **Galactomannan** | Tests for Aspergillus cell wall component. Sensitivity ~70% in serum; more sensitive on BAL. Rare false positives with TPN, piperacillin-tazobactam (less common with newer formulations). |
| **Cryptococcal antigen (CrAg)** | Tests for Cryptococcus antigen. Serum test useful in HIV/AIDS with CD4 <100 cells/μL. If positive, LP to rule out meningitis. |
| **Histoplasma antigen** | Tests for histoplasmosis antigen. Sensitivity >70% (urine) and ~65% (serum). Very sensitive for disseminated histoplasmosis in HIV. |
| **Cocci immunofixation and complement fixation** | Evaluates for coccidioidomycosis. Different sensitivity/specificity for blood vs. other specimens. |

## Sexually Transmitted Infections

### Chlamydia and Gonorrhea

**Pathogens:** Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae (most common bacterial STIs).

**Clinical features:** Purulent urethral discharge, scrotal pain or pelvic pain, dysuria, intermenstrual bleeding.

**Diagnosis:** Vaginal or urinary NAAT. Obtain pharyngeal and/or rectal swabs for NAAT in patients with oral sex or receptive anal intercourse history.

**Treatment:**
- **Chlamydia:** Azithromycin 1000 mg PO ×1.
- **Gonorrhea:** Ceftriaxone 250 mg IM ×1 + azithromycin 1000 mg PO ×1.

Sexual partners should be referred for evaluation and presumptive therapy.

---

### Disseminated Gonococcal Infection (DGI)

**Pathophysiology:** Systemic infection when genital N. gonorrhoeae infection spreads to bloodstream.

**Clinical features:**
- **Tenosynovitis, dermatitis, and polyarthralgias:** Pustular or vesiculopustular rash present in about half of cases.
- **Acute purulent arthritis:** One or multiple joints; common joints = wrists, knees, ankles.

**Diagnosis:** Blood cultures may return positive for N. gonorrhoeae. Positive NAAT testing of mucosal sites. Synovial fluid analysis in patients with acute arthritis.

**Treatment:** Ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily + azithromycin 1 g PO ×1 ± surgical joint drainage for acute arthritis not responding to therapy.

---

### Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)

**Subtypes:**
- **HSV-1:** Causes oropharyngeal or genital ulcers. Often asymptomatic with primary infection; virus remains dormant in trigeminal ganglia and can reactivate to cause lesions near mouth/lips.
- **HSV-2:** Causes recurrent genital ulcers with painful genital ulcers, prodrome burning/pruritus, lymphadenopathy.

**Treatment:** Acyclovir. Chronic suppressive therapy for ≥6 outbreaks/year or to prevent transmission; episodic therapy for fewer outbreaks.

**Complications:** Disseminated HSV (encephalitis, meningitis, keratitis, chorioretinitis in immunocompromised). Ocular disease (keratitis, blepharitis, keratoconjunctivitis). Herpetic whitlow (hand infection).

**Diagnosis:** PCR (most sensitive) or viral culture.

---

### Syphilis

**Microbiology & Pathogenesis:** Treponema pallidum is a slender spirochete with rotary-type movements. Visible with darkfield microscopy. Transmission occurs via direct contact with open lesions (chancre or condyloma lata) during intercourse or other physical/sexual contact.

**Phases:**
- **Primary:** Painless ulcerated lesion with raised, indurated edges. Regional lymphadenopathy. Resolves spontaneously within 2 weeks.
- **Secondary:** Systemic symptoms (fevers, chills, malaise) and multiple potential manifestations:
  - Rash (diffuse macular rash of trunk, extremities, palms, and soles)
  - Mucous patches (white erosive lesions of oral mucosa/tongue)
  - Condyloma lata (raised lesions in perineal or oral areas)
  - Alopecia (patchy, scattered, "motheaten" pattern; often irreversible)
- **Latent:** Asymptomatic phase between symptomatic phases. Positive syphilis tests. Early latent = infection in last 12 months; Late latent = unknown time or >12 months ago.
- **Tertiary:** Late manifestations of untreated syphilis (rare but still present):
  - **Cardiovascular:** Large vessel vasculitis causing aortitis or aortopathy (aortic insufficiency).
  - **Neurologic:** General paresis (dementia syndrome with memory deficits, psychosis); Tabes dorsalis (posterior column disease causing lancinating pains, sensory ataxia, Argyll Robertson pupil).
  - **Neurosyphilis:** Can develop at any phase. Symptoms include meningitis, uveitis (ocular syphilis), hearing loss/tinnitus (otosyphilis), rare CNS vasculitis.

**Diagnosis:**
- Direct visualization with darkfield microscopy from skin lesions with high organism burden.
- **Serologic testing:**
  - **Nontreponemal tests (monitor disease activity, not specific):** RPR, VDRL. Titers wane with time or treatment.
  - **Treponemal tests (remain positive after treatment):** FTA-ABS, TPPA, TPEIA, CIA.
- **Diagnosis of neurosyphilis:** Positive CSF analysis for neurosyphilis or symptoms consistent with ocular or otosyphilis.

**Treatment:**
- **Primary, secondary, early latent:** Penicillin G 2.4 million units IM ×1 dose.
- **Tertiary or late latent:** Penicillin G 2.4 million units IM once weekly ×3 weeks.
- **Neuro:** Penicillin G 3–4 million units IV q4 hours ×10–14 days.

### Chancroid

**Pathogen:** Haemophilus ducreyi.

**Clinical features:** Painful genital ulcer(s) with ragged border and unilateral painful inguinal lymphadenopathy (buboes) occurring with ulcer(s).

**Diagnosis:** No serologic tests available; rule out syphilis and HSV-2.

**Treatment:** Azithromycin (oral, one dose) OR ceftriaxone (IM, one dose).

---

### Lymphogranuloma Venereum (LGV)

**Pathogen:** Chlamydia trachomatis (serovars L1–L3).

**Clinical features:** Painless anal or genital lesion(s) often going unnoticed, followed by tender lymphadenopathy weeks later. Third phase includes genital/anal/rectal syndrome with pelvic lymphadenopathy, diarrhea, proctitis, and potentially abscesses or fistulas.

**Diagnosis:** C. trachomatis NAAT (urine, rectal), antibody testing.

**Treatment:** Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID ×21 days.

---

### Genital Warts (HPV)

**Clinical features:** Painless flesh-colored, exophytic lesions.

**Treatment:** Podophyllin, TCA, cryotherapy, surgery. Treatment does not prevent HPV transmission.

### HIV/AIDS

**Pathogenesis:**
1. **Viral entry:** HIV gp120 binds CD4 on CD4+ T cells, then interacts with chemokine receptor, and gp41 mediates fusion.
2. **Transcription:** Reverse transcriptase transcribes HIV RNA into double-stranded DNA.
3. **Integration:** Viral DNA integrates with host DNA via integrase.
4. **Cleavage:** Proteases cleave viral RNA into large polyproteins.

**Transmission:** Sexual intercourse (oral, vaginal, anal), exposure to infected blood (injection drug use, needlestick), perinatal.

**Stages:**
- **Stage 0:** Negative HIV test within 6 months of diagnosis (early infection).
- **Stage 1:** CD4 count ≥500 cells/µL or ≥14%.
- **Stage 2:** CD4 count 200–499 cells/µL or 14–19%.
- **Stage 3:** CD4 count <200 cells/µL or <14% OR stage-3 defining OI (AIDS).

**Diagnosis:** Three markers turn positive at different phases:
- HIV RNA viral load (~10 days)
- HIV p24 antigen (~15–20 days)
- HIV antibody (~20–30 days)

Fourth-generation HIV test (p24 Ag + HIV Ab) can be negative during the "window period" when only HIV RNA can detect acute infection.

**Treatment:** Antiretroviral therapy (ART) for all patients at diagnosis. Early treatment improves outcomes. Certain OIs prompt delayed ART: Cryptococcal meningitis (IRIS risk), tuberculous meningitis, CMV retinitis, patients who are intubated or hypotensive from non-CNS OI or bacterial infection.

**Opportunistic Infections:** See empiric treatment guidelines for specific infections and prophylaxis based on CD4 count.

**Prevention:**
- **Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP):** After high-risk exposure (unprotected sex, percutaneous blood exposure). Tenofovir/emtricitabine + dolutegravir or raltegravir.
- **Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP):** For high-risk populations; daily antiretroviral medication.

## Tickborne Illnesses

### Lyme Disease

**Etiology:** Borrelia burgdorferi spirochete spread by Ixodes scapularis tick in the Northeastern US and Northern Midwest.

**Stages:**
- **Stage 1 (Early localized, 1–4 weeks):** Single erythema migrans (target-shaped skin lesion).
- **Stage 2 (Early disseminated, weeks-months):** Multiple erythema migrans skin lesions, fever, headache, heart block/myocarditis, cranial nerve palsy, meningitis.
- **Stage 3 (Late infection, months-years later):** Oligoarticular arthritis, encephalopathy or encephalomyelitis.

**Post-Lyme disease syndrome:** No biologic evidence for "chronic Lyme"; no benefit to antibiotics for this syndrome.

**Diagnosis:** Two-tier testing with ELISA and Western blot confirmation.

**Treatment:** PO doxycycline. IV ceftriaxone for more severe Stage 2 and 3 disease (e.g., meningitis).
