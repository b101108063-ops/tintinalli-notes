## 1. Central Nervous System Infections

### Meningitis

Meningitis and encephalitis are two distinct clinical entities, each with their own list of causative organisms, though in practice the distinction may be blurred as these syndromes can share clinical features.

**Clinical features** of meningitis include the classic triad of fever, nuchal rigidity, and altered mental status, although fewer than **50%** of patients have all three symptoms. Physical exam findings that have limited diagnostic utility include Kernig's sign (inability to fully extend knees when patient is supine with hips flexed), Brudzinski's sign (flexion of legs and thighs brought on by passive flexion of neck), and the jolt test (painful to turn head side-to-side). Rashes may be present: *N. meningitidis* causes a maculopapular rash with petechiae or purpura in approximately **50%** of patients; HSV causes vesicular lesions; and VZV causes vesicular lesions with the potential for encephalitis to develop weeks before or after the onset of rash.

**Pathogens** vary by age group. In neonates, the most common organisms are Group B strep, *E. coli*, and *L. monocytogenes*. In children older than 3 months, the most common organisms are *N. meningitidis*, *S. pneumoniae*, and *H. influenzae*. In adults, *S. pneumoniae* accounts for more than **70%** of cases, followed by *N. meningitidis* (**12%**), Group B strep (**7%**), and *H. influenzae* (**6%**). In elderly or immunocompromised patients, *L. monocytogenes* accounts for less than **5%** of cases. Viral causes include enterovirus, HSV-2, VZV, HIV, mumps, and arbovirus. Other pathogens include *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, syphilis, *Cryptococcus* species, and *Coccidioides* species.

**Diagnosis** of meningitis requires a lumbar puncture (LP), though care should be taken to ensure there are no contraindications to LP such as deteriorating level of consciousness, anticoagulation, or epidural abscess. A noncontrast head CT (NCHCT) should be performed prior to LP if there are new focal neurologic deficits, altered mental status, age greater than 60 years, immunocompromise, concern for increased intracranial pressure (papilledema or vomiting), seizures, or known brain metastases. Blood cultures should be obtained prior to antibiotics, but empiric antibiotics should not be delayed while waiting for head imaging or LP. Studies that should be sent from the CSF include cell count and differential, gram stain and bacterial culture, glucose concentration (with simultaneous serum glucose to evaluate the ratio of CSF to serum glucose), and protein concentration.

| Condition | Cell Count (cells/µL) | Glucose (mg/dL) | Protein (mg/dL) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal** | 0–5 | 45–80 | 18–58 |
| **Bacterial meningitis** | >500 (PMN predominant) | <40 | >200 |
| **TB meningitis** | 5–1000 (lymphocyte predominant) | <10 | >400 |
| **Viral meningitis** | 100–1000 (lymphocyte predominant) | 45–80 | 50–200 |
| **Guillain-Barré** | 0–5 | 45–80 | 45–1000 |

**Treatment** of meningitis involves empiric therapy based on patient age and immune status. For adults younger than 50 years without immunocompromise, empiric therapy includes vancomycin plus ceftriaxone (2 g IV q12h) plus acyclovir (if encephalitis is suspected) plus adjunctive corticosteroids (dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg q6h for 4 days, with the first dose given before antibiotics if *S. pneumoniae* is suspected). For adults older than 50 years or with immunocompromise, empiric therapy includes vancomycin plus ceftriaxone (2 g IV q12h) plus ampicillin (2 g IV q4h) plus acyclovir plus corticosteroids.

**Prevention** of meningitis involves vaccinating all individuals aged 65 years and older and immunocompromised patients for *S. pneumoniae*, as well as vaccinating all asplenic patients for *S. pneumoniae*, *N. meningitidis*, and *H. influenzae*.

### Encephalitis

**Clinical features** of encephalitis include encephalopathy, seizures, and altered mental status. HSV-1 specifically infects the temporal lobe, causing aphasia, olfactory hallucinations, and personality changes.

**Pathogens** causing encephalitis include viral causes such as HSV-1, HSV-2, VZV, arbovirus (eastern equine encephalitis, West Nile virus, St. Louis encephalitis virus), enterovirus, measles, mumps, EBV, and CMV. Parasitic causes include toxoplasmosis. Noninfectious causes include autoimmune encephalitis such as anti-NMDA receptor antibody encephalitis, which is often associated with ovarian teratomas, and T-cell lymphoma.

**Diagnosis** involves performing an NCHCT followed by LP. An MRI of the brain may show increased T2 flair in the frontotemporal region if HSV-1 is present. Treatment is often supportive, and empiric therapy should be initiated as indicated.

### Brain Abscess

**Pathophysiology** of brain abscess involves complications from otitis media, sinusitis, odontogenic infection, or postsurgical infection. **Clinical features** are often nonspecific and include fever, headache, and focal neurologic deficits. The classic triad of all three is present in only approximately **20%** of cases.

**Pathogens** include *Streptococcus* species and *Staphylococcus* species as the most frequent organisms, and infections are often polymicrobial (approximately **40%**) containing both aerobic and anaerobic organisms.

**Diagnosis** is made by brain MRI. Needle aspiration and surgical excision are possible options for making a microbiologic diagnosis. **Treatment** involves empiric antibiotics with vancomycin plus ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h plus metronidazole 500 mg IV or PO q8h, along with immediate neurosurgical evaluation for consideration of neurosurgical excision or drainage. Adjunctive glucocorticoids may be considered if mass effect is seen on imaging.

### Spinal Epidural Abscess

**Pathophysiology** of spinal epidural abscess involves bacteria spreading from infected vertebrae or nearby soft tissues, or via hematogenous spread. **Clinical features** often include a slow, insidious presentation with symptoms lasting weeks to months, though more acute presentations can occur with aggressive organisms like *S. aureus*. Back pain is the most common symptom. Fever is variably present. Approximately **50%** of patients have a neurologic symptom such as radiculopathy or motor or sensory dysfunction, which often develops later in the disease course.

**Pathogens** include *Streptococcus* species and *Staphylococcus* species. **Diagnosis** is made by spinal MRI. **Treatment** involves empiric antibiotics with vancomycin IV plus ceftriaxone IV 2 g q12h, with ceftriaxone potentially substituted for an antipseudomonal beta-lactam such as cefepime 2 g IV q8h. Immediate neurosurgical evaluation and consideration of surgical drainage is essential.

## 2. Pulmonary Infections

### Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

**CAP** is defined as an acute pulmonary infection that develops outside of the hospital or within the first 48 hours of admission. The **pathophysiology** involves organisms that colonize the nasopharynx making their way to the lungs via microaspiration. Once in the lung, poor mucociliary clearance and the organism's virulence factors allow replication and establishment of infection, and the body's immune response creates the clinical features of pneumonia.

**Clinical features** include cough, shortness of breath, and fever. **Severe CAP** is defined by the presence of one or more major criteria or three or more minor criteria. Major criteria include septic shock requiring vasopressors and respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation. Minor criteria include respiratory rate of 30 or greater breaths per minute, PaO2/FiO2 ratio of 250 or less, multilobar infiltrates, confusion or encephalopathy, BUN of **20 mg/dL** or greater, leukopenia (WBC less than 4,000 cells/µL from infection alone), thrombocytopenia (platelets less than 100K cells/µL), hypothermia (temperature less than 36°C), and hypotension requiring fluid resuscitation.

**Pathogens** include typical bacteria such as *S. pneumoniae* (historically the most common cause, with rates decreasing due to pneumococcal vaccines), *H. influenzae* (especially prominent in those with underlying lung disease), and *M. catarrhalis*. Atypical bacteria include *M. pneumoniae* (transmitted with high frequency via close contact), *C. pneumoniae*, and *C. psittaci*. *Legionella* species are associated with exposure to aerosols from air conditioners, showers, spas, and fountains, as well as travel. Other clinically relevant bacteria include *S. aureus*, which includes both MSSA and MRSA isolates, with preceding influenza being a risk factor for *S. aureus* pneumonia. Gram-negative rods include *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, which is an uncommon cause of CAP but has specific risk factors including structural lung disease, recent broad-spectrum antibiotic use, severe immunocompromise, and frequent COPD exacerbations. *Klebsiella pneumoniae* is most common in patients with underlying lung disease or heavy alcohol use. Viral causes of CAP include influenza A and B, RSV, parainfluenza viruses, rhinovirus, human metapneumovirus, and coronavirus including SARS-CoV-2.

**Diagnosis** involves chest X-ray and or chest CT showing a focal consolidation with a compatible clinical syndrome suggesting pneumonia. Blood cultures, sputum gram stain, and sputum culture should be obtained in patients with severe CAP, immunocompromised hosts, and patients being empirically treated for MRSA or *P. aeruginosa*. A nasopharyngeal swab for influenza PCR testing should be obtained in all patients with suspected CAP during influenza season.

**Treatment** of CAP that can be managed in the outpatient setting includes amoxicillin, doxycycline, or azithromycin for patients with no recent antibiotics or comorbidities. For patients with recent antibiotics, more severe cases, or notable comorbidities, options include amoxicillin-clavulanate or cefpodoxime or cefuroxime combined with doxycycline or azithromycin. Respiratory fluoroquinolones such as levofloxacin or moxifloxacin are also options. For CAP requiring hospital admission to the general medical floor, ceftriaxone plus azithromycin or clarithromycin plus empiric oseltamivir during flu season until influenza testing returns is recommended. For severe CAP requiring ICU admission, vancomycin plus ceftriaxone or cefepime (if *P. aeruginosa* coverage is needed) plus azithromycin plus empiric oseltamivir is indicated. The duration of therapy is typically 5 days for most patients, with extension considered for those with delayed response to therapy. Patients with *S. aureus* or *Pseudomonas* should be treated for at least 7 days.

### Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia (HAP) and Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)

**HAP** is defined as pneumonia diagnosed more than 48 hours after hospital admission and not present at hospital admission. **VAP** is defined as pneumonia diagnosed more than 48 hours after intubation.

**Pathogens** include gram-negative rods such as *P. aeruginosa*, *Klebsiella* species, *E. coli*, *Enterobacter* species, and multidrug-resistant gram-negative rods including ESBL-producing organisms. Gram-positive cocci include *S. aureus*.

**Clinical features** are similar to CAP and include the presence of new systemic signs of infection plus suggestion of a new pulmonary process. **Diagnosis** involves obtaining sputum cultures, peripheral blood cultures, MRSA nasal swab, and chest imaging. **Treatment** should include coverage of MRSA (such as with vancomycin) and coverage of *P. aeruginosa* and gram-negative rods (such as with piperacillin-tazobactam or cefepime). The duration of therapy is typically 7 days, though complications such as bacteremia, lung abscess, or complicated pleural effusions may warrant longer courses.

### Parapneumonic Effusions

**Parapneumonic effusions** are pleural effusions that develop in the setting of and adjacent to pneumonia. **Pathogens** are most commonly pyogenic bacterial infections with gram-positive organisms, including *S. pneumoniae*, viridans streptococci, *S. aureus* (most common cause of hospital-acquired parapneumonic effusions), and anaerobic oral flora.

**Clinical features** are similar to pneumonia, and dullness to percussion on exam may increase suspicion. **Diagnosis** involves imaging and pleural fluid analysis. **Treatment** depends on the type of effusion. Uncomplicated parapneumonic effusions are free-flowing without loculations, have sterile pleural fluid with normal pH and glucose levels, and can be managed with antibiotics alone without drainage. Complicated parapneumonic effusions show loculations on imaging, are exudative on Light's criteria, and have a pH less than 7.2, pleural fluid glucose less than **40 mg/dL**, and or positive gram stain or culture. These require antibiotics including anaerobic coverage and drainage with a chest tube. Empyema shares characteristics of complicated parapneumonic effusion with the addition of frank pus on pleural fluid sampling.

### Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia (PJP)

**Risk factors** for PJP include HIV (CD4 less than 200 cells/µL), cancer, solid organ transplant, rheumatologic disease (particularly granulomatosis with polyangiitis), and patients on chronic steroids (prednisone 20 mg or greater for 20 or more days).

**Clinical features** in HIV-infected patients include a gradual onset of fever, cough, and dyspnea over days to weeks. In non-HIV-infected patients, symptoms may be more aggressive and abrupt in onset.

**Diagnosis** involves bronchoscopy as the gold standard, beta-D glucan testing, and LDH testing. Induced sputum samples may allow diagnosis if positive, and if negative, bronchoscopy should be performed. Treatment should be initiated empirically while awaiting bronchoscopy because organisms will be present for up to 2 weeks on bronchoalveolar lavage microscopy.

**Treatment** involves high-dose trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole at 15–20 mg/kg of trimethoprim divided in 3–4 daily doses for 21 days. Adjunctive glucocorticoids should be added if the A-a gradient exceeds **35 mmHg** or PaO2 is less than **70 mmHg**.

**Prophylaxis** with Bactrim is indicated for patients with HIV and CD4 count less than 200 cells/µL, individuals with other immunocompromising conditions treated with glucocorticoids at a dose of 20 mg prednisone or more for 20 days or more (the "**20/20**" rule), patients with acute lymphocytic leukemia, allogeneic stem cell transplant recipients and most autologous stem cell transplant recipients, solid organ transplant recipients, and patients with certain primary immunodeficiencies. Alternatives include dapsone, atovaquone, and inhaled pentamidine.

### Influenza

**Influenza** is caused by an influenza virus, with three of the four types (A, B, and C) infecting humans. **Clinical features** include spread by respiratory droplet with symptoms of fevers, chills, cough, and malaise, though asymptomatic infection can also occur. **Diagnosis** involves chest X-ray (normal or alveolar pattern) and influenza PCR. **Treatment** includes oseltamivir and supportive care.

### SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19)

**SARS-CoV-2** is the name of the virus that causes the disease known as COVID-19. Coronaviruses are a family of viruses that can cause mild illnesses such as the common cold or more severe illness such as SARS and MERS. In 2019, a new coronavirus was identified as the cause of a disease outbreak that originated in China and subsequently caused a global pandemic.

**Clinical features** include spread mainly by respiratory droplets, causing cough, fever, shortness of breath, muscle aches, gastrointestinal symptoms, loss of smell, and leukopenia. Approximately **40%** of cases may be asymptomatic.

**Diagnosis** involves molecular tests as the main method, with rapid antigen tests used in certain situations. **Treatment** includes supportive care as the mainstay, remdesivir in hospitalized patients with lower respiratory tract disease (200 mg IV once then 100 mg IV daily for 4 additional days, potentially extended to 10 days on a case-by-case basis), and dexamethasone 6 mg IV or PO for up to 10 days for patients undergoing mechanical ventilation, noninvasive ventilation, high-flow nasal cannula, supplemental oxygen, or whose trajectory suggests increasing severity of disease. Dexamethasone is not needed if patients are not hypoxic. Several highly effective and safe vaccines are now available.

### Mycobacterium tuberculosis

**Primary tuberculosis** presents when bacilli are inhaled and some bacteria are ingested by alveolar macrophages, with surviving ones forming granulomas. Patients are typically asymptomatic unless the immune system is weak, in which case progressive primary TB can develop. Chest X-ray findings include Ghon's complex (calcified primary focus with associated lymph nodes), and if fibrosis is present, it is known as Ranke's complex.

**Secondary (reactivation) tuberculosis** occurs when host immunity is weak and TB reactivation may occur at the apical or posterior portion of the lung. Symptoms include fever, night sweats, weight loss, malaise, and cough that may be dry or productive. Chest X-ray shows infiltrates in the upper lobe or superior segment of the lower lobes with cavitation and centrilobular nodules.

**Extrapulmonary tuberculosis** can spread via hematogenous or lymphatic spread to almost any organ, with lymph nodes being most common, followed by adrenal glands, genitourinary tract, and bones. Pleural effusions from TB are considered extrapulmonary TB.

**Miliary tuberculosis** involves hematogenous spread and presents with diffuse reticulonodular infiltrate on chest X-ray and innumerable randomly distributed nodules on CT chest.

**Nontuberculous mycobacteria (NTM)** include *M. avium* (MAC), which is the most common NTM infection often occurring in middle-aged men with underlying lung disease or female nonsmokers presenting with subacute-chronic cough with or without weight loss. *M. kansasii* is the second most common NTM infection with risk factors including COPD, HIV, and hematologic malignancy. *M. abscessus*, *M. fortuitum*, and *M. chelonae* can cause disease in those with underlying lung disease and immunosuppression.

**Diagnosis** of latent TB involves PPD (positive if greater than **15 mm** with no risk factors, greater than **10 mm** with medium risk factors, or greater than **5 mm** with high risk factors) or interferon gamma release assay (IGRA). Results are not useful for evaluating active infection. For active TB, chest X-ray or CT chest for pulmonary TB, CT imaging of the suspected area for extrapulmonary TB, core biopsy or excisional biopsy for TB lymphadenitis, and sputum AFB cultures and TB PCR are indicated.

**Treatment** of latent TB involves first ruling out active TB, then treating with rifampin for 4 months, or isoniazid and rifapentine for 3 months, or isoniazid for 9 months. All patients treated with isoniazid should also receive pyridoxine to prevent peripheral neuropathy. For active TB, RIPE therapy (rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) is used. Patients are no longer infectious when they have received adequate TB treatment for 2 weeks, show improvement of symptoms, and have three consecutive negative AFB smears.

## 3. Intraabdominal Infections

### Peritonitis and Intraabdominal Abscess

**Pathogenesis** involves disruption of or inflammation in the GI tract that allows normal bowel flora to enter the abdominal cavity and cause frank peritonitis and or the development of intraabdominal abscesses. These infections are almost always polymicrobial. Colonic organisms are predominant and include *E. coli*, *Klebsiella* species, *Proteus* species, *Enterobacter* species, *Enterococcus* species, and anaerobic organisms such as *B. fragilis*.

**Clinical features** include abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or bloating with or without fever, tachycardia, and hypotension. Some patients may appear relatively stable while others may have florid shock. Patients aged 65 years and older, immunocompromised patients, and those on chronic steroids may have especially occult presentations with less pronounced pain.

**Diagnosis** involves consulting general surgery if the patient is unstable to determine the need for immediate surgical intervention versus diagnostic imaging (typically CT abdomen and pelvis with contrast). **Treatment** involves procedural interventions including surgical intervention or percutaneous drainage, along with antibiotics to cover enteric gram-positive, gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms. Patients with certain risk factors are considered high-risk (age greater than 70 years, diffuse peritonitis, severe sepsis, immunocompromising conditions, healthcare-associated infections) and warrant coverage of MDR organisms.

### Clostridium difficile

**C. difficile** colitis can occur as a result of either community-acquired or nosocomial infections. Not all strains of *C. difficile* cause infection, and only toxin-producing strains cause diarrhea and colitis. **Risk factors** for *C. difficile* infection include recent antibiotic use (commonly clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, and cephalosporins), age of 65 years or older, and suppression of gastric acid with PPIs or H2 blockers.

**Clinical features** range from mild diarrheal illness to severe, fulminant, and potentially fatal colitis. **Severe *C. difficile* infection** is suggested by more frequent diarrhea and more severe abdominal pain, very high leukocytosis often greater than or equal to 15K cells/μL, hypotension and lactic acidosis due to hypovolemia and sepsis, AKI with creatinine greater than or equal to **1.5 mg/dL**, and colonic ileus with little to no diarrhea, known as toxic megacolon.

**Diagnosis** involves *C. difficile* stool testing in patients with diarrheal illness defined as three or more watery stools in a day without an alternative explanation. CT abdomen and pelvis should be considered if concern for severe or fulminant disease.

**Treatment** of first-line disease includes vancomycin 125 mg PO QID for 10 days or fidaxomicin 200 mg PO BID for 10 days. Metronidazole 500 mg IV TID should be added only if fulminant disease. **Recurrent disease** is defined by resolution of symptoms with therapy followed by return of symptoms within 2 months of treatment completion and requires a longer course of antibiotics.

## 4. Genitourinary Infections

### Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

**Asymptomatic bacteriuria** is defined as bacterial growth in a properly collected urine culture specimen from a patient without symptoms of inflammation of the GU system. **Management** does not warrant treatment in most individuals because there is a low risk for progression to a UTI. Three populations who warrant treatment include pregnant individuals (because asymptomatic bacteriuria confers worse outcomes in pregnancy including preterm birth, perinatal mortality, and low fetal birth weight), renal transplant recipients (treatment is indicated within one month of renal transplant because these patients are at higher risk for symptomatic UTI and acute graft rejection), and patients undergoing urologic procedures expected to cause mucosal bleeding (because untreated bacteriuria prior to urologic intervention is associated with increased rates of infectious complications).

### Simple Cystitis

**Simple cystitis** is defined as an infection of the urinary tract without signs or symptoms suggesting extension beyond the bladder, such as temperature of 100.0°F or 37.8°C or greater, rigors, chills, or significant malaise, flank pain or costovertebral angle tenderness, or perineal or pelvic pain in men suggesting prostatitis. **Pathophysiology** involves ascent of bacteria (usually intestinal flora) that gain access to the urinary tract.

**Pathogens** include *E. coli* (**80%**), *S. saprophyticus*, *Klebsiella* species, *Enterococcus* species, *Proteus* species, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Enterobacter* species, and Group B strep.

**Clinical features** include symptoms of lower urinary tract inflammation such as urinary frequency or urgency, dysuria, and suprapubic pain.

**Diagnosis** involves urinalysis demonstrating pyuria, which is required but not sufficient for diagnosis, along with urine culture to assist with identifying the causative organism and tailored antibiotic therapy.

**Treatment** with first-line options includes nitrofurantoin 100 mg BID for 5 days, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 1 DS tablet BID for 3 days, or alternative agents such as amoxicillin-clavulanate 500 mg BID for 5–7 days, cefpodoxime 100 mg BID for 5–7 days, or cephalexin 500 mg PO QID for 5–7 days.

### Pyelonephritis

**Pyelonephritis** arises when ascending infections extend beyond the bladder and enter the upper urinary tract. **Microbiology** includes gram-negative organisms (*E. coli*, *Klebsiella* species, *Enterobacter* species, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*) and gram-positive organisms (*Enterococcus faecalis*, *S. aureus* suggesting descending UTI from a bloodstream infection).

**Clinical features** include flank pain, costovertebral angle tenderness, and signs of systemic illness such as fevers, chills, and rigors.

**Diagnosis** involves clinical features suggestive of pyelonephritis plus pyuria and or bacteriuria. Urinalysis and urine culture should be obtained, though false negative UA and UCx can occur with obstructing kidney stones. Blood cultures should be obtained in patients with severe sepsis, septic shock, or other symptoms of bacteremia. Imaging is not necessary in all patients but should be considered for severely ill patients, those with persistent illness despite 48–72 hours of appropriate antibiotic therapy, and those with suspected urinary tract obstruction.

**Treatment** for outpatients includes oral antibiotic regimens such as ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, cefpodoxime, or cephalexin. For inpatients, ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily is used if there is low risk for multidrug-resistant organisms and the patient is not critically ill. Broader empiric antibiotic regimens should be considered if the patient is critically ill or has risk factors for multidrug-resistant organisms.

### Catheter-Associated UTI (CAUTI)

**CAUTIs** are the most common healthcare-acquired infection. **Microbiology** is similar to other UTIs with the addition of *Candida* species. **Clinical features** are often nonspecific, with possible dysuria and abdominal pain. **Diagnosis** requires pyuria and bacteriuria (105 CFU/mL or greater) or funguria in a patient with an indwelling urinary catheter plus systemic findings suggestive of infection, plus exclusion of an alternative source of infection. **Treatment** involves antibiotics and removal of the urinary catheter as soon as possible.

### Prostatitis

**Acute bacterial prostatitis** occurs when pathogens gain entrance to the prostate via the urethra. **Clinical features** include patients typically being acutely ill with high fevers, chills, dysuria, urgency, frequency, and pelvic or perineal pain as the most localizing feature to the prostate.

**Diagnosis** involves digital rectal exam revealing a boggy, exquisitely tender prostate. Urinalysis may show profound pyuria with sheets of WBCs on microscopy, and urine culture is almost always positive.

**Treatment** for mild illness includes outpatient management with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 1 DS tablet BID or ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID. For severe illness, admission to the hospital with IV ciprofloxacin or ceftriaxone is indicated, with carbapenems used in those with risk factors for multidrug-resistant organisms. Duration of antibiotics is 2–6 weeks depending on severity. Complications include bacteremia, prostatic abscess, or progression to chronic bacterial prostatitis.

**Chronic bacterial prostatitis** arises from incomplete treatment of acute prostatitis. **Clinical features** are more common than acute bacterial prostatitis and occur in men aged 40–70 years. Patients are often asymptomatic or only mildly ill and often have symptoms of recurrent UTIs with the same organism frequently isolated from urine culture. Dull back, scrotal, perineal, or suprapubic pain may be present.

**Diagnosis** is more difficult than for acute bacterial prostatitis. The diagnostic standard is finding bacteria at a higher concentration in prostatic fluid compared to urinary tract specimens, though testing is insensitive. **Treatment** involves prolonged antibiotics for 4 weeks, typically with fluoroquinolones or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, both of which achieve excellent concentrations in prostatic tissue.

## 5. Infective Endocarditis

**Pathogens** causing culture-positive endocarditis include gram-positive cocci as the most common organisms, particularly *Staphylococcus aureus* (the most common cause), coagulase-negative staphylococci, *Streptococcus* species such as viridans streptococci, and *Enterococcus* species. In culture-negative endocarditis, the HACEK organisms (which include *Haemophilus* species, *Aggregatibacter* species, *Cardiobacterium hominis*, *Eikenella corrodens*, and *Kingella* species) were traditionally considered predominant, though improved culture techniques have made them more easily identifiable. *Coxiella burnetii* and *Bartonella* species are now the most common causes of true culture-negative endocarditis. The most common reason for culture-negative endocarditis is that antibiotics were given prior to blood culture collection. For **prosthetic valve endocarditis**, the differential depends on time after valve replacement. In the first year after surgery, *S. aureus* and coagulase-negative staphylococci are most common. After 1 year or more, pathogens are similar to native valve endocarditis.

**Risk factors** include age greater than 60 years, injection drug use (which carries a high risk for bacteremia and seeding of the right side of the heart first, usually the tricuspid valve, and can also cause direct valve damage), poor dentition or dental infection, and structural heart disease such as valvular heart disease.

**Clinical features** include fever (the most common symptom) and inflammatory symptoms such as chills, weight loss, and myalgias. A new cardiac murmur is present in approximately **70%** of cases. Left-sided endocarditis can cause embolic phenomena from the left heart to systemic circulation, with septic emboli to any organ including brain, spleen, kidneys, and bone and joints. Splinter hemorrhages are microemboli visible in the nail beds. Janeway lesions are painless erythematous macules usually seen on the palms and soles, representing microabscesses from septic emboli. Osler nodes are painful nodules on the finger pads due to vasculitis or septic emboli. Right-sided endocarditis can cause cough, pleuritic pain, and shortness of breath, along with septic pulmonary emboli presenting as diffuse, peripheral, randomly distributed pulmonary nodules that may have cavitation.

**Diagnosis** involves collecting at least three sets of blood cultures prior to the administration of antibiotics, performing transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) in all patients, and obtaining transesophageal echocardiogram (TEE) if TTE is negative (TEE is more sensitive and preferred for evaluating vegetations in patients with prosthetic valves and when there is concern for perivalvular abscess). An ECG should be obtained to evaluate for emerging heart block, along with chest X-ray or CT chest and CT abdomen and pelvis as indicated. The modified Duke criteria are used for diagnosis, which require either any pathologic criteria or two major criteria, one major and three minor criteria, or five minor criteria.

| Criteria Type | Criteria |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Major Criteria** | Positive blood cultures meeting specific parameters; Echocardiographic findings of IE including vegetation, abscess, or dehisced prosthetic valve; A new valvular regurgitation |
| **Minor Criteria** | Fever; Risk factors such as injection drug use or valvular heart disease; Embolic phenomena; Immunologic phenomena; Positive blood cultures not meeting major criteria |

**Treatment** of native valve endocarditis involves empiric vancomycin IV plus ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily, then narrowing antibiotics based on culture results for 4–6 weeks total duration. For prosthetic valve endocarditis, empiric vancomycin IV plus an antipseudomonal cephalosporin such as cefepime plus or minus gentamicin and rifampin is used, with narrowing based on culture results for 6 weeks total duration. Valve replacement may be required in some patients to control infection and prevent severe hemodynamic complications from extensive valvular damage, with general indications including patients with heart failure, fungal endocarditis or endocarditis from MDR organisms, persistent infection despite effective therapy, complicated infections including paravalvular abscess, heart block, or fistula formation, recurrent emboli or enlarging vegetation despite appropriate antibiotics, and large vegetations greater than **1 cm** in size.

## 6. Bloodstream Infections

**Pathophysiology** involves a bloodstream infection arising from an endovascular source of infection such as endocarditis or septic thrombophlebitis, as a complication of another organ infection such as *E. coli* bacteremia due to pyelonephritis, or as a result of direct bloodstream inoculation such as from a central line infection or intravenous drug use.

**Risk factors** include any risk factor for severe infection, the presence of indwelling devices such as central venous catheters or hemodialysis catheters, and certain infections that carry an increased risk for bacteremia such as pyelonephritis, cholangitis, pyogenic liver abscess, severe HAP or VAP, and severe SSTI.

**Pathogens** include gram-positive organisms such as *S. aureus*, *S. pneumoniae* (often as a complication of *S. pneumoniae* pneumonia), viridans streptococci, *Enterococcus* species, and *S. lugdunensis*. Gram-negative organisms include *E. coli*, *K. pneumoniae*, *P. aeruginosa*, and *Enterobacter* species. Fungi, most often *Candida* species, are particularly common in immunocompromised or critically ill patients.

**Clinical features** include rigors suggesting a bloodstream infection, though other clinical features are nonspecific such as fevers and malaise. **Diagnosis** involves blood cultures times two. The presence of a positive blood culture does not always mean there is a clinically significant bloodstream infection.

| Organisms | Interpretation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Always clinically significant**: *S. aureus*, *S. lugdunensis*, *S. pneumoniae*, Group A streptococcus, *P. aeruginosa*, *E. coli*, *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, *Candida* species, *Enterococcus* species, Viridans streptococci | Should always be considered clinically significant |

**Treatment** involves source control including removing central venous catheters if possible, draining any fluid collection or collections if present, and ordering a TTE in all cases of *S. aureus* bacteremia or if there is suspicion for endocarditis. Empiric antibiotic therapy includes vancomycin for gram-positive cocci, ceftriaxone IV 2 g daily for gram-negative rods (or broader coverage if risk factors for *P. aeruginosa* or ESBL infection), and an echinocandin for *Candida* species. Duration of therapy depends on the source of infection and organism isolated.

**Special considerations** for bacteremia with certain organisms include consulting ID for all cases of *S. aureus* bacteremia due to improved mortality with ID involvement, obtaining a TTE for all cases (and TEE if TTE is negative with high clinical suspicion for endocarditis), managing *S. lugdunensis* similarly to *S. aureus* with TTE and ID consultation, and for *Candida* species, consulting ID and ophthalmology to evaluate for endophthalmitis and removing any central venous catheters.

## 7. Skin and Soft Tissue Infections

### Nonpurulent and Purulent Cellulitis

**Pathophysiology** involves infection of the deeper layers of the dermis, which can be nonpurulent or purulent (associated with pustules or a skin abscess). **Pathogens** include gram-positive cocci: betahemolytic streptococci especially group A streptococci for nonpurulent cellulitis, and *S. aureus* including MRSA for purulent cellulitis or abscess.

**Clinical features** include unilateral warmth, tenderness, and erythema, often of a distal extremity. Bilateral involvement is rare. Fever, chills, or other systemic manifestations can be present. An abscess features cellulitis plus a focal collection of pus within the subcutaneous tissue.

**Diagnosis** is clinical. No diagnostic tests are typically needed, though imaging such as CT with contrast may be considered if necrotizing infection or abscess is a concern. Blood cultures should be obtained for notable systemic symptoms, and gram stain and culture of purulent fluid after incision and drainage should be performed.

**Treatment** of nonpurulent cellulitis involves outpatient therapy with cephalexin 500 mg PO QID (preferred) or clindamycin 300 mg PO TID (alternative) for 5 days. If not responding after 72 hours, imaging should be obtained to rule out an underlying abscess and antibiotic coverage should be expanded to include MRSA coverage. Inpatient therapy involves cefazolin 1 g IV q8h, broadening to vancomycin IV if not responding. Duration is typically 5 days. **Treatment** of purulent cellulitis or abscess involves vancomycin, with incision and drainage of any drainable abscess. Clinical trials support the use of antibiotics after I&D, though most patients will be cured with or without antibiotics after I&D.

### Necrotizing Fasciitis

**Necrotizing fasciitis** is a severe, rapidly progressive infection of the fascia and subcutaneous tissue. **Pathogens** include *S. aureus*, Group A strep, anaerobes, and gram-negative rods. **Treatment** involves vancomycin plus ertapenem (1 g IV daily) or piperacillin-tazobactam plus clindamycin. Emergent surgical and ID consultation is essential because this is a surgical emergency.
