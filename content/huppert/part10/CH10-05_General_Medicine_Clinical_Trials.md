# Clinical Trials

This chapter covers key clinical trials and publications relevant to general medicine, including hypertension management, chronic pain management, diet and exercise, advanced care planning, and preventative health.

---

## Hypertension Management

### SPRINT Trial

*N Engl J Med* 2015; 373(22):2103–2116.

**Study Design:** Multicenter, open-label, randomized controlled trial that randomized patients at high risk for cardiovascular disease (CVD) who did not have a history of diabetes or stroke to an intensive (SBP less than 120 mmHg) versus standard (SBP less than 140 mmHg) systolic blood pressure target.

**Key Findings:**
- Intensive BP control (target SBP less than 120 mmHg) improved cardiovascular outcomes and overall survival compared to the standard SBP goal of less than 140 mmHg.
- There was a modest increase in the risk of some serious adverse events with intensive control.
- Similar benefits were demonstrated in a CKD subgroup analysis (*JASN* 2017; 28(9):2561–2563).

## Chronic Pain Management

### Pain Management With Opioids in 2019–2020

*JAMA* 2019; 322(19):1912–1913.

This paper offers guidance on the management of patients with chronic pain who are currently on opioids:

- Develop and use individualized treatment plans.
- Do not abruptly taper opioid treatment.
- Consider opioid agonist therapy (e.g., buprenorphine or naloxone) if there is evidence of opioid use disorder.

## Diet and Exercise

### Association of Step Volume and Intensity With All-Cause Mortality in Older Women

*JAMA Intern Med* 2019; 179(8):1105–1112.

**Study Design:** Cohort study of 16,741 women with a mean age of 72 years, measured steps per day over 7 days.

**Key Findings:**
- Women who averaged 4,400 steps per day had significantly lower mortality rates during follow-up of 4.3 years (HR 0.75) compared with less active women (2,700 steps per day).
- Mortality rates progressively decreased with increasing steps, with a plateau at 7,500 steps per day (HR 0.45).

### EPIC Trial

*JAMA Intern Med* 2019; 179(11):1479–1490.

**Study Design:** Population-based cohort study of 451,743 individuals from 10 countries in Europe who self-reported intake of soft drinks (both diet and regular).

**Key Findings:**
- Higher all-cause mortality was found among participants who consumed 2 or more glasses per day versus fewer than 1 glass per month (HR 1.17; 95% CI, 1.11–1.22; p less than 0.001).
- Both regular and diet soda are associated with mortality, especially cardiovascular disease.

## Advanced Care Planning

### Advanced Directives and Outcomes of Surrogate Decision Making Before Death

*N Engl J Med* 2010; 362(13):1211–1218.

**Study Design:** This study used data from survey proxies in the Health and Retirement Study involving adults 60 years and older who died between 2000 and 2006 to determine the prevalence of the need for decision making and lost decision-making capacity at the end of life.

**Key Findings:**
- Many patients needed decision making near the end of life when most lacked the capacity to do so.
- Patients who had prepared advanced directives received care associated with their preferences, supporting the continued use of advanced directives.

## Preventative Health

### ASPREE Trial

*N Engl J Med* 2018; 379(16):1–10.

**Study Design:** Multicenter, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial that randomized healthy, community-dwelling older adults (age 70 years and older, or 65 years and older in Hispanic or Black patients in the United States) without a history of CVD, cerebrovascular disease, dementia, or any other chronic condition that would likely limit survival to less than 5 years to receive either aspirin 100 mg daily or placebo, and followed prospectively for all-cause death, dementia, or physical disability.

**Key Findings:**
- Over almost 5 years of follow-up, rates of disability-free survival were similar between the aspirin and placebo groups (21.5 versus 21.2 events per 1,000 person-years in the aspirin and placebo groups, respectively).
- Aspirin was associated with higher incidence of major hemorrhage (8.6 versus 6.2 events per 1,000 person-years, respectively).

### COLONPREV Trial

*N Engl J Med* 2018; 379(16):1–10.

**Study Design:** Multicenter, randomized, controlled non-inferiority trial that randomized over 50,000 patients to fecal immunochemical testing (FIT) every 2 years versus one-time colonoscopy.

**Key Findings:**
- In the interim analysis, FIT was non-inferior to baseline colonoscopy in detecting colorectal cancer.
- Ten-year outcomes were expected to be reported in 2021.
