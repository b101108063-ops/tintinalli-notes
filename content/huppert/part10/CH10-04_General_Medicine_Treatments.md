# Treatments

This chapter covers key treatments, interventions, health care maintenance, disease screening, and relevant clinical trials in general medicine.

---

## Health Care Maintenance and Disease Screening

These health care maintenance and disease screening guidelines summarize recommendations from the U.S. Preventative Services Task Force (USPSTF) guidelines, other guidelines as indicated, and general best practices.

**Important notes:**
- Screening tests aim to identify disease in asymptomatic individuals.
- Recommendations are population-based and may need to be tailored to individual patients based on the clinical context.
- The decision to stop routine disease screening should be based on shared decision-making with the patient and should take into account the patient's functional status, life expectancy, goals, and preferences.

### All Patients

#### History

- Diet and weight.
- Exercise.
- Alcohol, tobacco, and substance use.
- Sexual history.
- Depression and anxiety.
- Intimate partner violence.
- **Advance care planning:** Discuss future health care decisions related to the patient's priorities and values, document code status, identify a surrogate medical decision maker, and complete appropriate documentation (may vary by state: advanced directive, durable power of attorney, and physician's orders for life-sustaining treatment [POLST]). Recommended for all adults, and especially important for patients with multiple comorbidities and or advanced age.

#### Screening Tests

- HIV screening for all patients age 15–65 years. Offer preexposure prophylaxis (PrEP) to persons who are at high risk of HIV acquisition.
- Latent tuberculosis infection (LTBI) screening in persons at increased risk.
- Hepatitis B virus (HBV) screening in persons at increased risk.
- Hepatitis C screening in adults age 18–79 years.
- Depression screening.

#### Physical Exam

- Body mass index (BMI) and blood pressure.

#### Vaccinations

- Td or Tdap every 10 years.

### Women 18–39 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about contraception and sexual health.

**Screening Tests:**
- Pap smear every 3 years if age 21–29 years; every 3 years or every 5 years if Pap or HPV co-testing if age 30 years or greater.
- If sexually active, gonorrhea or chlamydia screening annually until age 25 years, and then discretionary. If high-risk sexual behavior, offer STD testing more frequently (e.g., every 3–6 months).
- Rubella serology once.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, HBV series, varicella, and HPV vaccination if not already completed (recommended for individuals age 9–26 years; expanded approval up to age 45 years).

### Women 40–49 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids screening every 5 years starting at age 45 years, or earlier if risk factors.
- HgA1c every 3 years starting at age 45 years, or earlier if risk factors.
- USPSTF recommends biennial screening mammogram starting at age 50 years, but other guidelines recommend starting at age 40 years — can consider with shared decision-making.
- Pap smear every 3 years or every 5 years if Pap or HPV co-testing.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot.

### Women 50–64 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Mammogram every 2 years.
- Pap smear every 3 years or every 5 years if Pap or HPV co-testing.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years).
- Osteoporosis screening if high risk.
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot and shingles vaccine after age 60 years.

### Women 65 Years and Older

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about functional status and falls, safety in current living situation, caregiver support, nutritional status, vision and hearing, memory and cognition, urinary continence, and polypharmacy.

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Mammogram every 2 years; insufficient data to support continued mammographic screening in women 75 years or greater.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years); insufficient data to support continued colon cancer screening in patients 75 years or greater.
- Osteoporosis screening (DEXA).
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, pneumococcal vaccine, and replace one dose of the every-10-year Td booster with Tdap.

### Men 18–39 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about contraception and sexual health.

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipid screening every 5 years starting at age 35 years, or earlier if risk factors.
- Insufficient evidence to assess the benefits or risks of gonorrhea or chlamydia screening in men, but screening can be discussed and offered.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, HBV series, varicella, and HPV vaccination if not already completed (recommended for individuals age 9–26 years; expanded approval up to age 45 years).

### Men 40–49 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years starting at age 45 years, earlier if risk factors.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot.

### Men 50–64 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years).
- Consider discussion about prostate cancer screening with PSA (insufficient evidence per USPSTF, but some societies recommend it — utilize shared decision-making with the patient).
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot and shingles vaccine after age 60 years.

### Men 65 Years and Older

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about functional status and falls, safety in current living situation, caregiver support, nutritional status, vision and hearing, memory and cognition, urinary continence, and polypharmacy.

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years); insufficient data to support continued colon cancer screening in patients 75 years or greater.
- AAA screening in men age 65–75 years who have ever smoked.
- Consider discussion about prostate cancer screening with PSA in men less than 75 years.
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, pneumococcal vaccine, and replace one dose of the every-10-year Td booster with Tdap.

## Screening Recommendations by Condition

### Hypertension

Adults 18 years and older: Screen every 2 years or annually if pre-hypertension.

### Hyperlipidemia

Men 35 years and older, women 45 years and older, or any patient 20 years and older with risk factors for HLD (e.g., CAD, diabetes, and obesity). If total cholesterol less than 200 mg/dL and HDL greater than 35 mg/dL, repeat screening in 5 years.

### Diabetes

Screen all patients 45 years and older; consider earlier screening if risk factors for diabetes are present (e.g., obesity, family history, history of gestational diabetes, PCOS, HTN, and HLD). Testing options include HgA1c, fasting blood glucose, random blood glucose, and oral glucose tolerance test.

### Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)

Perform screening ultrasound for men age 65–75 years who have ever smoked.

### Gonorrhea and Chlamydia

- **Women:** If sexually active, screen annually until age 25 years, then discretionary screening after that based on risk factors.
- **Men:** Insufficient evidence to assess the benefits or risks of screening, but screening can be discussed and offered.

### HIV

Screen all adults age 15–65 years. Offer preexposure prophylaxis (PrEP) to persons who are at high risk of HIV acquisition.

### Hepatitis C

One-time screening for all patients born between 1945 and 1965.

### Breast Cancer

- **Breast self-exam:** USPSTF recommends against self-breast exams (no mortality benefit and more benign breast biopsies).
- **Physical exam:** Insufficient evidence for physician breast exam.
- **Mammography:**
  - Age 40–49 years: American Cancer Society and others recommend offering a mammogram at age 40 years, but USPSTF recommends starting at age 50 years. Consider in women age 40–49 years after shared decision-making with the patient.
  - Age 50–74 years: Screening mammogram every 2 years.
  - Age 75 years and older: Per USPSTF, not recommended (insufficient evidence).

### Cervical Cancer

- **Average risk:**
  - Age 21–29 years: Pap smear every 3 years (regardless of whether sexually active; no HPV testing below age 30 years).
  - Age 30–65 years: Pap smear every 3 years or Pap or HPV co-testing every 5 years.
  - Age greater than 65 years: Can stop testing if repeated negative tests and not at increased risk (more than one sexual partner in the last 5 years, immunosuppression, and other risk factors); also stop if hysterectomy.
- **High risk:** Every 1 year (HIV-positive, immunocompromised, in-utero DES exposure, CIN 2 or 3, and history of cervical cancer).

### Colorectal Cancer

- **Forms of screening:** FIT annually (if any one card is positive, recommend proceeding to colonoscopy), flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, or colonoscopy every 10 years.
- **Average risk:** Begin screening at age 50 years.
- **Moderate risk** (family history of colorectal cancer or adenomatous polyps in a first-degree relative): First colonoscopy at age 40 years or 10 years younger than the youngest case in the family; repeat every 3–5 years.
- **Familial adenomatous polyposis:** Consider genetic testing at age 10 years (if positive, consider colectomy) or colonoscopy every 1–2 years starting at puberty.
- **Hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer:** Consider genetic testing at age 21 years. If positive, recommend colonoscopy every 2 years until age 40 years, then annually.
- **Ulcerative colitis:** Recommend colonoscopy annually after greater than or equal to 8 years of disease.

### Prostate Cancer

Universal PSA screening is not currently recommended, but can be considered after shared decision-making with the patient. No screening if less than 10-year predicted survival (50% of men greater than 75 years have less than 10-year predicted survival).

### Lung Cancer

Annual low-dose CT in patients age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and currently smoke or have quit within the past 15 years.

### Osteoporosis

All women age greater than 65 years, or earlier if risk factors. Screen with DEXA scan. Per USPSTF, insufficient evidence to recommend universal screening in men, but screening can be considered if risk factors for osteoporosis are present. The frequency of repeat DEXA scans depends on the presence of low bone mass and risk factors for accelerated bone loss.

## Vaccinations

### COVID-19

Data forthcoming about recommended vaccination frequency.

### Hepatitis A

Given in two doses 6 months apart. Recommended for patients who travel internationally to certain regions and those with certain risk factors (e.g., HCV, chronic liver disease, and IV drug use).

### Hepatitis B

Given as a primary series to infants (0, 1, and 6 months). Individuals born outside of the United States may not have been vaccinated as children, so consider checking HBV titers.

### HPV

- Recommended for men and women ages 9–26 years.
- Two- or three-dose series depending on the age at initial vaccination.
- Shared decision-making for unvaccinated adults age 27–45 years (consider in high-risk patients).

### Influenza

- Annual flu vaccine recommended.
- Shot equals killed vaccine; nasal spray equals live vaccine.
- **Contraindicated:** Severe egg allergy.

### Meningococcal

Given as a single dose injection age 11–18 years. Patients at particular risk for meningococcal infection include college students and military personnel living in close quarters, asplenic patients, and travelers to endemic areas.

### MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella)

- Primary series in children; live vaccine.
- Check prior to pregnancy and administer to patients who are not rubella immune.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy and immunocompromised patients (OK in HIV if CD4 greater than 200 cells per microliter).

### Pneumococcal

- PCV13 and PPSV23.
- All adults age 65 years and older or those age 19–64 years with certain chronic medical problems (COPD, asthma, cirrhosis, diabetes, history of smoking, high-risk pregnancy, sickle cell disease, and asplenia).
- If a patient is vaccinated at age less than 65 years, revaccinate at age 65 years or 5 years after the first dose (whichever is longer; for example, if vaccinated at age 62 years, revaccinate at age 67 years).
- **PCV13:** Prevents invasive disease and pneumonia. Ideally administer before PPSV23 if possible. If the patient received PPSV23 first, wait at least 8 weeks before administering PCV13.
- **PPSV23:** Prevents invasive disease but not pneumonia. Administer 1 year after PCV13.

### Polio

Primary series in children. Not routinely given to unvaccinated adults unless traveling to an endemic area.

### Tetanus and Diphtheria

- Tdap administered as a primary series to infants (at 1, 2, and 6 months).
- Td booster every 10 years.
- In adults greater than 65 years, replace one dose of the every-10-year Td booster with Tdap.
- Revaccinate all pregnant women between 27–36 weeks gestation every pregnancy.

### Varicella

- Live vaccine given as a primary series in children and adults without a history of chickenpox.
- Two doses; administer the second dose 4–8 weeks after the first dose.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy and immunocompromised patients.

### Zoster (Shingles)

- Live vaccine given to adults 60 years and older.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy and immunocompromised patients.

### Live Vaccines

- Smallpox, yellow fever, chickenpox, shingles, Sabin's polio, MMR, intranasal influenza, BCG, and anthrax.
