# Introduction

This chapter focuses on general outpatient medicine. A whole textbook could be dedicated to this subject alone, but this chapter concentrates on key symptoms and chief complaints encountered in the outpatient setting, as well as health care maintenance pearls.

Anatomy, physiology, and diagnostic concepts are covered in the organ system-based chapters and are embedded throughout the topics in this chapter.

---

## Sore Throat

### Viral Pharyngitis

- **Epidemiology:** More common than bacterial pharyngitis.
- **Symptoms:** Sore throat. Patients with viral pharyngitis are typically not as ill as patients with bacterial pharyngitis.
- **Physical Exam:** May have tonsillar erythema but usually lacks exudate; inspect the oropharynx (tonsillar inflammation, exudate, soft palate edema, swollen or deviated uvula, oral ulcers) and evaluate for lymphadenopathy.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis after ruling out bacterial pharyngitis.
- **Treatment:** Supportive care; no antibiotics indicated.

### Streptococcal Pharyngitis

- **Organism:** Group A Streptococcus.
- **Symptoms:** Fever, odynophagia (painful swallowing).
- **Physical Exam:** Tonsillar erythema, edema, and exudate; lymphadenopathy.
- **Diagnosis:** Centor criteria — 1 point each for fever, tonsillar exudate, tender cervical lymphadenopathy, and lack of cough.

| Centor Score | Interpretation | Management |
|---|---|---|
| 0–2 | Low probability for strep pharyngitis | Do not send for rapid strep test |
| 3–4 | High probability | Perform rapid strep antigen test and treat; strep culture is unnecessary per IDSA guidelines |

- **Treatment:** Penicillin TID/QID for 10 days.

### Mononucleosis (EBV)

- **Symptoms:** Fever, extreme fatigue.
- **Physical Exam:** Fever, diffuse lymphadenopathy (symmetric; posterior cervical/auricular greater than anterior), hepatosplenomegaly, pharyngitis, and may have palatal petechiae.
- **Diagnosis:** CBC with differential (lymphocytosis, atypical lymphocytes); heterophile antibody test (though sensitivity and specificity are low); serology; peripheral blood smear (large atypical lymphocytes).
- **Treatment:** No antibiotics; supportive care.
- **Complication:** Splenic rupture — patients should refrain from contact sports for at least the first 3 weeks of illness.

### Peritonsillar Abscess

- **Organisms:** Group A Streptococcus, Streptococcus anginosus, *Staphylococcus aureus*, and anaerobes.
- **Symptoms:** Severe, unilateral throat pain; muffled voice; difficulty opening the mouth; drooling.
- **Physical Exam:** Erythema and edema of the affected tonsil and soft palate; trismus.
- **Diagnosis:** CT or MRI if concerned for deep neck space infection.
- **Treatment:** Drainage of abscess; IV antibiotics (ampicillin-sulbactam, with or without vancomycin).
- **Complications:** Airway obstruction, aspiration, and extension into deep neck tissues.

## Sinusitis

### Acute Viral Rhinosinusitis

- **Epidemiology:** Most common cause of sinusitis.
- **Symptoms:** Nasal congestion, sore throat. Patients are generally less ill than those with bacterial infection.
- **Physical Exam:** Palpate the sinuses (frontal, ethmoid, maxillary); inspect the oropharynx and nasal cavities; perform a cranial nerve exam.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis — no specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:** Supportive care; no antibiotics indicated.

### Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis

- **Organisms:** *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Haemophilus influenzae*.
- **Symptoms:** Facial pain, pressure, or fullness; purulent nasal drainage; nasal congestion; fever.
- **Physical Exam:** Pain with palpation of the facial sinus; nasal turbinate edema; purulent drainage in the nasal cavity or posterior pharynx.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis based on one of the following: persistent symptoms greater than 10 days, especially if severe or worsening symptoms greater than 3 days; OR initial improvement followed by worsening of symptoms. No specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:** Consider observation for immunocompetent patients with good follow-up; otherwise amoxicillin-clavulanate BID for 5–7 days (typically not amoxicillin alone due to increasing resistance of *S. pneumoniae* and *H. influenzae*).
- **Complications:**
  - Chronic rhinosinusitis: symptoms lasting greater than 12 weeks. Obtain CT scan and refer to ENT.
  - Orbital cellulitis: pain with extraocular movement, diplopia, ophthalmoplegia. Requires urgent management.
  - Cavernous sinus thrombosis: new headache, focal neurologic findings. Requires urgent imaging and intervention.

## Rhinitis

### Allergic Rhinitis

- **Symptoms:** Watery eyes, rhinorrhea, sneezing, congestion, nasal itching, often with an identifiable trigger (e.g., pollen).
- **Physical Exam:** Nasal mucosa may be normal, pale blue, or with pallor. Inspect the nasal cavities and oropharynx.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis — no specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:**
  - Topical intranasal glucocorticoid (e.g., fluticasone) used daily.
  - Warn patients about the risk of dry nose, which can cause nosebleeds.
  - Add a second-generation over-the-counter antihistamine or decongestant if a second-line agent is needed.
  - Recommend avoidance of identifiable triggers if feasible.
  - Add over-the-counter eye drops for allergic conjunctivitis.
  - Consider referral to allergy/immunology if no improvement with medical management.

### Vasomotor Rhinitis (Nonallergic Rhinitis)

- **Symptoms:** Nasal congestion and postnasal drainage. Symptoms occur throughout the year without a clear trigger.
- **Physical Exam:** Nasal mucosa may be normal or boggy.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis — no specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:** Topical intranasal antihistamine and glucocorticoids.
- **Note:** Nasal tumors should also be considered in the differential.

## Vertigo

### Approach to Vertigo

- **Physical Exam:** Perform a cranial nerve exam and a HINTS exam (Head-Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew); otoscopic exam and assessment for hearing loss; evaluate gait; perform the Dix-Hallpike maneuver.

### Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

- **Symptoms:** Dizziness triggered by changing head position; usually intermittent and brief episodes.
- **Physical Exam:** Positive Dix-Hallpike maneuver.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis — no specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:** Epley maneuver.

### Meniere's Disease

- **Symptoms:** Ear fullness, tinnitus, and unilateral hearing loss. Most patients cycle between periods with active symptoms followed by prolonged remissions.
- **Pathophysiology:** Excess fluid buildup in the endolymphatic spaces of the inner ear; the exact mechanism is not entirely clear.
- **Physical Exam:** Nystagmus during the acute attack.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis — no specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:**
  - Avoid substances that increase endolymphatic retention (e.g., salt, alcohol, caffeine, nicotine).
  - Consider a trial of a thiazide diuretic.
  - Provide symptomatic relief with meclizine or a scopolamine patch (avoid in the elderly given anticholinergic side effects).
- **Complication:** Refractory symptoms may require ENT referral to consider surgical interventions.

### Other Causes of Vertigo

- **Peripheral:** Acute vestibular neuritis, otosclerosis.
- **Central:** Vestibular migraine, posterior circulation TIA or stroke (isolated vertigo from TIA or stroke is very rare), medication-induced.

## Hearing Loss

### Approach to Hearing Loss

- **Physical Exam:** Otoscopic exam, Weber test (tuning fork handle pressed to the center of the forehead to compare lateralization), and Rinne test (comparing bone conduction via mastoid to air conduction near the ear to compare bone and air conduction).

### Conductive Hearing Loss

- **Symptoms:** Decreased perception of sound, especially at low frequencies; patients can often still hear loud noises well.
- **Etiologies:** Lesion in the external or middle ear.
  - **External canal:** Cerumen impaction, otitis externa, exostoses (bony outgrowths often due to cold water exposure).
  - **Tympanic membrane:** Perforation secondary to trauma or infection, clot in meatus, or tear.
  - **Middle ear:** Effusion (otitis media, allergic rhinitis), otosclerosis (bony immobilization of stapes), or neoplasm.
- **Physical Exam:** CARWA — conductive hearing loss presents with an abnormal Rinne test (bone conduction better than air) and Weber test with sound lateralizing to the affected side (louder in the bad ear).
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis — no specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:** Treat underlying cause; hearing aids; soften and irrigate cerumen; tympanoplasty if needed; stapedectomy for otosclerosis.

### Sensorineural Hearing Loss

- **Symptoms:** Hearing loss; may have more difficulty with high-frequency sounds.
- **Etiologies:** Lesion in the cochlea or CN VIII (auditory branch).
  - **Presbycusis (most common):** Gradual, symmetric hearing loss (especially at high frequencies) associated with aging, due to degeneration of sensory cells at the base of the cochlea.
  - **Noise-induced:** Chronic loud noise damages hair cells in the organ of Corti.
  - **Inflammatory/infectious:** Infection (e.g., viral cochleitis), congenital infections, or autoimmune hearing loss.
  - **CNS:** Acoustic neuromas (especially if marked hearing difference between ears), meningitis, multiple sclerosis, or syphilis.
  - **Drug-induced:** Aminoglycoside antibiotics, furosemide, ethacrynic acid, cisplatin, quinidine, or aspirin (tinnitus).
- **Physical Exam:** Normal Rinne test (air conduction better than bone); Weber test with sound lateralizing to the unaffected side (louder in the good ear).
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis — no specific labs or imaging required.
- **Treatment:** Treat underlying cause; hearing aids; cochlear implants.

## Vision Loss

### Approach to Vision Loss

- **Physical Exam:** Fundoscopic exam, pupillary exam, test visual acuity, extraocular motions, and peripheral vision.

### Anatomy of the Eye

The eye has three aspects: the anterior segment, the posterior segment, and the surrounding tissues.

- **Anterior segment** includes the conjunctiva, sclera, cornea, iris, ciliary body, and crystalline lens.
- **Posterior segment** includes the vitreous humor, retina, choroid, and optic nerve.
- **Other important structures** include the eyelids, extraocular muscles, and lacrimal drainage system.

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (ARMD)

#### Nonexudative ("Dry") ARMD — More Common

- **Pathophysiology:** Poorly understood; potentially primary senescence of the retinal epithelium.
- **Symptoms:** Gradual loss of unilateral or bilateral vision; often causes difficulty reading or driving.
- **Physical Exam:** Slit-lamp exam with yellow or white deposits (called "drusen") that form under the retinal pigment epithelium.
- **Treatment:** Smoking cessation; supplementation with zinc and antioxidant vitamins.

#### Exudative ("Wet") ARMD

- **Pathophysiology:** Choroidal neovascularization, likely due to isoforms of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF).
- **Symptoms:** Sudden vision loss due to neovascularization, which can be rapidly progressive. One of the earliest signs is when straight lines appear wavy (metamorphopsia).
- **Physical Exam:** Slit-lamp exam with subretinal leakage of serous fluid or blood.
- **Treatment:** Anti-VEGF intraocular injections (e.g., ranibizumab); supplementation with zinc and antioxidant vitamins.

### Glaucoma

#### Open-Angle Glaucoma — More Common

- **Description:** Progressive neuropathy of the optic nerve, due to poorly understood causes, but typically associated with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP).
- **Symptoms:** Painless, progressive peripheral vision loss; often initially undetected by the patient.
- **Physical Exam:** Fundus exam with evidence of nerve damage ("cupping" equals enlargement of the optic disc).
- **Diagnosis:** Requires evidence of optic disc abnormalities, adult onset, open anterior chamber angles, and absence of another known cause for glaucoma. Tonometry with increased IOP can be suggestive but is not always present.
- **Treatment:**
  - **Medical:** Increase aqueous outflow (prostaglandin analogues, alpha-agonists); decrease aqueous production (topical beta-blocker such as timolol, alpha-agonist, carbonic anhydrase inhibitor).
  - **Interventional:** Laser therapy or surgery (trabeculectomy).

#### Closed-Angle Glaucoma

- **Description:** Narrowing or closure of the anterior chamber angle, which normally allows drainage of aqueous humor, causing elevation in IOP and damage to the optic nerve.
- **Symptoms:** Painful red eye, sudden blurred vision with "halos," associated nausea and vomiting. Can be precipitated by dilation with atropine if a shallow anterior chamber was not recognized.
- **Physical Exam:** Mid-dilated, minimally reactive pupil; conjunctival redness; corneal edema or cloudiness; shallow anterior chamber.
- **Approach:** Patients with symptoms and signs of acute closed-angle glaucoma require emergent evaluation by an ophthalmologist, including evaluation of acuity, IOP, slit-lamp examination, and gonioscopy.
- **Diagnosis:** Gonioscopy to measure the anterior chamber angle is the gold standard; requires a slit lamp with a special lens to visualize the angle.
- **Treatment:** Requires emergent surgical intervention. Prior to surgery, attempt to decrease IOP with eye drops (0.5% timolol, 1% apraclonidine, 2% pilocarpine); IV acetazolamide or IV mannitol may be used with specialist assistance. Avoid atropine.

### Cataracts

- **Pathophysiology:** The lens of the eye is transparent due to highly organized cytoplasmic proteins called crystallins, but cannot shed nonviable cells, which occur with age or due to secondary causes (inflammation, trauma) and become cataracts.
- **Symptoms:** Slowly progressing loss of visual acuity, blurry vision with glare (e.g., difficulty with night driving due to oncoming headlights). Patients become progressively more nearsighted and may no longer require reading glasses ("myopic shift" or "second sight").
- **Physical Exam:** Clouding of lens.
- **Treatment:** Surgery if the cataract interferes with patient functioning.

### Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)

- **Pathophysiology:** Thrombus in the central retinal vein results in venous obstruction, causing retinal capillary nonperfusion and ischemia.
- **Symptoms:** Subacute painless unilateral vision loss of variable time course (hours to weeks); sometimes preceded by positive visual phenomena (e.g., scintillations).
- **Physical Exam:** Optic disc swelling; may also have venous dilation or tortuosity with intraretinal hemorrhage ("blood and thunder" fundus).
- **Treatment:** Anti-VEGF intraocular injections; laser surgery.

### Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)

- **Pathophysiology:** Occlusion of the central retinal artery (considered a form of stroke), typically due to carotid artery atherosclerosis or cardiogenic embolism, and rarely due to other vascular, hematologic, or inflammatory causes.
- **Symptoms:** Sudden, painless, unilateral vision loss.
- **Physical Exam:** Pallor of the optic disc, cherry-red fovea, and boxcar segmentation.
- **Treatment:** Requires emergent ophthalmology referral for consideration of advanced therapy such as revascularization. Temporizing measures include instructing the patient to breathe into a paper bag and hold their eye, ocular massage, high-flow O2, and medical therapy for reduction of IOP.

### Retinal Detachment

- **Pathophysiology:** Separation of the retina from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium and choroid; vision loss occurs if separation progresses to include the central retina.
- **Symptoms:** Painless, unilateral vision loss often associated with floaters, black spots, or flashes; symptoms progress at a variable pace.
- **Physical Exam:** Elevated retina with folds or tear; abnormal red reflex.
- **Treatment:** Requires emergent ophthalmology referral and surgical spot welding. If vision is still intact, it is even more emergent to try to save the patient's vision.

### Transient Monocular Visual Loss (Amaurosis Fugax)

- **Etiologies:** Carotid pathology, cardioembolic events, giant cell arteritis, vasospasm, or retinal migraine.
- **Symptoms:** Sudden transient unilateral vision loss; vision returns when reperfused.
- **Physical Exam:** May have a normal exam.
- **Diagnosis:** Carotid ultrasound; lipids; consider TIA or stroke workup or evaluation for GCA based on patient risk factors.
- **Treatment:** Attempt to identify the etiology and reduce risk factors.

### Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (AAION)

- **Pathophysiology:** Typically, occlusion of the posterior ciliary artery (branch of the ophthalmic artery from the internal carotid, which supplies the optic nerve). Can be associated with giant cell arteritis.
- **Symptoms:** Sudden vision loss, jaw claudication, and scalp tenderness.
- **Physical Exam:** Pallor and edema of the optic disc.
- **Diagnosis:** Temporal artery biopsy; elevated ESR and CRP.
- **Treatment:** High-dose steroids as soon as possible — do not wait for biopsy results.

## Acute Red Eye

### Approach to Acute Red Eye

- **Physical Exam:** Fundoscopic exam, pupillary examination, visual acuity, extraocular motions, and peripheral vision.

### Closed-Angle Glaucoma

- See the closed-angle glaucoma section under Vision Loss above.

### Conjunctivitis

#### Viral Conjunctivitis

- **Etiology:** Adenovirus.
- **Symptoms:** Often recent URI; may have itching.
- **Physical Exam:** Watery discharge; may have lymphadenopathy.
- **Treatment:** Hot compresses; self-limited.

#### Bacterial Conjunctivitis

- **Organisms:** *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Pseudomonas*.
- **Physical Exam:** Mucopurulent discharge.
- **Treatment:** Erythromycin eye drops; if contacts are worn, use a fluoroquinolone for *Pseudomonas* coverage.

#### Chlamydial Conjunctivitis

- **Symptoms:** Often associated with nasal discharge. Assess sexual history and genitourinary symptoms.
- **Etiologies:** Trachoma (serotypes A, B, C) — most common cause of blindness worldwide; Inclusion conjunctivitis (serotypes D–K) — genital hand-eye contact of an STD.
- **Physical Exam:** Neovascularization (pannus) of the cornea.
- **Diagnosis:** Giemsa stain.
- **Treatment:** Oral tetracycline, doxycycline, or erythromycin. Treat the sexual partner.

#### Allergic Conjunctivitis

- **Symptoms:** Itching, tearing, nasal congestion, usually bilateral.
- **Physical Exam:** May have eyelid edema.
- **Treatment:** Remove allergen; topical or systemic antihistamine.

#### Irritant Conjunctivitis

- **Etiologies:** Contact lens, chemicals, foreign bodies, or dryness.
- **Treatment:** Irrigation; avoid irritants.

### Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

- **Description:** Bleeding underneath the conjunctiva. May be associated with Valsalva maneuver, severe cough, or direct trauma. Other risk factors include coagulopathy, anticoagulant use, and hypertension.
- **Symptoms:** Usually unilateral bleeding underneath the conjunctiva without vision changes.
- **Physical Exam:** Redness in the conjunctiva; may be well-demarcated.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical diagnosis. Check INR if on warfarin.
- **Treatment:** Self-limited; may use artificial tears.

### Hyphema

- **Description:** Pooling or collection of blood inside the anterior chamber of the eye, usually associated with direct trauma or recent eye surgery.
- **Symptoms:** May have blurred vision or vision loss.
- **Physical Exam:** Layering of blood in the anterior chamber of the eye.
- **Diagnosis:** Check intraocular pressure (may be elevated).
- **Treatment:** Same-day ophthalmology evaluation. Can be medically managed with corticosteroids, beta blockers, or alpha agonists. If failure to improve with medical management, may require surgery.

### Keratoconjunctivitis (Dry Eye)

- **Description:** Inflammation of the cornea and conjunctiva.
- **Symptoms:** Sensation of "sand in the eyes," bilateral; may have frequent blinking.
- **Etiologies:** Medications (anticholinergics, antihistamines), autoimmune conditions (Sjögren's syndrome), CN V or VII lesions, or allergies.
- **Diagnosis:** Consider testing for anti-Ro and anti-La autoantibodies if risk factors for Sjögren's syndrome are present.
- **Treatment:** Treat the etiology; symptomatic relief with artificial tears versus gel; avoidance of anticholinergic and antihistamine medications.

### Blepharitis

- **Description:** Inflammation of the eyelids.
- **Symptoms:** Crusting, burning, or itching sensation. Patient may have a history of seborrhea or eczema.
- **Physical Exam:** Inflammation of the eyelids; crusting at the base of the eyelashes.
- **Treatment:** Lid scrubs (consider non-fragranced baby shampoo), warm compresses, and antibiotics if severe (can be associated with *S. aureus* superinfection).

### Scleritis

- **Description:** Inflammation of the tissue between the sclera and the conjunctiva.
- **Symptoms:** Pain and photophobia; may be episodic. Patient may have a history of autoimmune conditions (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, psoriatic arthritis).
- **Physical Exam:** Pain on gentle palpation of the globe.
- **Treatment:** Referral to ophthalmology.

### Acute Anterior Uveitis (Iritis, Iridocyclitis)

- **Description:** Inflammation of the middle layer of the eye (the uvea), which includes the iris and ciliary body. Can be associated with systemic inflammatory conditions (e.g., sarcoidosis, ankylosing spondylitis, IBD, or reactive arthritis).
- **Symptoms:** Blurred vision, usually unilateral; severe photophobia.
- **Physical Exam:** Inflammation of the iris and ciliary body; constricted pupil of the affected eye.
- **Diagnosis:** Consider workup for associated inflammatory conditions depending on risk factors.
- **Treatment:** Urgent referral to ophthalmology; topical steroids and dilating drops.

### Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) Keratitis

- **Symptoms:** Painful vision changes (rapidly progressing bilateral necrotizing retinitis can cause blindness).
- **Physical Exam:** Dendritic ulcer on cornea; peripheral pale lesion.
- **Treatment:** Trifluoridine eye drops or ganciclovir gel.

### Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV) Keratitis

- **Symptoms:** Unilateral painful vesicular rash, eye pain, and decreased vision.
- **Physical Exam:** Corneal ulcers; vesicular rash in trigeminal distribution; Hutchinson's sign (lesions on the nose).
- **Treatment:** Oral acyclovir or valacyclovir (most effective within 72 hours of vesicle outbreak); topical steroids.

### Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Retinitis

- **Symptoms:** Painless floaters or flashing lights. Patient may have a history of HIV/AIDS or immunosuppression.
- **Physical Exam:** Hemorrhages and fluffy granular lesions.
- **Treatment:** Oral valganciclovir; ensure ART compliance if HIV positive.

## Back Pain

### Approach to Back Pain

- **Physical Exam:** Palpate the spine for point tenderness; perform a neuro exam (assess strength, reflexes, sensation); straight leg raise test; gait evaluation; consider assessing rectal tone.

### Differential Diagnosis of Back Pain

#### Musculoskeletal

- **Lumbar Strain/Sprain:** Diffuse pain that worsens with movement and improves with rest. May have an inciting event. Physical exam may reveal tenderness to touch over the involved muscle or region.
- **Fracture:** Sudden onset of pain. May have a history of trauma or falls, or risk factors for atraumatic fractures (e.g., osteoporosis, osteosclerosis, neoplastic infiltration, or osteomyelitis). Physical exam may reveal point tenderness and worsened pain with flexion and limited range of motion.
- **Degenerative:** Typically a chronic pain course. Physical exam may reveal osteoarthritis in other joints.
- **Disc Herniation:** May worsen with coughing or straining. Physical exam shows a positive straight leg raise test and worsened pain with flexion.
- **Spinal Stenosis:** Claudication (buttock or leg pain with walking); may have numbness. Physical exam may show decreased pain with leaning forward.

#### Systemic

- **Malignancy:** Constant, dull pain unrelieved by rest. Pain may be worse at night. May be associated with systemic symptoms including weight loss and generalized fatigue. Etiologies include metastatic, hematologic, or primary bone tumors. Physical exam may reveal point tenderness.
- **Infection:** Back pain sometimes associated with systemic symptoms including fever and chills. Patient may have risk factors including IV drug use, bacteremia, endocarditis, TB (Pott disease), or recent instrumentation. Etiologies include vertebral osteomyelitis, epidural abscess, or discitis.
- **Inflammatory:** Gradual onset back pain that may improve with movement or throughout the day. Etiologies include rheumatoid arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis, IBD, and reactive arthritis.

#### Neurologic

- **Radicular Pain (Sciatica):** Pain radiating from the back down the posterior leg due to nerve root compression; may have numbness. Etiologies include disk herniation, spinal stenosis, neoplasm, and infection. Physical exam shows a positive straight leg raise test and may have sensory or motor deficits.
- **Cauda Equina Syndrome:** Bowel or bladder incontinence, urinary retention, and lower extremity weakness. Physical exam reveals loss of anal sphincter tone, saddle anesthesia, and weakness. Treatment: emergent surgical evaluation.

#### Other

- **Congenital:** Spondylosis, kyphoscoliosis, spina bifida occulta, tethered spinal cord.
- **Extraspinal:** Pancreatitis, nephrolithiasis, pyelonephritis, prostatitis, psoas abscess, retrocecal appendicitis, fibroids, aortic dissection, postural factors, psychiatric factors, or referred pain from the hip or SI joint.

### Diagnosis of Back Pain

Consider spinal MRI if any of the following red-flag features are present:

- **Patient History:** History of malignancy, intravenous drug use, recent trauma, osteoporosis, prolonged steroid use, or immunosuppression.
- **Clinical Features:** Unexplained weight loss, fever, bowel or bladder incontinence, urinary retention, or lower extremity weakness or numbness.
- **Physical Exam:** Neurologic deficits, saddle anesthesia, loss of anal sphincter tone, or lower extremity numbness or weakness.

### Treatment of Back Pain

Generally symptomatic management including:

- **Nonpharmacologic:** Patient education, encouraging continued physical activity, and physical therapy referral.
- **Pharmacologic:** Topical agents, acetaminophen or NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, and gabapentin for neuropathic pain.

## Vulvovaginitis

### Approach to Vulvovaginitis

- **Physical Exam:** Pelvic exam.

### Bacterial Vaginitis (BV)

- **Etiology:** Vaginal inflammation caused by the overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria naturally found in the vagina.
- **Symptoms:** Malodorous vaginal discharge; may be asymptomatic.
- **Physical Exam:** Fishy odor, grey discharge, no vaginal or vulvar inflammation.
- **Diagnosis:** Positive whiff test; clue cells on microscopy; pH greater than 4.5.
- **Treatment:** Oral metronidazole.

### Vulvovaginal Candidiasis

- **Etiology:** Vaginal and vulval symptoms caused by yeast, most often *Candida albicans*.
- **Symptoms:** Thick "cottage cheese" discharge and pruritus. Can occur in an atrophic or dry form.
- **Physical Exam:** Vulvar inflammation, thick white discharge, and may have vaginal dryness.
- **Diagnosis:** Pseudohyphae or hyphae on microscopy; pH 4 to 4.5.
- **Treatment:** Oral fluconazole or vaginal clotrimazole.

### Trichomonas

- **Etiology:** Sexually transmitted infection caused by the parasite *Trichomonas vaginalis*.
- **Symptoms:** Frothy yellow or green discharge.
- **Physical Exam:** Petechiae ("strawberry patches") in the vagina.
- **Diagnosis:** Organisms present on microscopy; pH 5 to 6.
- **Treatment:** Oral metronidazole for both the patient and their partner.

### General Management of Vulvovaginitis

In addition to condition-specific treatment, provide counseling on safe sex practices, offer additional STI testing, discuss contraception options, and screen for intimate partner violence.

## Urinary Incontinence

### Approach to Urinary Incontinence

- **Physical Exam:** Abdominal exam, rectal exam, pelvic exam in females; consider postvoid residual.

### Urge Incontinence

- **Symptoms:** Sudden urge to urinate such that the patient may not make it to the bathroom; nocturnal bedwetting. More common in elderly patients.
- **Diagnosis:** Urodynamics (incontinence is due to an overactive detrusor muscle).
- **Treatment:** Bladder training; anticholinergic (oxybutynin); TCA (imipramine).

### Stress Incontinence

- **Symptoms:** Spurts of urine loss during activities that increase intra-abdominal pressure (coughing, laughing, sneezing, exercising). More common in multiparous women.
- **Diagnosis:** Positive cough test; weakness of the pelvic floor or sphincter leads to loss of bladder support.
- **Treatment:** Pelvic muscle (Kegel) exercises; pessary placement; surgery (urethropexy).

### Overflow Incontinence

- **Symptoms:** Bladder does not empty completely; overdistension may lead to dribbling of urine. Patient may have a history of spinal cord injury or bladder outlet obstruction.
- **Diagnosis:** Elevated post-void residual (PVR) due to obstruction.
- **Treatment:** Bethanechol; alpha-blockers (tamsulosin); surgical intervention to relieve obstruction.

### Additional Evaluation of Urinary Incontinence

In addition to the above tests, exclude causes of secondary urinary incontinence:

- **Infection:** UA to assess for urinary tract infection.
- **Constipation:** Rectal exam to assess for stool impaction.
- **Functional Status:** Low mobility may be contributing to the physical ability to reach the toilet.
- **Mental Status:** Delirium or dementia may be contributing to cognitive ability to reach the toilet.
- **Lifestyle:** Assess for alcohol use and uncontrolled diabetes.
- **Medications:** Diuretics, sedating medications, and opioids.

## Contraception

### Approach to Contraception Counseling

Recommend discussion of contraception with all patients of reproductive age. Counsel patients on the continued use of condoms for protection from STIs, screen for contraindications to certain forms of contraception (e.g., cannot use OCPs if history of DVT or PE), and educate patients about possible side effects. Consider using conversations about the initiation or continuation of contraception as an opportunity to also offer STI screening and assess for intimate partner violence.

### Hormonal Birth Control

| Method | Details |
|---|---|
| Combination oral contraceptive pills (OCPs) | Progesterone (suppresses LH, preventing ovulation) plus estrogen (regularizes cycle, prevents breakthrough bleeding); requires daily pill compliance |
| Progestin only ("minipill") | Preferred in women who are breastfeeding, greater than 40 years of age, and those with a history of migraines with aura. Must be very consistent with pill compliance |
| Transdermal patch | Estrogen plus progestin patch; apply for 3 weeks, remove for 1 week |
| Vaginal ring | Estrogen plus progestin vaginal ring; patient inserts it and leaves it in place for 3 weeks, then removes it for 1 week |
| Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA) | Injectable progesterone administered in clinic every 3 months; safe to use while breastfeeding; long-term risk of osteopenia |
| Subcutaneous implant (Implanon) | Implanted by a clinician under the skin; effective for up to 3 years; may cause irregular bleeding |

**Contraindications:** Cigarette smoking; history of embolism; heart failure; cerebral vascular disease; migraines with aura; known pregnancy at time of initiation; caution if age greater than 35 years.

**Side effects:** VTE; stroke or cardiac event; elevated triglycerides. Decreased risk of ovarian or endometrial cancer and benign breast disease.

### Barrier Methods

| Method | Details |
|---|---|
| Condom | Provides protection from STIs; estimated 5–8% failure rate |
| Diaphragm | Inserted by patient; placed 6 hours before intercourse and should be left in place for 6 hours afterward; may increase risk of UTI |
| Other | Cervical cap, sponge, spermicide |

### Intrauterine Device (IUD)

| Type | Details |
|---|---|
| Mirena (levonorgestrel) | Inserted every 5 years; high rates of amenorrhea |
| Paraguard (copper) | Inserted every 10 years; non-hormonal; preferred if history of breast cancer; may cause heavier menses |

**Side effects:** Risk for ectopic pregnancy if the patient becomes pregnant with an IUD in place.

**Contraindications:** Anatomic abnormalities that prevent placement.

### Emergency Contraception

- **Example:** Plan B (levonorgestrel) — effective for up to 120 hours after intercourse to reduce the risk of pregnancy.
- **Side effects:** Nausea and vomiting.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy or undiagnosed abnormal vaginal bleeding.

### Sterilization

| Patient | Method |
|---|---|
| Men | Vasectomy (takes 8–10 weeks to be effective; may be reversible) |
| Women | Tubal ligation (effective immediately; non-reversible) |

## Menopause

### Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis made after 12 consecutive months of amenorrhea. Median age is 51 years. No labs or imaging are required for diagnosis. If younger age with menopausal symptoms, consider checking a pregnancy test and FSH level.

### Vasomotor Symptoms

- **Symptoms:** Hot flashes or flushing; night sweats.
- **Diagnosis:** Consider other etiologies of vasomotor symptoms (e.g., thyroid disorders, medications, alcohol consumption, or panic attacks).
- **Treatment:** Behavior modification; oral estrogen therapy (hormone replacement therapy) can be considered after appropriate patient counseling (typically recommend less than 5 years of total use; side effects include increased risk of MI, stroke, DVT or PE, and breast cancer).

### Vaginal Symptoms

- **Symptoms:** Vaginal dryness or itching; decreased libido; dyspareunia (pain with intercourse).
- **Physical Exam:** Vaginal atrophy, pallor, and dryness.
- **Treatment:** Vaginal estrogen and lubricants.

### Psychiatric Symptoms

- **Symptoms:** Mood changes, sleep disturbances, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, anxiety, and depression.
- **Treatment:** Oral estrogen therapy; SSRI.

## Chronic Noncancer Pain

### Clinical Assessment

- **Assess pain:** Use the OPQRST framework — Onset, Provocation or Palliation, Quality, Region or Radiation, Severity, and Timing.
- **Review history:** Past medical history, social history, and psychological history (depression, anxiety, stress, and ongoing substance abuse can exacerbate chronic pain).
- **Functional impairment:** Assess functional impairment.

### Treatment: Multimodal Approach

#### Nonpharmacologic

- Patient education and reassurance.
- Physical therapy (stretches and exercise).
- Relaxation techniques (meditation and mindfulness practices).
- Sleep hygiene.
- Psychotherapy (cognitive behavioral therapy).
- Acupuncture, massage, heat or cold packs.

#### Pharmacologic

| Agent | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Topical agents** | Good for localized pain; low side effect profile; may be cost-prohibitive for some patients. Examples: OTC analgesic balm, lidocaine patches or gel (best evidence in postherpetic neuralgia), diclofenac gel (not systemically absorbed; may be used in ESRD), capsaicin (avoid mucosal surfaces) |
| **Acetaminophen** | Most patients can take up to 4 grams per day; in liver disease, typically recommend only up to 2 grams per day |
| **NSAIDs** | Caution if history of gastritis or AKI or ESRD. Examples: ibuprofen, indomethacin, naproxen |
| **Gabapentin or pregabalin** | Useful in neuropathic pain. Side effects: sedation and dizziness. Renally cleared — adjust dose if renal impairment |
| **TCAs** | Useful in neuropathic pain; okay to use with or without coexisting depression. Examples: nortriptyline, amitriptyline. Side effects: sedation (highest with amitriptyline) and anticholinergic side effects |
| **SNRIs** | Useful in neuropathic pain; okay to use with or without coexisting depression. Examples: venlafaxine, duloxetine. Side effects: nausea and insomnia; avoid duloxetine if hepatic or renal impairment |
| **Muscle relaxants** | May be useful if muscular contractions or spasms (although limited evidence). Typically only prescribe a short course. Examples: baclofen, carisoprodol. Side effects: sedation, low muscle tone or weakness, risk of physical dependency and seizures with carisoprodol |
| **Tramadol** | Opioid agonist; lower risk of dependence than opioids. Prescribe only a short course (maximum 2 weeks). Caution if using other serotonergic agents given the risk of serotonin syndrome |
| **Opioids** | Generally not recommended for chronic noncancer pain; may be appropriate for acute pain episodes. Examples: oxycodone, hydrocodone, codeine. Side effects: sedation, nausea or vomiting, constipation, tolerance, and physical dependence |

### Monitoring

- Recommend frequent visits to monitor progress with pain relief.
- Use the PEG scale (Pain, Enjoyment of life, and General Activity) during visits.
- Set realistic expectations and goals.
- Consider early referral to a pain specialist for procedural interventions if indicated (e.g., intraarticular injections or nerve blocks).

## Edema

### Definition

Clinically evident excess of interstitial fluid; recognized as the persistence of an indentation of the skin after pressure is applied (e.g., "pitting edema").

### Physiology

Conditions that cause fluid to shift from the intravascular to interstitial space include:

- Increase in intracapillary hydrostatic pressure.
- Reduction in the oncotic pressure of the plasma.
- Increase in the oncotic pressure in the interstitial space.
- Inadequate lymphatic drainage.
- Damage to the capillary endothelial barrier.

### Etiology

- **Generalized or bilateral edema:** Heart failure, renal disease, nephrotic syndrome, hypoalbuminemia, hepatic cirrhosis, drug-induced edema (e.g., amlodipine), nutritional deficiencies, hypothyroidism (e.g., myxedema), hyperthyroidism, venous insufficiency, or pregnancy.
- **Localized or unilateral edema:** Venous obstruction (e.g., deep vein thrombosis), problems with lymphatic drainage, or cellulitis or other unilateral infection.

### Clinical Assessment

- **History:** Assess onset (acute versus chronic), associated symptoms (dyspnea, pain, skin changes, etc.), relevant PMH (malignancy, clotting disorder, recent immobility, surgery, or radiation), and current medications.
- **Diagnosis:** Based on suspected etiologies.
  - For bilateral edema: Consider TTE to rule out heart failure, LFTs, creatinine, albumin, check medication list, and TSH.
  - For unilateral edema: Consider DVT ultrasound and lymphatic evaluation.

### Treatment

- Treat the underlying etiology.
- Often furosemide (Lasix) or another diuretic can be useful to remove edema for certain conditions (e.g., heart failure or cirrhosis), but first determine the etiology of the edema.
