## 1. Acute Red Eye

* **Etiologies**: Trachoma (serotypes A, B, C) is the most common cause of blindness worldwide. Inclusion conjunctivitis can also cause red eye.
* **Physical Exam**: Fundoscopic exam, pupillary examination, visual acuity, extraocular movements (EOM), and slit lamp examination are essential. Assess for mucopurulent discharge.
* **Treatment**: Erythromycin eye ointment is used for bacterial conjunctivitis. Consider sexually transmitted infection screening and treatment of sexual partners.
* **Allergic Symptoms**: Itching, tearing, and nasal congestion are usually bilateral.
* **Physical Exam**: May show inflammation of the eyelids with crusting at the base of the eyelashes.
* **Risk Factors**: Valsalva maneuver, severe cough, direct trauma, coagulopathy, anticoagulant use, and hypertension.

---

## 2. Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

* **Symptoms**: Usually unilateral bleeding underneath the conjunctiva without vision changes.
* **Treatment**: Self-limited; artificial tears may be used. Check INR if patient is on warfarin.

---

## 3. Hyphema

* **Description**: Pooling or collection of blood in the anterior chamber.
* **Treatment**: Can be medically managed with corticosteroids, beta blockers, or alpha agonists. If medical management fails, surgery may be required.

---

## 4. Keratoconjunctivitis (Dry Eye)

* **Description**: Inflammation of the cornea and conjunctiva.
* **Patient History**: May have seborrhea or eczema.
* **Physical Exam**: Inflammation of the eyelids with crusting at the base of the eyelashes.
* **Treatment**: Artificial tears and lubricating ointments. May be associated with *Staphylococcus aureus* superinfection.

---

## 5. Scleritis

* **Description**: Inflammation of the tissue between the sclera and conjunctiva.
* **Patient History**: May have autoimmune conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis (RA), systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), or psoriatic arthritis.
* **Physical Exam**: Pain on gentle palpation of the globe.
* **Treatment**: Referral to ophthalmology for evaluation and management.

---

## 6. Uveitis

* **Symptoms**: Blurred vision, usually unilateral, with severe photophobia.
* **Patient History**: May have HIV/AIDS or immunosuppression.
* **Physical Exam**: Inflammation and hemorrhages with fluffy granular lesions.
* **Treatment**: Oral valganciclovir; ensure adequate immune reconstitution.

---

## 7. Musculoskeletal Symptoms

* **History**: May have inciting event or trauma/falls. Consider risk factors for atraumatic fractures such as osteoporosis or prolonged corticosteroid use.
* **Physical Exam**: May have tenderness to touch over involved muscle or region, point tenderness, worsened pain with flexion, and limited range of motion.
* **Red Flags**: Pain worse at night, systemic symptoms including weight loss and generalized fatigue.
* **Etiologies**: Metastatic, hematologic, or primary bone tumors. Consider vertebral osteomyelitis, epidural abscess, or discitis.
* **Risk Factors**: IVDU, bacteremia, endocarditis, TB (Pott disease), or other infections.

---

## 8. Vaginitis

* **Symptoms**: Can occur in an atrophic/dry form.
* **Physical Exam**: Vulvar inflammation with thick white discharge.
* **More Common**: In elderly patients.

---

## 9. Urinary Incontinence

* **Diagnosis**: Urodynamics can confirm that incontinence is due to an overactive detrusor muscle.
* **More Common**: In multiparous women.
* **Diagnosis**: Positive cough test; weakness of pelvic floor or sphincter leads to loss of bladder control.
* **History**: Patient may have spinal cord injury or bladder outlet obstruction.

---

## 10. Contraception

* **Counseling**: Discuss continued use of condoms for protection from STIs and screen for infections. Use conversations about initiation or continuation of contraception as an opportunity for education.

### Hormonal Methods
* **Combination Oral Contraceptive Pills (OCPs)**: Must be very consistent with pill compliance.
* **Transdermal Patch**: Estrogen and progestin patch applied for 3 weeks, then removed for 1 week.
* **Vaginal Ring**: Estrogen and progestin vaginal ring inserted for 3 weeks, then removed for 1 week.
* **Depot Medroxyprogesterone Acetate (DMPA)**: Injectable progesterone administered every 3 months. Safe during breastfeeding but associated with long-term risk of osteopenia.
* **Subdermal Implant (Implanon)**: Implanted under the skin by a clinician; effective for up to 3 years.
* **Benefits**: Decreased risk of ovarian/endometrial cancer and benign breast disease.

### Barrier Methods
* **Condom**: Provides protection from STIs with estimated 5–8% failure rate with typical use.

---

## 11. Menopause

* **Median Age**: 51 years.
* **Diagnosis**: No labs or imaging required for diagnosis in typical age group.
* **Younger Patients**: If younger age with menopausal symptoms, consider premature ovarian insufficiency and evaluate accordingly.

---

## 12. Pain Management

* **Medications**:
  * **Renally Cleared**: Adjust dose if renal impairment.
  * **Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)**: Useful in neuropathic pain; can be used with or without other analgesics.
* **Muscle Relaxants**: Typically only prescribe a short course. Examples include baclofen and carisoprodol.
* **Assessment**: Use PEG scale (Pain, Enjoyment of life, and General Activity) during visits.
* **Expectations**: Set realistic expectations and goals. Consider early referral to pain specialist for complex cases.
