# Diseases

This chapter covers diseases and pathophysiology in general medicine, including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, osteoporosis, and key health care maintenance topics.

---

## Hypertension

### Blood Pressure Goals (JNC8)

| Patient Population | Goal Blood Pressure |
|---|---|
| Patients without diabetes or CKD — Age less than 60 years | Less than 140/90 mmHg |
| Patients without diabetes or CKD — Age 60 years or greater | Less than 150/90 mmHg |
| Patients with diabetes (no CKD) | Less than 140/90 mmHg |
| Patients with CKD (with or without diabetes) | Less than 140/90 mmHg (some nephrologists recommend SBP less than 130 mmHg, especially if proteinuria) |

**Emerging evidence:** There is some evidence that targeting a goal SBP less than 120 mmHg reduces mortality and decreases nonfatal cardiac events, but this remains controversial (SPRINT trial, *N Engl J Med* 2016).

### Etiology

#### Essential Hypertension (Estimated 95% of Cases)

- **Risk factors:** Older age (men greater than 55 years; women greater than 65 years), male gender, Black or African American race, obesity, family history, salt intake, and alcohol use.

#### Secondary Hypertension (Estimated 5% of Cases)

- **Renal or renovascular:** Renal artery stenosis, chronic renal failure, and polycystic kidney disease.
- **Endocrine:** Hyperaldosteronism, Cushing's syndrome, pheochromocytoma, hypo- or hyperthyroidism, and acromegaly.
- **Medications or drugs:** Oral contraceptive pills, decongestants, estrogen, chronic steroids, TCAs, NSAIDs, and cocaine.
- **Coarctation of the aorta:** Differential hypertension in each arm with brachial-femoral pulse delay.
- **Sleep apnea.**

### Complications

- **Cardiac:** CAD can result in angina or MI; left ventricular hypertrophy, which can lead to CHF.
- **Neurologic:** Intracerebral hemorrhage or other stroke subtypes including TIAs, ischemic strokes, and lacunar stroke; posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES).
- **Kidneys:** Arteriosclerosis called nephrosclerosis; decreased GFR with eventual renal failure.
- **Eyes:** AV nicking (discontinuity in the retinal vein due to thick artery wall), scotomata, copper wiring, cottonwood spots, and papilledema.

### Diagnosis

Two elevated blood pressure measurements at least 1 week apart are required.

**Accurate blood pressure measurement depends upon technique:**
- The patient should be seated and resting (ideally for greater than 5 minutes prior).
- The arm should be at heart level.
- The blood pressure cuff should be the correct size.

**Physical Exam:** May be normal, but should evaluate for the following:

- **Eyes:** Copper wiring, AV nicks, and cottonwood spots.
- **Neck:** Elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP) and carotid bruits.
- **Heart:** S4 (LVH), S3 (dilated), and displaced point of maximal impulse (PMI).
- **Abdominal:** Abdominal aortic aneurysm (pulsatile epigastric mass), abdominal bruit, and renal bruit.

**At the time of initial diagnosis, check the following studies:**

- Urinalysis for occult hematuria and proteinuria.
- Chemistry panel (to evaluate for renal dysfunction).
- Lipid profile and hemoglobin A1c.
- **Women age less than 50 years:** Discuss fertility goals and consider checking a pregnancy test if indicated (important because thiazide diuretics, ACE inhibitors or ARBs, and CCBs are contraindicated in pregnancy or teratogenic).
- Consider baseline EKG and TTE.

**If severe hypertension, resistant hypertension (hypertension despite three antihypertensive agents at adequate doses), or age of onset less than 30 years (especially if the patient is not obese and does not have a family history of hypertension):** Consider further workup for etiologies of secondary hypertension (e.g., plasma aldosterone to renin ratio, TSH, and sleep study).

### Treatment

**Lifestyle modifications:**
- Weight loss (most effective).
- DASH diet (2–4 g salt per day).
- Exercise.
- Smoking and alcohol cessation.

**Antihypertensive medications:** There is an increasing preference to counsel patients to take antihypertensive medications at night.

### Antihypertensive Medication Classes

| Class | Examples | Side Effects | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Thiazide diuretics** | Hydrochlorothiazide (12.5 mg or 25 mg QD); chlorthalidone (12.5 mg or 25 mg QD) | Hypokalemia (check BMP 3 weeks after initiation), hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia, elevated cholesterol and triglycerides | — |
| **ACE inhibitors (ACEi)** | Lisinopril (QD), enalapril (BID), captopril (TID) | Hyperkalemia (check BMP 3 weeks after initiation), increased BUN or CR (acceptable if increase less than 35%), cough (5%), angioedema (2%) | **Contraindicated in pregnancy** — teratogenic |
| **ARBs** | Losartan, valsartan, telmisartan (most potent) | Decreases uric acid (useful in patients with gout); cough less common than with ACEi | **Contraindicated in pregnancy** — teratogenic |
| **CCBs** | Dihydropyridine (lowers BP but not HR): amlodipine, felodipine, nifedipine; Non-dihydropyridine (lowers BP and HR; useful in afib or aflutter): diltiazem and verapamil | Edema and gingival hypertrophy | Benazepril or amlodipine more effective at decreasing CV events than benazepril alone (ACCOMPLISH trial, *N Engl J Med* 2008) |
| **Beta blockers (BB)** | Atenolol (25–100 mg QD, take at night), metoprolol tartrate (12.5–25 mg BID), metoprolol succinate (25–50 mg QD) | Bradycardia, bronchospasm (avoid in patients with asthma), erectile dysfunction, can increase triglycerides and decrease HDL, may worsen depression | Generally not used as monotherapy for hypertension; more effective in younger patients; often used for patients with heart failure; can be used during pregnancy |
| **Alpha agonists and antagonists** | Central alpha agonist (decreases BP and HR): clonidine (0.1–0.3 mg BID or 0.1 mg per 7-day patch); peripheral alpha antagonists: terazosin (useful in men with BPH), doxazosin | — | If patients miss a dose of clonidine, there is a risk of rebound tachycardia |
| **Vasodilators** | Hydralazine (QID) | — | Often given to patients with heart failure; can be used during pregnancy |

### Monitoring

- Check blood pressure at every clinic visit and adjust antihypertensive medications as needed.
- Consider the use of a home blood pressure kit to compare home BPs to those obtained in clinic and to allow for more frequent BP monitoring.

## Hyperlipidemia

### Risk Factors

- **Lifestyle:** Diet (high saturated fats), inactivity, advanced age, family history, male gender (M greater than F).
- **Medications that cause or exacerbate hyperlipidemia:** Thiazide diuretics (increase LDL, increase triglycerides, increase cholesterol), beta blockers (increase TGs and decrease HDL), estrogens (increase TGs), corticosteroids, and antiretroviral therapy.
- **Other:** Endocrine (hypothyroidism, DM, and Cushing's), renal (nephrotic and uremia), and liver disease.

### Types of Lipids

#### LDL ("Bad" Cholesterol)

- **Calculation:** LDL equals total cholesterol minus HDL minus triglycerides divided by 5.
- Atherogenic.
- **Goals:**
  - Less than 160 mg/dL if no cardiovascular risk factors.
  - Less than 130 mg/dL if some CV risk factors.
  - Less than 100 mg/dL if CVD or DM.

#### HDL ("Good" Cholesterol)

- Removes excess cholesterol from arterial walls.
- **Goal:** High HDL greater than 60 mg/dL (very protective); low HDL less than 40 mg/dL is an independent risk factor for CAD.

#### Triglycerides (TG)

- Higher levels of TGs are associated with an increased risk of ASCVD events.
- Severe TG elevation is associated with an increased risk of pancreatitis.
- **Goal:** Less than 150 mg/dL; considered very high if greater than 250 mg/dL.

#### Total Cholesterol

- **Calculation:** Total cholesterol equals LDL plus VLDL (approximately TG divided by 5) plus HDL.

### Physical Exam

Typically no physical exam findings. If severe, may see xanthelasma (yellow plaques on eyelids) and xanthoma (on tendons).

### Screening

Recommendations about screening are variable, with some organizations recommending that adults should be screened with a cholesterol lab panel as early as after puberty. For patients with no cardiovascular risk factors, most practitioners start screening men at age 35 years and women at age 45 years.

**ASCVD risk calculator (pooled cohort risk assessment equation):**
- Takes into account age, gender, race, total cholesterol, HDL, SBP, diabetes, and smoking.
- Provides a prediction of a patient's 10-year risk of having a cardiovascular event.
- May overestimate risk especially in older men (extra points assigned for age and gender).

### Guidelines to Treat (2018 ACC/AHA Guidelines)

Four groups of patients warrant treatment:

| Group | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Patients with CAD, CVA, or PAD | Prescribe a high-intensity statin |
| Patients with LDL greater than 190 mg/dL | Prescribe a high-intensity statin |
| Patients with DM1 or DM2, age 40–75 years, and LDL greater than 70 mg/dL | If 10-year ASCVD risk less than 7.5%, prescribe a moderate-intensity statin; if 10-year ASCVD risk greater than 7.5%, prescribe a high-intensity statin |
| Patients age 40–75 years with LDL cholesterol greater than 70 mg/dL | If 10-year ASCVD risk greater than 7.5%, prescribe a moderate- or high-intensity statin |

**Other factors that may affect treatment decisions:**
- LDL greater than 160 mg/dL plus genetic disorder.
- Family history of premature ACS.
- CRP greater than 2.
- ABI less than 0.9.
- Elevated coronary artery calcium score.

### Treatment

#### Lifestyle Modifications

- Diet, exercise, and smoking cessation.

#### Statins (HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitors)

- **Effect:** Decrease LDL substantially.

| Intensity | LDL Reduction | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| High-intensity | Approximately 50% | Atorvastatin (Lipitor) 40–80 mg QD; rosuvastatin (Crestor) 20–40 mg QD |
| Moderate-intensity | Approximately 33% | Atorvastatin 10–20 mg QD; rosuvastatin 5–10 mg QD; simvastatin 20–40 mg QD; pravastatin 40–80 mg QD; lovastatin 40 mg QD |

- **Timing:** Statins should be taken at bedtime since hepatic synthesis of cholesterol is greatest at night.
- **Side effects:** Rhabdomyolysis or myositis (increased risk in patients with CKD), myalgias; monitor LFTs 12 weeks after initiation.

#### Second-Line Agents

| Agent | Mechanism | Effect | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ezetimibe** | Inhibits Niemann-Pick C1-like protein in the GI tract | Decreases LDL | Used as monotherapy or with a statin |
| **Niacin** | Inhibits lipolysis; inhibits VLDL formation in the liver | Decreases LDL, increases HDL substantially, decreases TGs | Used as monotherapy; no added benefit when used with a statin; side effects include flushing, hepatotoxicity, hyperglycemia, and hyperuricemia (do not use in patients with gout) |
| **Fibrates (gemfibrozil)** | — | Decreases LDL, increases HDL, decreases TGs substantially | Used as monotherapy; increased risk of side effects when administered with a statin; side effects include GI symptoms, gynecomastia, gallstones, weight gain, and myopathy |

### Monitoring

Repeat the lipid panel 4–12 months after initiation of therapy.

## Osteoporosis

### Bone Biology

- Osteoblasts promote bone formation and osteoclasts promote bone absorption or breakdown.
- Bone remodeling is a balance of osteoblast and osteoclast activity.
- Inflammatory diseases cause the upregulation of RANK-ligand, which activates osteoclasts and drives bone loss.

### Risk Factors

- **Demographic:** Older age and gender (F greater than M; accelerated bone loss after menopause due to decreased estrogen).
- **History:** Past fractures and family history of osteoporosis.
- **Lifestyle:** Low BMI, smoking, and alcohol use.
- **Medical conditions:** Endocrine disorders (e.g., hyperthyroidism, Cushing's, and hypogonadism), GI disorders (e.g., IBD, celiac disease, and post gastric bypass), and rheumatologic disorders (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis).
- **Medications:** Prolonged steroid use.

### Screening

- Screen all women greater than 65 years of age.
- Consider screening younger women if risk factors for osteoporosis are present (fragility fracture, low BMI, and high-risk medication use).
- Per USPSTF screening guidelines, there is insufficient evidence to recommend universal screening in men, but screening can be considered if risk factors for osteoporosis are present.

### Diagnosis

#### DEXA Scan

- Measures tissue absorption of photons to calculate the bone mineral density (BMD).

**WHO diagnostic criteria for osteoporosis based on DEXA score:**

| T Score | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| Greater than –1.0 | Normal bone density |
| –1.0 to –2.5 | Osteopenia |
| Less than –2.5 | Osteoporosis |

#### FRAX (Fracture Risk Assessment Tool)

- Estimates the 10-year probability of fracture if no bone-directed treatment is initiated.
- Incorporates DEXA femoral neck bone mineral density and risk factors.

### Treatment

**Treatment is indicated if osteoporosis or osteopenia AND one of the following:**
- History of hip or vertebral fracture.
- 10-year risk of hip fracture greater than 3%.
- 10-year risk of any osteoporosis fracture greater than 20%.

#### Universal Recommendations

- **Lifestyle modifications:** Exercise, smoking cessation, and limiting alcohol intake.
- **Calcium supplementation:** Typically 1200 mg daily.
- **Vitamin D supplementation:** Typically 800 IU daily.

#### Pharmacologic Treatment

| Agent | Mechanism | Administration | Side Effects | Contraindications |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Bisphosphonates** (first-line) | Inhibits osteoclasts | Most oral bisphosphonates are taken once weekly, 30 minutes before eating (empty stomach). Consider stopping after 5 years of treatment. Check calcium and vitamin D before and during treatment and ensure replete. | Heartburn, esophageal irritation, osteonecrosis of the jaw (ensure preventative dental work is done prior to starting), atypical subtrochanteric femur fracture (presents with thigh pain; may be bilateral) | Pregnancy and CKD |
| **Raloxifene (Evista)** | SERM that inhibits bone resorption | — | Hot flashes, DVT, and leg cramps | Pregnancy and prior DVT or PE |
| **Calcitonin** | Inhibits osteoclasts | Given as a daily intranasal spray | Risk of anaphylaxis | — |
| **Denosumab (Prolia)** | Anti-RANK ligand; inhibits osteoclasts | — | Hypocalcemia, osteonecrosis of the jaw, and atypical subtrochanteric femur fractures | Pregnancy |
| **Teriparatide (Forteo)** | Increases bone remodeling; analog of PTH | Daily injection; should not be administered for more than 2 years | Nausea, leg cramps, and dizziness | — |

### Monitoring

Repeat DEXA 2 years after initiating treatment.

## Health Care Maintenance and Disease Screening

These health care maintenance and disease screening guidelines summarize recommendations from the U.S. Preventative Services Task Force (USPSTF) guidelines, other guidelines as indicated, and general best practices.

**Important notes:**
- Screening tests aim to identify disease in asymptomatic individuals.
- Recommendations are population-based and may need to be tailored to individual patients based on the clinical context.
- The decision to stop routine disease screening should be based on shared decision-making with the patient and should take into account the patient's functional status, life expectancy, goals, and preferences.

### All Patients

#### History

- Diet and weight.
- Exercise.
- Alcohol, tobacco, and substance use.
- Sexual history.
- Depression and anxiety.
- Intimate partner violence.
- **Advance care planning:** Discuss future health care decisions related to the patient's priorities and values, document code status, identify a surrogate medical decision maker, and complete appropriate documentation (may vary by state: advanced directive, durable power of attorney, and physician's orders for life-sustaining treatment [POLST]). Recommended for all adults, and especially important for patients with multiple comorbidities and or advanced age.

#### Screening Tests

- HIV screening for all patients age 15–65 years. Offer preexposure prophylaxis (PrEP) to persons who are at high risk of HIV acquisition.
- Latent tuberculosis infection (LTBI) screening in persons at increased risk.
- Hepatitis B virus (HBV) screening in persons at increased risk.
- Hepatitis C screening in adults age 18–79 years.
- Depression screening.

#### Physical Exam

- Body mass index (BMI) and blood pressure.

#### Vaccinations

- Td or Tdap every 10 years.

### Women 18–39 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about contraception and sexual health.

**Screening Tests:**
- Pap smear every 3 years if age 21–29 years; every 3 years or every 5 years if Pap or HPV co-testing if age 30 years or greater.
- If sexually active, gonorrhea or chlamydia screening annually until age 25 years, and then discretionary. If high-risk sexual behavior, offer STD testing more frequently (e.g., every 3–6 months).
- Rubella serology once.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, HBV series, varicella, and HPV vaccination if not already completed (recommended for individuals age 9–26 years; expanded approval up to age 45 years).

### Women 40–49 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids screening every 5 years starting at age 45 years, or earlier if risk factors.
- HgA1c every 3 years starting at age 45 years, or earlier if risk factors.
- USPSTF recommends biennial screening mammogram starting at age 50 years, but other guidelines recommend starting at age 40 years — can consider with shared decision-making.
- Pap smear every 3 years or every 5 years if Pap or HPV co-testing.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot.

### Women 50–64 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Mammogram every 2 years.
- Pap smear every 3 years or every 5 years if Pap or HPV co-testing.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years).
- Osteoporosis screening if high risk.
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot and shingles vaccine after age 60 years.

### Women 65 Years and Older

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about functional status and falls, safety in current living situation, caregiver support, nutritional status, vision and hearing, memory and cognition, urinary continence, and polypharmacy.

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Mammogram every 2 years; insufficient data to support continued mammographic screening in women 75 years or greater.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years); insufficient data to support continued colon cancer screening in patients 75 years or greater.
- Osteoporosis screening (DEXA).
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, pneumococcal vaccine, and replace one dose of the every-10-year Td booster with Tdap.

### Men 18–39 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about contraception and sexual health.

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipid screening every 5 years starting at age 35 years, or earlier if risk factors.
- Insufficient evidence to assess the benefits or risks of gonorrhea or chlamydia screening in men, but screening can be discussed and offered.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, HBV series, varicella, and HPV vaccination if not already completed (recommended for individuals age 9–26 years; expanded approval up to age 45 years).

### Men 40–49 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years starting at age 45 years, earlier if risk factors.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot.

### Men 50–64 Years

**History:** See the list above under "all patients."

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years).
- Consider discussion about prostate cancer screening with PSA (insufficient evidence per USPSTF, but some societies recommend it — utilize shared decision-making with the patient).
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot and shingles vaccine after age 60 years.

### Men 65 Years and Older

**History:** See the list above under "all patients." Also ask about functional status and falls, safety in current living situation, caregiver support, nutritional status, vision and hearing, memory and cognition, urinary continence, and polypharmacy.

**Screening Tests:**
- Lipids every 5 years.
- HgA1c every 3 years.
- Colon cancer screening (options: FIT annually, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, colonoscopy every 10 years); insufficient data to support continued colon cancer screening in patients 75 years or greater.
- AAA screening in men age 65–75 years who have ever smoked.
- Consider discussion about prostate cancer screening with PSA in men less than 75 years.
- Annual low-dose CT if age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and current smoker or quit within the past 15 years.

**Vaccinations:** Annual flu shot, pneumococcal vaccine, and replace one dose of the every-10-year Td booster with Tdap.

## Screening Recommendations by Condition

### Hypertension

Adults 18 years and older: Screen every 2 years or annually if pre-hypertension.

### Hyperlipidemia

Men 35 years and older, women 45 years and older, or any patient 20 years and older with risk factors for HLD (e.g., CAD, diabetes, and obesity). If total cholesterol less than 200 mg/dL and HDL greater than 35 mg/dL, repeat screening in 5 years.

### Diabetes

Screen all patients 45 years and older; consider earlier screening if risk factors for diabetes are present (e.g., obesity, family history, history of gestational diabetes, PCOS, HTN, and HLD). Testing options include HgA1c, fasting blood glucose, random blood glucose, and oral glucose tolerance test.

### Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)

Perform screening ultrasound for men age 65–75 years who have ever smoked.

### Gonorrhea and Chlamydia

- **Women:** If sexually active, screen annually until age 25 years, then discretionary screening after that based on risk factors.
- **Men:** Insufficient evidence to assess the benefits or risks of screening, but screening can be discussed and offered.

### HIV

Screen all adults age 15–65 years. Offer preexposure prophylaxis (PrEP) to persons who are at high risk of HIV acquisition.

### Hepatitis C

One-time screening for all patients born between 1945 and 1965.

### Breast Cancer

- **Breast self-exam:** USPSTF recommends against self-breast exams (no mortality benefit and more benign breast biopsies).
- **Physical exam:** Insufficient evidence for physician breast exam.
- **Mammography:**
  - Age 40–49 years: American Cancer Society and others recommend offering a mammogram at age 40 years, but USPSTF recommends starting at age 50 years. Consider in women age 40–49 years after shared decision-making with the patient.
  - Age 50–74 years: Screening mammogram every 2 years.
  - Age 75 years and older: Per USPSTF, not recommended (insufficient evidence).

### Cervical Cancer

- **Average risk:**
  - Age 21–29 years: Pap smear every 3 years (regardless of whether sexually active; no HPV testing below age 30 years).
  - Age 30–65 years: Pap smear every 3 years or Pap or HPV co-testing every 5 years.
  - Age greater than 65 years: Can stop testing if repeated negative tests and not at increased risk (more than one sexual partner in the last 5 years, immunosuppression, and other risk factors); also stop if hysterectomy.
- **High risk:** Every 1 year (HIV-positive, immunocompromised, in-utero DES exposure, CIN 2 or 3, and history of cervical cancer).

### Colorectal Cancer

- **Forms of screening:** FIT annually (if any one card is positive, recommend proceeding to colonoscopy), flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years, or colonoscopy every 10 years.
- **Average risk:** Begin screening at age 50 years.
- **Moderate risk** (family history of colorectal cancer or adenomatous polyps in a first-degree relative): First colonoscopy at age 40 years or 10 years younger than the youngest case in the family; repeat every 3–5 years.
- **Familial adenomatous polyposis:** Consider genetic testing at age 10 years (if positive, consider colectomy) or colonoscopy every 1–2 years starting at puberty.
- **Hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer:** Consider genetic testing at age 21 years. If positive, recommend colonoscopy every 2 years until age 40 years, then annually.
- **Ulcerative colitis:** Recommend colonoscopy annually after greater than or equal to 8 years of disease.

### Prostate Cancer

Universal PSA screening is not currently recommended, but can be considered after shared decision-making with the patient. No screening if less than 10-year predicted survival (50% of men greater than 75 years).

### Lung Cancer

Annual low-dose CT in patients age 50–80 years, with greater than 20 pack-year history and currently smoke or have quit within the past 15 years.

### Osteoporosis

All women age greater than 65 years, or earlier if risk factors. Screen with DEXA scan. Per USPSTF, insufficient evidence to recommend universal screening in men, but screening can be considered if risk factors for osteoporosis are present. The frequency of repeat DEXA scans depends on the presence of low bone mass and risk factors for accelerated bone loss.

## Vaccinations

### COVID-19

Data forthcoming about recommended vaccination frequency.

### Hepatitis A

Given in two doses 6 months apart. Recommended for patients who travel internationally to certain regions and those with certain risk factors (e.g., HCV, chronic liver disease, and IV drug use).

### Hepatitis B

Given as a primary series to infants (0, 1, and 6 months). Individuals born outside of the United States may not have been vaccinated as children, so consider checking HBV titers.

### HPV

- Recommended for men and women ages 9–26 years.
- Two- or three-dose series depending on the age at initial vaccination.
- Shared decision-making for unvaccinated adults age 27–45 years (consider in high-risk patients).

### Influenza

- Annual flu vaccine recommended.
- Shot equals killed vaccine; nasal spray equals live vaccine.
- **Contraindicated:** Severe egg allergy.

### Meningococcal

Given as a single dose injection age 11–18 years. Patients at particular risk for meningococcal infection include college students and military personnel living in close quarters, asplenic patients, and travelers to endemic areas.

### MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella)

- Primary series in children; live vaccine.
- Check prior to pregnancy and administer to patients who are not rubella immune.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy and immunocompromised patients (OK in HIV if CD4 greater than 200 cells per microliter).

### Pneumococcal

- PCV13 and PPSV23.
- All adults age 65 years and older or those age 19–64 years with certain chronic medical problems (COPD, asthma, cirrhosis, diabetes, history of smoking, high-risk pregnancy, sickle cell disease, and asplenia).
- If a patient is vaccinated at age less than 65 years, revaccinate at age 65 years or 5 years after the first dose (whichever is longer; for example, if vaccinated at age 62 years, revaccinate at age 67 years).
- **PCV13:** Prevents invasive disease and pneumonia. Ideally administer before PPSV23 if possible. If the patient received PPSV23 first, wait at least 8 weeks before administering PCV13.
- **PPSV23:** Prevents invasive disease but not pneumonia. Administer 1 year after PCV13.

### Polio

Primary series in children. Not routinely given to unvaccinated adults unless traveling to an endemic area.

### Tetanus and Diphtheria

- Tdap administered as a primary series to infants (at 1, 2, and 6 months).
- Td booster every 10 years.
- In adults greater than 65 years, replace one dose of the every-10-year Td booster with Tdap.
- Revaccinate all pregnant women between 27–36 weeks gestation every pregnancy.

### Varicella

- Live vaccine given as a primary series in children and adults without a history of chickenpox.
- Two doses; administer the second dose 4–8 weeks after the first dose.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy and immunocompromised patients.

### Zoster (Shingles)

- Live vaccine given to adults 60 years and older.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy and immunocompromised patients.

### Live Vaccines

- Smallpox, yellow fever, chickenpox, shingles, Sabin's polio, MMR, intranasal influenza, BCG, and anthrax.
