## 1. Motor Neuron Diseases

**Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)** is characterized by degeneration of both upper motor neurons and lower motor neurons. ALS is a progressive and fatal disease.

**Axonal neuropathy** is typically length-dependent, meaning that longer nerves are affected first. **Demyelinating neuropathy** is typically patchy in distribution.

**Mononeuropathy** is usually due to nerve compression at common sites such as the carpal tunnel at the wrist or the cubital tunnel at the elbow. Progressive sequential mononeuropathy suggests mononeuritis multiplex and requires an urgent evaluation for an underlying systemic disease.

There are no fasciculations or atrophy in myopathies because there is no denervation.

### Myasthenia Gravis

**Myasthenia gravis** is an autoimmune disease in which autoantibodies target the acetylcholine receptor on the postsynaptic neuromuscular junction. Myasthenia gravis can involve ocular muscles causing ptosis and diplopia, or bulbar muscles causing dysarthria and dysphagia. Respiratory weakness from myasthenic crisis is a medical emergency requiring urgent airway management.

The physical examination demonstrates fatigable weakness, in which weakness becomes more pronounced with sustained activity such as sustained upgaze causing worsening ptosis. Monitor for respiratory compromise using maximum inspiratory pressure (MIF) and maximum expiratory pressure (MEP).

The diagnosis is established with an acetylcholine receptor antibody panel, normal creatine kinase (CK), and electromyography (EMG) with repetitive stimulation showing a decremental response. If myasthenic crisis is suspected, perform plasmapheresis or administer intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG). If a thymoma is present, surgical removal of the thymus is indicated.

### Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS)

**Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome** is a paraneoplastic syndrome in which small cell lung cancer cells produce autoantibodies against presynaptic voltage-gated calcium channels. Symptoms include proximal muscle weakness that paradoxically improves with continued use. Physical examination shows proximal muscle weakness and diminished reflexes. The diagnosis is confirmed by EMG with repetitive stimulation demonstrating an incremental response. Evaluate for an underlying malignancy. Treatment involves removing the malignancy and immunosuppression.

### Botulism

**Botulism** is a rare but serious illness caused by botulinum toxin. Botulism typically presents as descending paralysis starting with cranial nerves (diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia) and progressing to respiratory muscles. Botulism is a medical emergency; intubate the patient if respiratory compromise develops. The diagnosis is supported by normal pupillary responses (distinguishing botulism from myasthenia gravis), facial weakness, and absence of sensory involvement. Confirm the diagnosis with stool and serum botulinum toxin testing. Treatment is supportive care along withbotulinum antitoxin.

## 2. Myopathies

Myopathies include genetic conditions such as muscular dystrophies, mitochondrial myopathies, and congenital myopathies, as well as infectious causes. Myopathies typically cause proximal weakness with relatively preserved reflexes and no fasciculations.

| Disease | Key Features | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Dermatomyositis** | Heliotrope rash, Gottron's papules, photosensitivity; associated with malignancy | Immunosuppressants (glucocorticoids, methotrexate, azathioprine) |
| **Polymyositis** | Proximal muscle weakness; no skin findings; not associated with malignancy | Immunosuppressants (glucocorticoids, methotrexate, azathioprine) |
| **Inclusion body myositis** | Distal and proximal weakness, finger flexion weakness; age >50 yr; no skin findings | Physical therapy; immunosuppressants generally ineffective |

Side effects of immunosuppressive therapy for inflammatory myopathies include worsening orthostatic hypotension, nausea, and visual hallucinations from glucocorticoids, as well as hepatotoxicity and teratogenicity from methotrexate.

## 3. Movement Disorders

### Parkinson's Disease

**Parkinson's disease** is caused by degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta. The cardinal features are bradykinesia, resting tremor, rigidity, and postural instability. Onset is typically asymmetric. Most patients present after age 60 years. Prevalence increases with age.

**Levodopa-carbidopa** is the cornerstone of treatment. **Entacapone** is a catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT) inhibitor that extends the duration of levodopa effect. Side effects include hallucinations, orthostasis, and insomnia. **Dopamine agonists** such as pramipexole and ropinirole can be used and come in extended-release formulations useful for managing motor fluctuations. Side effects include hallucinations and impulse control disorder.

Atypical parkinsonian syndromes include multiple system atrophy (MSA), progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP), and corticobasal degeneration (CBD). These syndromes do not respond well to dopaminergic therapy.

### Dystonia

**Dystonia** is characterized by concomitant contraction of agonist and antagonist muscles resulting in sustained or intermittent muscle contractions causing abnormal postures. Etiologies include genetic diseases such as Wilson's disease and Huntington's disease, as well as drug-induced causes. Screen for iron deficiency, uremia, anemia, and neuropathies, and treat the underlying cause. Treatment options include anticholinergic agents such as trihexyphenidyl and benzodiazepines, botulinum toxin injections for focal dystonia, and deep brain stimulation for severe cases.

### Tics

**Tics** are brief, repetitive, stereotyped movements or sounds that can be voluntarily suppressed. Tics typically begin in childhood. Treatment includes behavioral therapy and pharmacotherapy with dopamine agonists such as pramipexole, ropinirole, or rotigotine patch.

### Essential Tremor

**Essential tremor** is an action tremor that increases with movement and typically affects the hands. Essential tremor often has a family history. Treatment includes propranolol or primidone.

### Huntington's Disease

**Huntington's disease** is a genetic neurodegenerative disease presenting with chorea, behavioral disturbance, and cognitive decline. Treatment includes tetrabenazine for chorea and antipsychotics for behavioral symptoms.

## 4. Cognitive Disorders

### Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH)

**Normal pressure hydrocephalus** is characterized by the triad of gait disturbance (magnetic gait due to apraxia), cognitive decline (dementia), and urinary incontinence. Typically, gait instability occurs first, followed by urge incontinence and then cognitive impairment.

The diagnosis is established by a large-volume lumbar puncture (removing 30–50 mL of CSF) with pre-procedure and post-procedure assessment of gait and cognition. The treatment is a ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt.

### Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease (CJD)

**Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease** typically has a median onset at 60 years. It presents as rapidly progressive ataxia and dementia that is uniformly fatal, with 70% of patients dying within 1 year. The diagnosis is supported by CSF analysis showing elevated 14-3-3 protein and real-time quaking-induced conversion (RT-QuIC) testing. EEG shows periodic sharp waves. There are no effective treatments.

### Pseudodementia

**Pseudodementia** refers to cognitive impairment caused by depression rather than a primary neurodegenerative process. Patients with pseudodementia may be apathetic and perform poorly on formal testing due to lack of effort. They typically respond to antidepressant treatment.

## 5. CNS Inflammatory and Demyelinating Diseases

### Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

**Multiple sclerosis** is a CNS disease characterized by demyelination. The typical presentation is a young adult with a relapsing-remitting pattern of neurological deficits. Common presentations include sensory deficits, optic neuritis (painful vision loss), and internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO).

Severe symptoms such as bilateral optic neuritis or transverse myelitis require urgent evaluation. The treatment for acute flares is high-dose IV methylprednisolone followed by oral prednisone taper. Long-term disease-modifying therapies include beta-interferon, glatiramer acetate, dimethyl fumarate, fingolimod, and natalizumab.

### Cauda Equina Syndrome

**Cauda equina syndrome** is caused by diseases affecting the lumbosacral nerve roots resulting in leg weakness, saddle anesthesia, and bowel or bladder dysfunction. Higher risk occurs in patients with a known history of malignancy. Cauda equina syndrome is a surgical emergency requiring urgent decompression.

Typically, CSF is acellular, though patients often have an abnormal IgG index and oligoclonal bands. MRI of the brain and spine may show abnormal findings in the limbic system, brainstem, or spinal cord. The diagnosis is established with MRI and lumbar puncture demonstrating elevated IgG index and oligoclonal bands in both serum and CSF.
