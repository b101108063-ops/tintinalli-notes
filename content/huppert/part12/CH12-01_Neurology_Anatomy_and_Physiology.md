## 1. Central Nervous System

The central nervous system (CNS) consists of the brain and spinal cord. It is composed of gray matter containing neuronal cell bodies and white matter containing axons. The brain and spinal cord are somatotopically organized, meaning that adjacent structures represent adjacent body regions. Signals travel along tracts, which are bundles of axons that carry motor signals (descending) or sensory signals (ascending).

### Motor Tracts

The pyramidal system includes the corticospinal and corticobulbar tracts. The corticospinal tract carries voluntary motor control from the brain through the brainstem and spinal cord to the neck and lower extremities. The corticobulbar tract carries voluntary motor control from the brain through the brainstem to the face and head. These tracts carry motor signals from the brain to the periphery in a descending fashion.

### Sensory Tracts

The anterolateral system includes the anterior and lateral spinothalamic tracts. The lateral spinothalamic tract carries pain and temperature sensation, and the anterior spinothalamic tract carries crude touch. These tracts carry sensory signals from the periphery to the brain in an ascending fashion, with a synapse in the brainstem and then the thalamus before reaching the cortex.

The dorsal column system carries fine touch, vibration, and proprioception. The fasciculus gracilis carries information from the legs, and the fasciculus cuneatus carries information from the upper extremities. These tracts ascend to the thalamus and then to the cortex.

### Cerebrum

The cerebrum is responsible for higher processing and cognitive function. The cerebral cortex is divided into lobes including the frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital lobes, each with distinct functions. A lesion in a specific lobe produces clinical effects corresponding to that lobe's function. The cerebral cortex receives blood supply from the internal carotid and vertebral arteries, which form the circle of Willis.

The cortical homunculus is a distorted representation of the human body on the cortex, with larger areas dedicated to regions with higher sensory receptor density such as the hands and face.

### Cerebellum

The cerebellum detects discrepancies between intended and actual movement and helps correct these errors. The cerebellar vermis controls truncal movements, while the cerebellar hemispheres (located laterally) control limb movements. A cerebellar lesion typically causes vertigo, nystagmus, nausea and vomiting, and usually ataxia.

### Basal Ganglia

The basal ganglia forms a circuit with the cortex on the same side and regulates movement. The substantia nigra produces dopamine and projects to the striatum via the nigrostriatal pathway. Dopamine is essential for normal movement. A lesion of the substantia nigra causes difficulties with speech, posture, and movement on the contralateral side.

### Hypothalamus

The hypothalamus regulates vital functions through the mnemonic **TAN HATS**: **T**hirst and water balance, **A**drenal (pituitary control), **N**ausea (chemoreceptor trigger zone), **H**unger (feeding center), **A**utonomic (sympathetic and parasympathetic), **T**emperature regulation, and **S**ex (sexual development and behavior). The hypothalamus also works with the limbic system. Lesions can cause diverse endocrine, autonomic, and behavioral effects. The hypothalamus forms circuits with the cortex and brainstem.

## 2. Peripheral Nervous System

The peripheral nervous system (PNS) includes all nerves and ganglia located outside the brain and spinal cord.

### Spinal Roots

The spinal roots include the motor and sensory nerve roots that exit the spinal cord through the intervertebral foramina.

### Cranial Nerves

| Number | Nerve | Function | Clinical Effect of Lesion |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Olfactory | Special sensory: smell (only cranial nerve without thalamic relay to cortex) | Anosmia |
| 2 | Optic | Special sensory: vision | Vision loss |
| 3 | Oculomotor | Somatic motor: eye movement (superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, inferior oblique), accommodation, eyelid opening; parasympathetic: pupil constriction | Ptosis, eye deviated outward and downward, dilated pupil |
| 4 | Trochlear | Somatic motor: eye movement (superior oblique) — intorsion and depression of adducted eye | Extorted eye that is difficult to adduct, vertical diplopia especially when reading or descending stairs |
| 5 | Trigeminal | Somatic sensory: sensation to face (ophthalmic V1, maxillary V2, mandibular V3); motor: mastication (masseter, temporalis, medial and lateral pterygoids) | Facial sensory loss, weakness of jaw opening and chewing |
| 6 | Abducens | Somatic motor: eye movement (lateral rectus) — abduction | Impaired abduction, horizontal diplopia |
| 7 | Facial | Somatic motor: facial movement, eyelid closure, stapedius in ear; somatic sensory: taste to anterior two-thirds of tongue; parasympathetic: lacrimal and salivary glands | Peripheral facial weakness affecting upper and lower face; loss of taste to anterior two-thirds of tongue |
| 8 | Vestibulocochlear | Special sensory: hearing from cochlea; balance from semicircular canals | Hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo |
| 9 | Glossopharyngeal | Somatic motor: innervates stylopharyngeus (elevates pharynx and larynx); special sensory: taste and sensation to posterior third of tongue; parasympathetic: parotid gland | Dysphagia, loss of gag reflex |
| 10 | Vagus | Somatic motor: pharyngeal movement except stylopharyngeus, swallowing, palate elevation; parasympathetic: thoracic and abdominal organs | Uvula deviates away from the lesion, hoarseness |
| 11 | Accessory spinal | Somatic motor: innervates sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles | Turn head away from affected side, shoulder droop, weakness on shoulder elevation |
| 12 | Hypoglossal | Somatic motor: tongue movement (all muscles except palatoglossus, which is CN X) | Tongue deviates toward side of lesion, possible atrophy or fasciculations |

### Memory Assessment

Working memory is assessed by forward digit span, with normal performance greater than 5–7 digits, and backward digit span. Delayed recall tests long-term memory by asking patients to recall information after a delay.
