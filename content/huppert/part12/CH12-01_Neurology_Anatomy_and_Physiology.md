# Anatomy and Physiology

## Introduction

In neurology, the clinical approach centers on "localizing" a lesion. This process involves two key steps:

1. **Step 1:** Determine the location of the lesion responsible for the patient's signs and symptoms.
2. **Step 2:** Based on the location, identify the possible etiologies of those signs and symptoms.

These steps require a clear understanding of the anatomy and pathophysiology of the central and peripheral nervous systems, as well as their blood supplies. A helpful tip is to learn to map signs and symptoms to a specific location. For example, vertigo, diplopia, and nystagmus together suggest a brainstem lesion.

## Central Nervous System (CNS)

### Overview

The CNS includes the brain and spinal cord. It is composed of gray matter (cell bodies) and white matter (axons). The brain and spinal cord are somatotopically organized, meaning that signals travel on "tracts"—specifically, descending motor tracts and ascending sensory tracts.

### Motor Tracts

#### Pyramidal Tracts

**Corticospinal and corticobulbar tracts** (see Figure 12.1A):

- **Corticospinal tracts:** Neurons originate in the primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus), premotor cortex, and supplemental motor cortex. Their axons descend through the posterior limb of the internal capsule, enter the brainstem (cerebral peduncle in the midbrain), pass through the basis pontis and medullary pyramids. The tract then divides at the cervicomedullary junction into two tracts: the contralateral lateral spinal tract and ipsilateral anterior spinal tract. These descend and synapse onto the lower motor neuron (LMN).
  - **Function:** Voluntary control of movement from the neck to the feet.

- **Corticobulbar tracts:** These share the same pathway origins as corticospinal tracts but synapse onto the brainstem motor nuclei instead.
  - **Function:** Voluntary control of face, head, and neck movement.

#### Extrapyramidal Tracts

The extrapyramidal system includes ventromedial bulbospinal and ventrolateral bulbospinal tracts.

**Note on Figure 12.1A:** The main descending motor pathways are shown, including the corticospinal (maroon) and corticobulbar (red) tracts, which carry motor signals from the brain to the periphery.

### Sensory Tracts

#### Anterolateral System

**Anterior and lateral spinothalamic tracts** (see Figure 12.1B):

- **Anatomy:** Smaller afferent fibers of peripheral nerves enter the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, cross, and ascend in the opposite anterior and lateral columns. They then enter the brainstem and proceed to the thalamus (VPL nucleus) and finally to the postcentral gyrus of the parietal cortex.
  - **Function:** Nociception (pain), temperature sensibility, and touch.

#### Posterior Column–Medial Lemniscal Pathway

- **Anatomy:** Larger afferent fibers of peripheral nerves enter the spinal cord and ascend in the ipsilateral posterior column. The first synapse occurs in the gracile or cuneate nucleus in the lower medulla. The fibers then cross and ascend in the medial lemniscus (located in the medial medulla and tegmentum of the pons and midbrain), synapse in the thalamus (VPL nucleus), and continue to the parietal cortex.
  - **Function:** Tactile sensation, position sense (proprioception), and kinesthesia.

**Clinical Note:** Other fibers carrying sensory information about pain, touch, vibration, and proprioception ascend in a diffuse pattern. Therefore, a lesion in the posterior column may not result in a sensory deficit.

**Note on Figure 12.1B:** The main ascending somatosensory pathways include the spinothalamic tract (dark blue; carries pain and thermal sense) and the posterior column–medial lemniscus pathway (light blue; carries proprioception and vibration). These tracts carry sensory signals from the periphery to the brain (ascending).

### Brain

#### Cerebral Cortex

- **Functions:** Higher processing.
- **Clinical effects of a lesion:** Contralateral motor, sensory, and visual symptoms.

##### Regional Anatomy and Vascular Supply

| Region | Function | Vascular Supply | Lesion Effects |
|--------|----------|----------------|---------------|
| **Frontal** | Motor, social judgment, executive function, voluntary horizontal gaze initiation | ACA (legs), MCA (face/arms > legs) | Disinhibition, apathy, personality/behavioral change; contralateral motor weakness |
| **Parietal** | Sensory, visuospatial, attention, vision | ACA, MCA | Contralateral visual, tactile, and auditory neglect |
| **Temporal** | Hearing, emotion, memory | MCA, PCA | Problems with speech and language, memory loss |
| **Occipital** | Vision | PCA (macula supplied by MCA) | Contralateral hemifield vision loss |

**Note on Figure 12.2:** The cerebrum has distinct regions (A) with corresponding vascular supplies (B). The cortical homunculus (C) is a distorted representation of the human body based on the neurologic "map" of areas dedicated to processing motor (red) and sensory (blue) functions for different body parts.

#### Cerebellum

- **Function:** Detects "motor error" and helps correct it.
  - **Vermis** = trunk control
  - **Hemisphere (lateral)** = limb control
- **Lesion effects:** Vertigo, nystagmus, nausea/vomiting, and (usually) ipsilateral ataxia.

#### Brainstem

- **Description:** Includes the midbrain, pons, and medulla.
- **Function:** Contains cranial nerve nuclei, motor tracts, and sensory tracts.
- **Lesion effects:** Diplopia, facial weakness, facial sensory loss, dysphagia, dysarthria; contralateral weakness and numbness in the body; "crossed" weakness (with UMN signs) and sensory abnormalities of the head and limbs (e.g., weakness in the right face and the left arm and leg).

#### Thalamus

- **Function:** Sensory and motor relay station; also involved in emotion.
- **Lesion effects:** Contralateral symptoms, based on which nuclei are affected.

#### Basal Ganglia

- **Function:** Initiation of purposeful movement; suppression of unwanted movement. Dopamine is essential for movement.
- **Lesion effects:** Difficulties with speech, posture, and movement on the contralateral side. The basal ganglia form a circuit with the cortex on the same side.

#### Hypothalamus

- **Function:** Memory aid: **TAN HATS to Bed**
  - **T**hirst/water balance
  - **A**denohypophysis control
  - **N**eurohypophysis and Hormones
  - **H**unger
  - **A**utonomic regulation
  - **T**emperature regulation
  - **S**exual urges
  - **C**ircadian rhythm
- **Lesion effects:** Depend on which nuclei are affected.

#### Limbic System

- **Description:** Includes the hippocampus (first affected by anoxic damage), amygdala, cingulate gyrus, fornix, mammillary bodies, and septal nucleus.
- **Function:** Emotions, behavior, eating, and memory.
- **Lesion effects:** Changes in the ability to perceive hunger/satiety, emotional changes, amnesia, and seizures.

#### Spinal Cord

- **Description:** Includes white matter tracts that contain ascending sensory pathways, descending motor pathways, and nerve cell bodies (see Figure 12.3).
- **Lesion effects:** Presence of a horizontal level below which sensory, motor, and autonomic function is impaired.

**Note on Figure 12.3:** A transverse section through the spinal cord shows the main descending motor tracts (red) and ascending sensory tracts (blue). In the dorsal column, the gracilis and cuneatus tracts are shown (gracilis = legs, cuneatus = arms).

## Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

The PNS includes nerves and ganglia outside the brain and spinal cord.

### Spinal Roots

- **Description:** Includes the motor and sensory nerve roots that exit the spinal cord proper and enter into the spinal foramina.

#### Lesion Effects by Level

| Cord Level | Root Levels | Clinical Features |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Cervical cord** | C5–6 | Loss of biceps power and reflexes |
| | C7 | Loss of triceps, wrist/finger extension power |
| | C8 | Impaired wrist/finger flexion |
| **Thoracic cord** | Localize using site of back pain and sensory level; T4 = nipples, T10 = umbilicus | Paralysis with leg weakness, bowel/bladder dysfunction |
| **Lumbar cord** | L2–4 | Paralysis of thigh flexion/adduction, leg extension at knee, absent patellar reflex |
| | L5–S1 | Paralysis of thigh extension, leg flexion at knee, ankle/foot movements, absent ankle jerk (S1) |
| **Sacral cord/conus medullaris** | S3–5 | Bilateral saddle anesthesia, urinary retention/lax anal tone |
| | S2–4 | Absent bulbocavernosus reflex |
| | S4–5 | Absent anal reflex |
| **Cauda equina** | — | Low back and radicular pain, asymmetric leg weakness and sensory loss, variable areflexia of lower extremities |

### Peripheral Nerves

- **Lesion effects:** Mid or distal limb pain, weakness (with LMN signs) or sensory abnormalities following the nerve distribution, "stocking or glove" distribution, and loss of reflexes.

### Neuromuscular Junction

- **Lesion effects:** Proximal weakness, diplopia, possible bulbar weakness (dysarthria, dysphagia, facial weakness) that may worsen with use (fatigability); sensation is spared.

### Muscle

- **Lesion effects:** Bilateral proximal or distal weakness (typically proximal weakness is more prominent than distal weakness); sensation is spared.

### Cranial Nerves

Cranial nerve reflexes are automatic responses to stimuli that do not require conscious thought.

#### Reflex Pathways

| Reflex | Pathway |
|--------|---------|
| **Pupillary light reflex** (direct and consensual pupil constriction to light) | Cranial nerve II → Cranial nerve III |
| **Corneal reflex** (touch cornea → blink) | Cranial nerve V → Cranial nerve VII |
| **Jaw jerk** (tap chin with reflex hammer → jaw protrudes forward) | Cranial nerve V3 (sensory) → Cranial nerve V3 (motor, masseter) |
| **Vestibuloocular reflex** (turn head laterally, eyes remain fixated on target) | Cranial nerve VIII → Cranial nerves III and VI |
| **Gag reflex** (touch posterior pharynx → gag) | Cranial nerve IX → Cranial nerve X |
| **Cough reflex** (advanced suction tube through endotracheal tube and suction deep in trachea → cough) | Cranial nerve X (sensory) → Cranial nerve X (motor) |
