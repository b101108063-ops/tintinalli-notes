# Medications

## Respiratory Support

### Supplemental Oxygen Delivery Devices

| Device | Flow | FiO₂ | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|------|------|-----------|--------------|
| Nasal Cannula | 1–6 LPM | 25%–40% (increases by 4% per additional LPM) | Cheap, tolerable, common device | Variable FiO₂ delivery based on RR, Vt, mouth breathing |
| Simple Face Mask | 6–10 LPM | 35%–50% | Accessible, easy to use | Not as well tolerated, especially with delirious/anxious patients; imprecise FiO₂ delivery |
| Nonrebreather (NRB) | 10–15 LPM | 80%–95% | High FiO₂ delivery | Less tolerable; must have one valve open to ensure patient safety |
| High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC) | 10–60 LPM | 21%–100% | High FiO₂ delivery with close titration; washes out dead space, decreasing work of breathing and RR; some PEEP at high flow rates | Less mobile device; variable FiO₂ delivery |

### Mechanisms by Which Positive Pressure Improves Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema

1. Increased intrathoracic pressure decreases preload
2. Increased alveolar pressure reduces filtration out of vessel via Starling forces (positive pressure increases interstitial pressure that opposes intravascular hydrostatic pressure)
3. PEEP reduces LV afterload by decreasing wall stress
4. Decreased LV transmural pressure also decreases myocardial oxygen consumption

## Noninvasive Positive Pressure Ventilation (NIPPV)

### CPAP vs. BiPAP

| Device | Settings | Indications | Contraindications |
|--------|---------|-------------|------------------|
| **CPAP** | Airway pressure (constant throughout respiratory cycle) | Hypoxemia, cardiogenic pulmonary edema, hypoxemic respiratory failure, obstructive sleep apnea | Unable to protect airway (encephalopathy, aspiration, vomiting, excessive secretions, UGIB); at risk of losing airway (recent facial trauma/surgery, evolving obstruction); NIPPV inappropriate for the cause of respiratory failure; unable to tolerate mask or inconsistent with goals |
| **BiPAP** | Inspiratory pressure (IPAP) + expiratory pressure (EPAP = PEEP) | Hypoxemia, hypercapnia, cardiogenic pulmonary edema, hypoxemic respiratory failure, COPD exacerbations with hypercapnia, obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS), at risk of hypoventilation after extubation (COPD, OSA, OHS) | Same as above |

## Intubation and Mechanical Ventilation

### Purpose

- Relieve respiratory distress
- Improve gas exchange for CO₂ (hypercarbia) and O₂ (hypoxemia)
- Decrease work of breathing
- Protect airway (low GCS <8, apnea, inability to clear secretions)

### Indications

Typically try noninvasive ventilation first, except in altered mental status, cardiac arrest, or severe respiratory compromise with impending arrest.

- **Hypoxemic respiratory failure:** PaO₂ <55 mmHg
- **Hypercarbic respiratory failure:** PaCO₂ >45 mmHg with acidemia (pH <7.35)
- **Upper airway obstruction:** Tumor, angioedema, stenosis
- **Severe acidemia** with inability to compensate: DKA, severe sepsis
- **Neuromuscular weakness:** ALS, Guillain-Barré, toxins, myasthenia gravis
- To safely perform procedures or imaging (relative)
- Hemodynamic instability (relative)

### How to Intubate

Various methods exist: endotracheal vs. nasal, awake vs. paralyzed and sedated, direct visualization vs. video-assisted. Most common is paralyzed and sedated orotracheal intubation by direct laryngoscopy (DL). Institutions have specific protocols (ED, anesthesia, critical care-trained clinicians). Difficult intubations may require ENT backup or options for tracheostomy.

### Ventilator Modes and Key Parameters

See Table 3.8 and Figure 3.5.

### Complications of Intubation

- Inability to intubate (abnormal anatomy, severe obesity), inability to ventilate
- Hypotension (due to auto-PEEP physiology or sedation/induction medications → decreased vascular tone and SVR)
- Aspiration (especially if patient not NPO)
- Hypoxemia, arrhythmia
- Risk factors for hemodynamic collapse: aortic stenosis, pulmonary hypertension (induction medications reduce vascular tone)

### Complications of Mechanical Ventilation

- Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP): Pneumonia diagnosed >48 hours after intubation
- Laryngeal edema, ulceration (consider tracheostomy after 14 days)
- Oxygen toxicity (higher risk with higher FiO₂, often >0.7)
- Oversedation and delirium
- Barotrauma: Pneumothorax, subcutaneous emphysema
- Ventilator-associated lung injury (VALI)
- Diaphragmatic weakness (atrophy from mechanical ventilation and muscle disuse)

### Managing Refractory Hypoxemia

- **Mechanical:** Increase FiO₂, increase PEEP, increase inspiratory time
- **Non-mechanical:** Neuromuscular blockade, prone positioning, inhaled pulmonary vasodilator (e.g., nitrous oxide, prostacyclin), ECMO

### Criteria for Extubation

**Perform daily assessment of readiness for spontaneous breathing trial (SBT):**

**Screening criteria:**
- Most important: The reason for intubation has been resolved or significantly improved
- Minimal ventilator settings: FiO₂ <0.5, PEEP <8 cm H₂O
- Intact gag/cough (ideally responsive to commands)
- Minimal secretions and ability to handle secretions after extubation
- Hemodynamically stable off vasopressors (or titrating off and stable on low-dose pressors)
- If prolonged intubation (>5–7 days): Consider checking "cuff leak" for upper airway edema

**Performing SBT:**
- Favored approach: 30–120 minutes of pressure support at PS 7 and PEEP 5
- Alternate: 30 minutes of T-piece with blow-by oxygen

**Rapid Shallow Breathing Index (RSBI):**
- RSBI = RR / Tidal Volume (in liters)
- Slow, deeper breaths are more reassuring
- RSBI >105 predicts extubation failure

### Rationale for Extubation Failure

**Physiology:** Imbalance between respiratory load and the combination of ventilatory muscle power and central drive.

- **Increased respiratory load:** Poor compliance, increased dead space, metabolic acidosis requiring higher minute ventilation
- **Diminished muscle power:** Deconditioning, often from prolonged intubation
- **Reduced/impaired central drive to breathe:** Often from sedation

**Causes:** Post-extubation stridor, respiratory muscle weakness, aspiration, mucus plugging, pulmonary edema (removal of positive pressure leads to increased preload and afterload; consider diuresis in patients with HFrEF or impaired myocardial function), failure to reverse the reason for intubation.

## Ventilator Modes

| Variable | Volume Control (VC) | Pressure Control (PC) | Pressure Support (PS) |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Independent Variables** | TV, RR, FiO₂, PEEP | Inspiratory pressure, time, PEEP, RR, FiO₂ | Inspiratory pressure, PEEP, FiO₂ |
| **Dependent Variables** | Pressure | TV (and minute ventilation) | TV and RR (minute ventilation) |
| **Tidal Volume** | Same every breath | Depends on patient effort and lung compliance | Depends on patient effort and lung compliance |
| **Airway Pressure** | Depends on lung compliance | Same every breath | Same every breath |
| **Respiratory Rate** | Minimum set, but patient can trigger | Minimum set, but patient can trigger | Completely spontaneous |
| **Flow Rate** | Same every breath | Depends on patient effort and lung compliance | Depends on patient effort and lung compliance |
| **Waveforms** | Pressure–time, Pressure–volume loop, Volume–time, Flow–time | — | — |
| **Notes** | Mode for ARDS for lung-protective ventilation | More comfortable so requires less sedation | Often a vent weaning and SBT mode; does not have mandatory minute ventilation |

## Key Parameters Affecting Oxygenation and Ventilation

| Parameters that Affect Oxygenation | Parameters that Affect Ventilation (CO₂) |
|----------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| FiO₂ (fraction of inspired air that is oxygen) | Respiratory rate (RR) |
| Mean airway pressure | Tidal volume (TV) |
| Positive end expiratory pressure (PEEP) | Minute ventilation (MV) = RR × TV |
| Peak inspiratory pressure (PIP) | |
| Inspiratory/expiratory ratio (I:E ratio) | |

> \*Increase PaO₂ by increasing FiO₂ and/or PEEP.
> \*Decrease PaCO₂ by increasing RR and/or TV (to increase minute ventilation).

**Additional Parameters:**
- **Plateau pressure (PPL):** Surrogate for compliance (stiffness); measured at end inspiration when there is no flow
- **Peak inspiratory pressure (PIP):** Surrogate for compliance and airway resistance; measured during inspiration when flow is present
- **Compliance (ΔV/ΔP):** Stiffness of the lung
- **Auto-PEEP:** Measured with end-expiratory hold maneuver; PEEP above set value; occurs when breath is delivered before full expiration of prior breath; can result in high intrathoracic pressure, decreased cardiac preload, and hypotension
- **I:E ratio:** Typically 1:2; goal is to maximize E time in conditions like COPD with risk of auto-PEEP by reducing RR

## Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation (ECMO)

### Definition

Temporary external bypass of the lungs and/or heart to treat life-threatening organ failure.

### Types

| Type | Treats | Provides | Circuit |
|------|--------|---------|---------|
| **Venovenous (VV)** | Hypoxemic/hypercarbic respiratory failure only | No circulatory support | Blood extracted from venous system → externally oxygenated → returned to venous system; relies on patient's own cardiovascular system |
| **Venoarterial (VA)** | Hypoxemic/hypercarbic respiratory failure and circulatory support | Both cardiac and respiratory support | Blood extracted from venous system → externally oxygenated → returned to arterial system; bypasses both heart and lungs |

### Indications

- Severe pulmonary and/or cardiac failure causing refractory hypoxemic/hypercarbic respiratory failure or cardiogenic shock
- Should exhaust all less invasive treatments first
- Etiology of organ failure is reversible (e.g., massive PE, ARDS) or with known definitive replacement planned (e.g., VADS, transplant)

### Contraindications

- **Absolute:** Etiology cannot be reversed and no definitive treatment options; other comorbidities that will prohibit survival
- **Relative:** Thrombocytopenia, severe brain injury, severe comorbidities, life-limiting condition

### Complications

- **Bleeding:** Systemic heparization required; patients are more coagulopathic with ECMO
- **Thrombosis:** Cannula-associated thrombus, LV thrombus
- **Cannulation-related:** Line infections, pneumothorax, leg ischemia with femoral artery insertion

## Analgesia, Sedation, Paralytics, and Vasoactive Medications

### Pain Management

**Goal:** Control pain adequately to achieve a maximally functional patient (not zero pain).

**General principles:**
- Try PRNs first to determine needs prior to starting drips
- Treat pain first (can also cause anxiety, delirium)
- Assess analgesic needs daily
- For patients on chronic opiates: Convert to oral morphine equivalents (OME), apply dose reduction for incomplete cross-tolerance (often 25–50%), divide into doses per day with additional PRNs

**Indications:** Patient-reported pain, postoperative pain, trauma, chronic opioid use, decreased air hunger with certain modes of mechanical ventilation, neuromuscular blockade (paralysis).

**Adverse effects:** Respiratory depression, constipation, withdrawal, nausea; magnified when used with other sedatives (e.g., benzodiazepines).

### Pain Medications Commonly Used in the ICU

| Agent | Typical Dosing | Pharmacokinetics | Adverse Effects | Notes |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|----------------|-------|
| **Fentanyl** | Bolus: 25–50 mcg; Maintenance drip: 25–300 mcg/hr | MOA: Opioid receptor agonist; Onset: seconds; Half-life: 1.5–6 hr; Metabolism: Liver; VD: Fat soluble; No active metabolites | Constipation, drowsiness, tolerance, opioid withdrawal with prolonged infusions, skeletal muscle rigidity | Fast onset; first line for pain and sedation in most intubated patients; fat soluble = prolonged duration even after drip is stopped |
| **Morphine** | Bolus: 2–4 mg; Maintenance drip: 1–15 mg/hr | Onset: 5–10 min; Half-life: 3–7 hr; Metabolism: Liver; VD: Water soluble; Excretion: Renal | Histamine release → hypotension and itchiness | Fast onset but slower than fentanyl; accumulates in renal failure |
| **Hydromorphone (Dilaudid)** | Bolus: 0.25–1 mg; Maintenance drip: 0.5–5 mg/hr | Onset: 5 min; Half-life: 1–4 hr; Metabolism: Liver; VD: Water soluble; Excretion: Renal | Similar to morphine | Safer in organ dysfunction |
| **Ketamine** | Bolus: 0.25–0.5 mg/kg; Maintenance drip: 0.05–1 mg/kg/hr | MOA: NMDA receptor antagonist; Metabolism: Liver; Excretion: Renal | Dissociation, agitation with emergence, bronchorrhea, hypertension (more common than hypotension) | Often managed by pain and anesthesia services; useful in opioid-tolerant patients |

## Sedation

**Goal:** Ensure comfort while using the minimal amount of sedative/hypnotics possible; patients should be calm, lucid, pain-free, and interactive.

**General principles:**
- Perform a daily sedation interruption (DSI) if possible
- Assess pain and sedation needs daily
- Target a sedation goal using a scale (often RASS); ideal is patient at RASS 0 (awake, cooperative)
- For neuromuscular blockade/paralysis, requires RASS –5
- Dexmedetomidine is not appropriate for paralyzed patients; propofol or benzodiazepine is needed

**Indications:** Ventilator dyssynchrony, anxiety not due to delirium, neuromuscular blockade, intracranial hypertension, refractory status epilepticus, severe respiratory failure, agitation when safety is at risk, procedural sedation.

### Medications Commonly Used for Sedation in the ICU

| Generic Name (Trade Name) | Typical Dosing | Pharmacokinetics | Adverse Effects | Notes |
|-------------------------|---------------|-----------------|----------------|-------|
| **Propofol** | Bolus: 2 mg/kg; Maintenance: 10–80 mcg/kg/min | MOA: Activates GABA receptors; Onset: 1 min; Half-life: 2–4 min; Metabolism: Liver | Hypotension, bradycardia, respiratory depression, hypertriglyceridemia, pancreatitis, PRIS (rare but fatal; higher risk in young <18, prolonged infusion >3 days) | Only used for intubated patients or procedural sedation; reduce or switch if TG >500 mg/dL |
| **Dexmedetomidine (Precedex)** | Bolus: 0.1 mcg/kg over 10 min; Maintenance: 0.2–1.5 mcg/kg/hr | MOA: Central alpha-2 agonist; Onset: 5–10 min; Half-life: 1–2 hr; Metabolism: Liver; No active metabolites | Hemodynamic instability (up or down), commonly hypotension and bradycardia | May obscure alcohol withdrawal adrenergic symptoms; used as adjunct agent; cannot achieve deep sedation, inappropriate for patients on NMBA |
| **Midazolam (Versed)** | Bolus: 0.5–2 mg; Maintenance: 0.5–8 mg/hr | MOA: GABA agonism; Onset: 2–3 min; Half-life: 2–4 hr; Metabolism: Liver; Excretion: Renal | Respiratory depression, hypotension if rapid infusion, nausea/vomiting, delirium | Patients develop dependence and withdrawal after prolonged infusion; used for procedural sedation or patients unable to tolerate propofol; active metabolites: lingering effects especially with organ dysfunction |
| **Ketamine** | Bolus: 1–2 mg/kg; Maintenance: 0.2–0.5 mg/kg/hr | MOA: NMDA receptor antagonist; Metabolism: Liver; Excretion: Renal | Dissociation, agitation with emergence, bronchorrhea, hypertension (more often than hypotension) | Used for procedural sedation, adjunct sedation agent, and with propofol intolerance |

## Neuromuscular Blocking Agents (NMBAs)

**General principles:** Mechanism blocks acetylcholine-mediated transmission at the neuromuscular junction.

**Indications:** Severe ARDS/hypoxemia and severe ventilatory dyssynchrony not controlled by sedation. NMBAs paralyze the patient, so deep sedation (RASS –5) and adequate pain control are required.

| Generic Name (Trade Name) | Typical Dosing | Pharmacokinetics | Notes | Adverse Events |
|--------------------------|---------------|-----------------|-------|---------------|
| **Vecuronium** | Bolus: 0.08–0.1 mg/kg; Maintenance: 0.8–1.7 mcg/kg/min; Duration: 45 min | Metabolism: Kidney, liver | Shorter acting than rocuronium | Prolonged paralysis, myopathy; requires higher levels of sedation |
| **Rocuronium** | Bolus: 0.6–1.0 mg/kg; Maintenance: 4–12 mcg/kg/min; Onset: 60–90 sec; Duration: 25–60 min | — | Used for RSI given quick onset | — |
| **Cisatracurium (Nimbex)** | Bolus: 0.1–0.2 mg/kg; Maintenance: 0.5–10 mcg/kg/min; Duration: 45–60 min | Metabolism: Hoffman degradation (immediate metabolization in serum without reliance on organs) | Expensive and not always readily available | — |

## Vasopressors and Inotropes

### Vasopressors

| Generic Name (Trade Name) | Mechanism | Clinical Effects | Usage | Typical Dose | Side Effects |
|-------------------------|-----------|-----------------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Epinephrine ("Epi")** | α1, α2, β1 (low dose), β2 | ↑HR, ↑SVR, ↑CO | Shock (cardiogenic, distributive), ACLS/cardiac arrest, bronchospasm, anaphylactic | ACLS: 1 mg IV q3–5 min; Anaphylactic: 0.1 mg IV or 0.3 mg IM; Shock maintenance: 0.1–0.5 mcg/kg/min | Arrhythmias, cardiac ischemia, intracranial hemorrhage |
| **Norepinephrine (Levophed, "Levo")** | α1 and β1 agonist | ↑SVR, ↑CO | First-choice pressor for shock: septic/distributive, cardiogenic, mixed | 1–30 mcg/min or weight-based dosing | Arrhythmias, digital ischemia |
| **Vasopressin (Pitressin, "Vaso")** | V1 and V2 receptors | ↑SVR, limited effect on PVR | Septic shock (second line), VT/VF/PEA arrest, shock in pulmonary HTN | 0.01–0.04 units/min; Max: 0.07 units/min | Coronary and peripheral vasoconstriction, ischemia, arrhythmias, skin necrosis |
| **Phenylephrine (Neo-Synephrine, "Neo")** | α1 agonist | ↑SVR | Distributive shock, useful in tachyarrhythmias | 20–200 mcg/min or 0.5–6 mcg/kg/min | Renal or peripheral vasoconstriction, reflex bradycardia, metabolic acidosis, skin necrosis |
| **Dopamine** | Low dose (1–2 mcg/kg/min): D1 agonist; Medium dose (5–20 mcg/kg/min): β1 > D1; High dose: α1 > β1 > D1 | ↑CO, (low dose) natriuretic effect | Bradycardia and cardiogenic shock, can augment diuresis | Initial: 2–5 mcg/kg/min; Max: 20 mcg/kg/min | Tachycardia and arrhythmias at higher doses, hyperglycemia |
