# Approaches

## Approach to Analysis of an Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)

Evaluating an ABG requires a different approach based on the primary derangement, with up to four steps:

1. Evaluate pH
2. Evaluate pCO₂
3. Evaluate anion gap and for a concomitant process (if appropriate)
4. Check for compensation

> **Notes:**
> - Normal anion gap is patient-specific, approximated by the formula: expected AG = 3 × albumin (normal AG is often said to be 12)
> - The body never overcompensates; the pH will never normalize in the absence of another primary disturbance
> - Respiratory compensation occurs more quickly based on changes in minute ventilation (RR × Vt); metabolic compensation relies on the kidneys and takes longer
> - **Metabolic acidosis compensation:** Compare measured pCO₂ to expected pCO₂ based on Winter's formula. If measured pCO₂ is greater than expected, there is an additional respiratory acidosis. If measured pCO₂ is less than expected, there is an additional respiratory alkalosis.

## Approach to Managing a Patient with Cardiac Arrest ("Code Blue")

### Three Key Principles

1. Confirm code status
2. Provide high-quality chest compressions with minimal interruptions
3. Determine whether the patient has a "shockable" rhythm (VT/VF) and shock EARLY if possible

### Approach to Code Leadership

The approach varies by provider; one approach considers three phases:

1. **Initial phase:** Setting up conditions for success (backboard, access, etc.) and establishing a rhythm
2. **Second phase:** Establishing the etiology of the arrest
3. **Post-arrest phase**

#### Phase 1: Initial Management and Establishing a Cardiac Rhythm

- **Introduce yourself clearly:** "My name is _____ and I am the code leader."
- **Gather crucial information:** "Does the patient have a pulse? What is their code status?"
- **Identify the team and delegate tasks:** Chest compressions, airway management, IV access, critical diagnostics
- **Designate:**
  - "Code whisperer" to assist code leader and crowd control
  - Timekeeper/medication recorder
  - Person to oversee medication administration (often a clinical pharmacist)

**Chest compressions:**
- Place backboard and initiate chest compressions right away
- Depth: 2 inches (~5 cm); rate: 100–120 compressions per minute
- Allow full chest recoil to promote venous return
- Rotate providers for subsequent rounds to minimize fatigue
- Minimize interruptions

**ECG/pads:** When the patient is on monitor, pause compressions briefly to do a rhythm check. If V-fib/V-tach is detected, set up to perform an early shock per ACLS algorithms.

**Airway management:**
- Identify who is in charge of the airway (often an anesthesiologist)
- Determine if advanced airway management is needed
- Avoid overventilating the patient (increases intrathoracic pressure, reduces preload)
- Goal: 10 breaths per minute per ACLS algorithm with an advanced airway in place

**Access:** Obtain IV access; if unable, obtain intraosseous (IO) access.

**Medications:** Give medications per ACLS algorithm:
- For PEA or V-fib/V-tach: Give 1 mg epinephrine right away, then every 3–5 minutes
- For V-fib/V-tach: Also give amiodarone
- Consider fluid bolus unless concern for cardiogenic shock
- Consider 1 gram calcium chloride, 2–3 amps bicarb, 1 gram magnesium sulfate, 1 amp D50

**Managing the room:** Summarize out loud frequently: Review patient one-liner, ACLS algorithm being used.

#### Phase 2: Diagnostics and Determining Etiology of Arrest

- **Use the knowledge in the room**
- **Talk through "Hs/Ts"** for PEA arrest (Table 3.2), invite input from others

### Differential Diagnosis for PEA and V-fib/V-tach Arrests

| PEA Arrest | V-fib/V-tach Arrest |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Myocardial infarction (ACS) | Arrhythmia |
| Cardiomyopathy | Scar/ischemia-based |
| Bradycardic arrest | Torsade de pointes |
| **5 Hs** | **5 Ts** |
| 1. Hypovolemia | 1. Tension pneumothorax |
| 2. Hypoxia | 2. Tamponade |
| 3. Hydrogen ions (acidosis) | 3. Toxins |
| 4. Hypo/hyperkalemia | 4. Thrombosis (PE) |
| 5. Hypothermia | 5. Thrombosis (ACS) |

**End tidal CO₂ (ETCO₂):**
- If EtCO₂ >10–20 mmHg, chest compressions are adequate
- If EtCO₂ <10 mmHg, assess if chest compressions can be improved and/or consider reasons for poor ventilation and V/Q mismatch (e.g., PE)
- If ROSC is achieved, a sudden rise to >35–45 mmHg will typically occur

**POCUS:** Can be used to identify certain etiologies such as tamponade or pneumothorax; presence of right heart strain can suggest PE.

**Labs:** ABG with electrolytes/lactate, CBC, CMP, coags, type and cross, point-of-care glucose, troponin.

**Review existing data:** Ask a colleague to review the patient's chart, check recent labs, ECG for QTc, review telemetry prior to the event, call primary team, and update the patient's family.

#### Phase 3: Post-Arrest Care and Debriefing

**Post-arrest care:**
- **Determine if ROSC obtained:** Return of pulse and/or sudden rise of EtCO₂ to >35–45 mmHg
- **Assess mental status:** If not responsive, intubate to protect airway and consider targeted temperature management (TTM); if completely responsive, do not need to cool; if any doubt, consult neurology
- **Assess hemodynamics:** Give vasopressors and/or fluids to maintain MAP goal (often >75 mmHg due to malfunctioning cerebral autoregulatory mechanisms)
- **Etiology of low MAP:** Post-arrest vasodilation from cytokine storm, transient reduction in cardiac function, possibly infectious
- **Ensure adequate sedation and pain management**

**Pulmonary optimization:**
- Normocapnia with goal PCO₂ 35–45 mmHg
- Normoxia (SpO₂ 92–96% or PaO₂ 80–150 mmHg)

**Obtain diagnostics:**
- **Labs:** POC glucose, CBC, CMP, coags, lactate, troponin, cultures, urine tox
- **Other studies:** ECG, CXR, POCUS, consider CT scans (NCHCT, CTPE, CTAP)

**Determine disposition:** ICU for most patients; cardiac catheterization lab if concern for STEMI or other primary cardiac etiologies.

**Notify:** Family, next of kin, or surrogate decision makers; attending(s). Write a code note.

**Post-code debrief:** Ask for feedback.

### Targeted Temperature Management (TTM)

- Variable institutional practices regarding goal temperature (33°C vs. 36°C given ongoing studies)
- Goal of avoiding hyperthermia is a major driver regardless of temperature goal
- **Protocol:** Cool for 24 hours (from initiation), then at hour 24 gradually increase temperature to 37°C with passive rewarming, then maintain normothermia thereafter
- **Complications of cooling and rewarming:** Hemodynamic changes, electrolyte shifts, coagulopathy

**Methods:**
- Many institutions use internal cooling catheters
- External cooling: ice packs, cooling blankets, NG lavage, IV saline methods
- Antipyretics are useful to prevent shivering and to prevent fevers during rewarming
- Adequate sedation and sometimes paralysis are needed to prevent shivering
