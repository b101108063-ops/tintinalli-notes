## 1. How to Write a History and Physical (H&P)

Early in training, writing an H&P can sometimes seem overwhelming. Developing a consistent approach, particularly to the History of Present Illness (HPI), can help reduce the time required for documentation and serves as a useful mental checklist during the patient encounter to ensure all necessary information is collected.

### 1.1 Structure of the Admission Note

The admission note for Internal Medicine is organized as follows:

| Order | Section |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Chief complaint (or chief concern) |
| 2 | History of present illness (HPI) |
| 3 | Past medical history (PMH) |
| 4 | Past surgical history (PSH) |
| 5 | Medications |
| 6 | Allergies |
| 7 | Family history (FHx) |
| 8 | Social history (SoHx) and health-related behaviors (HRB) |
| 9 | Physical exam (PE) |
| 10 | Labs/data |
| 11 | Problem representation |
| 12 | Assessment & Plan (A&P) |

The **problem representation** is a description of the patient's presentation that highlights the defining features of a case, helping the clinician to summarize their thoughts and create a differential diagnosis. The **Assessment & Plan (A&P)** is the most important part of the H&P and the oral presentation, as it demonstrates the ability to synthesize the history and physical, discuss a differential diagnosis, and formulate a care plan.

---

### 1.2 Chief Complaint and History of Present Illness

The chief complaint is traditionally described using the patient's own words.

**Example:**
> **CC:** "I'm short of breath"

The one-liner should include pertinent prior diagnoses and key information to characterize disease severity such as stage of cancer, most recent echocardiogram result, or key medications.

**Example:**
> **HPI:** The patient is a 76-year-old man with a history of hypertension (HTN), hyperlipidemia (HLD), and heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF with LVEF **30%** in **02/2018**), who presents with dyspnea for one week.

#### First Paragraph of the HPI

The first paragraph of the HPI can be scaffolded as follows:
> At baseline, [baseline function]. He was in his usual state of health until [number of days prior to admission] at which time he noted onset of [acuity] [symptom], with associated [other symptoms]. [Course over following days]. On the day of admission, [reason for coming to hospital].

**Example:**
> At baseline, the patient can walk 6–8 blocks before developing dyspnea and sleeps with a single pillow. He was in his usual state of health until 8 days prior to presentation, at which time he noted onset of slow, progressive swelling in his lower extremities with associated 4 lb increase in his weight. In the following 2–3 days, the patient developed onset of a cough productive of frothy sputum, worsening lower extremity swelling, dyspnea on exertion, and a two-pillow orthopnea. He attempted to double his home dose of furosemide without improvement in his symptoms. On the day of admission, the patient noted his dyspnea occurring after walking 15–20 feet, prompting him to activate EMS.

#### Second Paragraph of the HPI

The second paragraph of the HPI includes pertinent negatives from the Review of Systems (ROS) as well as historical information about etiology if the condition has a trigger such as an episode of acute decompensated heart failure.

**Example:**
> The patient denies chest pain or diaphoresis. He denies fevers, chills, or sputum production. He denies recent sick contacts. He endorses having access to all his medications. He has attempted to eat as recommended by his cardiologist but notes that a neighbor had recently brought him a casserole which "tasted salty" and that he has been eating it intermittently throughout the week.

#### Third Paragraph of the HPI

The third paragraph of the HPI includes the Emergency Department (ED) course. Common elements are triage vital signs, key initial findings, and what the ED has already done.

**Example:**
> In the ED, triage vital signs were T 37.2°C, HR 90, BP **90/60** mmHg, RR 22, SpO2 **90%** on room air. Initial workup revealed Cr 1.4, BNP 1600. Troponin and ECG were unremarkable, and CXR was consistent with pulmonary edema. The patient was given furosemide 40 mg IV and admitted to cardiology for management of suspected acute decompensated heart failure (ADHF).

#### Fourth Paragraph of the HPI

The fourth paragraph of the HPI provides a deep dive into the past medical history and can be started with, "In regards to his [organ system or pathology] history…"

**Example:**
> In regards to his cardiac history, the patient was diagnosed with HFrEF in 2018, at which time he was admitted to this hospital for dyspnea on exertion. At that time, TTE revealed LVEF **30%** with wall motion abnormalities, and cardiac catheterization performed that admission revealed stenosis of the LAD (**90%**) and LCx (**80%**).

### 1.3 Additional History

#### Past Medical History (PMH)

The past medical history lists all of the patient's medical conditions. Note that all conditions are listed here, whereas in the one-liner only the most pertinent conditions are included.

**Example:**
> **PMH:** HFrEF (LVEF **30%** in **02/2018**), Hypertension, Hyperlipidemia, GERD, Depression

#### Past Surgical History (PSH)

The past surgical history lists all prior surgeries. Careful documentation of prior abdominal surgeries is particularly important when the patient is presenting with abdominal pain.

**Example:**
> **PSH:** Status post cholecystectomy (1994), Status post tonsillectomy (1965)

#### Medications

Medications should be listed with doses and frequency of each medication. Grouping medications by organ system can help organize the list.

**Example:**
> **Medications:** Furosemide 40 mg PO daily, Metoprolol succinate 25 mg PO daily, Lisinopril 5 mg PO daily, Atorvastatin 40 mg PO daily, Omeprazole 40 mg PO daily, Fluoxetine 10 mg PO daily

#### Allergies

Allergies should be listed with the reaction that occurred when possible, as some allergies are more severe than others.

**Example:**
> **Allergies:** Penicillins (rash as a child; no recent rechallenge)

#### Family History (FHx)

Family history documents the medical history of first-degree relatives, starting with what is most relevant to the case. If possible, indicate the age at diagnosis for certain medical conditions.

**Example:**
> **FHx:** Father with coronary artery disease (diagnosed in his 50s), Mother with hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus, Paternal grandfather with coronary artery disease and heart failure, Son with colon cancer (diagnosed in his 40s)

#### Social History (SoHx)

The social history includes information about the patient as an individual, their unique life story, and relevant health-related behaviors (HRB). For elderly patients, indicate whether they are independent in activities of daily living (ADLs) and/or instrumental activities of daily living (iADLs).

ADLs include feeding, continence, transferring, toileting, dressing, and bathing. iADLs include using the telephone, shopping, preparing food, housekeeping, doing laundry, using transportation, handling medications, and handling finances.

**Example:**
> **SoHx:** Born in San Francisco, has lived there his whole life. Lives in the Sunset District with his husband. Previously worked as a math teacher, retired for 15 years. Never smoker. 1–2 alcoholic drinks per week. No other drug use. Enjoys hiking, sailing, and reading. At baseline, completely ambulatory and independent in ADLs and iADLs.

### 1.4 Physical Exam, Labs, and Other Data

#### Physical Exam

The physical exam should include vital signs and focus on organ systems relevant to the patient's presentation. Documenting a baseline neurologic exam for all patients is helpful, particularly for elderly patients who are at higher risk for hospital-induced delirium.

**Example:**
> **PE:** T 37.2°C, HR 90, BP **90/60 → 100/65** mmHg, RR 22, SpO2 **90%** RA → **94%** on 2 L O₂. General: Elderly man in moderate distress, able to speak in short sentences only. HEENT: NCAT, moist mucous membranes. CV: RRR, no murmur, rubs, or gallops. Pulm: Crackles at lung bases bilaterally. Abd: Soft, NT, ND, no hepatosplenomegaly. Extrem: 2+ pitting edema to the knees. Skin: No rashes. Neuro: A&Ox3, alert and conversant, grossly intact.

#### Labs and Data

Labs, imaging, and microbiology should be presented. For abnormal labs, include trends such as for hemoglobin, platelets, and creatinine.

**Example:**
> **Labs/Data:** WBC 9.0, Hg 12, Plt 250. Na 134, K 3.9, Cl 105, HCO₃ 25, BUN 34, Cr 1.4 (from baseline 1.0). BNP 1600 (previous admissions in 800 range). Troponin <0.02. ECG: normal sinus rhythm. CXR: bilateral small pleural effusions and patchy opacities consistent with pulmonary edema.

### 1.5 The Assessment and Plan

The Assessment and Plan (A&P) demonstrates the ability to synthesize the history and physical exam, discuss a differential diagnosis, and come up with a plan for patient care.

The problem representation is a description of the patient's presentation that highlights the defining features of a case, helping the clinician to summarize their thoughts and create a differential diagnosis. The first problem listed should always be the most important or critical issue, which is the indication for admission.

One way to organize the paragraph is as follows:
1. Sentence 1 (if needed): Summarize presenting symptom and severity.
2. Sentence 2: His hypoxia is most likely due to X because… (also identify a suspected trigger for the condition).
3. Sentence 3: Discuss other conditions on the differential diagnosis and why they seem less likely.

Organize the "to-do's" into diagnostic versus therapeutic groups. Add other problems based on symptoms, vital sign abnormalities, physical exam findings, lab or imaging abnormalities, and chronic medication conditions.

The inpatient bundle should include DVT prophylaxis, diet, IV access, and code status. Indicate who discussed code status with the patient and when.

**Example:**
> **A&P:** 76-year-old man with a history of HTN, HLD, and HFrEF (LVEF **30%** in **02/2018**) who presents with one week of dyspnea, found to have evidence of volume overload concerning for heart failure exacerbation.
>
> **#1 Dyspnea / HFrEF, acute on chronic with exacerbation:** The patient presented with 1 week of dyspnea requiring 2 L O₂ on admission. His hypoxia is most likely due to an exacerbation of his heart failure given paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, orthopnea, pitting edema, pulmonary edema on CXR, and elevated BNP. His heart failure was likely triggered by dietary changes. Hypoxia is less likely due to ACS given normal ECG and negative troponin, and there is no evidence of infection given he is afebrile without leukocytosis.
>
> *Diagnostics:* Follow up final read of CXR (prelim: pulmonary edema), follow up respiratory viral panel.
>
> *Treatment:* Admit to floor. Status post 40 mg IV furosemide in the ED; given an additional 40 mg IV this morning. Neurohormonal blockade: continue home metoprolol succinate 25 mg PO daily, lisinopril 5 mg PO daily. Strict ins/outs and daily weights.
>
> **#2 Acute kidney injury:** On admission, Cr 1.4 up from baseline 1.0. Suspect prerenal due to heart failure with poor forward flow. Reassuring that Cr improving status post diuresis to 1.2 today. Continue diuresis as above. Continue to monitor Cr. Avoid nephrotoxins.
>
> **#3 Hypertension:** Continue home metoprolol succinate 25 mg PO daily and lisinopril 5 mg PO daily.
>
> **#4 Hyperlipidemia:** Continue home atorvastatin 40 mg PO daily.
>
> **#5 GERD:** Continue home omeprazole 40 mg PO daily.
>
> **#6 Depression:** Continue home fluoxetine 10 mg PO daily.
>
> **#7 Inpatient bundle:** Telemetry: Yes, during IV diuresis. Continuous pulse oximetry: Yes. Diet: Cardiac diet. Access: PIVs. Code status: Full code (confirmed with patient on admission).

## 2. How to Call a Consult

### 2.1 Consult Question

Consults should be structured around a specific question regarding a patient's care.

**Examples:**
- "Should this patient with melena undergo inpatient endoscopic evaluation?"
- "Should this patient with suspected diffuse alveolar hemorrhage (DAH) in the presence of rheumatologic disease receive high-dose steroids?"

### 2.2 Consult Timing

Practice patterns differ across institutions, however when possible, non-urgent consults should generally be made early in the day to allow consulting teams to plan their day's workflow.

### 2.3 Consult Template

A consult template includes the following elements:

| Element | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| Label | Label as a new consult |
| One-liner | Abbreviated one-liner |
| Question | Specific consult question (indicate urgency if needed) |
| Contact info | Name, role on team, and multiple ways to call back (extension, telephone, pager) |

**Example page:**
> **CONSULT:** 55M h/o osteoarthritis (on home NSAIDs, no PPI) presenting with melena for 4 days. VSS, Hg 6 from baseline 10. Should this patient undergo inpatient colonoscopy?
>
> Name, Medicine intern, *x: 33333, *t: 9999999999, *p: 4444444

## 3. How to Preround

Prerounding refers to the activity of gathering data prior to rounds, typically including 24-hour and overnight events, nursing updates and concerns, the patient's subjective report, vital signs, physical exam, labs, and other test results. Gathering this data allows the clinician to formulate a plan for each patient to present on rounds.

---

## 4. Daily Presentations and Notes

Daily presentations about each patient are typically presented in a **SOAP** note format.

| Section | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **S (Subjective)** | What are the patient's symptoms today? |
| **O (Objective)** | Vitals, physical exam, ins/outs (if relevant), labs, new micro and imaging |
| **A/P (Assessment and Plan)** | Ensure the patient one-liner is up to date. Present by problem, for example: "Problem 1: Sepsis, likely from a pulmonary source; Problem 2: Diabetes." |

## 5. Preparing a Patient for Discharge

### 5.1 Is the Patient Ready for Discharge?

Discharges from the hospital are a transition that creates risk for breakdown in a patient's care. Key considerations include the following:

| Question | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- |
| Are all medications being administered PO? | IV medications often require special access and planning |
| Is the patient able to nourish themselves? | May require G-tube feeding or other arrangements |
| Has the patient passed stool in the last 24 hours? | Relevant depending on admission indication |
| Is the patient ambulating and/or at their physical baseline? | If not, physical therapy assessment may be required |
| Does the patient have a ride home from the hospital? | Transportation planning is essential |
| Is the patient or family comfortable with any new devices or equipment? | Training may be needed |

### 5.2 Discharge Checklist

| Item | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| □ Follow-up appointment(s) | Booked or requested |
| □ Discharge services | Ordered (e.g., home health services) |
| □ Medication reconciliation | Confirm pharmacy received prescriptions and patient can access and afford medications |
| □ Communication | With multidisciplinary team and primary care provider |
| □ Discharge summary | Completed |
| □ Huddle with bedside nurse | Follow-up plans, RN teaching on new devices or equipment, medication changes to highlight |
