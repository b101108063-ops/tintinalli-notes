# Diseases

## Esophagus

### Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

**Pathophysiology:** Decreased lower esophageal sphincter (LES) tone; may also have decreased esophageal motility, gastric outlet obstruction, and/or hiatal hernia.

**Clinical features:** Heartburn (burning sensation in retrosternal area), regurgitation (perception of refluxed gastric content flowing up into the mouth or hypopharynx).

**Diagnosis:** Treat empirically. Perform EGD and/or ambulatory pH monitoring in patients with refractory or alarm symptoms (new-onset GERD at age >60 yr, dysphagia/odynophagia, weight loss, anorexia, GIB or iron-deficiency anemia, persistent vomiting, or GI malignancy in a first-degree relative).

**Treatment:**
- **Mild:** Lifestyle modifications (weight loss, eliminate dietary triggers, avoid lying down after eating, use wedge pillow)
- **Moderate:** Add an H2 blocker
- **Severe:** Switch to a PPI

**PPI side effects:** Increased risk of C. difficile infection, osteoporosis, vitamin B12 deficiency. Consider long-term PPI in patients with refractory symptoms, recurrent GI bleeds, Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, Barrett's esophagus, severe esophagitis.

**Refractory GERD:** If no improvement with empiric treatment after 8 weeks, recommend EGD.

**Complications:** Erosive esophagitis, stricture, Barrett's esophagus, increased risk of esophageal cancer.

---

### Barrett's Esophagus

**Pathophysiology:** Normal squamous epithelium changes to columnar epithelium with goblet cells (intestinal metaplasia) due to repeated acid exposure from GERD. Can progress to esophageal adenocarcinoma.

**Diagnosis:** Consider screening in patients with multiple risk factors (male sex, older age, white race, chronic GERD, obesity, hiatal hernia, smoking, first-degree relative with GI malignancy).

**Treatment:**
- PPI indefinitely
- If no dysplasia: Surveillance EGD every 3–5 years
- If low- or high-grade dysplasia: Remove with radiofrequency ablation, photodynamic therapy, cryotherapy, or endoscopic resection
- Repeat endoscopy for surveillance within 3–12 months (timing based on severity and whether dysplasia was eradicated)

---

### Esophageal Motility Disorders

**Classification:** First differentiate oropharyngeal dysphagia (difficulty initiating swallow) vs. esophageal dysphagia (sensation of obstruction several seconds after swallowing).

**Clinical features:**
- **Dysphagia with solids only:** Often mechanical obstruction
  - **Intermittent:** Esophageal rings or webs (Plummer-Vinson syndrome, Schatzki's ring), eosinophilic esophagitis
  - **Progressive with chronic heartburn/GERD:** Peptic stricture
  - **Progressive, age >50 yr, weight loss:** Esophageal carcinoma, Zenker's diverticula
- **Dysphagia with solids and liquids:** Often motility problem
  - **Intermittent:** Esophageal rings
  - **Intermittent with chest pain:** Diffuse esophageal spasm (DES)
  - **Progressive with heartburn/GERD and skin tightening:** Scleroderma
  - **Progressive with regurgitation of food and saliva, weight loss:** Achalasia

**Diagnosis:** Barium swallow; perform EGD first if history of radiation, caustic injury, esophageal cancer, or stricture to avoid perforation risk; EGD to detect structural abnormalities; esophageal manometry if barium swallow and EGD are normal.

**Treatment:** EGD with dilation of strictures and rings; DES: first-line PPI, second-line calcium channel blocker, third-line botulinum injection; Achalasia: first-line dilation or surgical/endoscopic myotomy, second-line botulinum injection, third-line nitrates.

---

### Esophagitis

- **Infectious:** Candida albicans (treatment: fluconazole or itraconazole); HSV (treatment: acyclovir); CMV (treatment: ganciclovir)
- **Medication-induced:** Occurs several hours after taking oral medications (e.g., alendronate, doxycycline, aspirin); stop offending medication
- **Eosinophilic esophagitis:** Typical presentation is an atopic man in mid-30s with dysphagia; diagnosis requires eosinophils on biopsy plus exclusion of other causes; treatment: PPI ± topical steroids

---

### Esophageal Hiatal Hernia

**Sliding hiatal hernias (Type I):** Most common (>95%); GE junction displaced above diaphragm; typically benign; associated with increased GERD risk; treat only if symptomatic.

**Paraesophageal hiatal hernia (Types II–IV):** Less common (<5%). Type II: gastric fundus herniates through diaphragmatic defect while GE junction remains below diaphragm; Type III has features of both I and II; Type IV: defect large enough for other organs (colon, spleen, pancreas, small intestine) to protrude. Complications: volvulus, strangulation, obstruction. Treatment: elective surgical repair if symptomatic; emergent surgical repair if volvulus, uncontrolled bleeding, obstruction, strangulation, or respiratory compromise.

---

### Other Esophageal Conditions

- **Mallory-Weiss tear:** Forceful vomiting causes upper GI mucosal tear; classic history is initial non-bloody emesis followed by hematemesis; treatment: EGD with thermal coagulation, Hemoclips, or band ligation
- **Boerhaave syndrome:** Forceful vomiting causes esophageal transmural tear; presents with chest pain, odynophagia, fever, subcutaneous emphysema, possible shock; diagnosis: contrast esophagram (water-soluble gastrografin) or CT; treatment: admit to ICU, NPO, IV PPI/antibiotics, drainage of fluid collection; requires thoracic surgery if clinical deterioration or certain complications

## Stomach

### Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

**Pathophysiology:** Ulcers in the stomach or duodenum; common risk factors include H. pylori infection and NSAID use.

**Clinical features:** Asymptomatic (70%), abdominal pain, bleeding. Gastric ulcer: NSAIDs, H. pylori, high malignant potential (always biopsy), eating does NOT help pain. Duodenal ulcer: H. pylori (90%), low malignant potential (biopsy not needed), eating relieves pain.

**Diagnosis:**
- **Endoscopy:** High-risk gastric ulcers and duodenal ulcers require follow-up; high-risk patients (gastric ulcers with inadequate biopsy) require repeat endoscopy in 8–12 weeks
- **H. pylori testing:**
  - **IgG antibody test:** Positive if any prior exposure (cannot differentiate active vs. prior); negative result is helpful; no role if known prior infection
  - **Urea breath test:** Identifies active infection; costly; used to confirm eradication (~4 weeks after treatment) or reinfection; less accurate with PPI, bismuth, antibiotics, or during active PUD bleeding
  - **Fecal antigen test:** Identifies active infection; less expensive; same limitations as urea breath test
  - **EGD with biopsy:** Gold standard; less accurate with PPI, bismuth, antibiotics, or during active UGIB

**Treatment:**
- **Supportive care:** Stop ASA, NSAIDs; restrict alcohol; smoking cessation
- **Acid suppression:** PPIs (first-line); H2 blockers, antacids
- **H. pylori eradication:** Guidelines recommend triple therapy only in regions where clarithromycin resistance is known to be <15% and in patients with no prior macrolide exposure; quadruple therapy is increasingly first-line for most patients

**Triple therapy:** PPI BID + clarithromycin 500 mg BID + amoxicillin 1000 mg BID (or metronidazole 500 mg BID) × 14 days

**Quadruple therapy:** PPI BID + bismuth 420 mg QID + metronidazole 500 mg QID + tetracycline 500 mg QID × 10–14 days

**Note:** H. pylori infection is also associated with gastric MALT lymphoma; first-line treatment is H. pylori eradication, followed by serial endoscopies.

---

### Gastritis

- **Atrophic gastritis:** (1) H. pylori-associated (treat H. pylori); (2) Autoimmune (not curable; leads to pernicious anemia requiring lifelong B12 replacement)
- **Metaplastic (chronic) atrophic gastritis:** Precancerous lesion associated with H. pylori; treat H. pylori; risk factor for adenocarcinoma
- **Eosinophilic gastritis/gastroenteritis:** Rare; eosinophilic infiltration of stomach and/or duodenum; treatment: elimination diet, steroids
- **Lymphocytic gastritis:** Rare, benign; associated with H. pylori and Celiac disease; treat H. pylori

---

### Gastroparesis

**Clinical features:** Delayed stomach emptying; early satiety, postprandial fullness, nausea/vomiting, pain, bloating, weight loss.

**Diagnosis:** Perform EGD and upper GI barium radiography first to rule out obstruction; then perform gastric emptying study while off all medications that affect motility. Evaluate for cause: HgA1c, TSH, ± ANA or additional testing based on context.

**Treatment:** Dietary changes, diabetes control, metoclopramide (side effects: parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia), erythromycin, prucalopride (off-label). For refractory symptoms, consider PEG placement for decompression or gastric pacemaker (diabetic gastroparesis).

---

### Gastric Outlet Obstruction

**Pathophysiology:** Obstruction of the gastric outlet by malignancy, PUD, strictures, or caustic injury.

**Clinical features:** Nausea/vomiting, epigastric pain, early satiety; "succussion splash" on exam.

**Treatment:** Depends on cause; may require nasogastric tube, PPI, endoscopic dilation, stenting, or surgery.

---

### Bariatric Surgery

**Indication:** Can be offered to patients with BMI >35 despite diet/exercise and lifestyle interventions.

**Potential complications:**
- **Restrictive:** Band slippage, staple-line leakage
- **Malabsorptive:** Cholelithiasis, nephrolithiasis (increased urine oxalate excretion), dumping syndrome, short bowel syndrome, SBO, fistulas, stricture
- **Nutritional:** Micronutrient deficiencies
- **Bleeding-related:** Marginal ulcers

## Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

### Features of Ulcerative Colitis vs. Crohn's Disease

| Feature | Ulcerative Colitis (UC) | Crohn's Disease |
|---------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Age of onset** | Bimodal: 15–30 yr or 60–80 yr | — |
| **Tobacco** | Decreases risk (may flare when stop smoking) | Increases risk |
| **Location/Pathology** | Rectal involvement in all cases; ascends contiguously; mucosal/submucosal; crypt abscesses | Entire GI tract mouth to anus with "skip" lesions; ileal involvement >50%; transmural; noncaseating granulomas |
| **GI manifestations** | Bloody diarrhea, tenesmus | Diarrhea (± bloody), crampy abdominal pain |
| **Extraintestinal manifestations** | Uveitis, episcleritis; ankylosing spondylitis (30-fold increased risk); pyoderma gangrenosum (parallels bowel disease activity in ~50%); erythema nodosum; PSC (90% of PSC patients have underlying UC, but only 5% of UC patients have PSC) | Same extraintestinal manifestations as UC |
| **Complications** | Markedly increased risk of colon cancer, hemorrhage, toxic megacolon | Fistulas, abscesses, obstruction, cholelithiasis, nephrolithiasis; slight increased risk of colon cancer |
| **Surgery** | Curative | For complications (e.g., stricture) |

### IBD Workup

**History:** Rule out infection first; travel and dietary history, recent antibiotic use, immunocompromised risk factors; extraintestinal manifestations; quantify diarrhea and ask about tenesmus; family history of autoimmune disease or IBD.

**Diagnosis:**
- **General:** CBC with differential, BMP, INR
- **Rule out infection:** Bacterial stool culture, Shiga toxin, E. coli O157, C. difficile, Giardia, O&P; viral colitis (CMV) will not be evident on culture
- **Inflammatory markers:** ESR, CRP, fecal calprotectin (check at baseline when asymptomatic, then again if flare is suspected)
- **Imaging:** Consider abdominal plain film before colonoscopy in inpatient setting; CT abdomen/pelvis for patients with severe pain (toxic megacolon, perforation, intra-abdominal abscess, bowel obstruction)
- **Colonoscopy with biopsies:** Always indicated for new diagnosis of IBD

**Treatment:**
- **Induction therapy:** Steroids (prednisone, budesonide) — only for induction, not maintenance
- **Maintenance therapy:**
  - Mild/moderate: 5-ASA, sulfasalazine
  - Severe: TNF-alpha inhibitor (infliximab, adalimumab), thiopurines (azathioprine, 6-MP), methotrexate, anti-integrin (vedolizumab), IL-12/23 inhibitor (ustekinumab), JAK inhibitor (tofacitinib)
- **IBD flare:** Depends on severity; for severe flares, consider hospital admission; rule out infection (stool culture, C. difficile testing, O&P, fecal calprotectin); prepare for rescue therapy (PPD, hepatitis serologies, cocci testing if risk factors, check antibiotic levels to biologics if prior exposure); initiate IV corticosteroids; use symptoms, stool count/quality, and inflammatory markers to predict corticosteroid failure; if high probability, consider surgical consultation and rescue therapy
- **Other aspects of care:** Nutrition workup/consult; VTE prophylaxis (highly inflammatory state is prothrombotic); control pain with APAP or tramadol (avoid opiates unless perioperative)
- **Surveillance:** Patients with UC and Crohn's colitis have increased colorectal cancer risk; begin screening colonoscopies 8–10 years after initial diagnosis, then every 1–2 years

## Malabsorption

### Celiac Disease

**Pathophysiology:** Immune-mediated inflammatory disease of the small intestine resulting from sensitivity to gluten (wheat) and gluten-related proteins.

**Clinical features:** Steatorrhea (bulky, floating, foul-smelling stool), malabsorption (weight loss, nutritional deficiency), associated with dermatitis herpetiformis (papules and vesicles on forearms, knees, scalp, buttocks).

**Diagnosis:** IgA tissue transglutaminase (tTG) antibody AND duodenal biopsies with blunted villi while consuming gluten.

**Treatment:** Gluten-free diet.

---

### Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO)

**Pathophysiology:** Overcolonization of the small bowel with microbes; most common with motility disorders and chronic pancreatitis.

**Clinical features:** Watery diarrhea, bloating, flatulence, micronutrient deficiency; nutritional deficiencies can be severe (B12 deficiency, hypocalcemia) in jejunoileal bypass or short bowel syndrome.

**Diagnosis:** Positive carbohydrate breath test or endoscopy with jejunal aspirate >10³ organisms; may initiate treatment empirically.

**Treatment:** Rifaximin × 14 days; correct micronutrient deficiencies.

---

### Short Bowel Syndrome

**Pathophysiology:** Dysregulation of electrolytes, fluids, and proteins/nutrients due to resection or disease of a large portion of the small intestine, typically with <2 meters remaining.

**Clinical features:** Diarrhea, bloating, weight loss.

**Treatment:** Diet high in complex carbs, modest in fat and oxalate; oral rehydration; antidiarrheals.

---

### Carbohydrate Malabsorption

**Pathophysiology:** Lactose malabsorption is most common; fructose malabsorption can also occur.

**Treatment:** Dietary restriction, lactase supplementation.

---

### Whipple's Disease

**Pathophysiology:** Malabsorptive diarrheal disease caused by Tropheryma whipplei.

**Clinical features:** Chronic diarrhea, malabsorption, abdominal discomfort, weight loss, arthralgias; rarely CNS involvement, skin hyperpigmentation, endocarditis.

**Diagnosis:** Small bowel biopsy with PAS stain and PCR for T. whipplei; may also obtain tissue/fluid from extraintestinal site (CSF, vitreous fluid, cardiac valve, peripheral blood).

**Treatment:** Ceftriaxone or penicillin for 2–4 weeks, followed by TMP-SMX for 1 year.

---

### Vitamin Deficiencies

| Deficiency | Cause | Clinical Features |
|-----------|-------|-----------------|
| **Vitamin A** | Diet/malabsorption | Night blindness, dry skin, immunosuppression |
| **Vitamin B1 (thiamine)** | Heavy alcohol use | Wernicke-Korsakoff (nystagmus, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia), wet beriberi (heart failure), dry beriberi (neuropathy) |
| **Vitamin B3 (niacin)** | Carcinoid syndrome (low tryptophan), Hartnup disease, isoniazid | Pellagra: 3 Ds = Diarrhea, Dermatitis (sun-exposed areas), Dementia + glossitis |
| **Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine)** | Isoniazid, oral contraceptives | Seizures, neuropathy, sideroblastic anemia |
| **Vitamin B9 (folic acid)** | Inadequate dietary intake, pregnancy, medications (methotrexate, phenytoin, sulfonamides), alcohol | Macrocytic anemia |
| **Vitamin B12 (cobalamin)** | Malabsorption (meat/dairy), lack intrinsic factor (pernicious anemia, gastric bypass) | Macrocytic anemia, nerve damage due to abnormal myelination |
| **Vitamin C** | Diet (lack of fruits/vegetables) | Scurvy: swollen gums, bruises, corkscrew hairs, poor wound healing |
| **Vitamin D** | Diet/malabsorption, CKD, lack of sun | Rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults); hypocalcemic tetany |
| **Vitamin E** | Diet/malabsorption | Sensory/motor neuropathy (can mimic B12 deficiency), hemolytic anemia |
| **Vitamin K** | Diet/malabsorption, prolonged antibiotic exposure | Hemorrhage/bleeding (easy bruising, melena, hematuria) |
| **Zinc** | Diet/malabsorption, IBD, cystic fibrosis, sickle cell, liver disease, renal disease | Alopecia, loss of smell, dermatitis, impaired wound healing |

## Other Conditions Affecting the Small Bowel

### Microscopic Colitis

**Epidemiology:** Age of diagnosis 45–65 yr; more common in females.

**Etiology:** Chronic inflammatory disease of the colon with two histologic subtypes: lymphocytic and collagenous colitis. Associated with autoimmune diseases, NSAIDs, tobacco use.

**Clinical features:** Chronic, watery, non-bloody diarrhea.

**Diagnosis:** Colonoscopy with biopsy showing lymphocytic or collagenous colitis; rule out infectious causes and celiac sprue.

**Treatment:** Stop NSAIDs; loperamide for symptoms; budesonide for active disease; second-line: cholestyramine, bismuth, biologics.

---

### Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

**Pathophysiology:** Idiopathic and multifactorial; considered a functional disorder; associated with female sex, depression, anxiety, fibromyalgia.

**Clinical features:** Subtypes are IBS-C (constipation), IBS-D (diarrhea), IBS-M (mixed bowel movements).

**Diagnosis:** Exclude organic etiologies through history and labs (fecal calprotectin, stool studies, celiac disease studies); colonoscopy not typically needed but should be considered for alarm features (age >50 yr, GI bleeding, weight loss, progressive abdominal pain, lab abnormalities, family history of IBD or colon cancer).

**Rome IV criteria:** Abdominal pain at least once per week for ≥3 months with ≥2 symptoms related to defecation, change in stool frequency, change in stool form.

**Treatment:**
- **All subtypes:** Education/reassurance, low FODMAP diet, dicyclomine for abdominal cramping, encapsulated peppermint oil
- **IBS-D:** Loperamide, cholestyramine, 5-HT3 antagonists, rifaximin, TCAs, SNRIs
- **IBS-C:** Polyethylene glycol (MiraLAX), psyllium, prucalopride, plecanatide, lubiprostone, linaclotide, SSRIs (not paroxetine), SNRIs
- **IBS-M:** Fiber supplementation

---

### Mesenteric Ischemia

**Definition:** Ischemia affecting the small intestine is generally referred to as mesenteric ischemia; ischemia affecting the large intestine is referred to as colonic ischemia.

**Acute mesenteric ischemia:**
- **Pathophysiology:** Arterial embolism (50%; e.g., Afib), arterial thrombosis (15–25%; e.g., atherosclerosis), venous thrombosis (5%; e.g., infection, hypercoagulability, malignancy), nonocclusive (20–30%; e.g., hypoperfusion)
- **Clinical features:** Severe abdominal pain out of proportion to physical findings; anorexia, vomiting, mild GI bleed
- **Diagnosis:** CT angiogram
- **Treatment:** IV fluids, anticoagulation, broad-spectrum antibiotics; arterial: early surgical laparotomy with embolectomy; venous: anticoagulation; nonocclusive: treat underlying cause

**Chronic mesenteric ischemia:**
- **Pathophysiology:** Atherosclerosis causes abdominal angina
- **Clinical features:** Dull pain typically starting about one hour after eating; food anxiety with subsequent weight loss
- **Diagnosis:** CT angiogram
- **Treatment:** Antiplatelet therapy, surgical revascularization

---

### Carcinoid Syndrome

**Pathophysiology:** Neuroendocrine tumor that secretes serotonin.

**Clinical features:** (1) Flushing; (2) Diarrhea; (3) Wheezing; (4) Right-sided valvular heart disease (pathognomonic plaquelike deposits of fibrous tissue).

**Diagnosis:** Increased urine 5-HIAA; imaging for tumor localization (CT, somatostatin receptor imaging); EGD/colonoscopy if primary site undetermined.

**Treatment:** Surgical removal of tumor; if not resectable, octreotide injection (long-acting release), telotristat, loperamide.

---

## Diverticular Disease

### Diverticulosis

**Pathophysiology:** Multiple outpouchings of colonic mucosa/submucosa through focal weakness in setting of increased intraluminal pressure, particularly in the sigmoid colon.

**Clinical features:** Asymptomatic or LLQ pain, bloating; more common in elderly and those with low-fiber diet.

**Diagnosis:** CT, colonoscopy.

**Treatment:** High-fiber diet.

---

### Diverticular Bleed

**Pathophysiology:** Painless hematochezia due to exposure of vasa recta as diverticulum herniates.

**Treatment:** Usually self-limited; if bleeding does not stop, perform colonoscopy; consider angiography with embolization if site cannot be identified; surgery is last resort.

---

### Diverticulitis

**Pathophysiology:** Inflammation/focal necrosis due to erosion of diverticular wall by increased intraluminal pressure or inspissated food particles.

**Clinical features:** LLQ pain, fever, leukocytosis.

**Diagnosis:** No imaging needed; consider CT abd/pelvis if concern for complications (abscess, obstruction, fistula, perforation).

**Treatment:** Ciprofloxacin and metronidazole for 7–10 days; recommend colonoscopy 6–8 weeks later to rule out colon cancer if not done recently; do NOT avoid nuts, seeds, popcorn.

---

## Other Diseases Affecting the Colon

### Angiodysplasia

**Clinical features:** Tortuous dilated veins in the colon can rupture and cause a lower GI bleed; classic association is Heyde's syndrome (triad of aortic stenosis, acquired vWF deficiency due to shear stress, GI bleeding from angiodysplasia).

**Diagnosis:** Colonoscopy.

**Treatment:** Bleeding is typically self-limited.

---

### Ischemic Colitis

**Definition:** Ischemia in the mesenteric distribution specifically affecting the large intestine.

**Pathophysiology:** Colonic hypoperfusion in setting of hypotension (e.g., MI, aortoiliac surgery).

**Clinical features:** Sudden abdominal pain, diarrhea, hematochezia.

**Diagnosis:** CT abd/pelvis with contrast; early colonoscopy shows patchy segmental ulcerations.

**Treatment:** Usually supportive care; if severe and necrotic bowel, may need surgical resection.

---

### Colonic Polyps

- **Adenoma:** Premalignant; subtypes are tubular adenoma, tubulovillous adenoma, villous adenoma (most worrisome)
- **Serrated:** Hyperplastic polyp (benign), sessile serrated polyp (premalignant), traditional serrated adenoma (premalignant)
- **Other:** Hamartomatous polyp (variable potential), inflammatory polyp (benign)

After premalignant polyps are removed, recommend closer interval surveillance colonoscopy.

---

### Sigmoid Volvulus

**Pathophysiology:** Loop of sigmoid bowel twists around its own mesentery, causing obstruction and underperfusion.

**Clinical features:** Insidious onset of abdominal pain, nausea, distension, then severe continuous pain and colicky episodes; more common age >70 yr, M>F, patients with comorbid neuropsychiatric diseases.

**Diagnosis:** CT abd/pelvis with "whirl pattern" (dilated sigmoid around mesocolon/vessels) and "bird-beak" appearance; absence of rectal gas, separation of sigmoid walls, and two transition points are suggestive.

**Treatment:** Emergent surgery if peritonitis/perforation; if stable, sigmoidoscopy to diagnose and detorse, followed by surgery.

---

### Cecal Volvulus

**Pathophysiology:** Segment of cecum rotates/torses, causing obstruction and underperfusion; more common in younger women.

**Clinical features:** Presentation varies from insidious abdominal pain/vomiting to abdominal catastrophe.

**Diagnosis:** CT abd/pelvis with "whirl pattern" at the cecum.

**Treatment:** Primarily surgical.

---

### Radiation Proctitis

**Pathophysiology:** Inflammation of the rectum from pelvic radiation (prostate, cervical cancer).

**Clinical features:** **Acute:** Diarrhea, mucus discharge, urgency, tenesmus within 6 weeks of radiation. **Chronic:** Same symptoms >9 months after exposure; chronic can cause bleeding; treat with endoscopic argon plasma coagulation (APC).

**Diagnosis:** Diagnosis of exclusion; endoscopic findings are nonspecific (pallor with friability, telangiectasia); biopsy shows submucosal fibrosis.

**Treatment:** Supportive care, sucralfate enemas, hyperbaric oxygen.

---

### Hemorrhoids

**Pathophysiology:** Varicose veins of the anus/rectum; risk factors include constipation, pregnancy, portal hypertension, obesity.

**Clinical features:** Bright red blood on toilet paper when wiping; **External:** Dilated veins from inferior hemorrhoidal plexus distal to dentate line (sensate — painful); **Internal:** Dilated submucosal veins of superior rectal plexus above dentate line (insensate — painless).

**Treatment:** Sitz bath, ice packs, stool softeners, topical steroids; severe internal: rubber band ligation or surgery.

---

## Gallbladder Stone Diseases

### Cholelithiasis

**Etiology:** Stone in the gallbladder.

**Clinical features:** Asymptomatic or biliary colic.

**Diagnosis:** RUQ ultrasound.

**Treatment:** Elective cholecystectomy if symptomatic.

**Types of stones:**
- **Cholesterol:** Obesity, multiparity, OCPs, rapid weight loss
- **Pigmented:** Hemolysis, cirrhosis, biliary infections, TPN

---

### Cholecystitis

**Etiology:** Stone in the cystic duct.

**Clinical features:** Acute inflammation of gallbladder wall causing RUQ pain, N/V, fever, leukocytosis, +Murphy's sign (inspiratory arrest during deep palpation of RUQ).

**Diagnosis:** RUQ ultrasound; if inconclusive, consider HIDA scan.

**Treatment:** IVF, NPO, IV antibiotics; early cholecystectomy (within 24–48 hr); if poor surgical candidate, percutaneous cholecystostomy.

**Subtypes:**
- **Acalculous cholecystitis:** Cholecystitis without a stone; more common in very ill patients (ICU, burns); treatment: if necrosis, perforation, or emphysematous, needs emergent cholecystectomy or percutaneous drainage
- **Emphysematous cholecystitis:** Infection with gas-forming bacteria; ultrasound shows air-fluid levels and gas shadows; treatment: IVF, antibiotics, early cholecystectomy

---

### Postcholecystectomy Syndrome

Persistent abdominal pain postoperatively or months/years later due to retained stone or extrabiliary problem; patients may also have bile salt wasting and diarrhea. Diagnosis: RUQ ultrasound, MRCP, or ERCP. Treatment: Bile acid sequestrants.

---

### Choledocholithiasis

**Etiology:** Stone in the common bile duct.

**Clinical features:** Abdominal pain, fever; labs show initial transaminitis followed by cholestatic elevation; RUQ ultrasound may show biliary dilatation.

**Treatment:**
- **High risk:** ERCP with stone removal, followed by elective cholecystectomy
- **Intermediate risk:** MRCP, if positive proceed to ERCP
- **Low risk:** If gallstones/sludge seen on RUQ ultrasound, consider elective cholecystectomy

---

### Cholangitis

**Etiology:** Infection of the biliary ducts due to obstruction (stone, tumor, stricture), biliary stasis, or bacterial overgrowth.

**Classic symptoms:**
- **Charcot's triad:** (1) RUQ pain; (2) Jaundice; (3) Fever
- **Reynold's pentad:** Prior triad plus (4) Septic shock; (5) Altered mental status

**Diagnosis:** RUQ ultrasound, hyperbilirubinemia, leukocytosis, mild elevation of serum transaminases.

**Treatment:** IV antibiotics, IVF; perform ERCP with sphincterotomy, stone extraction, and/or stenting within 24–48 hours.

---

## Other Diseases Affecting the Biliary Tree

- **Biliary cysts:** Cysts in the biliary tree; cause abdominal pain, recurrent cholangitis, pancreatitis, increased risk for cholangiocarcinoma; diagnosis: ultrasound, ERCP with biopsy; treatment: surgical resection
- **Bile duct stricture:** May be benign or malignant; causes obstructive jaundice; treatment: endoscopic dilation, percutaneous transhepatic biliary drain, stenting, or surgical bypass
- **Biliary dyskinesia:** Motor dysfunction of the sphincter of Oddi; causes recurrent biliary colic without gallstones; diagnosis: HIDA scan with CCK to determine gallbladder ejection fraction; treatment: laparoscopic cholecystectomy, sphincterotomy

---

## Pancreas

### Acute Pancreatitis

**Etiology:** Premature activation of pancreatic digestive enzymes causes autodigestion of pancreatic tissue.

**Causes (GET SMASHED):** Gallstones (40%), EtOH (30%), Trauma, Scorpion, Mumps, Autoimmune, Steroids, Hypertriglyceridemia (>1000 mg/dL)/Hypercalcemia, ERCP, Drugs (sulfonamides, thiazides, lisinopril, furosemide, antiretrovirals).

**Clinical features:** Epigastric pain radiating to back, worse after eating; fever, nausea/vomiting. **Hemorrhagic signs:** Grey Turner (flank ecchymosis), Cullen (periumbilical ecchymosis), Fox (inguinal ligament ecchymosis).

**Diagnosis:** Need two of three criteria: (1) Typical pancreatic abdominal pain; (2) Elevation in lipase or amylase >3× ULN; (3) Imaging findings of acute pancreatitis.

**Prognosis:** BISAP (BUN >25 mg/dL, GCS <15, SIRS, age >60 yr, pleural effusion); Ranson's criteria.

**Treatment:** NPO, early aggressive IVF (5–10 cc/kg, Lactated Ringer's preferred), electrolyte repletion, pain control. Gallstone pancreatitis: early ERCP, followed by cholecystectomy after recovery.

**Complications:** Pancreatic necrosis (if sterile, monitor; if infection suspected, IV antibiotics and surgical debridement); pancreatic pseudocyst (fluid collection 2–3 weeks after; risk rupture, infection, obstruction; treat with observation if minimal symptoms, otherwise drain percutaneously, endoscopically, or surgically); ARDS, pancreatic ascites, pleural effusions, ascending cholangitis, ileus, renal failure, hemorrhagic pancreatitis, abdominal compartment syndrome.

---

### Chronic Pancreatitis

**Etiology:** Persistent inflammation with fibrotic tissue replacing pancreatic parenchyma, leading to endocrine/exocrine dysfunction; heavy alcohol consumption is the most common cause (>80%); gallstones usually do not cause chronic pancreatitis; consider cystic fibrosis in young patients without alcohol history.

**Clinical features:** Chronic intermittent abdominal pain, diabetes, steatorrhea.

**Diagnosis:** CT abd/pelvis or MRCP (calcifications, dilated main pancreatic duct); ERCP is gold standard but not routinely performed; labs not helpful; lipase/amylase often normal.

**Treatment:**
- **Non-operative:** Alcohol and smoking cessation, pain control, pancreatic enzyme supplementation, frequent small low-fat meals
- **Operative:** Pancreatojejunostomy (pancreatic duct drainage), total pancreatectomy with auto-islet cell transplant, or denervation (celiac plexus block)

---

### Autoimmune Pancreatitis (AIP)

**Subtypes:**
- **Type 1 ("lymphoplasmacytic sclerosing pancreatitis"):** IgG4-related; most patients have another concurrent IgG4-related condition (sclerosing cholangitis, lymphadenopathy); often presents as pancreatic mass or painless jaundice; can be mistaken for pancreatic cancer
- **Type 2 ("idiopathic duct-centric chronic pancreatitis"):** Much rarer; IgG4 may be elevated or normal; sometimes associated with IBD

**Clinical features:** Abdominal pain, pancreatic mass or enlargement with obstructive jaundice, extrapancreatic manifestations (IBD, IgG4-associated cholangitis, lung nodules, Sjogren's).

**Diagnosis:** HISORt criteria (Histology, Imaging, Serology [IgG4 >2× ULN], Other organ involvement, Response to steroids therapy). "Sausage-like pancreas" is the CT buzzword.

**Treatment:** Steroids.

## Acute Liver Failure (ALF)

### Definitions

- **Acute liver injury (ALI):** AST/ALT >100 IU/L
- **Acute liver failure (ALF):** All three criteria must be present: (1) Coagulopathy (INR >1.5); (2) Hepatic encephalopathy; (3) No pre-existing liver disease

### Etiology

**Major causes:** Ischemic hepatitis (shock liver, Budd-Chiari, hepatic artery thrombus), drug/toxin-induced liver injury (acetaminophen, Amanita mushrooms), viral hepatitis, autoimmune hepatitis.

**Other causes:** Fulminant Wilson's disease, malignancy with infiltration, pregnancy-related, hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH).

**Conditions that do NOT typically cause ALF:** Alcohol-related liver disease, hemochromatosis, NAFLD, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, PSC, PBC.

### Workup

- **Viral:** Hepatitis A IgM, hepatitis B surface ag/ab/core, hepatitis C antibodies/RNA, HSV, EBV, CMV, varicella zoster antibodies, hepatitis E IgM
- **Other labs:** Acetaminophen level, toxicology screen, ceruloplasmin, urine copper (patients <35 yr), pregnancy test, ANA, anti-SMA, IgG, HIV, amylase/lipase
- **Complete abdominal ultrasound with Doppler**
- **If hepatic encephalopathy present:** Consider NCHCT to rule out brain herniation

### Complications

Neurologic (hepatic encephalopathy, herniation), pulmonary (ARDS), cardiac (high-output heart failure), hepatic (hypoglycemia, lactic acidosis, coagulopathy), bone marrow suppression, adrenal insufficiency, renal failure.

### Prognosis

Multiple prognostic scoring systems available (e.g., King's College Criteria).

### Treatment

- **Supportive care:** N-acetylcysteine (NAC): historically only for acetaminophen-related ALF, but emerging evidence suggests use for all patients with ALF; CRRT if needed; intracranial hypertension prevention/treatment (hypertonic saline, then mannitol, goal Na 145 mEq/L, elevate HOB, intubate if needed); prophylactic antibiotics; phosphate repletion; glucose monitoring/dextrose administration; enteral feeding; frequent neurologic checks; lactulose is NOT used to treat hepatic encephalopathy in ALF
- **Treat underlying cause:** See table
- **Consider liver transplantation** if low probability of recovery

### Etiologies of ALF and Recommended Treatments

| Etiology | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|
| Acetaminophen (and potentially all patients) | NAC |
| Hepatitis B infection | PO antiviral |
| Amanita mushroom poisoning | Early activated charcoal; silibinin and penicillin G |
| Budd-Chiari | TIPS placement, surgical decompression, or thrombolysis |
| Herpes simplex virus infection | IV acyclovir |
| Wilson disease | Plasma exchange may temporize; requires liver transplantation |
| Autoimmune hepatitis | Steroids controversial in presence of ALF |

## Cirrhosis and Its Complications

### Decompensated Cirrhosis

**Definition:** Any one of: ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, and/or variceal bleed.

**Etiologies of new decompensation:** Infection, insults to liver (drugs, toxins, herbs, shellfish), GI bleeding, ischemia/clotting (e.g., Budd-Chiari).

### Portal Hypertension

**Definition:** Pressure gradient of >6 mmHg between portal and hepatic veins.

**Pathophysiology:** (1) Increased resistance to portal flow at the sinusoids due to scarring/formation of parenchymal nodules; (2) Increased portal venous blood flow from splanchnic circulation in response to nitric oxide release.

**Etiologies:** Intrahepatic (cirrhosis; much less commonly schistosomiasis, massive fatty change, diffuse fibrosing granulomatous disease, amyloidosis, nodular regenerative hyperplasia); pre-hepatic (portal vein thrombosis); post-hepatic (Budd-Chiari, severe right-sided heart failure, constrictive pericarditis).

### Ascites

**Description:** Accumulation of excessive fluid in the peritoneal cavity; cirrhosis is the most common cause (85%).

**SAAG:** Serum ascites albumin gradient = Serum albumin concentration – Ascites albumin concentration.

**Pathophysiology:** (1) Portal hypertension (high hydrostatic pressure); (2) Hypoalbuminemia (low oncotic pressure); splanchnic dilatation results in lower systemic blood pressure, activating RAAS and causing additional fluid and sodium retention.

**Diagnosis:** Paracentesis; ascites fluid characteristics inform differential diagnosis.

**Treatment:** (1) Salt restrict <2 g/day; (2) Furosemide + spironolactone; (3) Paracentesis.

### Differential Diagnosis for Ascites Based on Fluid Characteristics

| SAAG <1.1 | SAAG >1.1 |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Ascitic fluid protein <2.5 g/dL:** Nephrotic syndrome | **Ascitic fluid protein <2.5 g/dL:** Cirrhosis |
| **Ascitic fluid protein >2.5 g/dL:** Malignancy, tuberculosis | **Ascitic fluid protein >2.5 g/dL:** Right-sided heart failure, Budd-Chiari |

### Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

**Definition:** Infection of ascites without an evident surgically treatable intra-abdominal source; almost always occurs in patients with cirrhosis and ascites.

**Pathogenesis:** Translocation of bacteria from gut lumen across intestinal wall.

**Microbiology:** E. coli, Klebsiella, S. pneumoniae.

**Clinical features:** Fever, abdominal pain/tenderness, and/or altered mental status; however, >10% of patients have no symptoms.

**Diagnosis:** (1) Ascitic fluid with PMNs >250 cells/mm³; (2) Positive ascitic fluid bacterial culture; (3) Absence of a cause of secondary bacterial peritonitis.

**Treatment:** Ceftriaxone or cefotaxime.

**Prophylaxis:** Ciprofloxacin or TMP-SMX for patients with prior SBP, prior variceal hemorrhage, or ascitic fluid protein concentration <1 g/dL.

---

### Hepatohydrothorax

**Description:** Pleural effusion on CXR in the presence of ascites without another explanation.

**Pathophysiology:** Leakage of ascitic fluid through diaphragmatic defects; right-sided defects more common.

**Clinical features:** Dyspnea, cough, pleurisy, hypoxemia; 80% right-sided, 15% left-sided, 5% bilateral.

**Diagnosis:** CXR; thoracentesis shows transudative fluid (protein <2.5 g/dL, SAAG >1.1 g/dL).

**Treatment:** (1) Medical management identical to ascites (sodium restriction, diuresis); (2) If refractory, consider recurrent thoracenteses or TIPS.

---

### Portosystemic Shunts (Collaterals and Varices)

**Pathophysiology:** Portal hypertension causes reversal of blood flow from portal to systemic circulation, causing dilation of collateral vessels.

**Locations:** Esophagus/stomach (varices, portal hypertensive gastropathy), rectum (hemorrhoids), periumbilical/abdominal wall (caput medusae).

**Management of esophageal varices:** Prescribe beta blockers for prevention of bleeding; perform surveillance EGD at appropriate intervals. Modify routine management of GIB if concerned for variceal bleeding: two large-bore IVs, PPI, type and screen plus ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily and octreotide 50 mcg IV bolus plus 50 mcg/hr infusion. Consider TIPS for uncontrolled bleeding if low MELD-Na score and no pulmonary hypertension.

### EGD Surveillance for Patients with Cirrhosis

| Disease Status | Interval for Surveillance EGD |
|---------------|------------------------------|
| Compensated, no varices | Every 2–3 years |
| Compensated, small varices | Every 1–2 years |
| Decompensated | Annually |

---

### Splenomegaly

**Description:** Enlargement of the spleen to as large as 1000 gm.

**Pathophysiology:** Long-standing congestion from portal hypertension.

**Complications:** Thrombocytopenia (due to sequestration; low TPO production also contributes); less commonly pancytopenia.

---

### Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE)

**Description:** Spectrum of disturbances in consciousness/mental status, from subtle sleep-wake and behavioral changes to deep coma/death.

**Pathophysiology:** Ammonia is the most well-documented contributing neurotoxin; alters neuronal electrical activity and increases intracellular osmolarity, contributing to cerebral edema.

**Clinical features:** Confusion, asterixis (flap), hyperreflexia, fetor hepaticus (musty breath).

**West-Haven grading:** (1) Sleep-wake disturbance; (2) Dissociation, asterixis; (3) Marked confusion; (4) Coma.

**Triggers:** Infection, GIB, portal vein thrombosis, medications (benzodiazepines, opioids), inappropriate lactulose dosing.

**Treatment:** (1) Lactulose prevents ammonia absorption; (2) Rifaximin kills ammonia-producing gut flora; (3) Zinc supplementation sometimes administered (benefit understudied).

---

### Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

**Etiology:** Hypoxemia and intrapulmonary vascular dilatation in presence of liver disease; occurs in up to 30% of patients with decompensated cirrhosis.

**Pathophysiology:** Intrapulmonary vascular dilatations allow rapid blood flow through the capillary bed without enough time for oxygen diffusion, resulting in shunt physiology (Q>>V); blood flow in lung bases increases when upright, so shunting and symptoms worsen when sitting upright.

**Clinical features:** Dyspnea; easier to breathe while lying flat (platypnea) and worsening arterial oxygen saturation when upright (orthodeoxia).

**Diagnosis:** PaO₂ <80 mmHg on room air, echo with intrapulmonary shunting.

**Treatment:** Supplemental oxygen, liver transplantation.

---

### Portopulmonary Hypertension

**Description:** Pulmonary hypertension in the presence of portal hypertension; present in 5–9% of liver transplant candidates.

**Pathophysiology:** Poorly understood; relates to pulmonary vasoconstriction/remodeling in presence of portal hypertension.

**Clinical features:** Variable symptoms; most commonly dyspnea, clubbing, fatigue, chest pain, syncope.

**Diagnosis:** TTE may be suggestive; confirm with right heart catheterization.

**Treatment:** Patients may be candidates for PAH therapy based on functional class (prostacyclin analogues, endothelin antagonists, phosphodiesterase inhibitors); avoid TIPS due to risk of worsening right heart failure.

---

### Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS)

**Description:** Renal failure occurring in patients with liver failure who have no other morphologic or functional reasons for renal impairment.

**Pathophysiology:** Decreased renal perfusion pressure due to systemic (including splanchnic) vasodilatation and resultant hypoperfusion/RAAS activation.

**Subtypes:**
- **Type 1:** More severe; doubling of baseline Cr to >2.5 mg/dL in <2 weeks
- **Type 2:** Less severe; usually associated with diuretic-refractory ascites

**Diagnosis:** Hepatic disease with portal hypertension; AKI; absence of any other apparent cause of AKI (obtain renal ultrasound, urinalysis); no improvement in function after albumin challenge (cessation of diuretics plus IV albumin 1 g/kg/day for 2 days).

**Treatment:** MOA (Midodrine, Octreotide, Albumin); if critically ill, use norepinephrine and albumin; consider TIPS or dialysis for bridge to transplant if medical therapy fails.

---

### Other Clinical Features of Cirrhosis

- **Hyperestrogenism:** Spider angiomas, palmar erythema, gynecomastia, testicular atrophy, amenorrhea, low libido
- **Decreased hepatic synthetic function:** Hypoalbuminemia, decreased clotting factors (INR prolongation)
- **Thrombocytopenia:** Splenic sequestration, decreased TPO production; suggestive of portal hypertension

### Healthcare Maintenance in Cirrhosis

- **HCC screening:** q6mo abdominal imaging (ultrasound or quad-phase CT) and serum AFP
- **Hepatic osteodystrophy:** Check DEXA, calcium, vitamin D
- **Immunizations:** Hepatitis A, hepatitis B, influenza, PPSV23
- **Avoidances:** Alcohol, oysters/raw shellfish, opioids; minimize acetaminophen (up to 2 grams/day is classic limit), NSAIDs, sedating medications
- **Nutrition:** High-protein diet; muscle wasting is common

### Scoring Systems for Cirrhosis

| System | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **MELD-Na Score** | Uses serum bilirubin, creatinine, INR, and sodium to predict 3-month survival; range 6–40; higher scores = increased severity and 3-month mortality; used by UNOS for liver transplant prioritization |
| **Child-Pugh** | Uses total bilirubin, serum albumin, prothrombin time, ascites, and hepatic encephalopathy; MELD-Na is more widely used due to better prognostic value |

## Alcohol-Induced Liver Disease

### Alcoholic Hepatitis

**Clinical features:** Jaundice, anorexia, fever, tender hepatomegaly.

**Diagnosis:** Moderate elevation in transaminases (AST:ALT ratio ≥2) in a patient with history of prolonged and heavy alcohol use (>100 g/day for >20 yr); requires exclusion of other causes of liver disease.

**Management:**
- Evaluate for other causes of acute hepatitis (HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc, Hep A IgM, acetaminophen level, RUQ ultrasound with Doppler)
- Rule out mimics (sepsis, SBP)
- Calculate MELD-Na and Maddrey's discriminant function (MDF)
- If steroids indicated (MDF >32; indicates severe alcoholic hepatitis with 1-month mortality of 35–45%), plan for prednisolone 40 mg/day for 28 days in consultation with hepatology
- Calculate Lille score at day 7; if >0.45, suggests non-response to steroids
- Pentoxifylline is an alternative if contraindications to steroids (controversial; STOPAH Trial, NEJM 2015)
- If AKI present, consider gentle fluid resuscitation
- Consider peptic ulcer prophylaxis, particularly if steroids will be used
- Nutritional supplementation with thiamine, folate, and a multivitamin; adequate nutrition is the most important intervention for survival
- Enteral feeding can be considered if patients are not eating enough

### Alcoholic Cirrhosis

**Epidemiology:** Cirrhosis develops in 10–25% of patients with chronic heavy alcohol use.

**Treatment:** Alcohol cessation, liver transplantation if eligible (typically requires >6 months of abstinence).

## Drug/Toxin-Induced Liver Injury

### Acetaminophen

**Epidemiology:** Most common cause of intrinsic drug-induced liver injury; can occur from accidental overdose, intentional overdose, or co-ingestion with alcohol or other substances.

**Diagnosis:** Measure APAP level at 4 and 16 hours after ingestion; if timing unclear, draw immediately and 4 hours later.

**Treatment:** NAC, consult poison control/toxicology.

### Other Medications Causing Liver Injury

Antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate), antiepileptic drugs (phenytoin, valproate), antituberculosis drugs (isoniazid, rifampin), NSAIDs, azathioprine, Amanita mushroom poisoning.

---

## Infectious Etiologies of Liver Disease

### Hepatitis A and E

- **HAV:** Fecal-oral transmission; may be asymptomatic or present with malaise, fatigue, nausea/vomiting; diagnosis: HAV IgM; treatment: supportive; does not cause chronic hepatitis
- **HEV:** Fecal-oral transmission (especially waterborne); diagnosis: HEV IgM; treatment: (1) Acute: supportive care (infection can be much more severe in pregnant women); (2) Chronic: ribavirin for 12 weeks in non-pregnant patients

### Hepatitis D

**Pathophysiology:** Parenteral, sexual, and vertical transmission; vertical transmission is most common; requires outer envelope of HBsAg for replication; can present as acute hepatitis or exacerbate chronic hepatitis B.

**Diagnosis:** HDV RNA, HDV IgM, IgG.

**Treatment:** Supportive care.

### Hepatitis B

**Transmission:** Parenteral, sexual, and vertical; adults typically do not progress to chronic hepatitis B; children very frequently do.

**Detection and interpretation of serologic results:** See tables in clinical content.

**Treatment:**
- **Acute HBV:** No treatment
- **Chronic HBV:** Therapy is often lifelong and non-curative
- **Indications for treatment:** Compensated cirrhosis and DNA >2000 IU/mL; decompensated cirrhosis or acute liver failure with any detectable DNA; immune active (HBeAg positive: ALT ≥2× ULN and HBV >20,000 IU/mL; HBeAg negative: ALT ≥2× ULN and HBV >2000 IU/mL); patients on chronic immunosuppressive therapy, pregnant women with high viral loads, patients with HCC, patients with HCV coinfection being treated for HCV
- **Agents:** Tenofovir (TDF, TAF): lower rates of resistance; Entecavir: lower rates of resistance; Lamivudine: high rates of resistance; Interferon (IFN-alpha): can only be used in young patients with compensated liver disease

### Hepatitis C

**Transmission:** Parenteral (IVDU, blood), sexual, vertical.

**Detection:** HCV antibody, HCV RNA.

**Natural history:** 75–85% develop chronic infection; 60–70% develop chronic liver disease; 10–20% develop cirrhosis over 20–30 years; 1–5% die from cirrhosis or HCC.

**Diagnosis:** HCV antibody; if positive, check HCV RNA to confirm active infection.

**Treatment:** Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs); cure rates >95%.

**Screening:** One-time screening recommended for all adults born 1945–1965 (birth cohort), plus standard risk-based screening.
