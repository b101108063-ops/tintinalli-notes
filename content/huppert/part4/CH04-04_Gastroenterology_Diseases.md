## 1. Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

**Inflammatory markers** such as erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP), and fecal calprotectin can be elevated in inflammatory bowel disease. These markers are helpful to check at baseline when the patient is asymptomatic and then again if symptoms recur.

**Imaging** considerations include the following: Before initial colonoscopy in the inpatient setting, consider an abdominal plain film. There is no need for imaging if the patient is stable as an outpatient. CT abdomen/pelvis is indicated in patients with severe pain to rule out complications of IBD. Imaging can also be used to help rule out other causes of hematochezia, such as mesenteric ischemia.

**Colonoscopy** with biopsies is always indicated when evaluating for a new diagnosis of IBD. These should only be used for induction, not maintenance; patients with IBD should not be kept on chronic steroid therapy.

**Maintenance therapy** depends on the severity and extent of disease. For mild/moderate disease, consider appropriate maintenance medications. For severe flares, hospital admission should be considered.

**Diagnostics/labs** for flares include ruling out infection with stool pathogens, stool O&P, and fecal calprotectin. Prepare for the possibility of rescue therapy if the patient does not respond to initial treatment.

**Colon cancer screening** should begin 8–10 years after the initial diagnosis of IBD and continue at appropriate intervals.

## 2. Malabsorption

### Celiac Disease

**Pathophysiology**: Celiac disease is an immune-mediated inflammatory disease of the small intestine triggered by gluten exposure.

**Clinical features** include steatorrhea (bulky, floating, foul-smelling stool), malabsorption (weight loss, micronutrient deficiencies), and other systemic manifestations.

**Diagnosis** requires IgA tissue transglutaminase (tTG) antibody testing AND duodenal biopsies with blunted villi.

**Treatment** consists of a strict gluten-free diet.

### Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO)

**Pathophysiology**: SIBO occurs when there is excessive bacterial proliferation in the small intestine. It is most common in the setting of motility disorders and chronic pancreatitis, although any disorder affecting gut motility can contribute.

**Clinical features** include watery diarrhea, bloating, flatulence, and micronutrient deficiency. Nutritional deficiencies can be severe and symptomatic (e.g., vitamin B12 deficiency, hypocalcemia).

**Diagnosis** is established by a positive carbohydrate breath test or endoscopy with jejunal aspirate showing bacterial count greater than 10^3 colony-forming units per milliliter. Sometimes treatment may be initiated empirically.

**Treatment** includes rifaximin for 14 days to reduce small intestinal bacteria, along with correction of micronutrient deficiencies.

### Carbohydrate Malabsorption

**Pathophysiology**: Lactose malabsorption is the most common form; fructose malabsorption is another important cause.

**Clinical features** include bloating and diarrhea.

**Treatment** involves dietary restriction and lactase enzyme supplementation.

### Whipple Disease

**Clinical features** include chronic diarrhea, malabsorption, abdominal discomfort, weight loss, and systemic manifestations.

**Diagnosis** is made by small bowel biopsy with periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) stain and polymerase chain reaction (PCR) for *Tropheryma whipplei*. Tissue/fluid may also be obtained from sites of extraintestinal manifestation (e.g., cerebrospinal fluid).

**Treatment** consists of ceftriaxone or penicillin for 2–4 weeks, followed by trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole for 1 year.

## 4. Microscopic Colitis

**Associated conditions** include autoimmune diseases, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) use, and tobacco use.

**Clinical features** include chronic, watery, non-bloody diarrhea.

**Diagnosis** is made by colonoscopy with biopsy showing lymphocytic or collagenous colitis. The workup should include ruling out infectious causes (stool studies) and celiac sprue.

**Treatment** includes stopping NSAIDs or other offending medications, loperamide for symptom control, and second-line agents including cholestyramine, bismuth, and biologics if needed.

## 5. Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

**Pathophysiology**: IBS is idiopathic and multifactorial, sometimes related to visceral hypersensitivity and altered gut motility.

**Associated conditions** include female sex, depression, anxiety, and fibromyalgia.

**Clinical features** depend on the subtype: IBS-C (constipation-predominant), IBS-D (diarrhea-predominant), and IBS with mixed bowel habits.

**Diagnosis** is made by excluding other organic etiologies through history and laboratory studies including fecal calprotectin. Colonoscopy is typically not needed but should be considered for alarm features including age greater than 50 years.

**Rome IV criteria** for IBS require abdominal pain at least once per week for 3 or more months with at least 2 of the following symptoms: improvement with defecation, association with a change in stool frequency, or association with a change in stool form.

**Treatment** varies by subtype but generally includes education and reassurance, a low FODMAP diet, and dicyclomine for symptom relief.

## 6. Mesenteric Ischemia

### Acute Mesenteric Ischemia

**Etiology**: The most common cause is arterial embolism (approximately 50% of cases), followed by arterial thrombosis, venous thrombosis, and nonocclusive mesenteric ischemia.

**Treatment** depends on the etiology: arterial embolism requires early surgical laparotomy with embolectomy; venous thrombosis requires anticoagulation; nonocclusive mesenteric ischemia requires treating the underlying cause.

### Chronic Mesenteric Ischemia

**Pathophysiology**: Atherosclerosis can cause abdominal angina (postprandial pain) due to inadequate blood flow to the intestines after meals.

**Treatment** involves managing atherosclerotic disease and risk factors.

## 7. GI Bleeding

### Lower GI Bleeding

**Etiology**: Diverticulosis is the most common cause of significant lower GI bleeding. Diverticular bleeding is typically painless and presents with bright red or maroon blood. More common in elderly patients and those who consume a low-fiber diet.

**Diagnosis**: CT abdomen/pelvis can help identify the bleeding source. If bleeding does not stop, perform colonoscopy to evaluate for the site of active bleeding. Consider angiography with embolization if the bleeding site cannot be identified on colonoscopy.

**Treatment**: Colonoscopy with interventions as needed. Surgery is a last resort.

### Diverticulitis

**Pathophysiology**: Inflammation and focal necrosis due to erosion of the diverticular wall.

**Clinical features** include left lower quadrant pain, fever, and leukocytosis.

**Diagnosis**: No imaging is needed for uncomplicated cases in appropriate clinical settings; however, CT may be considered if the diagnosis is uncertain or to evaluate for complications.

**Treatment**: Antibiotics are typically prescribed. Recommend colonoscopy 6–8 weeks later to rule out colon cancer if the patient has not had recent colonoscopic evaluation.

**Important**: Patients do NOT need to avoid nuts, seeds, and popcorn—this is a common myth that has been debunked. Elective surgery remains controversial.

## 8. Other Diseases Affecting the Colon

### Angiodysplasia

**Definition**: Angiodysplasia refers to arteriovenous malformations of the colon.

**Classic association**: Heyde's syndrome (triad of aortic stenosis, acquired von Willebrand factor deficiency, and GI bleeding).

### Colorectal Polyps

**Subtypes** include tubular adenoma, tubulovillous adenoma, and villous adenoma (most likely to become malignant).

**Surveillance**: After premalignant polyps are removed, typically recommend closer interval surveillance colonoscopy.

### Volvulus

**Sigmoid volvulus** occurs when the sigmoid bowel twists around its mesentery. Clinical features include insidious onset of abdominal pain, nausea, and distension, eventually leading to obstruction. More common in patients older than 70 years, male predominance, and patients with comorbid neuropsychiatric diseases. Diagnosis is made by CT abdomen/pelvis showing a "whirl pattern" (dilated sigmoid around mesocolon and vessels) and proximal bowel dilation. Treatment includes emergent surgery if peritonitis or perforation is present; if stable, sigmoidoscopy to diagnose and detorse the twisted segment followed by surgery.

**Cecal volvulus** occurs when the cecum rotates or torses, resulting in bowel obstruction. More common in younger women. Clinical features vary widely from insidious abdominal pain and vomiting to acute obstruction. Treatment is primarily surgical.

### Radiation Proctitis

**Pathophysiology**: Inflammation of the rectum following radiation therapy.

**Clinical features**: Chronic radiation proctitis can cause bleeding.

**Treatment**: Endoscopic argon plasma coagulation (APC) for bleeding. Diagnosis is a diagnosis of exclusion. Endoscopic findings are nonspecific (pallor with friability, telangiectasia); biopsy may help exclude other conditions. Treatment includes supportive care, sucralfate enemas, and hyperbaric oxygen.

### Hemorrhoids

**Risk factors** include constipation, pregnancy, portal hypertension, and obesity.

**Clinical features**: Bright red blood on toilet paper when wiping.

**Types**:
- **External hemorrhoids**: Dilated veins from inferior hemorrhoidal plexus distal to the dentate line (sensate, can be painful).
- **Internal hemorrhoids**: Dilated submucosal veins of superior rectal plexus above the dentate line (insensate, typically painless).

**Treatment**: Sitz bath, ice packs, stool softeners, and topical steroids. If internal hemorrhoids are severe, rubber band ligation or surgery may be pursued.

## 9. Gallbladder Stone Disease

### Cholelithiasis

**Etiology**: Stone formation in the gallbladder.

**Clinical features**: Patients may be asymptomatic or may experience biliary colic.

### Cholecystitis

**Etiology**: Stone in the cystic duct causing inflammation.

**Clinical features**: Right upper quadrant pain, fever, and positive Murphy's sign.

**Treatment**: Early cholecystectomy (within 24–48 hours). If the patient is a poor surgical candidate, achieve drainage with percutaneous cholecystostomy.

**Subtypes**:
- **Acalculous cholecystitis**: Cholecystitis in the absence of a stone. More common in very ill patients (e.g., ICU patients, burn patients). Treatment: If necrosis, perforation, or emphysematous cholecystitis is present, emergent cholecystectomy is needed.
- **Emphysematous cholecystitis**: Infection of the gallbladder with gas-forming bacteria. Ultrasound shows air-fluid levels and gas shadows. Treatment: intravenous fluids, antibiotics, and early cholecystectomy.

### Postcholecystectomy Syndrome

**Definition**: Persistent symptoms after cholecystectomy.

**Clinical features**: Patients may have bile salt wasting and diarrhea.

**Diagnosis**: Right upper quadrant ultrasound, magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP), or endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP).

**Treatment**: Bile acid sequestrants.

### Choledocholithiasis

**Etiology**: Stone in the common bile duct.

**Clinical features**: Abdominal pain, jaundice. Laboratory findings notable for initial transaminitis followed by a cholestatic elevation. Right upper quadrant ultrasound may show biliary dilatation.

**Treatment**:
- **High risk**: ERCP with removal of stone, followed by elective cholecystectomy.
- **Intermediate risk**: MRCP; if positive, proceed to ERCP.
- **Low risk**: If gallstones or sludge seen on right upper quadrant ultrasound, consider elective cholecystectomy.

### Cholangitis

**Etiology**: Infection of the biliary ducts due to obstruction from gallstone, tumor, or other causes.

**Clinical features**:
- **Charcot's triad**: Right upper quadrant pain, jaundice, and fever.
- **Reynold's pentad**: The prior triad plus septic shock and altered mental status.

**Treatment**: ERCP with sphincterotomy, stone extraction, and/or stenting within 24–48 hours.

### Biliary Strictures

**Etiology**: May be benign or malignant.

**Clinical features**: Can cause biliary obstruction with jaundice and cholangitis.

**Treatment**: Endoscopic dilation, percutaneous transhepatic biliary drain, stenting, or surgical resection.

### Biliary Dyskinesia

**Definition**: Motor dysfunction of the sphincter of Oddi.

**Clinical features**: Causes recurrent biliary colic without evidence of gallstones.

**Diagnosis**: HIDA scan with administration of cholecystokinin (CCK) to determine the ejection fraction.

**Treatment**: Laparoscopic cholecystectomy or sphincterotomy.

## 10. Pancreas

### Acute Pancreatitis

**Etiology**: Premature activation of pancreatic digestive enzymes leading to pancreatic inflammation.

**Classic signs** include Grey Turner (flank ecchymosis) and Cullen (periumbilical ecchymosis) signs in hemorrhagic pancreatitis. Also present are fever and nausea/vomiting.

**Prognosis**: Multiple scoring systems exist including BISAP (a 5-point score to assess severity).

**Treatment**: If infection is suspected, give intravenous antibiotics and pursue surgical debridement.

**Complications**:
- **Pancreatic pseudocyst**: Fluid collection occurring 2–3 weeks after acute pancreatitis. Risk of rupture, infection, and obstruction. Treatment: observation if minimal symptoms and no pseudoaneurysm; if symptomatic or rapidly enlarging, drain percutaneously, endoscopically, or surgically.
- **Other complications**: ARDS, pancreatic ascites, pleural effusions, and multi-organ failure.

### Chronic Pancreatitis

**Etiology**: Persistent inflammation of the pancreas with fibrotic tissue replacement.

Heavy alcohol consumption is the most common cause (greater than 80% of cases); gallstones usually do not cause chronic pancreatitis. Consider the diagnosis of cystic fibrosis in young patients without a history of heavy alcohol use.

**Clinical features**: Chronic intermittent abdominal pain, diabetes, and steatorrhea (fat in the stool).

**Diagnosis**: ERCP is the gold standard but is not routinely performed. Laboratory studies are not helpful and lipase and amylase are often normal.

**Treatment**: Nonoperative management includes alcohol and smoking cessation, pain control, and pancreatic enzyme supplementation. Most patients have another concurrent IgG4-related condition, such as sclerosing cholangitis.

### Autoimmune Pancreatitis (AIP)

**Type 1 ("IgG4-related pancreatitis")**: Often presents as a pancreatic mass or painless jaundice and can be mistaken for pancreatic cancer. Associated with elevated serum IgG4.

**Type 2 ("idiopathic duct-centric chronic pancreatitis")**: Much more rare than type 1. IgG4 may be elevated or normal. Sometimes associated with IBD.

**Clinical features**: Abdominal pain, pancreatic mass or enlargement with obstructive jaundice.

**Key imaging finding**: The "sausage-like pancreas" is a characteristic CT finding in AIP.

**Treatment**: Corticosteroids.

## 11. Acute Liver Failure (ALF)

### Definitions

- **Acute liver injury (ALI)**: Elevation of AST/ALT.
- **Acute liver failure (ALF)**: Coagulopathy with INR greater than 1.5 and any degree of encephalopathy in a patient without pre-existing liver disease.

**Important**: Lactulose is not used to treat hepatic encephalopathy in acute liver failure.

**Treatment**: Treat the underlying cause. See differential diagnosis for ascites in Table 4.10.

---

## 12. Ascites

Cirrhosis is the most common cause of ascites (approximately 85% of cases).

**Serum ascites albumin gradient (SAAG)** = Serum albumin concentration (g/dL) – Ascites albumin concentration (g/dL).

### Differential Diagnosis

See Table 4.10 for the differential diagnosis of ascites based on ascites fluid characteristics.

### Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

**Definition**: Infection of ascitic fluid without an obvious intra-abdominal source.

It is crucial to distinguish SBP from secondary bacterial peritonitis because treatment differs significantly.

**Pathogenesis**: Translocation of bacteria from the gut lumen across the intestinal wall.

**Common organisms**: *Escherichia coli*, *Klebsiella*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*.

**Clinical features**: Ascites due to cirrhosis with fever, abdominal pain, and worsening hepatic encephalopathy.

### Hepatohydrothorax

**Location**: Right-sided in approximately 80%, left-sided in approximately 15%, and bilateral in approximately 5%.

**Diagnosis**: Chest X-ray.

---

## 13. Hepatic Encephalopathy

**Pathophysiology**: Ammonia is the most well-documented contributing neurotoxin. It is thought to alter neuronal electrical activity and increase intracellular osmolarity, leading to cerebral edema.

---

## 14. Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

**Etiology**: Hypoxemia and intrapulmonary vascular dilatation in patients with liver disease.

**Key mechanism**: Since blood flow is dependent (i.e., shifts with gravity) in the lungs, flow in the lung apices increases when upright. Therefore, for patients with hepatopulmonary syndrome, shunting and symptoms worsen when standing upright (platypnea and orthodeoxia).

---

## 15. Bone Disease in Cirrhosis

**Monitoring**: Check DEXA scan, calcium, and vitamin D levels.

**Immunizations**: Hepatitis A, hepatitis B, influenza, and 23-valent pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccines are recommended.

---

## 16. Liver Transplant Scoring Systems

In 2002, the MELD-Na score was accepted by the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS) for liver allocation.

**Child-Pugh score**: A scoring system to assess the prognosis of chronic liver disease. The score employs five clinical measures of liver disease: total bilirubin, serum albumin, INR, ascites, and hepatic encephalopathy.

**Clinical uses**: MELD-Na is more widely used, given its better prognostic value, but Child-Pugh is still employed in certain clinical scenarios.

## 17. Alcohol-Induced Liver Disease

### Alcoholic Hepatitis

**Clinical features**: Jaundice, anorexia, and other signs of liver dysfunction.

**Treatment considerations**: If the discriminant function is greater than 0.45, this suggests non-responsiveness to steroids and steroids can be stopped. Pentoxifylline may be considered as an alternative.

**Nutrition**: Adequate nutrition is the most important intervention for alcoholic hepatitis survival. It is not just about micronutrient deficiencies but also about ensuring adequate overall caloric intake. Protein intake in particular should be encouraged. Often these patients do not feel well enough to eat and may not eat enough, so enteral nutrition may be necessary.

### Alcoholic Cirrhosis

**Epidemiology**: Cirrhosis develops in 10–25% of patients with chronic alcohol use.

---

## 18. Drug-Induced Liver Injury

Can occur due to accidental overdose, intentional overdose, co-ingestion with alcohol or other substances.

**Acetaminophen toxicity**: If timing is unclear, draw acetaminophen levels immediately upon presentation and 4 hours later (levels may still be informative).

**Clinical features**: Patients may be asymptomatic or present with malaise, fatigue, and nausea/vomiting.

## 19. Viral Hepatitis

### Hepatitis A Virus (HAV)

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral route.

**Diagnosis**: HAV IgM is positive in acute infection.

**Treatment**: Supportive care. Hepatitis A does not cause chronic hepatitis.

### Hepatitis E Virus (HEV)

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral, especially waterborne.

**Diagnosis**: HEV IgM.

**Treatment**: Acute hepatitis E is treated supportively; infection can be much more severe in pregnant women. Chronic hepatitis E is almost exclusively seen in immunocompromised hosts and should be treated with appropriate antiviral therapy.

### Hepatitis D Virus (HDV)

**Transmission**: Parenteral (IV drug use, blood), sexual, and vertical. Vertical transmission is the most common route.

**Key requirement**: HDV requires the outer envelope of HBsAg for replication. If coinfection with hepatitis B occurs, severe hepatitis may result.

**Natural history**: Adults typically do not progress to chronic hepatitis B; however, children very frequently progress to chronic infection.

**Serologic interpretation**:
- Immune due to vaccine: HBsAg negative, anti-HBc negative, anti-HBs positive.
- Immune due to natural infection: HBsAg negative, anti-HBc positive, anti-HBs positive.
- Chronic HBV: HBsAg positive, anti-HBc positive, anti-HBs negative. Obtain HBV DNA if immunocompromised.

**Acute hepatitis B** is diagnosed by detecting HBsAg and IgM anti-HBc. IgM core antibodies are lost within 24–32 weeks of illness onset, whereas total anti-HBc persists.

The hepatitis B e antigen (HBeAg) is initially positive, and then antibody to HBeAg (anti-HBe) develops during recovery.

**Chronic hepatitis B** has several phases. During the immune tolerance phase, HBeAg is present with high viral load and normal ALT. During the immune clearance phase, HBeAg is still present but HBV DNA and ALT levels are typically both high and fluctuating.

Due to immune clearance, HBeAg is eventually lost and anti-HBe develops (seroconversion). Most patients then enter a period of low viral replication with normal ALT. Some patients later have reactivation of HBV, characterized by reappearance of HBeAg or detectable HBV DNA.

### Hepatitis B Testing Parameters

| Test | Description | Indicates |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| HBsAg | Surface antigen | Current infection |
| Anti-HBs | Antibody to surface antigen | Immunity |
| Anti-HBc | Antibody to core antigen | Exposure/infection |
| HBeAg | E antigen | Active viral replication |
| Anti-HBe | Antibody to E antigen | Low viral replication |
| HBV DNA | Viral load | Active replication |

### Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)

**Diagnosis**: Check HCV antibody; if positive, check HCV viral load; if viremic, check genotype (1–6, with genotype 1a most common in the United States).

**Treatment** depends on HCV genotype and whether the patient has cirrhosis. Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) are the standard of care.

**First-line regimens for treatment-naïve patients without cirrhosis**:
- NS5A inhibitor: Ledipasvir/sofosbuvir (Harvoni) or elbasvir/grazoprevir (Zepatier) for 12 weeks.
- NS5A + protease inhibitor: Pibrentasvir/glecaprevir (Mavyret) for 8–12 weeks.

For patients with CKD, appropriate DAAs should be selected. Monitor liver function tests during treatment as hepatotoxicity can rarely occur.

## 20. Autoimmune Etiologies of Liver Disease

**Indications for treatment**: AST greater than 10 times the upper limit of normal (ULN), AST greater than 2 times ULN with IgG level greater than 2 times ULN, bridging fibrosis, or cirrhosis.

**Advanced disease** may require liver transplantation.

## 21. Metabolic Etiologies of Liver Disease

### Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)/Nonalcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH)

**Histology** appears similar to cirrhosis due to alcohol use, but the patient reports no or minimal alcohol consumption.

**Treatment**: Control risk factors for insulin resistance, weight loss, and consider bariatric surgery if indicated. It is safe to use statins in patients with NAFLD/NASH. Vitamin E is sometimes used for nondiabetic NASH.

### Wilson's Disease

**Pathophysiology**: Autosomal recessive mutation of the ATP7B gene that causes copper accumulation.

**Typical age of onset**: 5–35 years.

**Clinical features**:
- **Liver**: Acute hepatitis, cirrhosis, liver failure.
- **Eyes**: Kayser-Fleischer rings (copper deposition in Descemet's membrane).

**Definitive diagnosis**: HFE gene testing with C282Y polymorphism.

**Treatment**: Repeat phlebotomy to reduce iron stores. Target ferritin to normal range. Counsel patients to avoid raw seafood (increased risk of *Vibrio* infection). Liver transplantation may be necessary for end-stage disease.

### Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency

**Pathophysiology**: Autosomal recessive disease that causes liver disease and emphysema due to abnormal alpha-1 antitrypsin protein accumulation.

**Treatment**: Second-line therapy is obeticholic acid in compensated cirrhosis. For advanced disease, liver transplantation may be necessary.

### Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)

**Etiology**: Fibroinflammatory disorder of bile ducts leading to strictures and cholestasis.

**Association**: Approximately 80% of PSC patients have inflammatory bowel disease (especially ulcerative colitis).

**Clinical features**: Patients are usually asymptomatic or may present with fatigue, pruritus, and jaundice.

**Diagnosis**: Liver biopsy showing fibrous obliteration of small bile ducts with an "onion skin" appearance. MRCP shows the characteristic beading pattern of bile ducts.

**Natural history**: Progressive disease that requires liver transplantation.

**Cancer risk**: High association with cholangiocarcinoma; screen with liver function tests every 6–12 months, Ca19-9, and MRCP.

## 23. Vascular Etiologies of Liver Disease

### Portal Vein Thrombosis

**Etiology**: Blood clot in the portal vein.

**Treatment**: Can consider anticoagulation for acute portal vein thrombosis after evaluation for varices.

### Budd-Chiari Syndrome

**Etiology**: Occlusion of the hepatic venous outflow by a thrombus.

**Associations**: Hypercoagulable states such as polycythemia vera, pregnancy, oral contraceptive use, and other conditions.

**Prognosis**: Transaminases should normalize in 1–2 weeks with appropriate treatment. Treat underlying condition (e.g., treat sepsis to prevent further hypotension).

### Hepatic Cysts

**Hydatid liver cysts**: The tapeworm *Echinococcus granulosus* can be transmitted from dogs to humans. Treatment involves surgical resection (pre-inject with ethanol prior to surgery to kill the tapeworm scolex).

**Pyogenic liver abscess**: Pus-filled area in the liver, often due to polymicrobial infection from biliary tract disease or portal vein bacteremia. Common organisms include *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, *Proteus*, *Enterococcus*, and anaerobes. Symptoms include fever, malaise, anorexia, and nausea/vomiting. Diagnosis is made by right upper quadrant ultrasound or CT abdomen/pelvis. Treatment includes intravenous antibiotics and percutaneous or surgical drainage.

## 24. Benign Liver Tumors

| Tumor | Features | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hepatic hemangioma** | Most common benign liver tumor. May increase in size with pregnancy or estrogen, but mechanism unknown. | No treatment required. |
| **Focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH)** | Second most common benign liver tumor. Predominantly in women of reproductive age. Not associated with oral contraceptive use. | No treatment required. |
| **Hepatocellular adenoma** | Benign liver tumor that typically affects women and is associated with oral contraceptive use. | Stop oral contraceptives. Resect tumors greater than 5 cm or if symptomatic. |

## 25. Paracentesis

**Indications**: (1) All patients with new-onset ascites of uncertain cause; (2) All patients with ascites being evaluated for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.
