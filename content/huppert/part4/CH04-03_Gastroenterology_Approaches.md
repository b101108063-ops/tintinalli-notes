## 1. Approach to Dysphagia

### Definition

Dysphagia refers to difficulty swallowing. It is important to distinguish from odynophagia (painful swallowing) and globus sensation (feeling of a lump in the throat without actual swallowing difficulty).

### Localization

**Oropharyngeal dysphagia**: Difficulty initiating swallowing, coughing, choking, nasopharyngeal regurgitation, and drooling. Causes include neurological conditions (stroke, Parkinson's disease, ALS), structural lesions (Zenker's diverticulum, head and neck cancer), and muscle disorders.

**Esophageal dysphagia**: Sensation of food sticking in the chest after swallowing. Causes include:

- **Mechanical obstruction**: Esophageal strictures, rings (Schatzki ring), web, tumors.
- **Motility disorders**: Achalasia, diffuse esophageal spasm, esophageal body contraction abnormalities, scleroderma esophagus.

### Key Questions

- Is dysphagia to solids, liquids, or both?
- What is the duration and progression?
- Is there associated heartburn, regurgitation, or weight loss?
- Is there history of caustic ingestion or radiation exposure?

### Diagnostic Approach

1. **History and physical examination** to localize the level of obstruction.
2. **Barium swallow** as initial study for many patients with esophageal dysphagia.
3. **Upper endoscopy (EGD)** with biopsy for suspected strictures, rings, or masses.
4. **Esophageal manometry** for suspected motility disorders when endoscopy is non-diagnostic.
5. **CT chest** for evaluation of mediastinal masses or esophageal wall thickening.

## 2. Approach to Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

### Definition

GERD is the backward flow of gastric contents into the esophagus causing symptoms or complications.

### Clinical Features

**Typical symptoms**: Heartburn (pyrosis), regurgitation, and sour taste.

**Atypical symptoms**: Dysphagia, chest pain, chronic cough, laryngitis, and asthma exacerbation.

**Alarm features** requiring prompt endoscopy: Dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss, GI bleeding, and failure of empiric therapy.

### Diagnosis

**Empiric therapy**: Proton pump inhibitor (PPI) trial for 8 weeks in patients with typical GERD symptoms without alarm features.

**Endoscopy**: Indicated for alarm features, failure of empiric therapy, long-standing symptoms, or concern for complications (Barrett's esophagus, stricture, erosive esophagitis).

**24-hour pH monitoring**: For patients with refractory symptoms despite PPI therapy or atypical symptoms.

### Complications

| Complication | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Erosive esophagitis** | Inflammation and erosion of esophageal mucosa |
| **Barrett's esophagus** | Intestinal metaplasia of the distal esophagus; premalignant |
| **Esophageal stricture** | Fibrotic narrowing from chronic inflammation |
| **Esophageal adenocarcinoma** | Malignancy arising from Barrett's esophagus |

## 3. Approach to Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

### Etiology

**Helicobacter pylori infection**: Most common cause worldwide.

**Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)**: Second most common cause.

**Other causes**: Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, hypersecretory states, Crohn's disease, and vascular insufficiency.

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms**: Epigastric pain (may be worse with meals in gastric ulcers, improved with meals in duodenal ulcers), bloating, and early satiety.

**Complications**: GI bleeding (most common), perforation, penetration, and gastric outlet obstruction.

### Diagnosis

**Endoscopy (EGD)**: Gold standard for diagnosis; allows biopsy for *H. pylori* and malignancy evaluation.

**Barium upper GI series**: Alternative when endoscopy is not feasible.

### Treatment

1. **H. pylori eradication**: Standard triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin or metronidazole) or bismuth-based quadruple therapy.
2. **NSAID cessation** and PPI therapy.
3. **Acid suppression**: PPI once or twice daily.
4. **Mucosal protective agents**: Sucralfate for selected patients.

## 4. Approach to GI Bleeding

### Upper vs. Lower GI Bleeding

**Upper GI bleeding**: Bleeding proximal to the ligament of Treitz. Presents with hematemesis, melena, or coffee-ground emesis.

**Lower GI bleeding**: Bleeding distal to the ligament of Treitz. Presents with hematochezia, melena, or occult blood.

### Initial Stabilization

1. **Resuscitation**: Establish IV access (two large-bore peripheral lines or central line), administer intravenous fluids, and transfuse blood products as needed.
2. **Monitor**: Vital signs, urine output, and mental status.
3. **Labs**: Complete blood count, coagulation studies, type and crossmatch, and chemistries.

### Upper GI Bleeding

**Common causes**: Peptic ulcer disease, esophageal varices, gastritis, and Mallory-Weiss tear.

**Diagnostic approach**: EGD within 24 hours of presentation for diagnosis and potential therapy.

**Therapeutic options**: Endoscopic hemostasis (injection therapy, thermal coagulation, band ligation for varices), proton pump inhibitor infusion, and octreotide for variceal bleeding.

### Lower GI Bleeding

**Common causes**: Diverticulosis, angiodysplasia, colorectal polyps, colorectal cancer, and inflammatory bowel disease.

**Diagnostic approach**: Colonoscopy after appropriate bowel preparation. CT angiography or tagged red blood cell scan for localization if colonoscopy is non-diagnostic.

**Therapeutic options**: Colonoscopic hemostasis (injection, coagulation, clipping), angiography with embolization, or surgical intervention.

## 5. Approach to Diarrhea

### Definition

Diarrhea is the passage of loose or watery stools typically more than three times per day.

### Classification

**Acute diarrhea**: Duration less than 14 days.

**Chronic diarrhea**: Duration greater than 30 days.

### Approach to Acute Diarrhea

**History**: Recent travel, food intake, antibiotic use, and sick contacts.

**Evaluation**: Stool studies (culture, ova and parasites, *C. difficile* toxin) if infectious etiology is suspected, there is bloody diarrhea, or the patient is hospitalized or immunocompromised.

**Treatment**: Supportive care with rehydration. Antimotility agents generally avoided in infectious diarrhea. Antibiotics indicated for specific infections (e.g., *Shigella*, *Campylobacter*, traveler's diarrhea).

### Approach to Chronic Diarrhea

**History**: Diet, travel, medications, and associated symptoms (weight loss, abdominal pain, flushing).

**Initial tests**: CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, TSH, celiac serology, and fecal calprotectin.

**Stool studies**: Fecal osmotic gap, stool qualitative fat, and infectious workup as indicated.

**Further evaluation** based on suspected etiology:

- **Malabsorption**: Small bowel biopsy, lactose hydrogen breath test, CT/MRI enterography.
- **IBD**: Colonoscopy with biopsies, fecal calprotectin.
- **Microscopic colitis**: Colonoscopy with colonic biopsies.
- **Pancreatic insufficiency**: Fecal elastase.

## 6. Approach to Constipation

### Definition

Constipation is defecation that is unsatisfactory due to infrequent stools, difficult stool passage, or both.

### Etiology

**Primary (functional)**: Normal transit constipation, slow transit constipation, and defecatory dysfunction (pelvic floor dysfunction).

**Secondary**: Medications (opioids, anticholinergics, calcium channel blockers), metabolic disorders (hypothyroidism, diabetes, hypercalcemia), neurologic disorders (spinal cord injury, Parkinson's disease), and structural abnormalities (colonic obstruction, rectocele).

### Evaluation

**History**: Stool frequency, consistency, straining, sensation of incomplete evacuation, and use of manual maneuvers.

**Alarm features** requiring further evaluation: Weight loss, GI bleeding, anemia, onset after age 50, family history of colorectal cancer or IBD, and acute onset.

**Diagnostic tests**: CBC, TSH, calcium, and glucose. Colonoscopy if alarm features or inadequate response to therapy.

### Treatment

1. **Lifestyle modification**: Increased fluid intake, dietary fiber (25–35 g/day), and regular exercise.
2. **Bulk-forming laxatives**: Psyllium, methylcellulose.
3. **Osmotic laxatives**: Polyethylene glycol (PEG), lactulose, sorbitol.
4. **Stimulant laxatives**: Senna, bisacodyl (short-term use).
5. **If refractory**: Evaluate for defecatory dysfunction with anorectal manometry; consider biofeedback therapy.

## 7. Approach to Abnormal Liver Function Tests

### Pattern Recognition

**Hepatocellular pattern**: ALT and AST disproportionately elevated compared to ALP.

**Cholestatic pattern**: ALP and GGT elevated out of proportion to transaminases.

**Mixed pattern**: Both hepatocellular and cholestatic features present.

### Evaluation of AST/ALT Elevation

**Mild elevation (less than 5 times upper limit of normal)**: Often non-specific; consider viral hepatitis, NAFLD, alcoholic liver disease, medication-induced injury, and hemochromatosis.

**Moderate to severe elevation (greater than 5 times upper limit of normal)**: Consider acute viral hepatitis, acetaminophen toxicity, ischemic hepatitis, and autoimmune hepatitis.

### Evaluation of Cholestatic Pattern

**Intrahepatic cholestasis**: Consider medications, primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, and infiltrative diseases.

**Extrahepatic cholestasis**: Consider choledocholithiasis, pancreatic head cancer, and biliary strictures.

### Key Tests

| Test | Indication |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Hepatitis serology** | Suspected viral hepatitis |
| **Iron studies, ferritin** | Suspected hemochromatosis |
| **Ceruloplasmin, copper** | Suspected Wilson's disease |
| **Alpha-1 antitrypsin** | Suspected AAT deficiency |
| **Autoimmune hepatitis panel** | Suspected autoimmune hepatitis |
| **Anti-mitochondrial antibody** | Suspected primary biliary cholangitis |
| **MRCP** | Suspected biliary obstruction |
| **Liver biopsy** | Indeterminate etiology or staging needed |

## 8. Approach to Jaundice

### Definition

Jaundice is the yellow discoloration of skin and sclera due to elevated serum bilirubin.

### Etiology

**Pre-hepatic**: Hemolysis, Gilbert's syndrome, and Crigler-Najjar syndrome.

**Hepatic**: Viral hepatitis, alcoholic liver disease, NAFLD, autoimmune hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury, and hepatocellular carcinoma.

**Post-hepatic**: Choledocholithiasis, pancreatic head cancer, cholangiocarcinoma, primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, and biliary strictures.

### Evaluation

**History**: Onset, dark urine, pale stools, pruritus, abdominal pain, fever, and risk factors for viral hepatitis.

**Physical exam**: Stigmata of chronic liver disease, hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, and palpable gallbladder.

**Laboratory evaluation**:

- Bilirubin (total, direct, indirect).
- AST, ALT, ALP, GGT.
- CBC with peripheral smear (hemolysis).
- Viral hepatitis serology.

**Imaging**: RUQ ultrasound as initial test to evaluate biliary dilation and liver parenchyma. MRCP or CT if further evaluation needed.

## 9. Approach to Abdominal Pain

### Localization

**Right upper quadrant**: Biliary colic, cholecystitis, cholangitis, hepatitis, peptic ulcer (gastric antrum or duodenal bulb), right lower lobe pneumonia, and myocardial ischemia.

**Left upper quadrant**: Splenic injury, peptic ulcer (gastric body), gastritis, pancreatitis, and myocardial ischemia.

**Right lower quadrant**: Appendicitis, Crohn's disease, cecal diverticulitis, ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, and renal colic.

**Left lower quadrant**: Sigmoid diverticulitis, IBD, ovarian pathology, ectopic pregnancy, and renal colic.

**Epigastric**: Peptic ulcer, gastritis, GERD, pancreatitis, and myocardial ischemia.

**Diffuse**: Small bowel obstruction, gastroenteritis, metabolic disorders, and early appendicitis.

### Alarm Features

- **Peritoneal signs**: Rebound tenderness, guarding, rigidity.
- **Hemodynamic instability**.
- **GI bleeding**.
- **Weight loss**.
- **Age greater than 60** with new-onset abdominal pain.

### Evaluation

1. **History and physical examination**.
2. **Basic labs**: CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, amylase, lipase, urinalysis.
3. **Imaging**: Plain radiography (upright chest X-ray for free air, abdominal X-ray for obstruction), ultrasound (RUQ for biliary disease), CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast (complex presentations).
