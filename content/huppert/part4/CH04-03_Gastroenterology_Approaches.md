# Approaches

## Abdominal Pain

The differential diagnosis for abdominal pain varies based on the area of pain. Pain does not always localize precisely; consider a broad differential regardless of location.

## Nausea/Vomiting

### Differential Diagnosis

- **Neuropsychiatric:**
  - **Intracerebral:** Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) due to malignancy, hemorrhage, abscess, hydrocephalus
  - **Labyrinthine:** Labyrinthitis, motion sickness, malignancy
  - **Psychiatric:** Anorexia, bulimia nervosa, depression (may be associated with delayed gastric emptying)
- **Cardiopulmonary:** Myocardial infarction, congestive heart failure (nausea/early satiety can be early symptoms), post-tussive emesis
- **GI:**
  - **Obstructive:** Pyloric obstruction, SBO, colonic obstruction, SMA syndrome
  - **Inflammatory:** Enteric infections (viral, bacterial), cholecystitis, cholangitis, pancreatitis, appendicitis, hepatitis
  - **Altered function/functional:** Gastroparesis, ileus, pseudoobstruction, GERD, cyclic vomiting syndrome, cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome
- **Other:** Medications, pregnancy, uremia, DKA/HHS, thyroid disease

## Diarrhea

### Definitions and Mimics

- **Diarrhea:** Passage of abnormally liquid or unformed stools at an increased frequency from baseline
- **Pseudodiarrhea:** Frequent passage of small volumes of stool, often with tenesmus, sensation of incomplete evacuation, or rectal urgency; commonly associated with IBS or proctitis
- **Fecal incontinence:** Involuntary fecal discharge, usually due to neuromuscular or anorectal structural etiologies

### "Localizing" Features

| Location | Characteristics |
|----------|---------------|
| **Small intestine** | Watery, large volume, associated with abdominal cramping, bloating, gas; fever, occult blood/inflammatory cells in stool are rare |
| **Large intestine** | Frequent, regular, small volume, often painful bowel movements; fever, bloody or mucoid stools, RBCs/inflammatory cells in stool are common |

> **Note:** Chronic watery diarrhea may be functional (most commonly IBS); organic causes are suggested by weight loss >5 kg, nocturnal diarrhea, GIB, anemia, hypoalbuminemia, and elevated inflammatory markers.
>
> **Fecal osmotic gap:** 290 – 2(fecal sodium + fecal potassium). If <50 mOsm/kg suggests secretory; 50–125 mOsm/kg is equivocal; >125 mOsm/kg suggests osmotic.

### Types of Chronic Diarrhea

| Feature | Secretory | Osmotic | Malabsorptive | Inflammatory |
|---------|---------|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **Diarrheal type** | Watery | Watery | Fatty (excess flatulence, foul-smelling, floating) | Blood/pus in stool |
| **Mechanism** | Increased secretion or decreased absorption of water/electrolytes | Presence of poorly absorbed osmotically active substance | Impaired nutrient absorption and impaired digestive function | Inflammation or invasive infections |
| **Effect of fasting** | No effect | No effect | Improves | Improves |
| **Diagnostics** | Low fecal osm gap (<50 mOsm/kg) | High fecal osm gap (>125 mOsm/kg) | Fecal fat | Fecal WBCs/RBCs, fecal calprotectin, colonoscopy with inflammation |
| **Examples** | Alcohol use, enterotoxins (e.g., cholera), bile acid malabsorption, SIBO, medications, endocrine disorders (thyroid), neuroendocrine tumors, vasculitis | Lactose/fructose malabsorption, celiac disease, osmotic laxatives, sugar alcohols | Celiac sprue, tropical sprue, Whipple disease, pancreatic insufficiency, lymphatic damage, mesenteric ischemia, gastric bypass, short bowel syndrome | IBD, diverticulitis, invasive infections (C. diff, yersiniosis, entamoeba), ulcerating viruses (CMV, HSV), neoplasia, radiation colitis |

### Acute Diarrhea (<3 Weeks)

#### Bloody Diarrhea

| Pathogen | Transmission | Incubation | Treatment |
|----------|------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Shigella** | Outbreaks in institutions (daycare), food supply (raw vegetables); fecal-oral, sexual contact, contaminated food/water, person-to-person | ~3 days | Fluoroquinolone |
| **Salmonella (nontyphonal)** | Ingestion of infected poultry, eggs, milk products; contact with pets or reptiles | <72 hours | Self-limited; support/rehydration; if severe, consider fluoroquinolone |
| **Campylobacter** | Ingestion of undercooked/raw meat (especially poultry) | ~3 days | Self-limited; support/rehydration; if severe, consider azithromycin; risk of reactive arthritis (HLA-B27+) and Guillain-Barré syndrome |
| **E. coli O157/STEC** | Undercooked beef, petting zoos; person-to-person | ~3 days (range 1–10) | Self-limited; support/rehydration; do NOT give antibiotics (increases HUS risk) |
| **Yersinia** | Foodborne (especially pork), untreated spring water, household pets, rare transfusion-related | ~5 days | Self-limited; support/rehydration; if severe, use fluoroquinolone or TMP-SMX |
| **Entamoeba histolytica (amebiasis)** | Fecal-oral route | Subacute (1–3 weeks) | Metronidazole + paromomycin, diiodohydroxyquinoline, or diloxanide furoate |

#### Non-Bloody Diarrhea

Any cause of bloody diarrhea can also present with non-bloody diarrhea.

**Viruses:**
| Virus | Transmission | Incubation | Presentation | Treatment |
|-------|------------|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Rotavirus** | Fecal-oral | <48 hours | Diarrhea, vomiting; most common in young children | Self-limited; support/rehydration |
| **Norovirus** | Fecal-oral, airborne droplets of vomit, contaminated food/water | <48 hours | Diarrhea, vomiting | Self-limited; support/rehydration |
| **Bacteria:** C. perfringens | Improperly refrigerated meat | ~12 hours | — | Self-limited; support/rehydration |
| **B. cereus** | Reheated rice | — | More commonly vomiting than diarrhea | Self-limited; support/rehydration |
| **ETEC ("traveler's diarrhea")** | Fecal-oral | — | Malaise, anorexia, abdominal cramps, sudden-onset diarrhea | Self-limited; support/rehydration; if severe, consider fluoroquinolone or azithromycin |
| **Vibrio vulnificus** | Infected shellfish | — | — | Self-limited; support/rehydration; if severe, consider doxycycline or fluoroquinolone |

**Parasites:** Only test for parasites if diarrhea persists ≥7 days.

| Parasite | Transmission | Incubation | Presentation | Treatment |
|----------|------------|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Giardia** | Contaminated water (outdoor streams), person-to-person (sexual contact) | 7–14 days | Steatorrhea (foul-smelling, fatty stool) with malabsorption | Tinidazole or nitazoxanide; alternative is metronidazole |

### Medications That May Cause Diarrhea

Antibiotics (side effects; also increase C. difficile risk), laxatives, prokinetic agents, antacids, digitalis, colchicine, chemotherapy agents, alcohol.

### "Early Chronic Diarrhea"

- Malabsorption/inflammatory: SIBO, celiac disease, exocrine pancreatic insufficiency
- Ischemia: Colonic ischemia
- Neuropathic: Diabetic autonomic neuropathy
- Functional: Irritable bowel syndrome

### Chronic Diarrhea (>3 Weeks)

**Workup:** Use history/physical to determine the probable subtype (watery, fatty, or inflammatory). Send further diagnostics based on the suspected subtype. Pursue additional testing based on narrowed differential.

### Diarrhea in a Patient with HIV and Low CD4 Count

**Infectious etiologies:**
- **Bacterial:** Campylobacter, Shigella, Salmonella, E. coli, C. difficile
- **Mycobacteria:** MAC (treatment: macrolide + ethambutol or rifabutin)
- **Viral:** CMV (CD4 <50 cells/µL; treatment: PO valganciclovir)
- **Fungal:** Microsporidium, disseminated histoplasmosis
- **Parasites:** Giardia, Entamoeba histolytica, Strongyloides, Cryptosporidium

**Other causes:** HIV enteropathy, HIV-associated malignancy, pancreatic insufficiency, ARV-associated diarrhea, other noninfectious causes.

## GI Bleed

### Upper GI Bleed (UGIB)

**Clinical features:** Hematemesis (bright red blood suggests rapid bleed; "coffee ground" suggests slower bleed), melena (black tarry stool from hemoglobin degradation by bacteria; typically high BUN with dissociated Cr due to urea production from blood breakdown), brisk UGIB can cause bright red blood per rectum.

**Causes:**
| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|---------|
| Peptic ulcer disease | 50% |
| Esophageal varices | 10–30% |
| Gastropathy, gastritis, duodenitis | 15% |
| Severe/erosive esophagitis | 10% |
| Mallory-Weiss tear | 10% |
| Other | Tumor, Dieulafoy's lesion, aortoenteric fistula (ask about prior aortic repairs), Cameron's lesion, hemobilia, hemosuccus pancreaticus |

### Lower GI Bleed (LGIB)

**Clinical features:** Hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum).

**Causes:**
| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|---------|
| Diverticulosis | 30% |
| Malignancy | 20% |
| Colitis (ischemic, IBD, radiation) | 20% |
| Hemorrhoids | 15% |
| Other | Postpolypectomy, solitary rectal ulcer, angiodysplasia, AVM; pediatrics: Meckel's diverticulum |

### Obscure GI Bleed

**Definition:** GIB with negative EGD/colonoscopy; presumed to represent an unvisualized small bowel bleed.

**Causes:** Angiodysplasia is the most common cause.

**Diagnosis:** Testing options include repeat EGD/colonoscopy, capsule endoscopy (first-line for obscure GIB except in the presence of a stricture), technetium-labeled nuclear scan, angiography (can identify bleed if rate >0.5 mL/min), push enteroscopy, deep enteroscopy (spiral, single- or double-balloon; risk of perforation).

### Approach to Managing a Patient with a GIB

1. **Assess hemodynamic stability and ability to resuscitate:**
   - Verify IV access; patient should have at least two large-bore IVs
   - Ensure access to blood products (verify active type & screen; consider massive transfusion protocol if rapid/significant bleed)

2. **Obtain additional historical details and data:**
   - **History:** Symptoms, timing, severity, risk factors, prior GIBs
   - **ROS:** Medications (anticoagulation, aspirin, NSAIDs, steroids), H. pylori risk/history, alcohol use, history of liver disease
   - **Exam:** Vital signs, rectal exam; consider nasogastric lavage
   - **Labs:** CBC, PT/PTT, type & screen, BMP (look for isolated BUN elevation in UGIB), LFTs

3. **Treatment:**
   - **Upper GIB:** Admit to the hospital; place ≥2 large-bore IVs (18 gauge or larger); make patient NPO; fluid resuscitate; order type and screen; transfuse blood products as needed; administer IV PPI prior to EGD. If variceal bleed suspected, also give IV octreotide, IV ceftriaxone, and urgent EGD. If bleeding cannot be stopped by EGD, consider embolization or surgery.
   - **Lower GIB:** Usually self-limited; manage as above with admission, fluid resuscitation, transfusion if severe/ongoing; consider colonoscopy for ongoing bleeding or high-risk features within 24 hours.

4. **Transfusion threshold:** Most commonly used: Hgb <7 g/dL (associated with improved mortality vs. higher threshold). Hgb <8 g/dL may be considered in patients with ongoing bleeding and/or acute coronary syndrome with ischemia.

## Jaundice

### Definition

Yellow discoloration of sclerae, mucous membranes, and/or skin due to overproduction or inadequate clearance of bilirubin. Scleral icterus is evident if bilirubin >3 mg/dL; sublingual icterus if >4 mg/dL; skin jaundice if >6 mg/dL.

### Diagnosis

Check whether bilirubin is unconjugated (indirect) or conjugated (direct).

### Unconjugated (Indirect) Bilirubin

Tightly bound to albumin, not water soluble, cannot be excreted; can cross the blood–brain barrier and cause neurologic deficits.

**Differential diagnosis:**
- **Excess production:** Hemolytic anemia
- **Reduced hepatic uptake or impaired conjugation:** Gilbert's syndrome, Crigler-Najjar, diffuse liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis), medications (sulfonamides, penicillin, rifampin, radiocontrast)

**Gilbert's syndrome:** Autosomal dominant disorder with decreased activity of uridine diphosphate glucuronyl transferase; causes mild jaundice with fasting; does not need treatment.

**Crigler-Najjar:** Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in the gene encoding UGT1A1. Type I: Infants with severe jaundice and kernicterus; requires liver transplantation. Type II: Adults; treatment is phenobarbital or clofibrate.

### Conjugated (Direct) Bilirubin

Loosely bound to albumin, water soluble; causes dark urine or pale stool when excreted.

**Differential diagnosis:**
- **Decreased intrahepatic excretion:** Hepatocellular disease (viral, alcoholic hepatitis, cirrhosis), inherited disorders (Dubin-Johnson, Rotor's syndrome), OCPs, PSC, PBC, biliary atresia
- **Extrahepatic:** Gallstones, pancreatic carcinoma, cholangiocarcinoma, periampullary tumors, extrahepatic biliary atresia

### Bilirubin Metabolism Summary

- ~80% of bilirubin is produced by breakdown of hemoglobin in RBCs and prematurely destroyed erythroid cells in bone marrow
- The remainder comes from turnover of heme-containing proteins in other tissues
- Macrophages break down RBCs into heme, then into unconjugated bilirubin
- Unconjugated bilirubin enters the bloodstream bound to albumin
- In the liver, bilirubin is conjugated with glucuronic acid by glucuronyltransferase
- Conjugated bilirubin is secreted into bile canaliculus → delivered to the small intestine → metabolized by bacteria → eliminated as stercobilin (feces) or urobilin (urine)

### Approach to Evaluating Patients with Suspected Hepatic/Biliary Pathology

**Step 1:** Take a thorough history (alcohol use, medications, family/personal history of autoimmune conditions, sexual history, travel history, IVDU/needle sticks, tattoos, vaccination history). Perform complete GI, GU, neurologic, and infectious ROS.

**Step 2:** Perform physical exam. Vital signs; neurologic (full exam including mental status, asterixis); HEENT (scleral icterus, Kayser-Fleischer rings for Wilson's disease); skin (jaundice, spider angiomas, palmar erythema, gynecomastia, caput medusae); abdominal (RUQ tenderness, hepatosplenomegaly, abdominal distension, fluid wave).

**Step 3:** Check LFTs and other labs as indicated.

**LFT patterns:**
- **Hepatocellular pattern:** Increased AST/ALT, normal or mildly increased AlkP
- **Cholestatic pattern:** Increased AlkP (and GGT), normal or mildly increased AST/ALT
- **Mixed:** Both AST/ALT and AlkP elevated

### Laboratory Tests Commonly Ordered to Evaluate Patients with Liver Disease

| Test | Relevance in Hepatic Disease |
|------|---------------------------|
| **LFTs (AST, ALT, Tbili, AlkP)** | AST released by damaged hepatocytes and damaged myocytes; ALT is more specific to hepatocyte damage. AST:ALT ratio can suggest etiology (4:1 → rhabdomyolysis; 2:1 → alcoholic hepatitis; 1:1 → other hepatitis). ALT typically greater in fatty liver disease. AST/ALT may be normal or low in cirrhosis. AlkP elevations can be due to hepatobiliary disease or bone breakdown. Check indirect vs. direct bilirubin if elevated. |
| **AlkP, GGT** | Elevated GGT confirms hepatobiliary etiology |
| **BMP, Mg²⁺, Phos** | Sodium may be low in cirrhosis; needed for MELD-Na; check creatinine for AKI/HRS; phosphate will be low if hepatic regeneration is occurring |
| **CBC** | Thrombocytopenia occurs due to portal hypertension (not typical in acute liver injury) |
| **Albumin, INR** | Markers of synthetic function; albumin decreased in cirrhosis; INR elevated in cirrhosis |
| **Ammonia** | Not well-correlated with mental status; is the pathophysiologic driver of cerebral edema |
| **Hepatitis serologies** | To evaluate for viral hepatitis |
| **ANA, SMA, IgG** | To evaluate for autoimmune hepatitis |
| **Antimitochondrial antibody, IgM** | To evaluate for primary biliary cholangitis |
| **Alpha-1 antitrypsin** | To evaluate for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency |
| **Iron saturation, serum ferritin, HFE genetic testing** | To evaluate for hemochromatosis |
| **AFP** | Useful for HCC screening |
| **Serum ceruloplasmin, urinary copper** | To evaluate for Wilson's disease |
| **MRCP** | To evaluate for primary sclerosing cholangitis |
| **TTE, right heart catheterization** | To evaluate for portopulmonary hypertension |

### Differential Diagnosis and Evaluation of Hepatocellular Liver Injury

| Differential Diagnosis | Diagnostic Tests |
|----------------------|-----------------|
| Alcoholic hepatitis | CAGE substance abuse screening tool |
| Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) | Advise weight loss if elevated BMI |
| Viral hepatitis | Hepatitis serologies |
| Medication-induced liver injury | Medication review (acetaminophen, antibiotics, statins, antiepileptics) |
| Autoimmune hepatitis | IgG, ANA, SMA, anti-LKM |
| Wilson's disease | Serum ceruloplasmin, urine copper |
| Hemochromatosis | Ferritin, % iron saturation, HFE genetic testing |
| Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency | Alpha-1 antitrypsin level, genetic testing |
| Ischemia | RUQ ultrasound with Doppler to rule out portal vein thrombus |
| Pregnancy-related conditions | Evaluation of pregnancy if indicated |

### Clues for Etiology Based on Time Course and Degree of AST/ALT Elevation

| Time Course | Degree of AST/ALT Elevation | Likely Etiology |
|------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Acute** | Mild (low hundreds) | Alcohol, NAFLD, most autoimmune |
| **Acute** | Moderate (high hundreds to low thousands) | Acute viral hepatitis, biliary obstruction |
| **Acute** | Severe (thousands) | Ischemia (e.g., Budd-Chiari, shock liver), toxicity (e.g., acetaminophen) |
| **Either acute or chronic** | — | Alcohol, Wilson's, Budd-Chiari, Hepatitis B, autoimmune |
| **Chronic** | — | NAFLD, hemochromatosis, alpha-1 antitrypsin |

### Workup of Chronically Elevated AST/ALT

**First-pass workup:**
- Discuss alcohol intake and recommend cessation
- Review medication list and stop hepatotoxic medications
- Hepatitis B surface antigen, antibody, core
- Iron, TIBC, ferritin

**Second-pass workup:**
- RUQ ultrasound (to evaluate for NAFLD)
- ANA, anti–smooth muscle
- Ceruloplasmin (if patient <40 years)
- Rule out non-hepatic causes (muscle disorders, hypo/hyperthyroidism, celiac disease, adrenal insufficiency)
- Consider liver biopsy if >2-fold unexplained, persistent increase despite above workup

### Differential Diagnosis and Evaluation of Cholestatic Liver Injury

| Differential Diagnosis | Diagnostic Tests |
|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Intrahepatic:** Medication-induced liver injury, PSC, PBC, infections (fungal, TB, echinococcus), intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy | Medication review (OCPs, antibiotics, anabolic steroids, TPN), antimitochondrial antibody (AMA), infectious workup/cultures, evaluation of pregnancy |
| **Extrahepatic:** Gallstones, malignancy, PSC, chronic pancreatitis, HIV cholangiopathy | RUQ ultrasound, MRCP, ERCP |

### Workup of Chronically Elevated AlkP

**First-pass workup:**
- Check GGT (if biliary origin, both AlkP and GGT will be elevated)
- Stop offending medications and recheck AlkP
- If AlkP still elevated, check RUQ ultrasound

**Next steps:**
- If dilated ducts seen: Perform MRCP and/or ERCP to rule out obstruction
- If ducts appear normal: Check AMA (associated with PBC)
  - If positive: Perform liver biopsy to confirm PBC
  - If negative: Consider liver biopsy and/or ERCP and assess for other etiologies
