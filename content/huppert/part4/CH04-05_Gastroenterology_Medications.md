# Medications

## Overlap of Diagnostic and Therapeutic Interventions in Gastroenterology

Several diagnostic tests commonly used by gastroenterologists also have therapeutic potential. For information about EGD, colonoscopy, ERCP, and other modalities, refer to the Diagnostics section.

---

## Paracentesis

### Indications

1. All patients with new-onset ascites of uncertain cause
2. All patients with pre-existing ascites in whom SBP is suspected
3. All patients with cirrhosis and ascites who are being admitted to the hospital

### Variations

Diagnostic (obtain small sample for testing) or therapeutic (large-volume paracentesis).

### Tests to Consider

| Test | Notes |
|------|-------|
| **Albumin** | Required for SAAG calculation |
| **Total protein** | <1 g/dL: indication for SBP prophylaxis; >1 g/dL: suggestive of secondary bacterial peritonitis |
| **LDH** | >ULN for serum suggests secondary bacterial peritonitis |
| **Glucose** | <50 mg/dL suggests secondary bacterial peritonitis |
| **CEA** | >5 mg/mL suggests hollow viscus perforation |
| **Alkaline phosphatase** | >240 U/L suggests hollow viscus perforation |
| **Amylase** | >5× ULN for serum suggests pancreatic ascites or hollow viscus perforation |
| **Triglyceride** | >200 mg/dL suggests chylous ascites |
| **Cytology** | Used to assess for malignant ascites; yield improves with multiple samples (>3) |
| **Bacterial culture** | Used for diagnosis of SBP; high load of bacteria suggests secondary bacterial peritonitis |
| **Gram stain** | High load of bacteria suggests secondary bacterial peritonitis |
| **Mycobacterial culture** | Used to assess for mycobacterial infection |

### Relative Contraindications

Fluid <2 cm deep from abdominal wall, severe thrombocytopenia (platelets <50K/µL), DIC; no specific INR cutoff for patients with cirrhosis.

### Complications

Complications of fluid shifts (hypotension, AKI), abdominal wall hematoma, intra-abdominal bleeding, wound infection, persistent leak from the puncture site.

## Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS)

### Concept

Placement of a TIPS (via angiographic techniques) creates a low-resistance channel between the hepatic vein and an intrahepatic branch of the portal vein, helping to reduce elevated portal pressure.

### Indications

Bleeding related to portal hypertension, management of refractory ascites.

### Contraindications

- **Absolute:** Congestive heart failure, severe pulmonary hypertension, severe tricuspid regurgitation, multiple hepatic cysts, active systemic infection, biliary obstruction
- **Relative:** Obstruction of all hepatic veins, hepatocellular carcinoma (especially if centrally located), portal vein thrombosis, thrombocytopenia (platelets <20K/µL)

### Complications

- **Intraprocedural:** Cardiac arrhythmias, liver capsule puncture
- **Post-procedural:** Hepatic encephalopathy, heart failure, liver failure, infection, TIPS dysfunction

## Key Medications in Gastroenterology

| Class | Examples | Mechanism | Uses | Side Effects |
|-------|---------|-----------|------|-------------|
| **Histamine-2 blockers** | Cimetidine, ranitidine, famotidine, nizatidine | Reversibly inhibits histamine H2 receptors → decreased H⁺ secretion by parietal cells | PUD, gastritis, mild GERD | Cimetidine inhibits cytochrome P450, has anti-androgenic effects, crosses blood-brain barrier and placenta; cimetidine and ranitidine → renal excretion of creatinine; all others largely free of these effects |
| **Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)** | Omeprazole, lansoprazole, esomeprazole, pantoprazole, dexlansoprazole | Irreversibly inhibits H⁺/K⁺ ATPase in stomach parietal cells | PUD, gastritis, GERD, Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, H. pylori, stress ulcer prophylaxis | Increased risk of C. difficile infection, pneumonia, acute interstitial nephritis; decreased Mg²⁺ and Ca²⁺ absorption (may mediate fracture risk in elderly) |
| **Antacids** | Aluminum hydroxide, calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide | Neutralizes hydrochloride in the stomach | Dyspepsia, mild GERD | May affect absorption, bioavailability, or urinary excretion of other drugs; all agents carry a risk of hypokalemia |
| **Antidiarrheal** | Bismuth subsalicylate | Exhibits both antisecretory and antimicrobial action | Dyspepsia, traveler's diarrhea (including prophylaxis), H. pylori eradication | Avoid concurrent use of other salicylates |
| **Antidiarrheal** | Loperamide | µ-opioid receptor agonist; slows gut motility | Diarrhea | Constipation, nausea |
| **Antiulcer** | Sucralfate | Forms a viscous paste-like substance that locally coats the gastric lining to protect against peptic acid, pepsin, and bile salts | PUD (especially duodenal ulcers) | Hyperglycemia in diabetics; use with caution in renal failure (aluminum complex) |
| **Antiemetic** | Ondansetron (Zofran) | 5-HT3 antagonist (central acting); decreased vagal stimulation | Nausea/vomiting (especially post-op or related to chemotherapy) | QT prolongation, serotonin syndrome, headache, constipation |
| **Antiemetic** | Metoclopramide (Reglan) | D2 receptor antagonist (central acting); increases GI tract response to ACh → increased LES tone, motility, and gastric emptying | Gastroparesis, persistent GERD | Drowsiness (dose-related), nausea, dystonic reaction (dose-related), drug-induced parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia |
| **Somatostatin analog** | Octreotide | Mimics effects of somatostatin in multiple axes; inhibits secretion of splanchnic vasodilatory hormones → decreased splanchnic blood flow | Acute variceal bleeds, carcinoid tumors and crisis, acromegaly, neuroendocrine tumors (e.g., VIPoma) | Depends on indication; more side effects when used for acromegaly: diarrhea, nausea, abdominal discomfort, hyperglycemia, bradycardia |
| **Laxative** | Lactulose | Provides osmotic load to draw water into GI lumen; bacterial degradation results in acidic pH that inhibits diffusion of NH₃ into blood (by converting NH₃ to NH₄⁺), reducing hepatic encephalopathy | Hepatic encephalopathy, constipation | Diarrhea, gas, nausea |
