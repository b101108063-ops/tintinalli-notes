## 1. Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) Medications

### Aminosalicylates

**Examples**: Mesalamine, sulfasalazine.

**Use**: Mild to moderate ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease.

**Mechanism**: Anti-inflammatory effect on the GI mucosa.

### Corticosteroids

**Examples**: Prednisone, budesonide, methylprednisolone.

**Use**: Induction of remission in moderate to severe IBD flares.

**Important**: These should only be used for induction, not maintenance. Patients with IBD should not be kept on chronic steroid therapy.

### Immunomodulators

**Examples**: Azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, methotrexate.

**Use**: Maintenance therapy for IBD, steroid-sparing.

**Monitoring**: Regular monitoring of complete blood count and liver function tests is required due to risk of bone marrow suppression and hepatotoxicity.

### Biologics

**Examples**: Infliximab, adalimumab, vedolizumab, ustekinumab.

**Use**: Moderate to severe Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis that is refractory to conventional therapy.

**Note**: Used for induction and maintenance therapy.

## 2. Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) Medications

### Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

**Examples**: Omeprazole, esomeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole, rabeprazole.

**Use**: GERD, peptic ulcer disease, H. pylori eradication.

**Mechanism**: Irreversibly inhibit the H+/K+ ATPase pump in gastric parietal cells.

**Dosing**: Typically once daily before breakfast; twice daily dosing may be used for severe cases or Barrett's esophagus.

### H2 Receptor Antagonists

**Examples**: Famotidine, ranitidine (withdrawn from market), cimetidine, nizatidine.

**Use**: Mild to moderate GERD, peptic ulcer disease.

**Mechanism**: Block histamine H2 receptors on gastric parietal cells, reducing acid secretion.

### Antacids

**Examples**: Calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide, aluminum hydroxide.

**Use**: Symptom relief in mild GERD and dyspepsia.

**Mechanism**: Neutralize gastric acid.

## 3. Peptic Ulcer Disease Medications

### H. pylori Eradication Therapy

**Standard regimens** typically include a PPI plus clarithromycin plus amoxicillin (or metronidazole) for 14 days. Alternative regimens include bismuth-based quadruple therapy.

### Mucosal Protective Agents

**Examples**: Sucralfate.

**Use**: Protection of gastric mucosa in ulcers and gastritis.

**Mechanism**: Forms a protective barrier over the ulcer base.

---

## 4. Gallbladder and Biliary Disease Medications

### Ursodiol

**Use**: Cholesterol gallstone dissolution, primary biliary cholangitis, and intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy.

**Mechanism**: Decreases cholesterol secretion into bile and promotes gallstone dissolution.

### Bile Acid Sequestrants

**Examples**: Cholestyramine, colestipol.

**Use**: Postcholecystectomy diarrhea, pruritus in cholestatic liver disease.

**Mechanism**: Bind bile acids in the intestine, preventing reabsorption.

---

## 5. Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement

**Examples**: Pancrelipase (Creon, Zenpep).

**Use**: Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency including chronic pancreatitis and cystic fibrosis.

**Dosing**: Taken with meals; dosing is weight-based and adjusted according to steatorrhea control.

---

## 6. Liver Disease Medications

### Hepatic Encephalopathy

**First-line**: Lactulose.

**Mechanism**: Lactulose is metabolized by colonic bacteria to lactic acid and acetic acid, lowering colonic pH and promoting excretion of ammonia.

**Second-line**: Rifaximin (add-on to lactulose for recurrent encephalopathy).

### Portal Hypertension and Variceal Bleeding

**Nonselective beta-blockers**: Propranolol, nadolol.

**Use**: Primary and secondary prophylaxis of variceal bleeding.

**Mechanism**: Reduce cardiac output and splanchnic blood flow, thereby reducing portal pressure.

### Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis Prophylaxis

**Antibiotics**: Norfloxacin, ciprofloxacin.

**Use**: Secondary prophylaxis of SBP in cirrhotic patients with ascites.

---

## 7. Diarrhea Medications

### Antimotility Agents

**Examples**: Loperamide, diphenoxylate/atropine.

**Use**: Symptomatic treatment of acute and chronic diarrhea.

**Caution**: Avoid in patients with suspected infectious diarrhea or inflammatory bowel disease flare as they may prolong symptoms.

### Bile Acid Sequestrants

**Examples**: Cholestyramine.

**Use**: Bile acid diarrhea following cholecystectomy or in ileal disease.

### Antibiotics

**Examples**: Rifaximin.

**Use**: Traveler's diarrhea, bacterial gastroenteritis, small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).

**Note**: Rifaximin is non-absorbable and has a favorable side effect profile.

## 8. Constipation Medications

### Laxatives

| Class | Examples | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Bulk-forming** | Psyllium, methylcellulose | Increase stool water content and bulk |
| **Osmotic** | Polyethylene glycol (PEG), lactulose, sorbitol | Draw water into the intestinal lumen |
| **Stimulant** | Senna, bisacodyl | Stimulate intestinal motility |
| **Stool softener** | Docusate sodium | Facilitate water and fat penetration into stool |

### Chloride Channel Activators

**Example**: Lubiprostone.

**Use**: Chronic idiopathic constipation, constipation-predominant IBS.

**Mechanism**: Activates chloride channel type 2 (ClC-2) in the intestinal epithelium.

### Guanylate Cyclase-C Agonists

**Example**: Linaclotide.

**Use**: Chronic idiopathic constipation, constipation-predominant IBS.

**Mechanism**: Increases intestinal fluid secretion and accelerates transit.

---

## 9. Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) Medications

### Antispasmodics

**Examples**: Dicyclomine, hyoscyamine.

**Use**: Abdominal pain and cramping in IBS.

### Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)

**Examples**: Amitriptyline, nortriptyline, desipramine.

**Use**: IBS-D (diarrhea-predominant IBS), especially with comorbid depression or anxiety.

**Mechanism**: Modulate visceral hypersensitivity and GI motility.

### 5-HT3 Antagonists

**Example**: Alosetron.

**Use**: Severe IBS-D in women who have not responded to conventional therapy.

**Warning**: Restricted due to risk of ischemic colitis; requires prescribing program enrollment.

### 5-HT4 Agonists

**Example**: Tegaserod (restricted use).

**Use**: IBS-C (constipation-predominant IBS).

### Guanylate Cyclase-C Agonists

**Example**: Linaclotide, plecanatide.

**Use**: IBS-C.

### Chloride Channel Activators

**Example**: Lubiprostone.

**Use**: IBS-C.

---

## 10. Lower GI Bleeding Medications

### Iron Supplementation

**Examples**: Ferrous sulfate, ferrous gluconate, ferric maltol.

**Use**: Iron deficiency anemia due to chronic GI blood loss.

**Note**: Oral iron can cause constipation and GI upset; intravenous iron may be needed in cases of intolerance or malabsorption.
