## 1. Gastrointestinal Tract Anatomy

### Overview

The gastrointestinal tract consists of the mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum), and large intestine (colon, rectum, anal canal). Accessory organs include the salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, and pancreas.

### Stomach

The stomach is divided into several regions: the cardia, fundus, body, antrum, and pylorus. The stomach wall consists of the mucosa (with gastric glands), submucosa, muscularis externa (with three layers of smooth muscle), and serosa.

**Gastric secretions**: Parietal cells secrete hydrochloric acid and intrinsic factor. Chief cells secrete pepsinogen, which is converted to pepsin in the acidic environment. G cells secrete gastrin.

### Small Intestine

The small intestine is approximately 6 meters long and consists of the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. The mucosal lining has circular folds (plicae circulares), villi, and microvilli, which greatly increase the surface area for absorption.

**Duodenum**: Receives bile from the common bile duct and pancreatic enzymes from the pancreatic duct at the ampulla of Vater.

**Jejunum**: Primary site of nutrient absorption.

**Ileum**: Absorbs vitamin B12 and bile salts. Contains Peyer's patches (lymphoid tissue).

### Large Intestine

The large intestine consists of the cecum, ascending colon, transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum, and anal canal. The main functions are water and electrolyte absorption, and storage of fecal material.

**Appendix**: A vestigial structure attached to the cecum; contains lymphoid tissue.

## 2. Gastrointestinal Motility

### Neural Control

The enteric nervous system (myenteric and submucosal plexuses) controls GI motility. Extrinsic innervation includes parasympathetic (vagal and pelvic splanchnic nerves, generally excitatory) and sympathetic (thoracolumbar, generally inhibitory) pathways.

### Peristalsis

A wave of coordinated contraction that propels intestinal contents aborally. Segmentation contractions mix intestinal contents with digestive secretions.

### Gastric Emptying

The rate of gastric emptying varies with the osmolality and composition of gastric contents. Fats and acids slow gastric emptying through the enterogastric reflex.

## 3. Accessory Organs

### Liver

**Anatomy**: The liver is divided into right and left lobes. It receives a dual blood supply from the hepatic artery (25–30%) and portal vein (70–75%). Blood drains via the hepatic veins into the inferior vena cava.

**Functions**:

- **Carbohydrate metabolism**: Glycogenesis, glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis.
- **Lipid metabolism**: Lipogenesis, beta-oxidation, ketogenesis, lipoprotein synthesis.
- **Protein metabolism**: Urea synthesis, plasma protein synthesis (albumin, clotting factors).
- **Detoxification**: Drug metabolism, ammonia conversion to urea.
- **Bile production**: Bile acids aid in fat emulsification and absorption.

### Gallbladder

**Functions**: Stores and concentrates bile between meals. In response to cholecystokinin (CCK) and vagal stimulation, the gallbladder contracts to release bile into the duodenum.

### Pancreas

**Exocrine function**: Pancreatic acinar cells secrete digestive enzymes including amylase, lipase, and proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin, carboxypeptidase).

**Endocrine function**: Islets of Langerhans contain alpha cells (glucagon), beta cells (insulin), delta cells (somatostatin), and PP cells (pancreatic polypeptide).

## 4. Gastrointestinal Hormones

| Hormone | Source | Stimulus | Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Gastrin** | G cells (stomach) | peptides, amino acids, distention | Stimulates gastric acid secretion |
| **Cholecystokinin (CCK)** | I cells (duodenum, jejunum) | Fats, acids, peptides | Stimulates gallbladder contraction, pancreatic enzyme secretion |
| **Secretin** | S cells (duodenum) | Acids, fatty acids | Stimulates pancreatic bicarbonate secretion |
| **Somatostatin** | D cells (stomach, pancreas) | Acids | Inhibits gastric acid, pancreatic enzyme secretion |
| **Motilin** | M cells (duodenum) | Fasting | Stimulates gastric emptying, intestinal motility |
| **Gastric inhibitory peptide (GIP)** | K cells (duodenum, jejunum) | Glucose, fats | Inhibits gastric acid, stimulates insulin release |

## 5. Hepatobiliary System

### Bile

**Composition**: Bile acids (70%), phospholipids, cholesterol, bilirubin, water, and electrolytes.

**Bile acids**: Primary bile acids (cholic acid, chenodeoxycholic acid) are synthesized from cholesterol in the liver. Secondary bile acids (deoxycholic acid, lithocholic acid) are formed by bacterial modification in the colon.

**Enterohepatic circulation**: Bile acids are reabsorbed primarily in the ileum, returned to the liver via the portal vein, and re-secreted into bile. This circulation occurs 4–5 times daily.

### Portal Venous System

The portal vein drains blood from the spleen, pancreas, gallbladder, and most of the GI tract (from the lower esophagus to the upper rectum) to the liver.

**Portal-systemic anastomoses** occur at the gastroesophageal junction (esophageal varices), hemorrhoidal plexus (rectal varices), paraumbilical veins (caput medusae), and retroperitoneal connections.

## 6. Intestinal Absorption

### Carbohydrate Absorption

Monosaccharides (glucose, galactose) are absorbed via SGLT1 (sodium-glucose cotransporter). Fructose is absorbed via GLUT5.

### Protein Absorption

Amino acids and small peptides are absorbed via various transporters. Most protein digestion products are absorbed as amino acids.

### Fat Absorption

Bile acids emulsify dietary fats into micelles, which transport fatty acids and monoglycerides to the intestinal mucosa. Inside enterocytes, fats are reassembled into triglycerides and packaged into chylomicrons, which enter the lymphatic system.

### Vitamin and Mineral Absorption

| Nutrient | Site | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Iron (Fe)** | Duodenum | Active transport; enhanced by acidic environment |
| **Calcium (Ca)** | Duodenum | Active transport and passive diffusion |
| **Vitamin B12** | Ileum | Intrinsic factor-mediated absorption |
| **Bile salts** | Ileum | Active transport |
| **Folate** | Jejunum | Active transport |
