## 1. Chemotherapy

**Cell Cycle Nonspecific Phase**: Activity is not dependent upon the agent being available during a certain phase of the cell cycle. It may be administered over a shorter period of time but may need higher drug concentration (peak).

**Cell Cycle–Specific Phase**: Activity is primarily in one phase of the cell cycle. These agents often need to be administered over a longer period of time.

---

## 2. Hormone Therapy

**Definition**: Medications that suppress endogenous hormones that drive growth of certain tumor types such as breast and prostate cancer.

**Examples**: Selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs) such as tamoxifen are used to treat hormone-receptor positive breast cancer. Aromatase inhibitors (AIs) such as anastrozole, letrozole, and exemestane are used to treat hormone-receptor positive breast cancer. GnRH agonists such as leuprolide are used for testosterone suppression in prostate cancer and ovarian suppression in breast cancer. GnRH antagonists such as degarelix are also used. Selective androgen receptor antagonists such as flutamide, bicalutamide, enzalutamide, and apalutamide are used. CYP17 inhibitors such as abiraterone block synthesis of androgens.

---

## 3. Targeted Therapies

**Nomenclature**: Small molecule inhibitors end in "inib." Monoclonal antibodies end in "mab."

**Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors**: These are small molecule inhibitors given orally. Imatinib (Gleevec) and dasatinib bind to the inactive form of BCR-ABL and prevent activation and are used for treatment of CML. Osimertinib and erlotinib are EGFR inhibitors used in NSCLC. Sorafenib and sunitinib are VEGF inhibitors used in HCC, RCC, and thyroid cancer.

**Monoclonal Antibodies**: Given via infusion. Anti-HER2 antibodies such as trastuzumab (Herceptin) and pertuzumab (Perjeta) are used in breast cancer, with trastuzumab also used in esophageal/gastric cancer. Anti-CD20 antibodies such as rituximab target B cells and are used in B-cell lymphomas. Anti-VEGF antibody such as bevacizumab (Avastin) inhibits angiogenesis and is used in colon cancer and RCC.

---

## 4. Immunotherapy

**Mechanism**: Checkpoint inhibitors enable the patient's own immune system to better target and destroy malignant cells by blocking the signals that turn the immune system off. They release the brakes on the immune response so that the immune system can more effectively fight malignancy.

**Indications**: Approved for multiple types of cancer such as melanoma, RCC, NSCLC, HCC, and many more tumor types. PD1 inhibitors are the first class of drugs with an FDA approval for a genetic pattern rather than an anatomical site, approved for microsatellite instability (MSI)-high or mismatch repair deficient cancers and tumor mutational burden (TMB)-high cancers.

**Examples of Checkpoint Inhibitors by Mechanism**: Anti-PD1 includes nivolumab (Opdivo) and pembrolizumab (Keytruda). Anti-PDL1 includes atezolizumab (Tecentriq), avelumab (Bavencio), and durvalumab (Imfinzi). Anti-CTLA4 includes ipilimumab (Yervoy).

**Side Effects**: Immune-related adverse events (IRAEs) are autoimmune side effects due to excessive immune system activation. Most common include thyroid disease, colitis, hepatitis, and rashes. Less common but more serious include hypophysitis, pneumonitis, myocarditis, myositis, and neurologic autoimmune processes. IRAEs usually develop within the first few weeks to months after treatment initiation but can occur at any time during or after treatment.

**Treatment**: Depending on the grade of toxicity, sometimes clinicians can treat through and other times need to stop immunotherapy and provide immunosuppression.
