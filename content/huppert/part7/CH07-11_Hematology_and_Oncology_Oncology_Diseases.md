## 1. Breast Cancer

**Epidemiology**: Breast cancer is the most common cancer in women. Risk factors include older age, BRCA1 or 2 mutation, family history, menopause after age 55 yr, first birth after age 30 yr, nulliparity, and smoking.

**Screening**: USPSTF recommends screening mammogram every 2 years for patients age 50–74 yr, and for women age 40–49 yr the choice is an individual one.

**Clinical Features**: Breast mass, asymmetry, nipple inversion, edema/thickening of the skin (peau d'orange), and axillary/supraclavicular mass.

**Diagnosis**: Mammogram and ultrasound, and if a mass is identified, get a core biopsy.

**Treatment**: Depends on receptor status and stage.

**Prognosis**: Locally advanced disease (stage I–III) has a goal of cure. Metastatic disease (stage IV) is not curable but treatable, with prognosis depending on receptor status and organs involved.

**Hormone-Receptor Positive (ER/PR+)**: For early stage hormone receptor positive disease (stage I, II, or III), locoregional disease control involves partial mastectomy (lumpectomy) versus mastectomy with sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) with or without radiation therapy. If the patient undergoes partial mastectomy, typically perform postoperative radiation to that breast, which can sometimes be omitted in early stage disease for elderly patients. If SLNB is positive, axillary lymph node dissection or axillary radiation is needed. Adjuvant endocrine therapy is given for 5–10 years. Premenopausal women typically receive tamoxifen first line unless high-risk disease, in which case consider ovarian suppression plus aromatase inhibitor. Side effects of tamoxifen include VTE, hot flashes, and increased risk of endometrial cancer requiring annual GYN screening. Postmenopausal women receive aromatase inhibitors such as anastrazole, letrozole, or exemestane. Side effects include joint aches, decreased bone density, vaginal dryness, and hot flashes. If high-risk disease, also consider chemotherapy. MammaPrint or OncotypeDX tumor testing can help identify patients who will derive the most benefit from chemotherapy, and these tests are only validated for hormone receptor positive disease. Chemotherapy options include AC/T (doxorubicin (Adriamycin) + cyclophosphamide, followed by paclitaxel (Taxol)) and TC (docetaxel (Taxotere) + cyclophosphamide). Discuss egg harvesting prior to chemotherapy with women of childbearing age if interested in future fertility. For metastatic hormone receptor positive disease (stage IV), endocrine therapy is typically first line. Often treat with multiple lines of endocrine therapy first before considering chemotherapy unless there is evidence of visceral crisis. CDK 4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib, abemaciclib) can be combined with aromatase inhibitors. Side effects include lower ANC (although neutropenic fever less common), fatigue, and GI side effects. If metastatic disease to bone, also consider adding zoledronic acid or denosumab after dental clearance due to risk of osteonecrosis of the jaw.

**HER2+**: For early stage HER2+ disease (stage I, II, III), consider neoadjuvant chemotherapy/HER2 targeted therapy prior to surgery. Common neoadjuvant regimens include TCH(P) with docetaxel (Taxotere), carboplatin, trastuzumab (Herceptin or biosimilar), and pertuzumab (Perjeta), or weekly paclitaxel, carboplatin, trastuzumab, and pertuzumab. If the patient achieves a pathologic complete response (pCR) at the time of surgery, finish with 9 months of trastuzumab/pertuzumab. If pCR is not achieved, consider adjuvant trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1) for 14 cycles. If ER+ or PR+ disease, 5–10 years of adjuvant endocrine therapy is also recommended. For metastatic HER2+ disease (stage IV), typical first-line regimen is paclitaxel or docetaxel plus trastuzumab/pertuzumab for 6–8 cycles, then stop chemotherapy and continue trastuzumab/pertuzumab until disease progression.

**Triple Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC) (ER/PR/HER2−)**: For early stage TNBC (stage I, II, III), if triple negative disease and tumor >**1.5 cm**, recommend neoadjuvant chemotherapy first. Common regimen is AC/T plus pembrolizumab with doxorubicin (Adriamycin)/cyclophosphamide followed by paclitaxel (Taxol) with pembrolizumab in the neoadjuvant setting and continuing in the adjuvant setting for a total of 1 year. After neoadjuvant chemotherapy, proceed to surgery. If there is still residual disease at the time of surgery, consider adjuvant capecitabine (Xeloda) for 6–8 cycles. For metastatic TNBC (stage IV), atezolizumab plus paclitaxel is first-line for triple negative metastatic disease if PDL1+.

**BRCA-Mutated Breast Cancer**: BRCA1 and BRCA2 are the most common germline genetic mutations that increase the risk for breast cancer. Treatment depends on receptor type and stage. BRCA+ breast cancers respond well to PARP inhibitors such as olaparib or talazoparib and platinum-based chemotherapy. BRCA 1/2 mutations are also associated with other malignancies, so discuss screening with the genetic counseling team.

## 2. Lung Cancer

**Epidemiology**: Risk factors include smoking, asbestos exposure, older age, and male gender.

**Screening**: USPSTF recommends low-dose CT scan for patients 50–80 yr with 20+ pack-year smoking history who are current smokers or quit within the last 15 years.

**Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC)**: Approximately **85%** of lung cancers. Pathology includes adenocarcinoma (50–**60%**), squamous (**25%**), and large cell (**10%**). Molecular drivers include KRAS, EGFR, BRAF, ALK, ROS1, RET, NTRK, MET, HER2, and PDL1. Staging involves PET/CT unless already known stage IV, MRI brain to evaluate for brain metastases, and molecular testing to evaluate for driver mutations if stage IV. Treatment by stage includes stage I/II with goal of cure where surgical resection is preferred if the patient is a surgical candidate, and adjuvant chemotherapy is usually not recommended for stage I disease but more controversial for stage II. Stage III also has a goal of cure, and typically surgery is recommended if the patient is a surgical candidate as well as adjuvant chemotherapy. If unresectable, chemoradiation followed by immunotherapy is standard. Stage IV is not curable but treatable. First evaluate for driver mutations (targeted therapy) and PDL1 status (immunotherapy). Examples of driver mutations and possible targeted therapy include EGFR mutation treated with osimertinib and ALK mutation treated with alectinib, brigatinib, or ceritinib. If isolated brain metastases, consider surgical resection and/or radiation.

**Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC)**: Approximately 10–**15%** of lung cancers. Pathology shows small round blue cells that are dedifferentiated and aggressive. Clinical features include cough and weight loss. It can cause paraneoplastic syndromes such as SIADH. Staging is divided into limited (hemithorax and regional nodes) or extensive (more widespread). Treatment involves considering radiation to the thorax if limited disease, chemotherapy plus immunotherapy, and prophylactic cranial irradiation for all patients with limited or extensive disease who have a complete or very good partial response after induction chemotherapy.

## 3. Anterior Mediastinal Mass

**Anterior mediastinal mass (4Ts)**: Thymus, Teratoma/germ cell, (Terrible) lymphoma, and Thyroid. Middle mediastinal mass involves lymph nodes and cysts. Posterior mediastinal mass involves neurogenic tumors.

## 4. Gastrointestinal Malignancies

**Esophageal Cancer**: **Epidemiology**: Squamous cell carcinoma occurs in the top third of the esophagus and is more common in Asia, Africa, and associated with alcohol, tobacco, diet (nitrosamines, hot tea), HPV, achalasia, and Plummer-Vinson. Adenocarcinoma occurs in the bottom third and is more common in the United States, associated with GERD, Barrett's esophagus, obesity, and smoking. **Clinical features**: Solid food dysphagia is common. **Staging**: CT C/A/P or PET/CT if not known to have stage IV disease. **Treatment**: If a surgical candidate, chemotherapy/radiation therapy then surgery. If not a surgical candidate, definitive chemotherapy/radiation therapy. Metastatic disease involves chemotherapy and add trastuzumab if HER2+.

**Gastric Adenocarcinoma**: **Pathophysiology**: Adenocarcinomas are rare in the US but common in Japan. Risk factors include atrophic gastritis, gastric polyps, H. pylori infection, pernicious anemia, and Menetrier's disease. Pathology includes ulcerative, polypoid, superficial, and linitis plastica (leather bottle). **Clinical features**: Abdominal pain, weight loss, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, anemia, and melena. **Common sites of metastasis**: Krukenberg tumor (ovary), Blumer's shelf (rectum), Sister Mary Joseph's node (periumbilical), Virchow's node (supraclavicular), and Irish's node (left axillary adenopathy). **Staging**: CT C/A/P or PET/CT if not known to have stage IV disease. **Treatment**: Surgical resection with wide margins (>**5 cm**), chemotherapy, and add trastuzumab if HER2+.

**Colorectal Cancer (CRC)**: **Epidemiology**: Risk factors include adenomatous polyps (villous, sessile worse), IBD (UC), family history (1st degree relative with CRC <60 yr), diet (low fiber), and genetic predispositions. **Hereditary Syndromes**: Lynch I involves early-onset CRC without preceding polyps. Lynch II is the same as Lynch I but also with increased number of polyps, earlier recurrences, and risk of other cancers such as GYN, skin, stomach, pancreas, and brain and breast cancer. FAP (familial adenomatous polyposis) is autosomal dominant. MYH-associated polyposis (MAP) is autosomal recessive and requires prophylactic colectomy. Gardner syndrome involves GI tract polyps plus osteomas, dental problems, benign soft tissue tumors, and sebaceous cysts. Turcot's syndrome is autosomal recessive with higher risk of GI tract polyps and cerebellar medulloblastoma or GBM. Peutz-Jeghers syndrome involves single or multiple hamartomas with pigmented spots on lips/face/genitalia with low malignant potential but increased risk for stomach, ovary, breast, cervical, lung, and testicular cancer, and can cause intussusception and bleeds. Familial juvenile polyposis syndrome is rare in childhood with only a small risk of CRC, and the colon can be removed if complicated by GI bleeding from vascular polyps. **Screening**: Start CRC screening at age 45–50 yr or 40 yr if positive family history. Options include colonoscopy every 10 years, flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years with fecal occult blood test every 3 years (USPSTF) or every 1 year, or FOBT every 1 year. **Clinical features**: Melena, hematochezia, iron-deficiency anemia, and occasionally obstruction. **Pathology**: Mostly adenocarcinomas, less commonly carcinoid, lymphomas, and Kaposi's sarcoma. **Staging**: Colonoscopy, CT C/A/P, CEA, pelvic MRI for rectal primary, and genetic testing for RAS, BRAF, and MSI status. **Treatment**: For disease confined to colon (stage I) or local invasion (stage II), resection for cure. For rectal cancer, neoadjuvant chemotherapy and radiation is often used. For metastatic to regional lymph nodes (stage III), resection and adjuvant chemotherapy with 5FU + oxaliplatin (FOLFOX) or 5FU + irinotecan (FOLFIRI). For distant metastases (stage IV), resection of primary lesion for palliation if needed and chemotherapy with FOLFOX or FOLFIRI or immunotherapy if MSI high. For KRAS/NRAS mutation, add EGFR inhibitor. For MSI high tumors, consider addition of checkpoint inhibitor. **Surveillance**: Check CEA every 3–6 months. Follow-up CT abd/pelvis and CXR for 5 years. Colonoscopy at 1 year then every 3 years.

**Pancreatic Adenocarcinoma**: **Epidemiology**: Older patients, rare before age 40 yr. Location of the tumor within the pancreas: head (**75%**), body (**20%**), tail (**10%**). Poor prognosis with 5-year survival of only **5%**. Risk factors include cigarette smoking, diabetes, chronic pancreatitis, alcohol use, and BRCA 1 or 2 mutation. **Symptoms**: Abdominal pain, jaundice, weight loss, recent mild diabetes, Courvoisier sign (palpable gallbladder due to distal bile duct compression), and Trousseau syndrome (migratory thrombophlebitis). **Diagnosis**: Right upper quadrant ultrasound, CT abdomen/pelvis with pancreatic protocol, endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) with biopsies or ERCP if biliary obstruction, and check CA 19-9. **Staging**: CT chest or PET/CT if not already known to have stage IV disease. **Treatment**: Surgical resection if possible followed by concurrent chemotherapy/radiation therapy. If not resectable, chemotherapy and/or radiation.

**Pancreatic Neuroendocrine Tumors (PNET)**: Nonfunctional (**75%**) has no hormone secretion with symptoms associated with mass such as jaundice or abdominal pain. Genetic predisposition includes MEN1 and VHL. Functional (**25%**) includes gastrinoma secreting gastrin causing Zollinger-Ellison syndrome with peptic ulcer disease, diarrhea, and esophagitis, associated with MEN1 syndrome, treated with PPI and tumor resection. Insulinoma secreting insulin causing hypoglycemia, associated with MEN1 syndrome, treated with tumor resection. VIPoma secreting vasoactive intestinal peptide causing watery diarrhea, hypokalemia, and hypochlorhydria. Glucagonoma secreting glucagon causing dermatitis (necrolytic migratory erythema), diabetes, diarrhea, and glossitis, treated with tumor resection. Somatostatinoma secreting somatostatin with the triad of gallstones, diabetes, and diarrhea. GHRHoma secreting growth hormone releasing hormone causing acromegaly.

**Cholangiocarcinoma**: **Definition**: Tumor of intra/extrahepatic bile ducts, typically adenocarcinomas. **Risk factors**: Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) is a major risk factor in the United States. Other risk factors include UC, choledochal cysts, cirrhosis, viral hepatitis, and liver flukes such as Clonorchis common in Asia. **Clinical features**: Abdominal pain, weight loss, and obstructive jaundice. **Diagnosis**: MRCP versus EUS/ERCP with stenting and biopsy. **Staging**: CT C/A/P with contrast. **Locations**: Proximal third of the common bile duct (Klaskin's tumor) has poor prognosis. Distal extrahepatic has the best prognosis. Intrahepatic is rare. **Treatment**: Easier to resect distal bile duct tumors than proximal. Stents can relieve obstruction. Chemotherapy/immunotherapy is also used.

**Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)**: **Epidemiology**: Risk factors include cirrhosis (but hepatitis B can proceed to HCC without cirrhosis), chemicals such as aflatoxin, vinyl chloride, or thorotrast, alcohol, tobacco, obesity, and iron overload from hemochromatosis or chronic transfusions. **Clinical features**: Abdominal pain, weight loss, and possible paraneoplastic syndromes such as erythrocytosis, thrombocytosis, and hypercalcemia. **Diagnosis**: Diagnosed radiographically with quad phase CT. If imaging is convincing for HCC (LI-RADS 5), then can diagnose radiographically and do not need a biopsy. Check AFP level. **Staging**: CT C/A/P. **Treatment**: For localized disease, surgical resection, radiofrequency ablation, transarterial chemoembolization, or liver transplantation. For metastatic disease or disease no longer controlled with locoregional therapies, treat with a tyrosine kinase/VEGF inhibitor such as sorafenib or lenvatinib and/or immunotherapy.

## 5. Genitourinary Malignancies

**Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC)**: **Epidemiology**: Median age at diagnosis is 64 yr. Most cases are sporadic, with **2%** associated with von Hippel-Lindau. Higher risk in males (M:F = 2:1). **Risk factors**: Cigarette smoking, dialysis, heavy metal exposure such as cadmium, asbestos exposure, autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease, and obesity. **Clinical features**: Hematuria, abdominal/flank pain or mass, weight loss, fever, anemia due to decreased EPO production, and paraneoplastic conditions including polycythemia vera or hypercalcemia from PTHrP production. Left-sided scrotal varices fail to empty when the patient is recumbent because the tumor obstructs venous outflow. Tumor thrombus in the renal vein/IVC causes hematologic spread to lung, liver, brain, and bone. **Diagnosis**: CT A/P or MRI with or without biopsy. **Treatment**: Surgical resection with partial versus radical nephrectomy (excision of kidney and adrenal gland). Medical therapies include VEGF inhibitors such as bevacizumab, sunitinib, or pazopanib, mTOR inhibitors such as temsirolimus and everolimus, and immunotherapy such as nivolumab or ipilimumab.

**Bladder Cancer**: **Epidemiology**: Median age at diagnosis is 69 yr in men and 71 yr in women. **90%** are transitional cell carcinoma with local extension and likely recurrence. **Risk factors**: Cigarette smoking, aniline/azo dyes, and cyclophosphamide. **Clinical features**: Painless hematuria and irritable bladder with dysuria and frequency. **Diagnosis**: Patients over age 40 yr with microscopic or gross hematuria should have a cystoscopy to evaluate for malignancy. **Treatment**: Stage is determined by depth of invasion. For non-muscle invasive disease, transurethral resection of bladder tumor (TURBT) with or without intravesicular chemotherapy. Intravesicular immunotherapy with BCG involves live attenuated Mycobacterium bovis that generates a local inflammatory response activating the host immune system to respond to the tumor. For muscle invasive disease, radical cystectomy with or without systemic neoadjuvant or adjuvant chemotherapy (usually with cisplatin), or bladder preservation therapy with concurrent chemotherapy/radiation.

**Prostate Cancer**: **Epidemiology**: Median age at diagnosis is 66 yr. Lifetime risk is approximately **11%**. It is the second most common cancer in men. **95%** are adenocarcinoma. **Risk factors**: Older age, high-fat diet, positive family history, and pesticides. **Screening**: Controversial with limited evidence. USPSTF recommends PSA screening for men 55–69 yr only after shared decision-making discussion. AFP recommends against PSA screening. **Clinical features**: Early disease is often asymptomatic. Later, obstructive symptoms with difficulty voiding and dysuria occur. Metastatic disease often presents with bone pain since bone metastases are common. **Diagnosis**: Digital rectal exam (DRE), PSA, and transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) with biopsy if abnormal DRE, PSA >10 ng/mL, or >0.75 ng/mL annual increase. **Staging**: Gleason score is a pathological grade incorporated into risk stratification. Higher risk disease needs CT A/P and bone scan. **Treatment**: For low-risk disease, active surveillance versus radiation (external beam versus brachytherapy) versus prostatectomy. For intermediate to high-risk disease, prostatectomy or radiation with or without androgen deprivation therapy (ADT). ADT uses a GnRH agonist such as leuprolide which causes an initial surge in testosterone, so it must be paired with a selective androgen receptor antagonist such as bicalutamide. A GnRH antagonist such as degarelix causes no testosterone surge. Additional agents for advanced disease include abiraterone (CYP17 inhibitor that blocks the synthesis of androgens, given with low-dose prednisone to counteract side effects from decreased cortisol and mineralocorticoid excess) and enzalutamide/apalutamide (androgen receptor inhibitors).

**Testicular Cancer**: **Epidemiology**: Most common in men 20–35 yr. **Risk factors**: Cryptorchidism (surgical correction does not eliminate risk), Klinefelter's syndrome, and infertility. **Germ cell tumors** comprise **95%** and are highly curable. Seminomas (**35%**) have slow growth and late invasion. Nonseminomatous tumors (**65%**) include embryonal, choriocarcinoma, yolk sac, and teratoma. **Non-germ cell tumors** comprise **5%** and are usually benign. Leydig cell tumors secrete estrogen/androgens causing precocious puberty in children or gynecomastia. Sertoli cell tumors are usually benign. **Clinical features**: Nodule or painless swelling of one testicle and rarely gynecomastia. **Diagnosis**: Scrotal ultrasound and serum tumor markers (AFP, βhCG, LDH). Radical inguinal orchiectomy is performed for pathologic diagnosis. NO needle biopsy is given due to concern for tumor seeding in the scrotal sac or metastatic spread into the inguinal lymph nodes. **Treatment**: All receive orchiectomy after semen cryopreservation if the patient desires fertility. Check postoperative βhCG and AFP. Seminomas are very sensitive to radiation, and chemotherapy with single-agent carboplatin or multi-agent chemotherapy is used for higher stage disease. Nonseminomatous tumors are relatively radiation resistant. Consider retroperitoneal lymph node dissection and chemotherapy. Common regimen is BEP (bleomycin, etoposide, cisplatin) for 3–4 cycles.

## 6. Gynecologic Malignancies

**Ovarian Cancer**: **Epidemiology**: Median age at diagnosis is 63 yr with lifetime risk of **1.3%**. **Risk factors**: BRCA 1 or 2 mutation, older age, infertility, endometriosis, PCOS, and cigarette smoking. **Protective factors**: OCPs, previous pregnancy, and history of breastfeeding. **Screening**: No screening is indicated, but if high risk, screening with CA125, pelvic exams, and transvaginal ultrasounds can be considered, though data are limited. **Clinical features**: Subacute presentation with adnexal mass, ascites, early satiety, and weight loss. **Diagnosis**: Transvaginal/abdominal ultrasound usually visualizes a complex adnexal mass. Elevated CA125 is present. Pathologic diagnosis requires surgical exploration because needle biopsy can rupture the mass and cause a worse prognosis. **Treatment**: If a surgical candidate, exploratory laparotomy for surgical excision with or without intraperitoneal chemotherapy (survival benefit in patients with small amounts of residual disease confined to the peritoneal cavity following surgery). Adjuvant chemotherapy involves paclitaxel and carboplatin. Surgical debulking improves survival in metastatic disease. **Surveillance**: Obtain CA125 before surgery. Following CA125 longitudinally post-surgery may help identify recurrence but has not been shown to impact overall survival.

**Cervical Cancer**: **Epidemiology**: Median age at diagnosis is 50 yr. **Risk factors**: HPV, immunosuppression, HIV, and smoking. **Screening**: Pap smear every 3 years for women age 21–29 yr, then every 5 years with HPV co-testing for women age ≥30 yr. **Clinical features**: Abnormal vaginal bleeding such as postmenopausal, postcoital, or intermenstrual bleeding. **Diagnosis**: Pap smear cervical cytology, cervical biopsy, and colposcopy. **Treatment**: For early stage disease (stage I), loop electrosurgical excision procedure (LEEP) or cervical conization. After childbearing, hysterectomy is recommended. For advanced disease (stage III–IV), concurrent chemotherapy/radiation therapy (cisplatin-based regimen) or chemotherapy alone for metastatic disease.

**Endometrial Cancer**: **Epidemiology**: Median age at diagnosis is 60 yr. **Risk factors**: Excess estrogen such as obesity, postmenopausal estrogen without progesterone opposition, tamoxifen therapy, nulliparity, diabetes, and Lynch syndrome. **Screening**: No routine screening is recommended. **Clinical features**: Postmenopausal or irregular bleeding. **Diagnosis**: Transvaginal ultrasound with thickened endometrial stripe prompts an endometrial biopsy. **Treatment**: Surgical resection of the uterus, cervix, and adnexa in early stages. Later stages may require chemotherapy/radiation therapy.

## 7. Melanoma

**Epidemiology**: Fifth most common cancer in the United States. **Risk factors**: Older age, sun exposure, fair complexion, red/blonde hair, high nevi count, and immunosuppression. **Clinical features**: Atypical mole with ABCDE criteria: Asymmetry, irregular Border, Color variation, Diameter ≥**6 mm**, and Evolution. **Diagnosis**: Biopsy. Depth of invasion determines stage. **Treatment**: For stage I/II, wide local excision and sentinel lymph node biopsy are usually sufficient. For stage III, surgery followed by adjuvant immunotherapy for 1 year to reduce the risk of recurrence. For stage IV (metastatic), immunotherapy or BRAF/MEK inhibitor if BRAF V600E mutated. **Systemic therapy options**: Immunotherapy includes anti-PD1 monotherapy such as pembrolizumab or nivolumab or anti-PD1/anti-CTLA4 combination therapy with nivolumab plus ipilimumab. Targeted therapy includes BRAF inhibitor such as vemurafenib, dabrafenib, or encorafenib plus MEK inhibitor such as cobimetinib, trametinib, or binimetinib if the patient has a BRAF V600E mutation. Radiation can treat symptomatic localized areas of disease such as brain metastases.

## 8. Head & Neck Malignancies

**Thyroid Cancer**: **Diagnosis**: Thyroid ultrasound and biopsy. **Risk factors**: Older age, M>F, head/neck radiation, and MEN2A/B (medullary thyroid cancer). **Types of thyroid cancer**: **Papillary** comprises **85%** and is more aggressive if BRAF-mutated. Treatment involves thyroid lobectomy or total thyroidectomy if >**3 cm**, with adjuvant TSH suppression therapy, radioiodine therapy for larger tumors, and BRAF inhibition if indicated for metastatic disease. **Follicular** comprises **12%** where malignant follicular cells invade through the fibrous capsule. Diagnosis involves RAS mutation in **40%** with more aggressive cancers. Treatment is total thyroidectomy with postoperative iodine ablation. **Medullary** comprises 1–**2%** and involves malignant proliferation of parafollicular C cells with elevated calcitonin that deposits as amyloid and stains with Congo red. Diagnosis involves checking calcitonin and CEA. Perform genetic testing for RET mutations and MEN2 syndrome. Treatment is total thyroidectomy. **Anaplastic** comprises **1%** with mortality nearly **100%**, and **90%** of patients have invasion of local structures at time of diagnosis such as larynx, trachea, or esophagus causing dysphagia or respiratory compromise. Treatment involves surgical resection if possible. If locally advanced inoperable disease, concurrent chemotherapy/radiation therapy can be considered. BRAF/MEK inhibition is used if a BRAF V600E mutation is present.

## 9. Sarcoma

**Soft Tissue Sarcoma**: **Epidemiology**: Rare, heterogeneous tumor of mesenchymal origin comprising <**1%** of adult tumors. The most common soft tissue sarcoma is GI stromal tumor (GIST). **Clinical features**: Presents as a gradually enlarging, painless mass. May have neuropathy or pain due to local compression. Common locations are thigh/buttock/groin (**46%**), torso (**18%**), and upper extremity or retroperitoneum (**13%** each). **Diagnosis**: Percutaneous core biopsy. **Staging**: CT chest. **Treatment**: Surgical excision if possible followed by radiation and chemotherapy with AIM (doxorubicin (Adriamycin), ifosfamide, and mesna). Consider neoadjuvant therapy for large tumors to shrink before surgery. For metastatic disease, give chemotherapy and consider resection of metastatic sites if limited.

**Osteosarcoma**: **Epidemiology**: Rare bone tumor, many are secondary to other conditions in adults such as Paget disease of bone. **Clinical features**: Localized bone pain with rare systemic symptoms. **Diagnosis**: MRI of suspected primary site and biopsy (open versus core), coordinating the location of biopsy with the orthopedic surgeon. **Staging**: CT chest. **Treatment**: Neoadjuvant chemotherapy with doxorubicin plus cisplatin, surgical resection, and possibly radiation therapy. Chemotherapy for metastatic disease with or without metastasectomy.

## 10. Central Nervous System (CNS) Tumors

**Epidemiology**: In adults ≤30 yr, primary CNS tumors are more common. In adults >30 yr, brain metastases are more common from breast, lung, prostate, colorectal cancer, and melanoma. **Clinical features**: Small lesions can be asymptomatic. If symptomatic, there may be headache, signs of increased intracranial pressure with nausea/vomiting, new onset seizures, and focal neurologic deficits. **Diagnosis**: MRI with contrast is much superior to head CT. LP is not typically needed unless concern for leptomeningeal disease (LMD). Tissue diagnosis via stereotactic biopsy versus open surgery. Biopsy is often not pursued for metastatic cancer with a known primary site unless surgical resection is indicated for symptom-related reasons. **Treatment**: Glucocorticoids can reduce cerebral edema. Treatment depends on tumor type.
