## 1. Transfusion Medicine

**Standard Testing**: ABO typing determines A/B antigens present on RBCs (blood type A, B, AB, or O). Rh(D) typing tests for D antigen on RBCs such as A+ versus A−. Type and screen tests for unexpected antibodies in patient plasma that might react with transfused product and cause hemolysis, and must be done every 72 hours. Type and cross is the final confirmation test performed by mixing the patient's plasma and the donor's RBCs and is performed when transfusion is imminent or very likely.

**Antibody Testing**: The direct antiglobulin test (DAT or Coombs) tests for autoantibodies on the patient's RBCs. The indirect Coombs tests for antibodies in the patient's plasma.

**Apheresis**: Plasmapheresis removes high molecular weight proteins from plasma and is used in TTP, hyperviscosity syndrome, cryoglobulinemia, myasthenia gravis, Guillain-Barré syndrome, anti-GBM, and some ANCA vasculitidities. Cytapheresis removes cellular components such as leukapheresis for acute leukemia.

**Complications of Transfusion**: Hypocalcemia occurs because citrate used to preserve pRBCs chelates calcium. Volume overload occurs because blood products stay mostly intravascular, so 250 cc of pRBCs (**1 unit**) is equivalent to 1000 cc normal saline, and rapid increases can lead to circulatory overload.

**Special Preparations of Blood**: Leukoreduced blood has decreased risk of febrile reactions and HLA alloimmunization. CMV-reduced-risk blood prevents CMV transmission. Irradiated blood prevents donor T cells from attacking host marrow. Washed blood removes anti-IgA antibodies and prevents anaphylaxis in severe IgA deficiency.

**Types of Blood Products**: Packed red blood cells (pRBCs) at **1 unit** equals 250 cc and raises Hgb by approximately **1 g/dL**. Platelets from one pheresis unit (6pk) equals 300 cc and raises platelets by approximately 30K/μl. Fresh frozen plasma (FFP) at 1 unit equals 250 cc and contains all coagulation factors. Cryoprecipitate at 10 units equals 150 cc and contains fibrinogen, fibronectin, factor XIII, VIII, and vWF.

**Transfusion Reactions**: Urticaria/hives requires stopping the transfusion and giving diphenhydramine. Anaphylaxis requires stopping the transfusion and giving IM epinephrine. Acute hemolytic reaction requires stopping the transfusion, giving fluids, and aiming for urine output >100 cc/hr. Bacterial sepsis requires antibiotics. Febrile nonhemolytic reaction requires stopping the transfusion and giving antipyretics. TRALI requires supplemental oxygen. TACO requires diuretics. Delayed hemolytic reaction is managed supportively.

## 4. Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) T Cell Therapy

**Mechanism**: T cells are collected from the patient and genetically modified with a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) to direct T cells against tumor cells.

**Indications**: B-cell ALL and NHL use CAR directed against CD19. Multiple myeloma uses CAR directed against BCMA.

**Toxicities**: Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS) involves a systemic inflammatory response with fever, fatigue, headache, rash, myalgias, diarrhea, and can progress to SIRS with hypotension and multiorgan failure. CRS occurs in 25–**50%** of patients receiving CART for B-ALL. Diagnosis shows increased CRP, ferritin, and IL-6. Treatment involves supportive care for SIRS, and steroids and/or tocilizumab for severe CRS.

Neurotoxicity involves headache, tremor, expressive aphasia, impaired attention, seizures, and cerebral edema, usually occurring 4–5 days after CART infusion. Treatment involves steroids.

## 5. Stem Cell Transplant Basics

**Types**: Autologous transplant uses the patient's own HSCs to rescue bone marrow after high-intensity chemotherapy and is used for multiple myeloma, some lymphomas, and germ cell tumors. Allogeneic transplant uses donor HSCs to replace bone marrow after chemotherapy and for graft versus tumor effect and is used for AML, ALL, CML, and CLL.

**Sources of Cells**: Peripheral blood stem cells are mobilized with growth factors and are less invasive. Bone marrow is aspirated from the iliac crests. Umbilical cord blood has many HSCs but limited volume.

**Matching for Allogeneic Transplant**: Donors are tested by HLA matching with a **12/12** match being best.

**Side Effects**: Graft versus tumor (GVT) is the desired effect where donor T cells attack tumor cells. Graft versus host disease (GVHD) is the undesired effect where donor T cells attack host cells, affecting the skin, liver, and GI tract. Acute GVHD occurs within 100 days. Chronic GVHD occurs after 3 months. Prophylaxis involves immunosuppressive medicines. Treatment involves increasing immunosuppression and steroids.
