# Hematology Diagnostics

## Evaluation of a Peripheral Blood Smear

A systematic and thorough approach is used to evaluate a peripheral blood smear:

### Step 1: Examine the Feathered Edge

Examine the entire feathered edge first. Large structures such as platelet clumps or blasts can sometimes be found at the feathered edge, so look for these first to avoid missing them.

*(See Figure 7.3A.)*

### Step 2: Find the Monolayer

Start at the very edge of the feathered edge with the microscope on 10× power. Move back toward the body of the slide until the cells form a smooth monolayer.

### Step 3: Evaluate WBCs

Note any morphologic changes (toxic changes, reactive lymphocytes) and any abnormal cell populations. Blast cells have a large nucleus, immature chromatin, a prominent nucleolus, scant cytoplasm, and few or no cytoplasmic granules.

*(See Figure 7.3B for normal and pathologic WBC morphologies.)*

### Step 4: Evaluate RBCs

*(See Figure 7.3C for normal and pathologic RBC morphologies.)*

**Evaluate RBC size**:

- **Microcyte**: Small RBCs
- **Macrocyte**: Large RBCs

**Evaluate hemoglobin distribution**:

- **Hypochromasia**: Light RBCs with more central pallor. Often present with iron deficiency anemia.
- **Polychromasia**: Darker RBCs. Often present with reticulocytosis/regeneration.

**Evaluate RBC shape**: Acanthocytes, schistocytes, spherocytes, etc.

**Evaluate for red cell inclusions**:

- **Nuclei**: Not typically present in RBCs; presence suggests vigorous bone marrow response or myelophthisic process.
- **Basophilic stippling**: Blue dots in the RBC; common in lead poisoning.
- **Howell-Jolly bodies**: Blue inclusions from nuclear remnants; common with splenic dysfunction.
- **Parasites**: Such as malaria and babesiosis.

**Note red cell distribution**: Agglutination, rouleaux formation.

### Step 5: Evaluate Platelets

First, check the feathered edge for platelet clumps, as these will invalidate a low platelet count (pseudothrombocytopenia). Note the platelet size.

*(See Figure 7.3A for blood smear preparation and features.)*

### Figure 7.3: Blood Smear Preparation and Features

- **A**: A blood smear has a thumbprint appearance. The body of the smear is the thickest part where cells are densely packed. The cells become more thinly spread until you reach the monolayer, the area where the red cells form a single layer with little space between them. It is easiest to count and view cells by looking at the monolayer. At the far edge of the smear is the feathered edge, where there are large white spaces between cells and there is a reticulated appearance.
- **B**: Normal mature white blood cell morphology.
- **C**: Normal and pathologic red blood cell morphology.
