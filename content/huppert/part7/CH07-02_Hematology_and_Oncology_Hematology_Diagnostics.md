## 1. Evaluation of a Peripheral Blood Smear

Evaluate a peripheral blood smear using a systematic and thorough approach. First, examine the entire feathered edge because large things such as platelet clumps and blasts can sometimes end up on the feathered edge. Next, find the monolayer by starting at the very edge of the feathered edge with the microscope on 10x and moving back toward the body of the slide until the cells form a smooth monolayer.

**Evaluate WBCs**: Note any morphologic changes such as toxic changes or reactive lymphocytes and any abnormal cell populations such as blast cells, which have a large nucleus, immature chromatin, a prominent nucleolus, scant cytoplasm, and few or no cytoplasmic granules.

**Evaluate RBCs**: Evaluate the RBC size where microcyte means small and macrocyte means large. Evaluate hemoglobin distribution where hypochromasia means light RBCs with more central pallor and is often present with iron deficiency anemia, and polychromasia means darker RBCs and is often present with reticulocytosis or regeneration. Evaluate the RBC shape such as acanthocytes, schistocytes, or spherocytes. Evaluate for red cell inclusions including nuclei (presence suggests vigorous bone marrow response or myelophthisic process), basophilic stippling (blue dots in the RBC common in lead poisoning), Howell-Jolly bodies (blue inclusions from nuclear remnants common with splenic dysfunction), and parasites such as malaria or babesiosis. Note red cell distribution including agglutination and rouleaux formation.

**Evaluate platelets**: First check the feathered edge for platelet clumps as these will invalidate a low platelet count and cause pseudothrombocytopenia. Note the platelet size.
