## 1. General Approaches to Treating Cancer

**Surgery, Radiation, and Medications**: **Surgery (surgical oncology)** involves resecting the tumor with or without local lymph nodes. **Radiation therapy (radiation oncology)** can be external beam therapy or internal such as brachytherapy. **Medications (medical oncology)** include chemotherapy which is cytotoxic therapy that targets rapidly dividing cells, hormone therapy which suppresses endogenous hormones that drive the growth of certain tumor types, targeted therapy which targets specific genes and proteins that drive the growth of certain tumor types, and immunotherapy which activates the patient's own immune system to attack their cancer.

**Local vs. Systemic Treatment**: Local treatment includes surgery and radiation, which are sometimes sufficient for localized disease. Systemic treatment includes medications as above and aims to treat cancer cells that may have spread throughout the body.

**Timing/Order of Therapies**: **Neoadjuvant** therapy is given before surgery to shrink the tumor to make it easier to resect and/or to better evaluate how the tumor responds to therapy before it is resected. **Adjuvant** therapy is given after surgery to kill any residual cancer cells and reduce recurrence risk. **Concurrent chemoradiation** involves giving chemotherapy and radiation together, where the chemotherapy is usually radiosensitizing.

## 2. Oncologic Emergencies

**Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP) from Brain Lesions**: Pathophysiology involves either a primary brain tumor or metastasis causing vasogenic edema, leading to increased ICP. Symptoms include headache, blurry vision, focal neurologic deficits, and stroke. Diagnosis involves NCHCT versus contrast MRI depending on urgency, with MRI being superior. Treatment involves mannitol, steroids, and emergent neurosurgical intervention or radiation. Call neurosurgery and radiation oncology.

**Neoplastic Epidural Spinal Cord Compression**: Pathophysiology involves compression of the spinal cord from lesions in vertebral bones, which is more common than paraspinal mass extending locally into the epidural space. The annual incidence is 3–**5%** among patients with metastatic cancer, and approximately **50%** of cases are from prostate, lung, and breast cancer. Symptoms include back or SI joint pain, asymmetric leg weakness, saddle anesthesia, urinary retention, and fecal incontinence, with bowel/bladder problems being typically late findings. Diagnosis involves urgent MRI of the total spine with and without contrast. Treatment involves calling neurosurgery and radiation oncology ASAP, starting steroids such as dexamethasone 10 mg once then 4 mg every 6 hours. Emergent neurosurgical intervention and/or radiation therapy is usually required.

**Superior Vena Cava (SVC) Syndrome**: Pathophysiology involves extrinsic compression of the SVC by a tumor or mediastinal lymph nodes that cause increased upper body venous pressure. It is most common in NSCLC, small cell lung cancer, and NHL. Symptoms include sudden appearance of dilated veins on the chest, which is the herald onset of SVC syndrome. Patients may also have facial swelling, "head fullness," shortness of breath, blurry vision, and hypotension from decreased venous return to the right atrium. Diagnosis involves imaging demonstrating SVC compression by tumor. Treatment depends on the severity of symptoms. If life-threatening symptoms such as stridor or CNS symptoms are present, consider endovascular stent, radiation, or rarely tumor resection. If no life-threatening symptoms, chemotherapy alone may be sufficient for chemotherapy-responsive tumors such as SCLC or lymphoma. Anticoagulate if thrombus is detected.

**Hypercalcemia of Malignancy**: Pathophysiology involves multiple possible mechanisms. Tumor secretion of PTHrP is most common and often occurs with SCC of the lung, breast cancer, or RCC. Osteolytic metastases cause increased bone turnover and are commonly seen in MM or breast cancer. Tumor production of 1,25-OH vitamin D can occur with Hodgkin's and NHL. Symptoms are remembered as "stones, groans, moans, psychiatric overtones": kidney stones, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, bony pain, and altered mental status. Diagnosis involves checking the calcium level corrected for albumin and dehydration with increased Cr and Na⁺. Treatment involves aggressive hydration at 200–300 mL/hr to maintain UOP of 100–150 ml/hr with caution in heart failure and volume overload. Generally only use diuretics if there is concern for iatrogenic hypervolemia. Calcitonin at 4 IU/kg every 6–12 hours for up to 48 hours is used because patients develop tachyphylaxis after 48 hours. Bisphosphonates such as zoledronic acid are given as a one-time dose so full dose is okay in renal dysfunction and no dental evaluation is needed prior to treatment. Denosumab is a monoclonal antibody to RANK-ligand that blocks activation of osteoclasts. Generally, bisphosphonates are preferred over denosumab for acute treatment.

**Neutropenic Fever**: See details in the hematology section under oncologic emergencies.

**DIC and TLS**: Less common with solid than hematologic malignancies but can occur with solid tumor malignancies. See details in the hematology section under oncologic emergencies.
