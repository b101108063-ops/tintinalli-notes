## 1. Rheumatoid Arthritis

**Methotrexate and mortality in patients with rheumatoid arthritis: a prospective study** (Lancet 2002;359:1173–1177). This prospective observational study demonstrated a **60%** decrease in all-cause mortality and a **70%** decrease in cardiovascular mortality among adults with RA treated with methotrexate compared to other DMARDs or prednisone. Decreased all-cause and CV mortality was not seen for sulfasalazine or hydroxychloroquine. This study underscores the notion that systemic inflammation in RA contributes to cardiovascular mortality and that methotrexate ameliorates this.

**The Tight Control for Rheumatoid Arthritis (TICORA) Study** (Lancet 2004;364:263–269). This single-blind randomized controlled trial evaluated monthly visits with DMARD titration (MTX, sulfasalazine, HCQ) to a goal disease activity score less than 2.4 versus quarterly visits with titration per usual care. Patients in the intensive management group had higher rates of remission (**65%** versus **16%**) at 18 months. This study supported a treat-to-target approach using a disease activity score to guide therapy escalation.

## 2. Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

**A randomized study of the effect of withdrawing hydroxychloroquine sulfate in systemic lupus erythematosus** (N Engl J Med 1991;324:150–154). This small double-blind randomized controlled trial randomized patients to either continue hydroxychloroquine therapy or replace it with placebo for 24 weeks. The relative risk of clinical flare was 2.5 times higher in the placebo arm than the hydroxychloroquine arm. This demonstrated that patients with quiescent SLE on hydroxychloroquine were less likely to have a clinical flare if they were maintained on the drug.

**Mycophenolate mofetil versus cyclophosphamide for induction treatment of lupus nephritis** (J Am Soc Nephrol 2009;20:1103–1112). This randomized single-blind study compared IV cyclophosphamide (0.5–1 g per m squared monthly) to oral mycophenolate (3 g per day) for treatment of class III and IV lupus nephritis. Mycophenolate was non-inferior to cyclophosphamide for achieving the primary outcome of decrease in UPCR and stabilization in serum creatinine at 24 weeks. Prior studies established fewer infections, alopecia, and amenorrhea with mycophenolate compared to cyclophosphamide.

**Development of autoantibodies before the clinical onset of systemic lupus erythematosus** (N Engl J Med 2003;349:1526–1533). This retrospective study of 130 armed forces personnel who provided serum on enlistment and biennially thereafter showed that autoantibodies including ANA and anti-dsDNA develop years before SLE diagnosis (mean of 3 years and 2.2 years respectively). This study suggests that a break in humoral immune tolerance is not sufficient to cause pathology and raises questions about whether additional immunologic and environmental modifying factors are needed to cause disease.

## 3. Systemic Sclerosis

**Mycophenolate mofetil versus oral cyclophosphamide in scleroderma-related interstitial lung disease (SLS II Study)** (Lancet Respir Med 2016;4:708–719). This randomized controlled double-blind parallel group trial randomized patients with scleroderma-related ILD to receive mycophenolate mofetil (target dose 1,500 mg twice daily) for 24 months or oral cyclophosphamide (target dose 2.0 mg per kg per day) for 12 months followed by placebo for 12 months. A post hoc analysis demonstrated significant improvements in lung function (in terms of percent FVC) for both groups, but mycophenolate mofetil was better tolerated and demonstrated less toxicity.

## 4. Vasculitis

**Rituximab versus cyclophosphamide for ANCA-associated vasculitis (RAVE Trial)** (N Engl J Med 2010;363:221–232). This multicenter randomized double-blind double-dummy non-inferiority trial studied rituximab versus cyclophosphamide for patients with ANCA-positive granulomatosis with polyangiitis or microscopic polyangiitis. The primary endpoint was remission of disease without prednisone at 6 months. The trial demonstrated non-inferiority for rituximab compared to cyclophosphamide.

**Trial of tocilizumab in giant cell arteritis** (N Engl J Med 2017;377:317–328). This randomized trial assigned 251 patients with giant cell arteritis to tocilizumab SC weekly, every other week, or placebo, combined with prednisone tapers of various durations. The primary endpoint was sustained glucocorticoid-free remission at 1 year. The trial demonstrated that tocilizumab (received weekly or every other week) plus a 26-week prednisone taper was superior to either a 26-week or 52-week prednisone taper plus placebo, with 53–**56%** remission versus **18%** in the placebo group.
