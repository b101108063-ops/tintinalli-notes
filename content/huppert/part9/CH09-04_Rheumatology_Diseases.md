# Diseases

This chapter covers diseases and pathophysiology in rheumatology.

---

## ANA-Associated Connective Tissue Disorders

### Overview

Rheumatologic conditions associated with a positive ANA:
1. Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
2. Systemic sclerosis (SSc)
3. Myositis (often ANA-negative; approximately two-thirds of dermatomyositis/polymyositis cases are ANA-positive)
4. Mixed connective tissue disease (MCTD)
5. Primary Sjögren's

### ANA Testing Pearls

Antinuclear antibodies (ANA) are autoantibodies that bind to the contents of the cell nucleus.

- ANA can be elevated in many disorders including autoimmune conditions, infection, and malignancy.
- A small proportion of healthy women have a positive ANA, so it is nonspecific and should only be sent if clinical suspicion is high.
- The level of autoantibody is reported as a titer, which is the highest dilution of the serum at which the autoantibodies are still detectable (e.g., 1:640 is more dilute than 1:80, suggesting more autoantibodies are present in the 1:640 sample).
- The probability of autoimmunity increases with higher ANA titers:
  - ANA ≥1:80 is required by EULAR/ACR SLE classification criteria.
  - ANA ≥1:640 is highly specific for SLE.
- If an ELISA-based ANA test is negative but clinical suspicion for CTD is high, ask the lab to carry out an immunofluorescence (IF) microscopy-based ANA test, which is more specific.

## Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

### Pathogenesis

Decreased clearance and increased responsiveness to self-nucleic acids leads to excessive IFNα production by innate immune cells (especially plasmacytoid dendritic cells), resulting in inappropriate activation of autoreactive B cells, autoantibody production, and immune complex deposition in skin, glomeruli, joints, and other tissues.

### Epidemiology

Approximately 9 in 10 affected patients are women of childbearing age (F:M = 1:1 before adolescence, 9:1 after). US prevalence is 100 per 100,000 white women, 400 per 100,000 Black women.

### Clinical Features

Features in the 2019 EULAR/ACR SLE Classification Criteria are noted with point values:

**Constitutional:** Fever, fatigue, weight loss.

**Musculoskeletal:**
- Arthralgias/myalgias (very common).
- Inflammatory polyarthritis of the hands/wrists/knees.
- Jaccoud arthropathy (reducible finger swan neck deformities).

**Dermatologic:**
- Acute cutaneous lupus (malar "butterfly" rash).
- Discoid lesions.
- Subacute cutaneous lupus lesions.
- Oral ulcers.
- Non-scarring alopecia.
- Raynaud's phenomenon.
- Photosensitivity.

**Hematologic:**
- Leukopenia.
- Thrombocytopenia.
- Autoimmune hemolysis.
- Antiphospholipid antibodies and clinical thrombosis.
- Seven-fold increased risk of non-Hodgkin's lymphoma.

**Central Nervous System:**
- Acute confusional state.
- Psychosis.
- Seizures.
- Transverse myelitis.

**Cardiac:**
- Acute pericarditis.
- Pericardial effusion.
- Myocarditis.
- Libman-Sacks endocarditis.
- Three- to ten-fold increased risk of ASCVD.

**Pulmonary:**
- Pleural effusion (30–40% lifetime prevalence).
- Chronic ILD.
- Acute lupus pneumonitis.
- Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage (high DLCO).
- Shrinking lung syndrome.

**Renal:**
- Proteinuria ≥0.5 g/24 hours (30–40% lifetime prevalence).
- Class II or V nephritis.
- Class III or IV nephritis.
- Anti-dsDNA is associated with increased risk of lupus nephritis.

**Gastrointestinal:** Hepatitis, peritonitis, mesenteric vasculitis (all rare).

**Autoantibodies/Hypocomplementemia:**
- Antiphospholipid antibodies (lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin, or anti-β2GP1).
- Decreased C3 and/or C4.
- Anti-dsDNA or anti-Smith antibody.

### Notable Syndromes

**Drug-induced lupus:**
- Arthritis/arthralgias, serositis, and rash. Notably, no CNS or renal involvement.
- **Culprit medications associated with +antihistone:** Hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid.
- **Other medications associated with +dsDNA:** Minocycline, penicillamine, TNF inhibitors.
- Additional culprits include HCTZ, ACE inhibitors, and chlorpromazine.

**Discoid lupus erythematosus (DLE):**
- Cutaneous plaques with central hypopigmentation and peripheral hyperpigmentation.
- Most patients with DLE do NOT have systemic lupus symptoms.

### Diagnosis

**2019 EULAR/ACR Classification Criteria:** For patients with ANA ≥1:80, score the clinical features. Only count the highest weighted criteria in each category and do not count features better explained by another disease process. Patients with a score ≥10 are classified as having SLE.

**Important caveat:** Clinical diagnosis is still the gold standard. These classification criteria are more specific than sensitive; intended to ensure homogeneity of clinical trial patients, not as diagnostic criteria.

### Lupus Flare vs. Infection

| Favors Lupus Flare | Favors Infection |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Predominant ESR elevation with normal CRP | Concomitant ESR and CRP elevation |
| No fever | Fever |
| C3/C4 below baseline | C3/C4 at baseline |
| Anti-dsDNA above baseline | Anti-dsDNA at baseline |
| Leukopenia | Leukocytosis |
| Thrombocytopenia | Thrombocytosis |

### Serial Disease Assessment

Each clinic visit: CBC with differential, BMP, ESR, CRP, C3/C4, urinalysis, UPCR, anti-dsDNA.

### Treatment

**Non-organ threatening disease:**
- Start hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil) for all patients regardless of disease activity unless contraindicated. Reduces flare frequency and incidence of renal disease; also effective for arthritis/arthralgias and skin disease. Requires annual ophthalmologic exams.
- **Arthritis:** Low-dose prednisone can be helpful.
- **Arthralgias:** Limited evidence to guide sequence of therapy.
- **Skin disease:** Depending on severity: sunscreen/hydroxychloroquine → topicals (steroids, tacrolimus) → mycophenolate → belimumab.

**Moderate organ-threatening disease:**
- Profound cytopenias, acute CNS lupus with seizures, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, myocarditis.
- Prednisone 1 mg/kg OR methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily ×3–5 days followed by prednisone taper.
- IVIG may be effective for SLE-related ITP.

**Severe organ-threatening disease:**
- Proliferative class III/IV nephritis. See features and management below.

### Features and Management of Lupus Nephritis

| Class | Histopathology | Management |
|-------|---------------|------------|
| **Class I (Minimal Mesangial)** | LM appearance normal; immune complexes visible in the mesangium by IF or EM. | Best prognosis. Monitor proteinuria. ACEi/ARB, blood pressure control, sodium bicarbonate and phosphate binder if needed. |
| **Class II (Mesangial Proliferative)** | Mesangial proliferation visible on LM. | Monitor; may not require treatment. |
| **Class III (Focal Proliferative)** | Thickened capillary loops on LM, less than half of glomeruli affected. Subendothelial immune complexes on EM. Lesions scored as active or chronic. | If lesions are active on biopsy, treat with: 1 g IV methylprednisolone daily ×3–5 days, then prednisone taper. Mycophenolate 2–3 g daily ×6 months (non-inferior to cyclophosphamide with lower risk of infection, alopecia, amenorrhea). |
| **Class IV (Diffuse Proliferative)** | Thickened capillary loops on LM, more than half of glomeruli affected. Subendothelial immune complexes on EM. Lesions scored as active or chronic. | Same as Class III. Worst prognosis. |
| **Class V (Membranous)** | Subepithelial immune complex deposition with GBM thickening. Commonly manifests with nephrotic range proteinuria. | Moderately poor prognosis due to extrarenal effects of nephrotic syndrome. Treatment approach usually similar to Class III/IV due to risks from proteinuria. |
| **Class VI (Advanced Sclerosing)** | More than 90% globally sclerotic glomeruli. Final common outcome of other classes. | Poor prognosis; often advanced CKD/peridialysis. Not responsive to immunosuppression. ACEi/ARB if GFR/serum potassium allow, blood pressure control, sodium bicarbonate and phosphate binder if needed. |

## Systemic Sclerosis (SSc)

### Pathogenesis

Poorly understood confluence of:
1. Microvasculopathy
2. Fibrosis
3. Humoral autoimmunity

### Epidemiology

Prevalence is 275 cases per 1 million. SLE is ten times more common than SSc.

### Clinical Features

Features in the 2013 EULAR/ACR SSc Classification Criteria are noted with point values:

**Dermatologic:**
- Skin thickening of the hands (9 points if proximal to MCPs, 4 points if distal).
- Puffy fingers/non-pitting edema.
- Facial skin tightening.
- Telangiectasias.
- Calcinosis.
- Skin involvement is typically most severe over the first 18–24 months, then improves with or without therapy.

**Vascular:**
- Raynaud's phenomenon (present in most patients).
- Nailfold capillary disease (reduced density of nailfold capillaries).
- Fingertip ulcers or pitting scars.

**Pulmonary:**
- ILD (especially in diffuse SSc).
- WHO Group I pulmonary HTN (especially in limited SSc).

**Cardiac:** Arrhythmias, HFpEF, rarely myocarditis.

**Gastrointestinal:**
- Reduced oral aperture.
- GERD.
- Gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE, "watermelon stomach," usually heralded by iron deficiency anemia).
- Colonic pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie's).
- SIBO.

**Renal:** Scleroderma renal crisis (SRC). Can be conceptualized as "Raynaud's of the kidneys": Narrowing of renal arterioles leads to hypoperfusion of the juxtaglomerular apparatus and consequent excess renin production. Presents with hypertensive emergency (may have retinopathy, PRES), proteinuric AKI, and MAHA. SRC is the presenting manifestation in approximately 1 in 10 patients with SSc. Most patients are Anti-RNA Pol III positive.

**Musculoskeletal:** Inflammatory non-erosive polyarthritis of the hands and wrists, involving the DIPs.

**Autoantibodies:** Anti-Scl70, anticentromere, anti-RNA Pol III (max points from autoAb = 3).

### Limited vs. Diffuse Disease

These terms refer ONLY to the extent of skin involvement — both can feature organ fibrosis and vasculopathy.

| Feature | Limited SSc | Diffuse SSc |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Extent of skin involvement | Skin distal to knees and elbows only | Skin proximal to knees/elbows, including trunk |
| Proportion of SSc patients | Most patients | Fewer patients |
| Associated autoAb | Anti-centromere | Anti-Scl70 |
| Pulmonary fibrosis | Less common | More common |
| Pulmonary HTN | More common | Less common |
| Scleroderma renal crisis | Less common | More common |

### Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis, but score ≥9 on 2013 EULAR/ACR SSc Classification Criteria is highly specific.

### Treatment

Treatments are tailored to the involved organ system(s):
- **Derm:** Mycophenylate if severely symptomatic and early in disease course (e.g., <1 year; symptoms often improve without treatment within 2 years).
- **ILD:** Mycophenylate is treatment of choice depending on ILD severity. Lung transplantation may be considered.
- **Pulmonary HTN:** Monotherapy with endothelin receptor antagonist (e.g., ambrisentan) or PDE inhibitor (e.g., tadalafil) for WHO functional class I symptoms; combination therapy for class II and III symptoms. Consider evaluation for lung transplantation.
- **Myocarditis:** Glucocorticoids.
- **Scleroderma renal crisis:** Titrate ACE inhibitor to max dose (captopril PO or enalaprilat IV), then add CCB (amlodipine PO or nicardipine IV). Do not reduce MAP by more than 10–20 mmHg in the first 24 hours; goal is normotension within 72 hours. No role for immunosuppression.

## Inflammatory and Noninflammatory Myopathies

### Differential Diagnosis for Inflammatory Myopathies

- Dermatomyositis (DM)
- Polymyositis (PM)
- Immune-mediated necrotizing myositis (IMNM)
- Inclusion body myositis (IBM)

### Differential Diagnosis for Noninflammatory Myopathies

**Endocrine:**
- Hypothyroid myopathy
- Glucocorticoid-related myopathy (Cushing's or exogenous steroids)

**Medication/Toxin-induced:**
- Alcohol, cocaine, NRTI (AZT in particular), colchicine, gemcitabine, statins

**Inherited:**
- Glycogen storage diseases
- Disorders of muscle lipid export (e.g., carnitine transporter deficiency)
- Mitochondrial myopathies (impairment in oxidative phosphorylation, e.g., MERRF)
- Duchenne/Becker muscular dystrophy
- Limb-Girdle muscular dystrophy

### Distinguishing Inflammatory vs. Noninflammatory Myopathies

| Feature | Favors Inflammatory | Favors Noninflammatory |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **History** | Decreased strength over weeks to months | Decreased strength over years |
| **Exam** | Gottron sign/papules or heliotrope rash (DM) | — |
| **Muscle biopsy** | Perifascicular atrophy (specific for DM); endomysial/perimysial/perivascular lymphocyte infiltrate (IBM, Duchenne/Becker, Limb-Girdle) | — |
| **Treatment response** | Rapid increase in strength with glucocorticoids | — |

**Anti-Synthetase Syndrome:** Constellation of ILD, mechanic's hands (fissuring lateral aspect/tip of index fingers), Raynaud's, non-erosive peripheral arthritis occurring in patients with autoantibodies to one or more tRNA synthetase (Jo1, Ha, PL7, PL12, OJ, Zo, KS, EJ).

## Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD)

### Pathogenesis

Not known in detail; presumably similar to constituent diseases SLE, SSc, and myositis.

### Epidemiology

Rare (exact prevalence not described); F:M = 4:1.

### Diagnosis

MCTD is an overlap syndrome in which patients satisfy classification criteria for at least 2 of SLE, SSc, and DM/PM, AND have high titer anti-U1RNP autoantibodies.

| Diagnostic Label | Classification Criteria Met | Anti-U1RNP |
|-----------------|---------------------------|------------|
| **MCTD** | ≥2 | Positive |
| **Overlap Syndrome** | ≥2 | Negative |
| **UCTD** | 0* | Negative |

### Clinical Features

- **Vascular:** Raynaud's, pulmonary hypertension. Vascular manifestations are common and are the main driver of mortality.
- **Other:** Arthritis, puffy fingers, sclerodactyly, serositis, esophageal dysmotility, myositis, ILD. Renal disease is uncommon.

### Treatment

Targeted toward organ-system-specific manifestations; see SLE/SSc/myositis sections.

## Sjögren's Syndrome

### Pathogenesis

Autoimmune destruction of the lacrimal and salivary glands. Unclear underlying mechanism but increased risk associated with specific HLA alleles implicates cell-mediated autoimmunity.

### Epidemiology

Prevalence of 0.3–1 per 1000 worldwide. F:M = 9:1.

### Clinical Features

**Glandular manifestations:**
- **Severe dry eye:** May cause corneal ulceration.
- **Dry mouth:** May cause dental caries and result in malnutrition.

**Extraglandular manifestations:**
- **Constitutional:** Fatigue.
- **Derm:** Xerosis; cutaneous leukocytoclastic vasculitis (small vessel; approximately half of cases associated with cryoglobulins); subacute cutaneous lupus rash (annular photosensitive lesions) associated with anti-Ro antibodies.
- **Articular:** Inflammatory polyarthritis resembling RA except non-erosive.
- **Pulmonary:** Five to ten percent overall incidence including bronchiolitis obliterans, ILD.
- **Renal:** Five to ten percent overall incidence including type 1 RTA, interstitial nephritis, immune-complex GN.
- **Vascular:** Cryoglobulinemic vasculitis; Raynaud's phenomenon.
- **Neurologic:** Eight to ten percent overall incidence including CNS demyelinating lesions, myelopathy/transverse myelitis, cranial neuropathies, mononeuritis multiplex, distal symmetric polyneuropathy.
- **GI:** Primary biliary cholangitis; autoimmune hepatitis.
- **Heme:** Lymphoma; increased risk of non-Hodgkin lymphoma, largely MALT lymphoma in salivary glands.

**Immunologic:** Cryoglobulinemia; positive RF in approximately three-quarters of patients.

### Disease Categories

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Primary Sjögren's** | Not meeting criteria for another CTD or rheumatic disease. |
| **Secondary Sjögren's** | Occurring in the context of another rheumatic disease, most commonly RA or SLE (10–30% prevalence in these groups), but also scleroderma, myositis. |

### Diagnosis

**2017 EULAR/ACR Sjögren's Classification Criteria:** Sicca symptoms and ≥4 points below classified as Sjögren's.

| Criterion | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Salivary gland biopsy with ≥1 focus of lymphocytic infiltrate per 4 mm² | 3 |
| Anti-Ro/SSA autoantibodies | 3 |
| Fluorescein/lissamine ocular staining score ≥5 (0–12) in at least one eye | 1 |
| Schirmer test ≤5 mm/5 min in at least one eye | 1 |
| Unstimulated whole saliva flow rate ≤0.1 mL/min | 1 |

**Special tests:**
- **Ocular fluorescein/lissamine staining:** Performed by ophthalmology; scored 0–12.
- **Schirmer test:** Filter paper strip applied to conjunctiva; total distance moistened measured over 5 minutes. Normal is >10 mm.

**Differential diagnosis for sicca symptoms:**
- Inflammatory/malignant diseases with tropism for parotid glands: Sarcoidosis, IgG4-related disease, GPA, lymphoma.
- Viral infections: Mumps, HCV, HIV.
- Medications: Anticholinergics, cannabinoids.

### Treatment

- **Eye care:** Artificial tears, topical cyclosporine, pilocarpine or cevimeline (muscarinic agonists).
- **Oral care:** Sugar-free candies to stimulate saliva flow, meticulous oral hygiene and regular dental exams, pilocarpine or cevimeline.
- **Extraglandular manifestations:** Methotrexate, hydroxychloroquine.
- **Severe CNS disease (demyelinating disease, transverse myelitis):** Cyclophosphamide + rituximab.

## Osteoarthritis (OA)

### Pathogenesis

Progressive cartilage and meniscal degradation due to complex interplay of pathologic joint loading (obesity, joint malalignment, specific types of exercise or occupation, etc.) and dysregulated joint repair processes.

### Risk Factors

Age >55 years, female sex, obesity, acute or chronic joint injury.

### Clinical Features and Diagnosis

OA is a clinical diagnosis made in a patient with compatible risk factors, joint pain that is worse with activity and relieved by rest, and absence of inflammatory features. Joint effusions may occur (particularly knees) but WBC count will be <2K cells/μL.

**Radiographic hallmarks:**
- Joint space narrowing
- Subchondral sclerosis
- Subchondral cysts
- Osteophytes
- No erosions (except inflammatory or "erosive" OA of the hand)

### OA Clinical Phenotypes

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Primary OA** | Most common; no identifiable secondary cause. Typical joints: hands (especially 1st CMC, DIPs, PIPs), hips, knees, cervical and lumbar spine. Involvement may be asymmetric. |
| **Secondary OA** | Disease of synovial joints resulting from a predisposing condition. |
| — Local | Prior trauma, inflammatory/infectious arthritis, or surgical intervention at affected joint/joint space. |
| — Systemic/metabolic | Hemochromatosis (2nd and 3rd MCPs and CMCs), hyperparathyroidism (wrists, MCPs), CPPD (MCPs, wrists, knees, hips), neuropathic arthropathy (such as in DM2), or OA-like changes accompanying inflammatory arthritis. |
| **Inflammatory hand OA** | Associated with intermittent flares of swelling and redness of DIPs, PIPs and erosions on hand X-ray. Erosions are characteristically found in the central joint space, as opposed to the marginal (peripheral) erosions seen in RA. |
| **Diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis (DISH)** | Characterized by calcification and ossification of spinal ligaments and entheses; presents with back pain and flowing osteophytes in the thoracic spine on plain films. |

### Treatment

- **Joint offloading:** Work with OT/PT for assistive devices (e.g., cane) or orthotics (e.g., thumb brace for 1st CMC OA).
- **Exercise:** Strengthen periarticular muscles.
- **Pharmacotherapy:** NSAIDs, duloxetine. Intraarticular glucocorticoids can be considered for knee and hip OA pain every 3 months.
- **Surgical therapy:** Knee or hip joint replacement for patients who have failed conservative therapy.

## Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

### Pathogenesis

Mucosal inflammation triggers loss of tolerance to self-antigens in susceptible hosts, culminating in autoimmune destruction of synovial tissue.

### Epidemiology and Risk Factors

Approximately 1% prevalence in the general population, F:M = ~3:1. Increased risk in patients with periodontal disease and those who smoke cigarettes.

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms:** Typically morning stiffness lasting one hour, improves with activity, and is aggravated by rest.

**Physical exam:** Inflammatory polyarthritis with a predilection for the MCPs, PIPs, and MTPs with sparing of the DIPs. Variable involvement of wrists, elbows, shoulders, hips, knees, ankles, and cervical spine (characteristically involves C1–C2 articulation; thoracic and lumbar spine typically spared). Involvement is symmetric but severity may be asymmetric.

**Hand deformities:** Ulnar deviation of MCP, Boutonniere deformities (PIP flexion/DIP hyperextension), Swan-neck deformity (PIP hyperextension/DIP flexion).

**Radiographic hallmarks:** Periarticular osteopenia (earliest finding), symmetric joint space narrowing, erosions (typically at medial and lateral joint margins including the ulnar styloid), alignment deformities including subluxation and ulnar deviation of MCPs.

**Lab findings:**
- Rheumatoid factor (RF): Many patients have positive RF in first 6 months; most by 2 years.
- Anti-CCP antibodies: More specific than RF.
- ESR and/or CRP elevated in most patients.
- Ten to twenty percent of cases are seronegative.

**Extraarticular manifestations:**
- **Derm:** Rheumatoid nodules (approximately one-quarter of patients) at pressure points including the olecranon; neutrophilic dermatoses including pyoderma gangrenosum and Sweet syndrome; small vessel cutaneous vasculitis.
- **Cardiac:** Independent risk factor for CAD and CHF; pericarditis.
- **Pulmonary:** Air trapping reflecting small airway inflammation; clinically apparent ILD in some patients; bronchiolitis; bronchiectasis; pleuritis; cricoarytenoid arthritis (hoarseness, dysphagia, stridor).
- **Eyes:** Sicca symptoms/secondary Sjögren's; episcleritis; scleritis; keratitis.
- **CNS:** Mononeuritis multiplex; cervical spine subluxation can lead to cervical myelopathy.
- **Heme:** Anemia of inflammation; Felty syndrome (triad of neutropenia, splenomegaly, and longstanding seropositive RA); increased risk of large B cell lymphomas.

### Diagnosis

**2010 ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria:**

| Criterion | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| **Joint involvement (≥1 joint with clinical synovitis)** | |
| 1 large joint (shoulders, elbows, hips, knees, ankles) | 0 |
| 2–10 large joints | 1 |
| 1–3 small joints (MCPs, PIPs, wrists, 2–5 MTPs) | 2 |
| 4–10 small joints | 3 |
| >10 small joints (at least 1 small joint) | 5 |
| **Serology** | |
| Negative RF and anti-CCP | 0 |
| Low-positive RF or anti-CCP (<3× ULN) | 2 |
| High-positive RF or anti-CCP (>3× ULN) | 3 |
| **Acute phase reactants** | |
| Normal CRP and ESR | 0 |
| Abnormal CRP or ESR | 1 |
| **Duration** | |
| <6 weeks | 0 |
| ≥6 weeks | 1 |

Six points needed for classification as RA.

**Symptom monitoring:** The Simplified Disease Activity Index (SDAI) incorporates tender and swollen joint counts, CRP level, and clinician and patient-derived global assessment. SDAI <3.3 defines remission.

### Treatment

- **Methotrexate** is the first-line DMARD. Titrate to maximum tolerated dose (up to 25 mg weekly) at 12-week intervals. Consider subcutaneous dosing at doses >15 mg weekly. Contraindications: liver disease, advanced CKD, heavy alcohol use.
- **Leflunomide** can be used as first-line DMARD if there are contraindications to methotrexate use.
- Thirty to fifty percent of patients respond to methotrexate monotherapy.
- For patients who do not respond to methotrexate, consider adding:
  - Sulfasalazine and hydroxychloroquine
  - A TNFα inhibitor
  - Rituximab
  - Tocilizumab
  - Tofacitinib
- **Prednisone** 5–10 mg daily may be used to rapidly improve symptoms while awaiting DMARD effect.
- **Pregnancy:** Preexisting RA improves during pregnancy in approximately three-quarters of cases. Methotrexate and leflunomide are contraindicated in pregnancy. Hydroxychloroquine and sulfasalazine are safe in pregnancy.

## Spondyloarthritis (SpA)

Group of disorders with shared features of inflammatory arthritis with varying degrees of axial skeleton involvement, enthesitis (inflammation of tendon-bone insertion points), overt or subclinical intestinal inflammation, association with uveitis, and association with the HLA-B27 MHC allele.

### Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS)

**Pathogenesis:** Not fully elucidated but several hypotheses exist regarding the role of the HLA-B27 risk allele, including presentation of self-peptides to CD8+ T cells and pathologic formation of oligomers that activate NK cells.

**Epidemiology:** Prevalence 0.9–1% in the United States, M:F = ~3:1. Age of onset typically between 20–30 years.

**HLA-B27 genetics:**
- Overall prevalence of HLA-B27 in Northern Europeans is approximately 6–8%.
- Prevalence of SpA in HLA-B27+ individuals is 2–20%.
- HLA-B27 is present in approximately three-quarters of patients with spondyloarthritis overall and approximately 90% of patients with ankylosing spondylitis.

**Definition:** Updated ASAS nomenclature: Ankylosing spondylitis → Axial spondyloarthritis ± radiographic sacroiliitis ("with radiographic sacroiliitis" included only if associated sacroiliitis is severe enough to be detected on plain film X-ray).

**Clinical features:**
- **Axial spine involvement:** Begins with the sacroiliac (SI) joints in nearly all cases, presenting with pain localizing to the hip or buttock. Initially MRI is most sensitive (detects subchondral marrow edema). As disease progresses, damage becomes visible on PA pelvic plain film (reveals erosive changes, joint space narrowing, eventual ankylosis). Following SI joint involvement, spinal involvement is typically next (causing inflammatory back pain with morning stiffness). Joint involvement proceeds cranially in a continuous fashion. End-stage disease = ankylosis of vertebral facet joints and heterotopic ossification of spinal ligaments, resulting in "bamboo spine" (occurs in some patients).
- **Peripheral articular/periarticular involvement:** Enthesitis at Achilles tendon or plantar fascia; asymmetric oligoarthritis of hips, knees, ankles, shoulders; dactylitis of the toes.
- **Ocular:** Recurrent anterior uveitis.
- **Cardiovascular:** Increased risk of CAD; aortitis may occur.
- **Pulmonary:** Restrictive lung disease due to fusion of costovertebral joints.

**Diagnosis:** ASAS Diagnostic Algorithm for Axial Spondyloarthritis.

**Treatment:**
- Continuous NSAIDs are first-line and can slow radiographic disease progression.
- TNFα inhibitors are typically started in patients with persistent symptoms or radiographic progression on NSAIDs.
- Anti-IL-17a therapy with secukinumab or ixekizumab is recommended for patients with persistent symptoms after trial of one TNFα inhibitor.
- Intraarticular glucocorticoids can be helpful for SI joint pain.
- Methotrexate is NOT efficacious.

---

### IBD-Associated Arthritis

**Epidemiology:** Occurs in 6–15% of patients with Crohn's and ulcerative colitis (UC); disease onset can occur any time in the course of IBD.

**Definition:** Updated ASAS nomenclature: IBD-associated arthritis → Peripheral spondyloarthritis with IBD.

**Clinical features:**
- **Articular — three clinical phenotypes:**
  1. **Sacroiliitis/spondylitis:** Most common. Half are HLA-B27 positive. Axial disease activity does not track with enteric disease activity. Patients may be asymptomatic.
  2. **Chronic inflammatory polyarthritis:** Occurs in some patients; typically involving PIPs, MCPs, wrists, elbows, knees, shoulders and ankles. Does not track with IBD activity.
  3. **Acute oligo or polyarthritis:** Occurs in others; typically involves the knees and tracks with IBD activity (may co-occur with an IBD flare).
- **Derm:** Pyoderma gangrenosum and erythema nodosum.
- **Ocular:** Anterior uveitis.

**Treatment:**
- Non-biologic DMARDs: Sulfasalazine, azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, and methotrexate are first-line.
- TNFα inhibitors are useful for persistent symptoms. Only select agents are effective for both bowel and joint manifestations: Infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab, certolizumab pegol (etanercept not effective).
- NSAIDs are not recommended as they may worsen IBD.

---

### Psoriatic Arthritis

**Epidemiology:** Occurs in 7–20% of patients with psoriasis. Overall prevalence 0.3–1% in the US.

**Definition:** Updated ASAS nomenclature: Psoriatic arthritis → Peripheral spondyloarthritis with psoriasis.

**Clinical features:**
- Psoriasis precedes or co-occurs with arthritis in most patients; in a small proportion, joint disease can precede skin findings.
- **Clinical phenotypes:** All involve inflammatory arthritis with morning stiffness ± erosions:
  - Symmetric polyarthritis
  - Asymmetric oligoarthritis
  - DIP-predominant polyarthritis
  - Spondyloarthritis
  - Arthritis mutilans (end stage of hand polyarthritis with subluxation, ligamentous laxity, and telescoping of the phalanges).
- **Other periarticular manifestations:** Enthesitis of Achilles tendons or plantar fascia is common; dactylitis of the toes. Nail dystrophy/nail pitting is a risk factor for joint disease.
- **Axial spine involvement:** Unlike AS, axial disease in PsA may begin in the cervical spine and involvement may be noncontiguous along the spine.

**Treatment:**
- Continuous NSAIDs for nondestructive/nonsevere disease.
- Methotrexate is effective for pain in patients with non-severe disease who fail NSAIDs, but does not impact radiographic progression.
- TNFα inhibitors slow radiographic damage in patients with erosive or severe disease.
- Ustekinumab (anti-IL-12/23) and secukinumab (anti-IL-17a) are considered in patients who have failed two different TNFα inhibitors.

---

### Reactive Arthritis

**Pathogenesis:** Preceding diarrhea (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia) or Chlamydia trachomatis, followed by joint pain 2–6 weeks later. If sudden onset, rule out HIV.

**Epidemiology:** Prevalence of reactive arthritis after an episode of bacterial dysentery ranges from 2–15%. HLA-B27 is positive in many Yersinia-related cases and approximately one-third of cases associated with other pathogens.

**Definition:** Updated ASAS nomenclature: Reactive arthritis → Peripheral spondyloarthritis with preceding infection.

**Clinical features:**
- **Articular:** Inflammatory non-erosive oligoarthritis with predilection for knees, ankles, and wrists, occurring 2–3 weeks after bacterial inflammatory diarrhea or urethritis. Enthesitis of Achilles tendon and plantar fascia is common. Dactylitis is fairly common. Spine involvement is uncommon, but SI joint involvement may occur.
- **Ocular:** Sterile conjunctivitis.
- **Derm:** Keratoderma blenorrhagicum begins as vesicular or waxy papular rash of palms and soles and becomes erythematous, scaly, and hyperkeratotic. Circinate balanitis is a serpiginous shallow ulceration on the glans or shaft of the penis.
- **Genitourinary:** In patients with preceding urethritis, sterile urethritis, prostatitis, or cervicitis may persist.

**Treatment:**
- Continuous NSAIDs are first-line. Most patients will have resolution of symptoms within 3–6 months.
- Disease activity persisting >3–6 months can be treated with sulfasalazine or methotrexate.
- Antibiotics are not typically needed except in rare cases of demonstrated persistent infections.

## Crystal-Induced Arthritis

### Gout

**Pathogenesis:** Hyperuricemia above the uric acid saturation point leads to crystal formation in synovial fluid. This causes activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome in tissue macrophages/recruited monocytes, leading to IL-1β production and PMN recruitment, resulting in acute joint swelling and pain.

Hyperuricemia can occur due to:
- **Uric acid underexcretion:** Largely due to genetic variation in renal tubular urate handling, but also CKD, thiazide/loop diuretics, pyrazinamide/ethambutol.
- **Uric acid overproduction:** Less common; inborn errors of purine metabolism, hematologic malignancies.

**Clinical features:**
- **Asymptomatic hyperuricemia:** Population prevalence of hyperuricemia is approximately 4%, but many remain asymptomatic with no treatment required.
- **Acute gouty arthritis:** Acute monoarticular swelling, erythema, warmth, and exquisite tenderness that peaks within 12–24 hours of onset. Periarticular soft tissue involvement is common and can clinically mimic SSTI. In men, first gout attack typically occurs age 20–40 years and most commonly involves the 1st MTP. After years of disease, attacks may involve other joints. In women, first gout attack typically occurs after menopause.
- **Tophaceous gout:** After 10–20 years of intermittent gout attacks and poorly controlled hyperuricemia, some patients develop tophi (conglomerates of uric acid crystals and inflammatory cells with a fibrous rind). Common locations: extensor surfaces of elbows, distal Achilles tendon, PIPs, ear cartilage. Tophi may directly erode bone, cause functional impairment, or become superinfected.

**Diagnosis:**
- Perform arthrocentesis (mandatory for larger joints to rule out infection with gram stain and culture; infection may coexist with gout flare!).
- Diagnostic gold standard: Detection of needle-shaped, negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals (yellow when parallel to polarizing axis).
- Synovial WBC >2K cells/μL and may be >100K cells/μL with PMN-predominance.
- Checking serum uric acid is only sometimes useful; it may be low during an acute attack.

**Treatment of acute flares:**
- **Colchicine:** 1.2 mg loading dose followed 1 hour later by 0.6 mg, continued daily until flare resolves. Most effective if started within 24 hours of symptom onset. Avoid in patients with GFR <60 mL/min.
- **Prednisone:** 0.5 mg/kg/day for 5 days (or longer if longstanding disease). Best for patients with CKD.
- **NSAIDs:** Max dose NSAID (e.g., naproxen 500 mg BID) for 5–7 days. Avoid in patients with CKD, CHF, GIB risk.
- **IL-1β blockade (anakinra):** For refractory flares in patients with contraindications to and/or failure of other therapies. Not used as maintenance therapy.
- **Intraarticular glucocorticoids:** Consider as monotherapy for monoarticular flares, particularly in the 1st MTP. Avoid or proceed with caution if synovial culture is pending.

**Urate-lowering therapy:**
- **Indications:** ≥2 attacks per year; CKD stage 2 or higher; tophaceous disease; uric acid nephrolithiasis.
- **Serum uric acid targets:** ACR/EULAR recommends targeting uric acid <6 mg/dL for patients with tophi and <6 mg/dL for others.
- **Timing:** May be started during an acute flare as long as patient is also on appropriate systemic anti-inflammatory therapy.
- **Auxiliary measures:** Alcohol cessation, low purine diet (limit shellfish, red meat), avoid thiazide/loop diuretics/ASA if feasible.

**Medications:**
- **Xanthine oxidase inhibitors:** Prevent uric acid generation from upstream purine metabolites.
  - **Allopurinol:** First-line therapy; start 100 mg daily (or 50 mg daily if CKD) and uptitrate to max dose of 800 mg/day. Check HLA-B*5801 in patients of Asian descent (associated with severe cutaneous adverse reactions).
  - **Febuxostat:** 40–120 mg daily.
- **Uricosuric agents:** Promotes renal tubular uric acid excretion. Probenecid is most widely available. Less potent than xanthine oxidase inhibitors as monotherapy but can be added if needed. Avoid in patients with CKD.
- **Pegloticase:** Recombinant uricase infused every two weeks. Indications: recurrent and/or tophaceous gout with intolerance to or failure of other therapies. Many patients develop antidrug antibodies.

**Anti-inflammatory prophylaxis during active uric acid lowering:** Colchicine 0.6 mg daily, low-dose NSAID (e.g., naproxen 250 mg BID), or low-dose prednisone (5 mg daily) should be provided to prevent flares during active titration of uric acid lowering therapy. Continue for 3 months after reaching serum uric acid target (6 months in patients with tophi).

### Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition (CPPD) Arthropathies

**Pathophysiology:** Calcium pyrophosphate crystals accumulate in joints with age (many patients age >80 have detectable chondrocalcinosis). Secondary chondrocalcinosis may develop in younger patients due to metabolic disturbances (chronic hypomagnesemia, hypophosphatemia, hyperparathyroidism, hemochromatosis).

**Clinical syndromes and diagnosis:**

| Syndrome | Features |
|----------|----------|
| **Asymptomatic chondrocalcinosis** | Four most common locations: knees, triangular fibrocartilage of wrist, pubic symphysis, MCPs |
| **Pseudogout** | Acute monoarticular arthritis with synovial fluid showing rhomboid-shaped, positively birefringent crystals (blue when parallel to polarizing axis). Synovial WBC typically >2K cells/μL. |
| **"Pseudo-OA"** | Characterized by OA-like radiographic changes in joints with preceding or concurrent chondrocalcinosis, often with deformity out of proportion to duration of arthritis. Knees most common; valgus deformity is suggestive. |
| **"Pseudo-RA"** | Rare entity characterized by inflammatory polyarthritis of wrists and MCP joints with chondrocalcinosis that is typically non-erosive and does not meet criteria for RA. |

**Treatment:**
- No therapy to dissolve CPP crystals.
- **Pseudogout flares:** Management similar to gout flare; prophylaxis with colchicine 0.3 mg daily or low-dose NSAIDs if ≥3 attacks per year.
- **Pseudo-OA:** Management similar to OA without chondrocalcinosis.
- **Pseudo-RA:** Low-dose daily colchicine or low-dose daily NSAIDs; serial clinical/radiographic/laboratory diagnostic reassessment (patients may prove to have true RA).

## Large Vessel Vasculitis

### Temporal Arteritis/Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)

**Pathogenesis:** Not fully elucidated; association with HLA-DRB*04 suggests cell-mediated autoimmunity resulting in granulomatous inflammation of vessel walls. Most commonly affects major aortic branch vessels or secondary branch vessels (external carotid, subclavian, axillary, temporal).

**Epidemiology:** Incidence of 10–30 per 100,000 individuals annually in the United States; F:M = 2:1.

**Clinical features:** Headache, scalp pain, jaw claudication. Ophthalmic artery involvement may present with amaurosis fugax and can cause blindness. Approximately half of patients with GCA also have PMR.

**Diagnosis:** New headache in an adult ≥50 years with temporal artery tenderness, ESR ≥50 mm/hr (normal ESR does not exclude diagnosis), and temporal artery biopsy showing mononuclear cell infiltration of vessel wall, potentially with giant multinucleated macrophages and granulomas. Do not delay steroids to get biopsy. Diseased tissue remains abnormal for up to two weeks after treatment initiation.

**Treatment:** Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (started immediately without waiting for biopsy results) and ASA 81 mg daily. Followed by prolonged, slow steroid taper. Tocilizumab: Anti-IL-6 receptor monoclonal antibody given weekly or biweekly. Tocilizumab + prednisone resulted in higher rate of glucocorticoid-free remission at 1 year compared to prednisone alone.

---

### Takayasu's Arteritis

**Pathogenesis:** Histology similar to GCA but typically involves larger vessels, most commonly the aorta followed by subclavian, common carotid, renal, and pulmonary arteries.

**Epidemiology:** Prevalence 40/million in Japan and 4.7–8/million elsewhere. Predominantly affects young women.

**Clinical features:** Involvement of subclavian arteries can cause limb claudication, absent or reduced brachial artery pulses, subclavian bruits, blood pressure discrepancies between arms. Carotid involvement can cause neck pain and tenderness. ESR and CRP are typically elevated.

**Diagnosis:** MR angiography demonstrates thoracic vessel stenoses and vessel wall inflammation.

**Complications:** Limb ischemia, aortic aneurysm, aortic regurgitation, stroke, renal artery stenosis with secondary hypertension.

**Treatment:** High-dose prednisone (1 mg/kg) with prolonged slow taper. May need surgery or angioplasty to recannulate stenosed vessels, but defer until resolution of active vessel inflammation.

---

## Medium Vessel Vasculitis

### Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)

**Epidemiology:** Incidence 3–4 per million. Average age of onset 50 years, M>F. Only a small proportion of cases are associated with HBV in the post-HBV vaccine era.

**Clinical features:**
- **Neuro (many patients):** Mononeuritis multiplex or distal symmetric polyneuropathy.
- **Constitutional (most patients):** Fever, malaise, weight loss.
- **Cutaneous (half of patients):** Livedo reticularis, nodules, necrotic ulcers of lower extremities.
- **Renal (many patients):** Can lead to hypertension, AKI/CKD if bilateral, hematuria, proteinuria.
- **GI (some patients):** Acute or chronic mesenteric ischemia.

**Diagnosis:** Confirmed with biopsy of involved tissue (e.g., sural nerve) showing focal segmental panmural necrotizing inflammation. If no clear biopsy targets but GI or renal involvement is suspected, mesenteric or renal angiography can show a combination of aneurysms and stenoses at vessel branch points. Avoid renal biopsy given high risk of bleeding.

**Treatment:** Prednisone and cyclophosphamide for severe non-HBV-associated cases. Antivirals for HBV-related disease.

---

### Primary Angiitis of the Central Nervous System (PACNS)

**Epidemiology:** Incidence 2 per million annually. Median age of onset is 50 years.

**Clinical features:** Medium vessel vasculitis with isolated CNS involvement. Presents with triad of headache, cognitive impairment or altered mental status, and new or progressive focal neurologic deficits due to strokes.

**Diagnosis:** MRA or conventional angiography may show beading of CNS artery branches. Gold standard is brain or leptomeningeal biopsy showing lymphocytic vessel wall inflammation.

**Treatment:** High-dose glucocorticoids and cyclophosphamide. Permanent disability commonly occurs due to strokes.

---

### Kawasaki Disease

**Clinical features:** Medium vessel vasculitis most common in children (most cases present prior to age 5 years), typically Asian boys. Manifestations include high fevers, conjunctivitis, mucositis (strawberry tongue), non-suppurative cervical adenitis, truncal rash, and palmar/plantar erythema. Coronary aneurysms occur in some cases.

**Treatment:** IVIG and aspirin. Excellent prognosis. Coronary aneurysms may develop and cause CHF and/or need for CABG in adulthood.

---

## Small Vessel Vasculitis: ANCA-Associated Vasculitides (AAV)

### Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA)

**Epidemiology:** Incidence 7–12 per million individuals annually. Typical age of onset 45–60 years.

**Diagnosis:** Most patients with systemic disease are c-ANCA and anti-PR3 positive. Tissue biopsy showing pauci-immune necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis is gold standard.

**Clinical features:**
- **ENT (many patients):** Sinusitis, rhinorrhea, otitis media, chondritis of ears and nose with saddle nose deformity, nasal septal perforation.
- **Pulmonary (approximately half):** Cavitary nodules, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, tracheal subglottic stenosis.
- **Renal (approximately half):** Pauci-immune necrotizing glomerulonephritis.
- **Derm (some patients):** Palpable purpura, nodules, pyoderma gangrenosum.
- **Neurologic (some patients):** Mononeuritis multiplex, distal symmetric polyneuropathy, pachymeningitis.
- **Ocular (some patients):** Posterior uveitis, scleritis, episcleritis, retroorbital pseudotumor, dacryoadenitis.
- **Cardiac (few patients):** Pericarditis, myocarditis, conduction disorder.
- **GI (few patients):** Ulceration.

**Treatment:**
- **Systemic organ-threatening disease:** Induction therapy: Pulse dose glucocorticoids (1 g per day × 3–5 days) followed by 1 mg/kg, then add rituximab or cyclophosphamide. Rituximab is non-inferior to cyclophosphamide for induction and associated with less infertility and alopecia.
- **Maintenance therapy:** Rituximab for 12–24 months after remission, azathioprine, or methotrexate.
- **Limited upper airway disease:** Glucocorticoids plus methotrexate or rituximab.

---

### Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)

**Epidemiology:** Incidence 2.7–94 per million individuals annually. Average age of onset is 50–60 years.

**Diagnosis:** Small vessel vasculitis of lungs (diffuse alveolar hemorrhage) and kidneys (crescentic RPGN). Many patients are ANCA+ (generally p-ANCA and anti-MPO positive). Tissue biopsy shows non-granulomatous necrotizing pauci-immune vasculitis (as opposed to granulomatous in GPA).

**Clinical features:**
- **Renal (most patients):** Necrotizing glomerulonephritis.
- **Derm (many patients):** Palpable purpura with leukocytoclastic vasculitis; livedo reticularis; nodules; necrotic skin ulcers.
- **Neurologic (many patients):** Mononeuritis multiplex; distal symmetric polyneuropathy; pachymeningitis.
- **Pulmonary (some patients):** Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, organizing pneumonia, ILD with radiographic phenotype of usual interstitial pneumonia.
- **ENT (some patients):** Sinusitis; sensorineural hearing loss.

**Treatment:** Similar to GPA.

---

### Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA)

**Epidemiology:** Rarest AAV; 0.1–2.66 per million individuals annually.

**Diagnosis:** Small vessel vasculitis typically occurring in patients with preceding adult-onset asthma and nasal polyps. Many patients are ANCA+ (generally p-ANCA and anti-MPO positive). Eosinophilia >1500 cells/μL often present. Diagnostic gold standard is biopsy of involved tissue showing pauci-immune necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis with eosinophilic infiltration.

**Clinical features:**
- **Neurologic (most patients):** Mononeuritis multiplex or distal sensory polyneuropathy.
- **Pulmonary (nearly all patients):** Preceding asthma that often improves during vasculitic phase. Migratory pulmonary consolidations may occur during vasculitis phase.
- **Cardiac (some patients):** Pericarditis, endomyocarditis, conduction system disease, CHF.
- **Renal (some patients):** Pauci-immune glomerulonephritis.

**Treatment:** Glucocorticoids alone may be sufficient unless there is major organ involvement, in which case cyclophosphamide is indicated. Lowest mortality among all AAV (5-year survival is favorable).

---

## Small Vessel Vasculitis: Immune-Complex Mediated

Palpable purpura on exam, leukocytoclastic vasculitis on skin biopsy.

### Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis

**Pathogenesis:** Immune complex-mediated small vessel vasculitis caused by Ig molecules that precipitate at temperatures lower than 37°C. Types:
- **Type I:** IgM rheumatoid factor associated with multiple myeloma.
- **Type II and III:** "Mixed cryoglobulinemia" because precipitating antibodies are a mixture of polyclonal IgG and either polyclonal IgM (Type II) or monoclonal IgM (Type III) rheumatoid factor.

**Diagnosis:** Many cases of mixed cryoglobulinemia are associated with HCV infection (test for HCV if cryos are identified). SLE and Sjögren's can also cause Type III. Additional lab findings include positive RF and very low C4. **Pearl about sample collection:** Must keep at >37°C to avoid premature precipitation and false negative result.

**Clinical features:**
- **Derm (many patients):** Palpable purpura, Raynaud's, ulcers, skin necrosis, livedo reticularis.
- **Neurologic (many patients):** Distal symmetric polyneuropathy.
- **Arthritic (many patients):** Arthritis.
- **Renal (some patients):** Immune complex glomerulonephritis.

**Treatment:** Treat HCV or control underlying rheumatic disease (e.g., Sjögren's or SLE). For organ-threatening disease such as acute renal failure: plasmapheresis or glucocorticoids plus rituximab or cyclophosphamide.

---

### IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein Purpura, HSP)

**Epidemiology:** Childhood vasculitis that rarely occurs in adults. Incidence 14 per million annually.

**Clinical features:** Often preceded by viral URI or strep pharyngitis. Main manifestations include small vessel vasculitis causing abdominal pain, GI bleeding, arthritis and arthralgias, and glomerulonephritis.

**Diagnosis:** Gold standard is skin biopsy with leukocytoclastic vasculitis and heavy IgA deposits, or renal biopsy showing IgA nephropathy.

**Treatment:** Self-limited in children. Adults may require glucocorticoids and/or cyclophosphamide for severe organ-threatening disease.

---

### Hypersensitivity Vasculitis

**Clinical features:** Small vessel vasculitis with isolated cutaneous involvement (palpable purpura) triggered by exposure to a known or unknown antigen.

**Diagnosis:** Skin biopsy shows leukocytoclastic vasculitis without IgA staining in the correct clinical context. The vasculitis will typically resolve 7–10 days after removal of the offending antigen. Persistent symptoms for more than a month should raise suspicion for an alternative diagnosis.

**Treatment:** Removal of the offending antigen.

---

## Other Rheumatologic Conditions

### Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

**Clinical features:** Syndrome of neck, shoulder, and hip girdle stiffness that is typically worse after immobility; associated with elevations in markers of systemic inflammation (ESR, CRP). Many patients with GCA have PMR, and many patients with PMR have GCA.

**Diagnosis:** A diagnosis of exclusion made after ruling out myopathies (for myopathy, weakness > pain; for PMR, pain > weakness) or non-systemic periarticular syndromes such as adhesive capsulitis. May be difficult to distinguish from seronegative RA as some patients have distal polyarthritis; when present, erosive disease favors RA. Improves briskly with prednisone 12.5–20 mg daily.

**Treatment:** Prednisone 20 mg/day and prolonged taper (often 1–2 years).

---

### Behçet Syndrome

**Pathogenesis:** Not fully elucidated; increased risk with HLA-B51 allele implicates cell-mediated autoimmunity (US cases tend not to be associated with this HLA type).

**Epidemiology:** Incidence 0.1–7 per 100,000 individuals annually in the US; much higher in Turkey, Iran, Saudi Arabia, Greece, Japan, Korea, and China. Age of onset typically 20–30 years.

**Clinical features:**
- **Derm:** Recurrent genital and oral ulcers.
- **Ocular:** Anterior uveitis (potentially severe and leading to blindness).
- **Other:** May include skin lesions, pathergy reaction, arthritis, GI ulcers, CNS involvement.

**Treatment:** Topical and systemic therapies depending on severity and organ involvement.
