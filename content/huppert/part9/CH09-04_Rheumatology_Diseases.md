## 1. Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

**Pathogenesis** of SLE involves decreased clearance and increased responsiveness to self-nucleic acids, which leads to excessive IFN-α production by innate immune cells (especially plasmacytoid dendritic cells), followed by inappropriate activation of autoreactive B cells, resulting in autoantibody production and immune complex deposition in skin, glomeruli, joints, and other tissues.

**Epidemiology** shows that **90%** of affected patients are women of childbearing age, with a female to male ratio of 9:1 after adolescence.

**Clinical features** include constitutional symptoms such as fever and fatigue, and musculoskeletal manifestations including arthralgias and myalgias with **95%** prevalence, and inflammatory polyarthritis of the hands, wrists, and knees with **60%** prevalence. Jaccoud arthropathy (reducible finger swan neck deformities) may also occur.

Dermatologic manifestations include acute cutaneous lupus (malar "butterfly" rash), discoid lesions, subacute cutaneous lupus lesions, oral ulcers, and nonscarring alopecia. Other features include Raynaud's phenomenon and photosensitivity.

Hematologic manifestations include leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, autoimmune hemolysis, antiphospholipid antibodies, and a sevenfold increased risk of non-Hodgkin's lymphoma. Central nervous system manifestations include acute confusional state, psychosis, seizures, and transverse myelitis. Cardiac manifestations include acute pericarditis, pericardial effusion, myocarditis, Libman-Sacks endocarditis, and a three- to tenfold increased risk of ASCVD. Pulmonary manifestations include pleural effusion with a 30–**50%** lifetime prevalence, chronic ILD, acute lupus pneumonitis, and diffuse alveolar hemorrhage. Renal manifestations include proteinuria, lupus nephritis, and antidsDNA antibodies are associated with an increased risk of lupus nephritis.

### Lupus Nephritis

| Class | Histopathology | Management |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **I (Minimal Mesangial)** | LM appearance normal; immune complexes visible in the mesangium by IF or EM | Monitor proteinuria; ACEi/ARB, blood pressure control |
| **II (Mesangial Proliferative)** | Mesial proliferation visible on LM | Monitor proteinuria; ACEi/ARB |
| **III (Focal Proliferative)** | Thickened capillary loops on LM, less than **50%** of glomeruli affected | 1 g IV methylprednisolone daily ×3–5 days, then prednisone taper; mycophenolate 2–3 g daily ×6 months |
| **IV (Diffuse Proliferative)** | Thickened capillary loops on LM, greater than **50%** of glomeruli affected | Same as Class III |
| **V (Membranous)** | Subepithelial immune complex deposition with GBM thickening; nephrotic range proteinuria | Similar approach to Class III/IV due to risks from proteinuria |
| **VI (Advanced Sclerosing)** | Greater than **90%** globally sclerotic glomeruli | ACEi/ARB if GFR allows; not responsive to immunosuppression |

### Drug-Induced Lupus

Drug-induced lupus presents with arthritis, arthralgias, serositis, and rash, but notably without CNS or renal involvement. Associated medications include those linked to positive antihistone antibodies (**95%**) such as hydralazine, procainamide, and isoniazid, and those linked to positive anti-dsDNA such as minocycline, penicillamine, and TNF inhibitors.

### Diagnosis

The 2019 EULAR/ACR Classification Criteria for SLE require an ANA of 1:80 or greater, and patients with a score of 10 or more are classified as having SLE. The classification criteria are more specific than sensitive and are intended for clinical trial homogeneity, not as diagnostic criteria. Clinician diagnosis remains the gold standard.

### Treatment

For **non-organ threatening disease**, hydroxychloroquine is recommended for all patients regardless of disease activity unless contraindicated, as it reduces flare frequency and incidence of renal disease and is effective for arthritis and skin disease. Low-dose prednisone can help with arthritis, and annual ophthalmologic exams are needed while taking hydroxychloroquine.

For **moderate organ-threatening disease** (profound cytopenias, acute CNS lupus with seizures, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, myocarditis), prednisone 1 mg/kg or methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily for 3–5 days followed by a prednisone taper is indicated, along with possible IVIG for SLE-related ITP.

For **severe organ-threatening disease** such as proliferative class III or IV nephritis, more aggressive immunosuppression is required.

### Lupus Flare vs. Infection

Favors lupus flare: predominant ESR elevation with normal CRP, no fever, C3/C4 below the patient's baseline, elevated dsDNA, leukopenia, and thrombocytopenia. Favors infection: concomitant ESR and CRP elevation, fever, C3/C4 at baseline, dsDNA at baseline, leukocytosis, and thrombocytosis.

## 2. Systemic Sclerosis (SSc)

**Pathogenesis** involves a poorly understood confluence of microvasculopathy, fibrosis, and humoral autoimmunity.

**Epidemiology** shows a prevalence of 275 cases per 1 million, and SLE is ten times more common than SSc.

**Clinical features** include dermal manifestations such as skin thickening of the hands (sclerodactyly), puffy fingers, facial skin tightening, and telangiectasias. Skin involvement is typically most severe over the first 18–24 months, then improves with or without therapy. Vascular manifestations include Raynaud's phenomenon (present in **95%** of SSc patients), nailfold capillary disease, and fingertip ulcers or pitting scars. Pulmonary manifestations include ILD (especially in diffuse SSc) and WHO Group I pulmonary hypertension (especially in limited SSc). Cardiac manifestations include arrhythmias, heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, and rarely myocarditis. Gastrointestinal manifestations include reduced oral aperture, GERD, gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE or "watermelon stomach"), and colonic pseudoobstruction. Renal manifestations include scleroderma renal crisis, which can be conceptualized as "Raynaud's of the kidneys" where narrowing of renal arterioles leads to hypoperfusion of the juxtaglomerular apparatus and excess renin production. Scleroderma renal crisis presents with hypertensive emergency and proteinuric AKI, and **60%** of patients are anti-RNA Pol III positive.

### Limited vs. Diffuse SSc

| Feature | Limited SSc | Diffuse SSc |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Extent of skin involvement** | Skin distal to knees and elbows only | Skin proximal to knees and elbows, including trunk |
| **Proportion of SSc patients** | **70%** | **20%** |
| **Associated autoantibody** | Anti-centromere | Anti-Scl70 |
| **Raynaud's** | **99%** | **98%** |
| **Digital ulcers** | **50%** | **25%** |
| **Esophageal disease** | **90%** | **80%** |
| **ILD** | **35%** | **65%** |
| **Pulmonary HTN** | **15%** | **15%** |
| **Myopathy** | **11%** | **23%** |
| **Renal crisis** | **2%** | **15%** |

### Treatment

Treatment is tailored to the involved organ system. For skin disease, mycophenylate may be used if severely symptomatic and early in disease course. For ILD, mycophenylate is the treatment of choice depending on severity, and lung transplantation may be considered. For pulmonary hypertension, monotherapy with an endothelin receptor antagonist or PDE inhibitor is used for WHO functional class I symptoms, while combination therapy is used for class II and III symptoms. For myocarditis, glucocorticoids are indicated. For scleroderma renal crisis, ACE inhibitors should be titrated to maximum dose, and CCBs should be added. The MAP should not be reduced by more than **20 mmHg** in the first 24 hours, with a goal of normotension within 72 hours. Immunosuppression has no role in scleroderma renal crisis.

## 3. Inflammatory and Noninflammatory Myopathies

### Differential Diagnosis for Inflammatory Myopathies

Inflammatory myopathies include dermatomyositis (DM), polymyositis (PM), immune-mediated necrotizing myositis (IMNM), and inclusion body myositis (IBM).

### Differential Diagnosis for Noninflammatory Myopathies

Noninflammatory myopathies include endocrine causes such as hypothyroid myopathy and glucocorticoid-related myopathy, medication or toxin-induced myopathies such as from alcohol, cocaine, NRTIs, colchicine, gemcitabine, and statins, and inherited myopathies such as glycogen storage diseases, disorders of muscle lipid export, mitochondrial myopathies, and Duchenne or Becker muscular dystrophy.

### Inflammatory Myopathies Comparison

### Distinguishing Inflammatory vs. Noninflammatory Myopathies

Favors inflammatory myopathy: history of decreasing strength over weeks to months without culprit medications, and physical exam findings such as Gottron sign or papules or heliotrope rash suggesting dermatomyositis. Biopsy findings of perifascicular atrophy are specific for DM, while endomysial or perimysial or perivascular lymphocyte infiltrate can be seen in IBM and muscular dystrophies. A rapid response to glucocorticoids with decreasing CK supports inflammatory myopathy.

## 4. Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD)

**Pathogenesis** is not known in detail but is presumably similar to the constituent diseases SLE, SSc, and myositis.

**Epidemiology** shows that MCTD is rare with a female to male ratio of 4:1.

**Diagnosis** requires that patients satisfy classification criteria for two or more of SLE, SSc, and DM/PM and have high titer anti-U1RNP autoantibodies.

**Clinical features** include vascular manifestations such as Raynaud's and pulmonary hypertension, which are common and are the main driver of mortality. Other features include arthritis, puffy fingers, sclerodactyly, serositis, esophageal dysmotility, myositis, and ILD. Renal disease is uncommon.

**Treatment** is targeted toward organ system-specific manifestations.

| Diagnostic Label | Classification Criteria Met | Anti-U1RNP |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD)** | 2 or more | Positive |
| **Overlap Syndrome** | 2 or more | Negative |
| **Undifferentiated Connective Tissue Disease (UCTD)** | 0 | Negative |

## 5. Sjögren's Syndrome

**Pathogenesis** involves autoimmune destruction of the lacrimal and salivary glands, with an unclear underlying mechanism, though increased risk associated with specific HLA alleles implicates cell-mediated autoimmunity.

**Epidemiology** shows a prevalence of 0.3–1 in 1,000 worldwide, with a female to male ratio of 9:1.

**Clinical features** include glandular manifestations such as severe dry eye (which may cause corneal ulceration) and dry mouth (which may cause dental caries and result in malnutrition). Extraglandular manifestations include constitutional symptoms such as fatigue, dermatologic manifestations such as xerosis and cutaneous leukocytoclastic vasculitis, articular manifestations such as inflammatory nonerosive polyarthritis resembling RA, pulmonary manifestations including bronchiolitis obliterans and ILD in 5–**9%** of patients, renal manifestations including type 1 RTA, interstitial nephritis, and immune complex GN in 5–**6%** of patients, vascular manifestations including cryoglobulinemic vasculitis and Raynaud's phenomenon in **16%** of patients, neurologic manifestations including CNS demyelinating lesions, myelopathy, cranial neuropathies, mononeuritis multiplex, and distal symmetric polyneuropathy in 8–**27%** of patients, gastrointestinal manifestations including primary biliary cholangitis and autoimmune hepatitis, and hematologic manifestations including lymphoma with an overall **2%** risk and a 15- to 20-fold increase in risk of non-Hodgkin lymphoma, largely MALT lymphoma in the salivary glands.

**Disease categories** include primary Sjögren's (not meeting criteria for another CTD) and secondary Sjögren's (occurring in the context of another rheumatic disease, most commonly RA or SLE, with 10–**30%** prevalence in these groups).

**Diagnosis** is based on the 2017 ACR/EULAR classification criteria, which require sicca symptoms and 4 or more points from the following: salivary gland biopsy with 1 or more focus of lymphocytic infiltrate per 4 mm squared (3 points), anti-Ro/SSA autoantibodies (3 points), fluorescein/lissamine ocular staining score of 5 or greater in at least one eye (1 point), Schirmer test of 5 mm or less per 5 minutes in at least one eye (1 point), and unstimulated whole saliva flow rate of 0.1 mL per minute or less (1 point).

**Treatment** for eye care includes artificial tears, topical cyclosporine, and pilocarpine or cevimeline to stimulate tear production. For oral care, sugar-free candies to stimulate saliva flow, meticulous oral hygiene, and regular dental exams, along with pilocarpine or cevimeline to stimulate saliva production are recommended. For extraglandular manifestations, methotrexate or hydroxychloroquine is used for arthritis. For severe CNS disease, cyclophosphamide plus rituximab is indicated.

## 6. Osteoarthritis (OA)

**Pathogenesis** involves progressive cartilage and meniscal degradation due to a complex interplay of pathologic joint loading (due to obesity, joint malalignment, specific types of exercise or occupation) and dysregulated joint repair processes.

**Risk factors** include age greater than 55 years, female sex, obesity, and acute or chronic joint injury.

**Clinical features and diagnosis** involve OA being a clinical diagnosis made in a patient with compatible risk factors, joint pain that is worse with activity and relieved by rest, and absence of inflammatory features. Joint effusions may occur but the WBC count will be less than 2K cells/μL. Radiographic hallmarks include joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, subchondral cysts, and osteophytes. No erosions are present with the exception of inflammatory or erosive OA of the hand.

**Clinical phenotypes** include primary OA (most common with no identifiable secondary cause, typically affecting the hands especially first CMC, DIPs, PIPs, hips, knees, and cervical and lumbar spine) and secondary OA (due to predisposing conditions such as prior trauma, inflammatory or infectious arthritis, hemochromatosis, hyperparathyroidism, CPPD, neuropathic arthropathy, or OAlike changes accompanying inflammatory arthritis).

**Treatment** involves joint offloading with occupational therapy or physical therapy, exercise to strengthen periarticular muscles, NSAIDs, duloxetine, and intraarticular glucocorticoids for knee and hip OA pain. Surgical therapy with knee or hip joint replacement is indicated for patients who have failed conservative therapy.

## 7. Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

**Pathogenesis** involves mucosal inflammation triggering loss of tolerance to self-antigens in susceptible hosts, culminating in autoimmune destruction of synovial tissue.

**Epidemiology** shows a prevalence of 0.5–**1%** in the general population with a female to male ratio of approximately 3:1. Increased risk is seen in patients with periodontal disease and those who smoke cigarettes.

**Clinical features** include morning stiffness lasting approximately one hour that improves with activity and is aggravated by rest. Physical exam shows inflammatory polyarthritis with a predilection for the MCPs, PIPs, and MTPs with sparing of the DIPs, with variable involvement of wrists, elbows, shoulders, hips, knees, ankles, and the C-spine. Hand deformities include ulnar deviation of MCP, Boutonniere deformities, and swan-neck deformity. Radiographic hallmarks include periarticular osteopenia (earliest finding), symmetric joint space narrowing, erosions, and alignment deformities. Lab findings include rheumatoid factor (with **50%** of patients positive in the first 6 months and 75–**85%** by 2 years) and anti-CCP antibodies (sensitivity **70%**, specificity **95%**). ESR and or CRP is elevated in **75%** of patients.

**Extraarticular manifestations** include dermatologic manifestations such as rheumatoid nodules occurring at pressure points, neutrophilic dermatoses including pyoderma gangrenosum and Sweet syndrome, and small vessel cutaneous vasculitis. Cardiac manifestations include pericarditis and an independent risk factor for CAD and CHF. Pulmonary manifestations include air trapping reflecting small airway inflammation in **50%** of patients, clinically apparent ILD in **10%**, bronchiectasis, pleuritis, and cricoarytenoid arthritis. Ocular manifestations include sicca symptoms, episcleritis, scleritis, and keratitis. Neurologic manifestations include mononeuritis multiplex and c-spine subluxation leading to cervical myelopathy. Hematologic manifestations include anemia of inflammation, Felty syndrome (triad of neutropenia, splenomegaly, and longstanding seropositive RA), and increased risk of large B cell lymphomas.

**Diagnosis** is based on the 2010 ACR/EULAR classification criteria, which require 6 or more points from joint involvement (ranging from 0 for large joints to 5 for more than 10 small joints), serology (0 for negative, 2 for low-positive, 3 for high-positive RF or anti-CCP), acute phase reactants (0 for normal, 1 for abnormal), and symptom duration (0 for less than 6 weeks, 1 for greater than 6 weeks).

**Treatment** involves methotrexate as the first-line DMARD, titrated to maximum tolerated dose up to 25 mg weekly at 12-week intervals, with consideration of subcutaneous dosing at doses greater than 15 mg weekly. Contraindications include liver disease, advanced CKD, and heavy alcohol use. Leflunomide can be used as first-line DMARD if methotrexate is contraindicated. Approximately 30–**50%** of patients respond to methotrexate monotherapy. For non-responders, adding sulfasalazine and hydroxychloroquine, a TNF inhibitor, rituximab, tocilizumab, or tofacitinib may be considered. Prednisone 5–10 mg daily may be used while awaiting DMARD effect. For pregnancy, hydroxychloroquine and sulfasalazine are safe, but methotrexate and leflunomide are contraindicated.

## 8. Spondyloarthritis (SpA)

Spondyloarthritis is a group of disorders with shared features of inflammatory arthritis with varying degrees of axial skeleton involvement, enthesitis, overt or subclinical intestinal inflammation, association with uveitis, and association with the HLA-B27 MHC allele.

### Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS)

**Pathogenesis** is not fully elucidated but several hypotheses exist regarding the role of HLA-B27, including presentation of self-peptides to CD8+ T cells and pathologic oligomer formation.

**Epidemiology** shows a prevalence of 0.9–**1.4%** in the United States with a male to female ratio of approximately 3:1. Age of onset is typically between 20–30 years. HLA-B27 is present in 74–**90%** of patients with spondyloarthritis overall and **95%** of patients with ankylosing spondylitis.

**Clinical features** include axial spine involvement beginning with the sacroiliac joints, presenting with pain localizing to the hip or buttock. MRI is initially the most sensitive imaging modality, showing subchondral marrow edema in the SI joints. As disease progresses, damage becomes visible on plain film with erosive changes, joint space narrowing, and eventual ankylosis. Spinal involvement typically follows, causing inflammatory back pain with morning stiffness. End-stage disease results in "bamboo spine" (occurring in 10–**15%** of patients). Peripheral manifestations include enthesitis at the Achilles tendon or plantar fascia, asymmetric oligoarthritis of the hips, knees, ankles, and shoulders, and dactylitis. Ocular manifestations include recurrent anterior uveitis in **30%** of patients. Cardiovascular manifestations include increased risk of CAD and aortitis in less than **1%** of patients. Pulmonary manifestations include restrictive lung disease from fusion of the costovertebral joints.

**Treatment** involves continuous NSAIDs as first-line therapy, which can slow radiographic progression. TNF inhibitors are used for patients with persistent symptoms or radiographic progression on NSAIDs. Anti-IL17a therapy with secukinumab or ixekizumab is recommended for patients with persistent symptoms after trying a TNF inhibitor. Intraarticular glucocorticoids can help with SI joint pain, and methotrexate is not efficacious.

### IBD-Associated Arthritis

**Epidemiology** shows this occurs in 6–**46%** of patients with Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.

**Clinical phenotypes** include sacroiliitis or spondylitis (most common, in **25%** of patients, with 50–**75%** being HLA-B27 positive), chronic inflammatory polyarthritis occurring in less than **5%** of patients, and acute oligo or polyarthritis occurring in **5%** of patients that tracks with IBD activity.

**Treatment** involves sulfasalazine, azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, and methotrexate as first-line, with TNF inhibitors for persistent symptoms. Only select TNF inhibitors are effective for both bowel and joint manifestations (infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab, certolizumab). NSAIDs are not recommended as they may worsen IBD.

### Psoriatic Arthritis

**Epidemiology** shows this occurs in 7–**42%** of patients with psoriasis, with an overall prevalence of 0.3–**2%** in the US.

**Clinical features** include psoriasis preceding or co-occurring with arthritis in **90%** of patients. Clinical phenotypes include symmetric polyarthritis, asymmetric oligoarthritis, DIP-predominant polyarthritis, spondyloarthritis, and arthritis mutilans. Periarticular manifestations include enthesitis, dactylitis, and nail dystrophy. Treatment involves continuous NSAIDs for nondestructive or nonsevere disease, methotrexate for pain in patients with nonsevere disease who fail NSAIDs, and TNF inhibitors to slow radiographic damage in patients with erosive or severe disease. Ustekinumab (anti-IL-12/23) and secukinumab (anti-IL17a) are considered for patients who have failed two different TNF inhibitors.

### Reactive Arthritis

**Pathogenesis** involves preceding diarrhea from Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, or Yersinia, or infection with Chlamydia trachomatis, followed by joint pain 2–6 weeks later. HIV should be ruled out if sudden onset.

**Epidemiology** shows a prevalence of reactive arthritis after an episode of bacterial dysentery ranging from 2–**33%**. HLA-B27 is positive in **90%** of Yersinia-related cases and 30–**50%** of cases associated with other pathogens.

**Clinical features** include inflammatory nonerosive oligoarthritis with predilection for the knees, ankles, and wrists, occurring 2–3 weeks after bacterial diarrhea or urethritis. Enthesitis of the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia is seen in **90%** of cases. Dactylitis occurs in **40%** of patients with Chlamydia-triggered disease. Ocular manifestations include sterile conjunctivitis in **25%** of patients. Dermatologic manifestations include keratoderma blenorrhagicum and circinate balanitis. Genitourinary manifestations include sterile urethritis or prostatitis.

**Treatment** involves continuous NSAIDs as first-line, with most patients having resolution of symptoms within 3–6 months. For disease persisting greater than 3–6 months, sulfasalazine or methotrexate may be used. Antibiotics are not typically needed except for rare cases of demonstrated persistent infections.

## 9. Crystal-Induced Arthritis

### Gout

**Pathogenesis** involves hyperuricemia above the uric acid saturation point of **6.8 mg/dL** leading to crystal formation in synovial fluid, which activates the NLRP3 inflammasome and leads to IL-1β production and PMN recruitment. Hyperuricemia can occur due to uric acid underexcretion (largely due to genetic variation in renal tubular urate handling, but also CKD, thiazide or loop diuretics, pyrazinamide, or ethambutol) or less commonly by uric acid overproduction from inborn errors of purine metabolism or hematologic malignancies.

**Clinical features** include asymptomatic hyperuricemia (with approximately **95%** remaining asymptomatic with no treatment required), acute gouty arthritis (acute monoarticular swelling, erythema, warmth, and exquisite tenderness peaking within 12–24 hours of onset), and tophaceous gout (after 10–20 years of poorly controlled hyperuricemia, with tophi in extensor surfaces of elbows, distal Achilles tendon, PIPs, and ear cartilage). In men, the first gout attack typically occurs age 20–40 years with more than **50%** involving the first MTP. In women, the first attack typically occurs after menopause.

**Diagnosis** involves arthrocentesis for larger joints to rule out infection. The diagnostic gold standard is detection of needle-shaped, negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals (yellow when parallel to the polarizing axis). Synovial WBC is greater than 2K cells/μL and may be greater than 100K cells/μL with PMN predominance.

**Treatment of acute flares** includes colchicine (1.2 mg loading dose followed by 0.6 mg one hour later, continued daily until flare resolves, most effective within 24 hours of onset, avoid if GFR less than 60 mL/min), prednisone (0.5 mg/kg/day for 5 days or longer if longstanding disease, best for patients with CKD), NSAIDs at maximum dose for 5–7 days (avoid in patients with CKD, CHF, or GIB risk), IL-1β blockade with anakinra for refractory flares, and intraarticular glucocorticoids for monoarticular flares.

**Urate-lowering therapy** is indicated for two or more attacks per year, CKD stage 2 or higher, tophaceous disease, or uric acid nephrolithiasis. The target is uric acid less than **5 mg/dL** for patients with tophi and less than **6 mg/dL** for others. Medications include xanthine oxidase inhibitors (allopurinol as first-line, starting at 100 mg daily and uptitrating to 800 mg/day, with HLA-B5801 testing recommended in patients of Asian descent; febuxostat 40–120 mg daily), uricosuric agents (probenecid, less potent than xanthine oxidase inhibitors), and pegloticase (recombinant uricase infused every two weeks for recurrent or tophaceous gout with intolerance to other therapies, with 30–**50%** of patients developing antidrug antibodies). Antiinflammatory prophylaxis during active uric acid lowering includes colchicine 0.6 mg daily, low-dose NSAID, or low-dose prednisone 5 mg daily for 3 months after reaching target (6 months in patients with tophi).

### Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition (CPPD) Arthropathy

**Pathophysiology** involves calcium pyrophosphate crystals accumulating in joints with age, with approximately **50%** of patients age greater than 80 years having detectable chondrocalcinosis. Secondary chondrocalcinosis may develop in younger patients due to metabolic disturbances such as chronic hypomagnesemia, hypophosphatemia, hyperparathyroidism, or hemochromatosis.

**Clinical syndromes** include asymptomatic chondrocalcinosis (most commonly affecting the knees, triangular fibrocartilage of the wrist, pubic symphysis, and MCPs), pseudogout (acute monoarticular arthritis with rhomboid-shaped, positively birefringent crystals on polarized light microscopy, with synovial WBC typically greater than 2K cells/μL), pseudo-OA (characterized by OA-like radiographic changes in joints with chondrocalcinosis, often with deformity out of proportion to arthritis duration, most commonly in the knees with valgus deformity being suggestive), and pseudo-RA (a rare entity with inflammatory polyarthritis of wrists and MCP joints that is typically non-erosive).

**Treatment** of pseudogout flares is similar to gout flare. Prophylaxis with colchicine 0.3 mg daily or low-dose NSAIDs is reasonable if three or more attacks per year occur. There is no therapy to dissolve CPP crystals.

## 10. Large Vessel Vasculitis

### Temporal Arteritis / Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)

**Pathogenesis** involves association with HLA-DRB*04, suggesting cell-mediated autoimmunity resulting in granulomatous inflammation of vessel walls. It most commonly affects major aortic branch vessels or secondary branch vessels.

**Epidemiology** shows an incidence of 10–**20 per 100,000** individuals annually in the United States, with a female to male ratio of 2:1.

**Clinical features** include headache, scalp pain, jaw claudication, and ophthalmic artery involvement presenting with amaurosis fugax that can cause blindness. Approximately **50%** of patients with GCA also have PMR.

**Diagnosis** involves new headache in an adult aged 50 years or older with temporal artery tenderness, ESR of **50 mm** per hour or greater, and temporal artery biopsy showing mononuclear cell infiltration with potential giant multinucleated macrophages and granulomas. Steroids should not be delayed to get biopsy because diseased tissue remains abnormal for up to two weeks after treatment initiation.

**Treatment** involves prednisone 1 mg/kg/day and aspirin 81 mg daily, followed by a prolonged slow steroid taper. Tocilizumab (anti-IL6 receptor monoclonal antibody) given weekly or biweekly with prednisone resulted in a higher rate of glucocorticoid-free remission at one year compared to prednisone alone (53–**56%** versus **18%**).

### Takayasu's Arteritis

**Pathogenesis** involves histology similar to GCA but typically involves larger vessels, most commonly the aorta followed by the subclavian, common carotid, renal, and pulmonary arteries.

**Epidemiology** shows a prevalence of 40 per million individuals in Japan and 4.7–8 per million individuals elsewhere, predominantly affecting young women.

**Clinical features** include involvement of subclavian arteries causing limb claudication, absent or reduced brachial artery pulses, subclavian bruits, and blood pressure discrepancies between arms. Carotid involvement can cause neck pain and tenderness. ESR and CRP are typically elevated.

**Complications** include limb ischemia, aortic aneurysm, aortic regurgitation, stroke, and renal artery stenosis with secondary hypertension.

**Treatment** involves high-dose prednisone (1 mg/kg) with a prolonged slow taper. Surgery or angioplasty to recannulate stenosed vessels may be needed but should be deferred until resolution of active vessel inflammation.

## 11. Medium Vessel Vasculitis

### Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)

**Epidemiology** shows an incidence of 3–4.5 per 100,000, with an average age of onset of 50 years and male to female ratio greater than 1. Only **5%** of cases are associated with HBV in the post-HBV vaccine era.

**Clinical features** include neurologic involvement in 75–**80%** of patients (mononeuritis multiplex or distal symmetric polyneuropathy), constitutional symptoms in **65%** (fever, malaise, weight loss), cutaneous manifestations in 50–**60%** (livedo reticularis, nodules, necrotic ulcers of lower extremities), renal involvement in **40%** (leading to hypertension, AKI or CKD if bilateral, hematuria, proteinuria), and gastrointestinal involvement in 35–**40%** (acute or chronic mesenteric ischemia).

**Diagnosis** is confirmed with biopsy of involved tissue showing focal segmental panmural necrotizing inflammation. If no clear biopsy targets but GI or renal involvement is suspected, mesenteric or renal angiography can show a combination of aneurysms and stenoses at vessel branch points. Renal biopsy should be avoided given high risk of bleeding.

**Treatment** involves prednisone and cyclophosphamide for severe non-HBV-associated cases, with antivirals for HBV-related disease.

### Primary Angiitis of the Central Nervous System (PACNS)

**Epidemiology** shows an incidence of 2.4 per 100,000 annually, with a median age of onset of 50 years.

**Clinical features** include medium vessel vasculitis with isolated CNS involvement, presenting with headache, cognitive impairment or altered mental status, and new or progressive focal neurologic deficits due to strokes.

**Diagnosis** involves MRA or conventional angiography showing beading of CNS artery branches, with the gold standard being brain or leptomeningeal biopsy showing lymphocytic vessel wall inflammation.

**Treatment** involves high-dose glucocorticoids and cyclophosphamide, though permanent disability commonly occurs due to strokes.

### Kawasaki Disease

**Clinical features** include medium vessel vasculitis most common in children (**80%** of cases present prior to age 5 years), typically in Asian boys, with manifestations including high fevers, conjunctivitis, mucositis (strawberry tongue), nonsuppurative cervical adenitis, truncal rash, and palmar and plantar erythema. Coronary aneurysms occur in **25%** of cases.

**Treatment** involves IVIG and aspirin, with an excellent prognosis. Coronary aneurysms may develop and cause CHF and or require CABG in adulthood.

## 12. Small Vessel Vasculitis: ANCA-Associated Vasculitides (AAV)

### Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA)

**Epidemiology** shows an incidence of 7–12 per million individuals annually, with a typical age of onset of 45–60 years.

**Diagnosis** shows most patients with systemic disease are cANCA and anti-PR3 positive. Tissue biopsy showing pauci-immune necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis is the gold standard.

**Clinical features** include ENT involvement in 70–**100%** of patients (sinusitis, rhinorrhea, otitis media, chondritis of ears and nose with saddle nose deformity, nasal septal perforation), pulmonary involvement in 50–**90%** (cavitary nodules, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, tracheal subglottic stenosis), renal involvement in 40–**100%** (pauci-immune necrotizing glomerulonephritis), dermatologic involvement in 10–**50%** (palpable purpura, nodules, pyoderma gangrenosum), neurologic involvement in **30%** (mononeuritis multiplex, distal symmetric polyneuropathy, pachymeningitis), and ocular involvement in 15–**60%** (posterior uveitis, scleritis, episcleritis, retroorbital pseudotumor, dacryoadenitis).

**Treatment** for systemic organ-threatening disease involves induction therapy with pulse dose glucocorticoids followed by rituximab or cyclophosphamide. Rituximab is non-inferior to cyclophosphamide for induction therapy. Maintenance therapy involves rituximab for 12–24 months after remission, azathioprine, or methotrexate. For limited upper airway disease, glucocorticoids plus methotrexate or rituximab is used.

### Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)

**Epidemiology** shows an incidence of 2.7–94 per million individuals annually, with an average age of onset of 50–60 years.

**Diagnosis** shows small vessel vasculitis of the lungs and kidneys. Approximately 50–**75%** of patients are ANCA positive, generally pANCA and anti-MPO positive. Tissue biopsy shows non-granulomatous necrotizing pauci-immune vasculitis.

**Clinical features** include renal involvement in 80–**100%** (necrotizing glomerulonephritis), dermatologic involvement in 30–**60%** (palpable purpura with leukocytoclastic vasculitis, livedo reticularis, nodules, necrotic skin ulcers), neurologic involvement in 30–**70%** (mononeuritis multiplex, distal symmetric polyneuropathy, pachymeningitis), pulmonary involvement in 25–**55%** (diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, organizing pneumonia, ILD), and ENT involvement in 10–**30%** (sinusitis, sensorineural hearing loss).

**Treatment** is similar to GPA.

### Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA)

**Epidemiology** shows this is the rarest ANCA-associated vasculitis, with an incidence of 0.1–2.66 per million individuals annually.

**Diagnosis** shows approximately **50%** of patients are ANCA positive and usually pANCA and anti-MPO positive. Eosinophilia greater than 1,500 cells/μL is often present. Diagnostic gold standard is biopsy of involved tissue showing pauci-immune necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis with eosinophilic infiltration.

**Clinical features** include neurologic involvement in **70%** (mononeuritis multiplex or distal sensory polyneuropathy), pulmonary involvement (most patients have preceding asthma that often improves during the vasculitic phase, with migratory pulmonary consolidations), cardiac involvement in 20–**40%** (pericarditis, endomyocarditis, conduction system disease, CHF), and renal involvement in **25%** (pauci-immune glomerulonephritis).

**Treatment** involves glucocorticoids alone for non-organ-threatening disease, with cyclophosphamide added for major organ involvement. The lowest mortality among all ANCA-associated vasculitidies with a 5-year survival of **97%**.

## 13. Small Vessel Vasculitis: Immune Complex Mediated

### Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis

**Pathogenesis** involves immune complex-mediated small vessel vasculitis caused by Ig molecules that precipitate at temperatures lower than 37°C.

**Types** include Type I (IgM rheumatoid factor associated with multiple myeloma) and Types II and III ("mixed cryoglobulinemia" because the precipitating antibodies are a mixture of polyclonal IgG and either polyclonal IgM rheumatoid factor in Type II or monoclonal IgM rheumatoid factor in Type III).

**Diagnosis** shows **90%** of cases of mixed cryoglobulinemia are associated with HCV infection. Lab findings include positive RF and very low C4. Note that samples must be kept at greater than 37°C to avoid premature precipitation.

**Clinical features** include dermatologic involvement in 70–**90%** (palpable purpura, Raynaud's, ulcers, skin necrosis, livedo reticularis), neurologic involvement in **60%** (distal symmetric polyneuropathy), arthritis in **40%**, and renal involvement in **40%** (immune complex glomerulonephritis).

**Treatment** involves treating HCV or controlling the underlying rheumatic disease. For organ-threatening disease such as acute renal failure, plasmapheresis, glucocorticoids plus rituximab, or cyclophosphamide are options.

### IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein Purpura, HSP)

**Epidemiology** shows this is a childhood vasculitis that rarely occurs in adults, with an incidence of 14 per million individuals annually.

**Clinical features** include often being preceded by viral URI or strep pharyngitis, with main manifestations including abdominal pain and GI bleeding in **65%**, arthritis and arthralgias in **63%**, and glomerulonephritis in **40%**.

**Diagnosis** involves skin biopsy with leukocytoclastic vasculitis and heavy IgA deposits, or renal biopsy showing IgA nephropathy.

**Treatment** is self-limited in children. Adults may require glucocorticoids and or cyclophosphamide for severe organ-threatening disease.

### Hypersensitivity Vasculitis

**Clinical features** include a small vessel vasculitis with isolated cutaneous involvement triggered by exposure to a known or unknown antigen, presenting with palpable purpura.

**Diagnosis** involves skin biopsy showing leukocytoclastic vasculitis without IgA staining. The vasculitis will typically resolve 7–10 days after removal of the offending antigen. Persistent symptoms for more than a month should raise suspicion for an alternative diagnosis.

**Treatment** involves removal of the offending antigen.

## 14. Other Rheumatologic Conditions

### Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

**Clinical features** include a syndrome of neck, shoulder, and hip girdle stiffness that is typically worse after immobility, associated with elevations in markers of systemic inflammation such as ESR and CRP. Approximately **50%** of patients with GCA have PMR, and **20%** of patients with PMR have GCA.

**Diagnosis** is a diagnosis of exclusion made after ruling out myopathies (where weakness is greater than pain, while in PMR pain is greater than weakness) or nonsystemic periarticular syndromes. PMR improves briskly with prednisone 12.5–20 mg daily, and failure to improve should raise suspicion for an alternative diagnosis.

**Treatment** involves prednisone 20 mg per day with a prolonged taper, often lasting 1–2 years.

### Behçet Syndrome

**Pathogenesis** is not fully elucidated, but increased risk with HLA-B51 allele implicates cell-mediated autoimmunity.

**Epidemiology** shows an incidence of **1 per 100,000** individuals annually in the United States, with much higher rates in Turkey (**400 per 100,000** annually). It is also relatively more prevalent in Iran, Saudi Arabia, Greece, Japan, Korea, and China. Age of onset is typically 20–30 years.

**Clinical features** include dermatologic manifestations such as recurrent genital and oral ulcers (the latter occur in nearly **100%** of patients and are painful, unlike those in SLE), erythema nodosum with ulceration, and pseudofolliculitis or nodular cystic acne of the face and neck. Vascular manifestations include vasculitis with potential involvement of small, medium, and large vessels, with venous and arterial thrombi potentially including IVC thrombosis, dural venous sinus thrombosis, arterial thromboses, and aneurysms. Gastrointestinal manifestations include apthous ulcers of the ileum and cecum presenting with abdominal pain, anorexia, diarrhea, and GI bleeding. Articular manifestations include inflammatory oligoarthritis in approximately **50%** of patients with large joint predominance, most commonly the knees. Ocular manifestations include uveitis (anterior, posterior, or panuveitis) and retinal vasculitis. Neurologic manifestations include headache, altered mental status, and cranial neuropathies including CN VIII.

**Diagnosis** requires 4 or more points from the following: oral ulcers (2 points), genital ulcers (2 points), pathergy (1 point), skin lesions (1 point), and eye lesions (2 points). Pathergy testing involves a skin prick with a sterile needle, with patients developing a pustule being deemed positive at 48 hours.

**Treatment** for oral and genital ulcers involves topical glucocorticoids for active lesions and colchicine for prevention of new genital lesions and erythema nodosum. For arthritis, colchicine for acute flares and azathioprine or TNF inhibitors for chronic arthritis are used. For thrombotic events, anticoagulation with consideration of immunosuppression is indicated. For intestinal ulcer flares, high-dose glucocorticoids with mesalamine or azathioprine are used. For CNS disease, high-dose glucocorticoids with subsequent introduction of azathioprine is recommended.

### Sarcoidosis

**Pathogenesis** is incompletely characterized, with evidence supporting both antigen-driven and non-antigen-driven granulomatous inflammation.

**Epidemiology** shows a prevalence of 10–**40 per 100,000** in the US, with **20%** of the general population having non-caseating granulomas at autopsy (most of whom were asymptomatic). Black individuals have a threefold higher incidence than white individuals. Most diagnoses are made between ages 20–40 years.

**Clinical features** involve non-caseating granulomas that can affect any organ. Pulmonary involvement includes bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, which is the most common radiographic finding, along with interstitial lung disease. Cutaneous involvement includes erythema nodosum, lupus pernio (purplish plaques on the face that are pathognomonic), and papular and plaque lesions. Ocular involvement includes anterior uveitis, which is the most common ocular manifestation, along with posterior uveitis, conjunctival granulomas, and lacrimal gland involvement. Neurologic involvement includes cranial neuropathies with CN VII palsy being most common, along with optic neuritis and CNS involvement causing headache and altered mental status. Cardiac involvement can cause conduction abnormalities and heart failure. Hypercalcemia occurs in approximately **10%** of patients due to extrarenal production of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D by activated macrophages in granulomas. Hepatic involvement is common with granulomas but rarely causes clinical liver disease.

**Diagnosis** involves clinical and radiographic findings plus histologic evidence of non-caseating granulomas. Serum ACE is elevated in **60%** of patients and is **95%** specific for sarcoidosis, and ACE elevation can obviate the need for biopsy. **Heerfordt syndrome** is the triad of parotitis, fever, and anterior uveitis.

**Subsyndromes** include Lofgren syndrome (acute presentation with erythema nodosum, bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, migratory polyarthralgias, and ankle periarthritis) and Heerfordt syndrome (parotitis, fever, and anterior uveitis).

**Treatment** generally involves treating symptomatic disease, as asymptomatic patients may just be monitored. First-line treatment is glucocorticoids for symptomatic involvement, with TNF inhibitors for refractory disease. Cardiac involvement is treated with glucocorticoids, and cardiac PET or MRI should be considered if cardiac involvement is suspected. Neurologic involvement requires MRI of the brain and spine.
