## 1. Diagnostic Reasoning in Rheumatology

Most rheumatologic diseases are diagnosed clinically, meaning a rheumatologist weighs the exam findings, labs, imaging, and tissue biopsy results and makes a diagnosis based on the strength of concordance between these findings and a given disease entity.

**Classification criteria** exist to ensure homogeneity of patients with rheumatologic diseases who enroll in clinical trials. Therefore, these criteria are biased in favor of specificity and are NOT intended to diagnose rheumatic diseases.

## 2. Approach to Reading a Hand X-Ray

When evaluating a hand X-ray, several key features should be assessed systematically.

**Alignment** involves checking for misaligned joints, which can help suggest specific pathologies. For example, ulnar deviation of the metacarpophalangeal joints (MCPs) is suggestive of rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

**Bone density** involves using lucency to assess bone density, with denser regions appearing brighter on X-ray. When the lucency of juxtaarticular bone is similar to that of the central metacarpal, there is said to be periarticular osteopenia, which is an early pathologic change in RA. Conversely, subchondral sclerosis (increased brightness of the juxtaarticular bone) is suggestive of osteoarthritis (OA).

**Cortical edges** should be examined for erosions, which appear as interruptions in the smooth contour of the periarticular cortex and raise concern for the inflammatory arthropathies or erosive OA. Osteophytes appear as irregular outgrowths of bone and suggest OA.

**Joint spaces** should be evaluated for narrowing, which suggests arthritis but does not distinguish inflammatory from noninflammatory pathology.

**Soft tissues** should be examined, particularly the triangular fibrocartilage which sits between the ulna and the carpal bones. This is a good site to assess for chondrocalcinosis, which is calcium pyrophosphate crystal buildup commonly due to pseudogout.
