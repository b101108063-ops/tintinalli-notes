# Diagnostics

This chapter covers diagnostics in rheumatology.

---

## Diagnostic Reasoning in Rheumatology

Most rheumatologic diseases are diagnosed "clinically" — a rheumatologist weighs the exam findings, labs, imaging, and/or tissue biopsy results, and makes a diagnosis based on the strength of concordance between these findings and a given disease entity.

**Classification criteria** exist to ensure homogeneity of patients with rheumatologic diseases who enroll in clinical trials. These criteria are biased in favor of specificity and are **NOT intended to diagnose** rheumatic diseases.

## Approach to Reading a Hand X-Ray

| Feature | Assessment | Pathologic Significance |
|---------|------------|----------------------|
| **Alignment** | Misaligned joints | Ulnar deviation of MCPs suggests rheumatoid arthritis (RA). |
| **Bone density** | Assess lucency | Periarticular osteopenia (lucency similar to central metacarpal) is an early pathologic change in RA. Subchondral sclerosis (increased brightness) suggests osteoarthritis (OA). |
| **Cortical edges** | Look for interruptions | Erosions suggest inflammatory arthropathies or erosive OA. Osteophytes (irregular bone outgrowths) suggest OA. |
| **Joint spaces** | Assess narrowing | Joint space narrowing suggests arthritis but does not distinguish inflammatory from noninflammatory pathology. |
| **Soft tissues** | Triangular fibrocartilage | Located between the ulna and carpal bones; assess for chondrocalcinosis (calcium pyrophosphate crystal buildup, pseudogout). |
