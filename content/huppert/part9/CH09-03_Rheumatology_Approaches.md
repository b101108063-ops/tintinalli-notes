## 1. Diagnostic Approach to Joint Pain

The evaluation of joint pain involves a systematic approach that considers multiple factors.

### Consider Nonarticular Mimics of Joint Pain

Articular structures that can cause joint pain include the synovium, synovial fluid, articular cartilage, intraarticular ligaments, joint capsule, and juxtaarticular bone. Nonarticular or periarticular structures that can mimic joint pain include extraarticular ligaments, tendons, bursae, muscle, fascia, bone, nerve, and overlying skin.

### Characterize the Joint Pain

**Temporality** involves distinguishing between acute pain (less than 6 weeks) and chronic pain (more than 6 weeks). **Inflammation** involves determining whether the joint pain is inflammatory or noninflammatory. Inflammatory features to assess include morning stiffness lasting more than 30–60 minutes on history, and on exam the classic signs of "rubor, calor, tumor" representing periarticular erythema, warmth, and joint swelling. Laboratory findings of elevated ESR and or CRP and synovial fluid WBC greater than 2K cells/μL also suggest inflammation. **The number of joints involved** helps classify the arthritis as monoarthritis (single joint), oligoarthritis (2–4 joints), or polyarthritis (5 or more joints).

### Analyze Synovial Fluid

Synovial fluid analysis is particularly critical in patients with acute monoarthritis. The cell count provides important diagnostic information. WBC less than 2K cells/μL suggests noninflammatory arthritis. A bloody tap can result from trauma, coagulopathy, or tumor. Non-bloody fluid with this WBC count suggests OA, avascular necrosis, or Charcot arthropathy (peripheral neuropathy resulting in unwitting pathologic joint trauma typically in the feet and ankles). WBC of 2–10K cells/μL suggests inflammatory arthritis, gout, or pseudogout. WBC greater than 20K cells/μL raises high concern for infectious arthritis, though gout and pseudogout are also possible, and orthopedic surgical consultation for consideration of washout is essential.

### Crystal Analysis

**Monosodium urate crystals** are needle-shaped and negatively birefringent, appearing yellow when parallel to the microscope polarization axis. These are suggestive of gout in the setting of acute monoarthritis. **Calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate crystals** are rhomboid with weak positive birefringence, appearing blue when parallel to the polarization axis. These are suggestive of pseudogout in the setting of acute monoarthritis.

### Differential Diagnosis Based on Clinical Characteristics

| | Inflammatory | Non-Inflammatory |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Acute Mono** | Gout, pseudogout, septic arthritis, gonococcal arthritis | Bony or ligamentous trauma, hemarthrosis, osteonecrosis or AVN |
| **Acute Oligo** | Disseminated gonococcal infection, rheumatic fever, acute leukemia | — |
| **Acute Poly** | HIV, HBV, HCV, parvovirus B19, chikungunya, rubella, acute leukemia | — |
| **Chronic Mono** | Borrelia (Lyme), mycobacterial, fungal | OA (knee or hip), primary bone tumor, bony metastases |
| **Chronic Oligo** | Axial spondyloarthritis, peripheral spondyloarthritis (e.g., reactive arthritis, IBD-associated arthritis), sarcoidosis, AAV, Whipple's disease | OA (hand, knee, or hip), DISH, infiltrative diseases (amyloid arthropathy, hemochromatosis, Wilson's disease) |
| **Chronic Poly** | RA, PsA, CTDs (SLE, Sjögren's, SSc, PM/DM, MCTD), CPPD, pseudoRA | CPPD pseudo-OA, infiltrative diseases |

## 3. ANA-Associated Connective Tissue Disorders

Rheumatologic conditions associated with a positive ANA include systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), systemic sclerosis (SSc), myositis (approximately **50%** of dermatomyositis/polymyositis cases are ANA-positive), mixed connective tissue disease (MCTD), and primary Sjögren's syndrome.

### ANA Testing Pearls

Antinuclear antibodies (ANA) are autoantibodies that bind to the contents of the cell nucleus. The ANA test detects the autoantibodies present in the patient's serum. ANA can be elevated in many disorders including autoimmune conditions, infection, and malignancy. Approximately **20%** of healthy women have a positive ANA, so it is nonspecific and should only be sent if clinical suspicion is high. The probability of autoimmunity increases with higher ANA titers. An ANA titer of 1:80 or greater is required by EULAR/ACR SLE classification criteria. An ANA titer of 1:640 or greater is **95.8%** specific for SLE. If an ELISA-based ANA test is negative but clinical suspicion for CTD is high, asking the lab to carry out an immunofluorescence microscopy-based ANA test is more specific.
