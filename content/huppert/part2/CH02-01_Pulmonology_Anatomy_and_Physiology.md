## 1. Overview of Pulmonary Mechanics & Gas Exchange

* **Statics**: Refers to forces acting on the lung that affect volumes and elastic behavior.
  * The lungs possess collapsing forces, whereas the chest wall has expanding forces.
  * At the end of a normal exhalation, the volume of air remaining is the **functional reserve capacity (FRC)**, where the expanding and collapsing forces are in balance.
  * Inhalation requires shifting away from this equilibrium point and is an active process requiring the use of respiratory muscles.
* **Dynamics**: Refers to the forces moving air, including flow patterns and resistance.
* **Gas Exchange**: The physiological handling of O$_2$ and $\text{CO}_2$.

## 2. Statics: Volumes, Capacities, Compliance, and Surface Tension

### Lung Volumes and Capacities

| Measurement | Type | Definition |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| TV | Volume | Tidal volume – Volume of air taken with normal, quiet breath. |
| E/IRV | Volume | Expiratory / inspiratory reserve volume – Volume that can be expired/inspired beyond a normal breath. |
| RV | Volume | Residual volume – Volume retained in lungs after maximal expiration. |
| TLC | Capacity | Total lung capacity – Volume after maximal inspiration. |
| FRC | Capacity | Functional residual capacity – Volume after normal expiration. |
| VC | Capacity | Vital capacity – Volume that can be expired following maximal inspiration. |
| IC | Capacity | Inspiratory capacity – Volume that can be inspired after normal expiration. |

### Changes in Pathologic States

* **Obstructive lung disease** (e.g., asthma, emphysema): $\uparrow$TLC, FRC, RV; $\downarrow\downarrow$FEV1, $\downarrow$FVC, FEV1/FVC < 0.7.
* **Restrictive lung disease** (e.g., fibrosis): $\downarrow$TLC, FRC, RV; $\downarrow$FEV1, $\downarrow\downarrow$FVC, $\uparrow$ or normal FEV1/FVC.
* At a given lung volume, transpulmonary pressures (difference between pleural and alveolar pressure) are higher in restrictive disease and lower in obstructive disease.

### Compliance and Elastance

* **Compliance** ($C = \Delta V/\Delta P$): A filling term describing the ability of the lungs to stretch.
* **Elastance**: An expiration term describing the ability of the lungs to collapse from a stretched position.
* **Obstructive diseases**: $\uparrow$Compliance, $\downarrow$Elastance.
* **Restrictive diseases**: $\downarrow$Compliance, $\uparrow$Elastance.
* **Hysteresis**: Compliance changes with inspiration and expiration; it is lower during expiration and at extreme volumes.

### Surface Tension

* **Law of Laplace** ($P = 2T/r$): Large alveoli remain open due to a high radius, whereas small alveoli tend to collapse, causing atelectasis.
* **Surfactant**: Decreases surface tension, which decreases pressure, increases compliance, and reduces alveolar collapse.
  * Produced by Type II alveolar cells via Choline + diacylglycerol $\rightarrow$ dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC).
  * Contains lecithin; a lecithin:sphingomyelin ratio $\ge$ 2:1 in amniotic fluid indicates lung maturity.

## 3. Dynamics: Flow, Resistance, and V/Q Matching

### Airway Flow and Resistance

* **Ohm's Law** ($\Delta P = Q \times R$): Defines the relationship between pressure ($P$), flow ($Q$), and resistance ($R$).
* **Laminar flow**: $\Delta Q$ is proportional to $\Delta P$ (peripheral airway); Reynold's # < 2000.
* **Turbulent flow**: $\Delta Q$ is proportional to $\sqrt{\Delta P}$ (central airway); Reynold's # > 2000.
* Reducing the density of gas favors laminar flow over turbulent flow, which decreases the work of breathing.
* **Resistance (Poiseuille's Law: $R = 8\eta l/\pi r^4$)**:
  * Medium-sized airways have the highest resistance because small-sized bronchi exist in parallel, reducing overall resistance.
  * **Sympathetic stimulation**: $\beta_2$ receptors $\rightarrow$ smooth muscle relaxation $\rightarrow$ airway dilation $\rightarrow$ decreased resistance (e.g., $\beta_2$-agonist inhalers).
  * **Parasympathetic stimulation**: Smooth muscle contraction $\rightarrow$ airway constriction $\rightarrow$ increased resistance (e.g., anticholinergic toxicity).
  * High lung volumes provide more traction to hold airways open, decreasing resistance.

### The Breathing Cycle

* **At rest**: Intra-alveolar pressure = 0 cm H$_2$O (equal to atmospheric); Pleural pressure = $-$5 cm H$_2$O; Transmural pressure = +5 cm H$_2$O; Lung volume = FRC.
* **Inspiration**: Inspiratory muscles contract $\rightarrow$ Thoracic volume increases $\rightarrow$ Intra-alveolar pressure $<$ 0 cm H$_2$O $\rightarrow$ Air flows in $\rightarrow$ Intrapleural pressure becomes more negative.
* **Expiration (passive)**: Elastic forces compress air in alveoli $\rightarrow$ Alveolar pressure $>$ atmospheric pressure $\rightarrow$ Air flows out $\rightarrow$ Intrapleural pressure returns to baseline ($-$5 cm H$_2$O).
* **Forced expiration**: Musculature (abdominal wall, internal intercostals) creates an even more positive intrapleural pressure, rapidly forcing air out.

### V/Q Matching & Dead Space

* **Minute ventilation** = Tidal volume $\times$ breaths/min.
* **Alveolar ventilation** = (Tidal volume $-$ dead space) $\times$ breaths/min.
* **Dead Space** (Ventilation without Perfusion):
  * Anatomic dead space: Volume of conducting airways (~150 mL) that do not participate in gas exchange.
  * Physiologic dead space formula: $V_D = V_T \times (P_{A_{CO_2}} - P_{E_{CO_2}})/P_{A_{CO_2}}$.
  * If physiologic dead space $>$ predicted anatomic dead space, pathology is present (e.g., Pulmonary embolism where $Q = 0 \rightarrow V/Q = \infty$). Treating with 100% O$_2$ will help.
* **Shunt** (Perfusion without Ventilation): e.g., Airway obstruction where $V = 0 \rightarrow V/Q = 0$. Treating with 100% O$_2$ does NOT help.
* V/Q mismatch is more likely to cause hypoxemia than hypercapnia because oxygen has a sigmoidal hemoglobin binding curve (hyperventilating does not help since beds are generally already saturated), whereas $\text{CO}_2$ has a linear curve (hyperventilating can compensate and increase removal).

## 4. Gas Exchange & Hemoglobin

### Oxygen Transport

* Oxygen is transported dissolved in blood and bound to Hemoglobin (majority).
* **Hemoglobin (Hgb)**: Composed of $2\alpha2\beta$ subunits and a heme moiety with an iron-containing porphyrin.
* **O$_2$ Capacity**: The maximum amount of O$_2$ that can be bound to Hgb, measured at 100% saturation.
* **O$_2$ Content formula**: $(1.34 \times [\text{Hgb}] \times \% \text{ Sat}) + (0.0031 \times PaO_2)$.
* **Oxygen–Hgb dissociation curve is SIGMOIDAL**:
  * **Shift Left** (Increased O$_2$ affinity): $\downarrow$PCO$_2$, $\downarrow$2,3 DPG, $\downarrow$Exercise, $\downarrow$Temperature, $\downarrow$H+, Fetal Hb, $\text{CO}$.
  * **Shift Right** (Lower O$_2$ affinity): $\uparrow$PCO$_2$, Altitude, $\uparrow$2,3 DPG, Exercise, $\uparrow$Temperature, $\uparrow$H+ (CADET).

### Carbon Dioxide Transport

* Forms of $\text{CO}_2$ transport: 1) HCO$_3^-$ formed by carbonic anhydrase (~90% of $\text{CO}_2$), 2) Dissolved $\text{CO}_2$, 3) Carbaminohemoglobin.
* **The Hgb–$\text{CO}_2$ dissociation curve is LINEAR.**
* **Haldane effect**: In the lungs, Hgb is oxygenated, causing the Hgb–$\text{CO}_2$ curve to shift right and down, unloading $\text{CO}_2$.

### Abnormalities in Hemoglobin

* **Methemoglobinemia (MetHgb)**: Iron in Hgb converts from ferrous ($\text{Fe}^{2+}$) to ferric ($\text{Fe}^{3+}$) form, rendering it unable to bind oxygen.
  * *Medication culprits*: Dapsone, nitrates.
  * *Treatment*: Methylene blue.
* **Carboxyhemoglobin (COHgb)**: Carbon monoxide reacts with hemoglobin and releases more slowly than $\text{CO}_2$.
  * *Symptoms*: Tiredness, dizziness, unconsciousness, death.
  * *Treatment*: Non-rebreather, hyperbaric chamber, involve toxicology.

## 5. Chest X-Ray (CXR) Diagnostics

### Types of CXR

* **PA and lateral CXR**: Radiation moves posterior $\rightarrow$ anterior, obtained with lateral images; this is the most common modality for lung imaging.
* **AP portable CXR**: Radiation moves anterior $\rightarrow$ posterior; used for unstable or hospitalized patients.
  * *Drawbacks*: Subject to patient rotation issues, enlarges the cardiac silhouette, and lacks lateral images.
* **Lateral decubitus CXR**: Patient is placed on their side to assess free-flowing vs. loculated pleural fluid; increasingly replaced by POCUS.

### Approach to CXR Interpretation

* Verify patient name and date, and identify the CXR type.
* **Evaluate Quality:**
  * *Rotation*: Clavicular symmetry, trachea midline.
  * *Inspiration*: Count for 8.5–11 rib spaces above the diaphragm.
  * *Penetration*: The film is overexposed if the thoracic spine can be seen through the heart.
* **Systematic Reading Order**: Airway (trachea) $\rightarrow$ Bronchoalveolar markings (linear, reticular, nodular, or reticulonodular) $\rightarrow$ Cardiac silhouette and mediastinum $\rightarrow$ Diaphragm $\rightarrow$ Extras (lung parenchyma, MSK, lines/tubes).
