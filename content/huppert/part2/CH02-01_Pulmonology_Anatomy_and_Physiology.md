# Anatomy and Physiology

## Anatomy of the Lungs

> **Note:** See Figure 2.1 for the five lobes of the lung and the anatomy of the tracheobronchial tree.

## Overview: Mechanics

**Mechanics** encompasses two main areas:

| Type | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Statics** | Forces acting on the lung that affect volumes and elastic behavior |
| **Dynamics** | Forces moving air, including flow patterns and resistance |

- **Lungs** = collapsing forces
- **Chest wall (CW)** = expanding forces

> **See Figure 2.2** for the lung–chest wall system. The chest wall has a tendency toward expansion, while the lungs have a tendency toward collapse.

**Functional Reserve Capacity (FRC):**
- At the end of a normal exhalation, the volume of air remaining in the lungs is the FRC
- At FRC, the expanding chest wall forces and the collapsing lung forces are in balance
- Since initiating a new breath requires the lung–chest wall system to shift away from this equilibrium point, **inhalation is an active process** that requires the use of respiratory muscles

## Statics: Volumes/Capacities, Compliance, and Surface Tension

### Lung Volumes and Capacities

> **See Table 2.1** for definitions and **Figure 2.3** for changes in pathologic states.

#### Volumes

| Abbrev | Name | Definition |
|--------|------|------------|
| TV | Tidal volume | Volume of air taken with normal, quiet breath |
| ERV | Expiratory reserve volume | Volume of air that can be expired beyond a normal breath |
| IRV | Inspiratory reserve volume | Volume of air that can be inspired beyond a normal breath |
| RV | Residual volume | Volume of air retained in lungs even after maximal expiration |

#### Capacities

| Abbrev | Name | Formula |
|--------|------|---------|
| TLC | Total lung capacity | Volume in lungs after maximal inspiration |
| FRC | Functional residual capacity | ERV + RV |
| VC | Vital capacity | ERV + TV + IRV |
| IC | Inspiratory capacity | TV + IRV |

> **See Figure 2.3** for lung volumes and capacities in health and disease. Panel A shows the four components of volume; Panel B shows changes in obstructive and restrictive lung diseases.

#### Changes in Pathologic States

**Obstructive Lung Disease** (e.g., asthma, emphysema):
- ↑ TLC, FRC, RV
- ↓↓ FEV1, ↓ FVC
- FEV1/FVC < 0.7

**Restrictive Lung Disease** (e.g., fibrosis):
- ↓ TLC, FRC, RV
- ↓ FEV1, ↓↓ FVC
- ↑ or normal FEV1/FVC

### Compliance

$$C = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta P}$$

Where:
- C = Compliance
- V = Volume
- P = Pressure

| Property | Definition | Clinical Correlation |
|----------|------------|---------------------|
| **Compliance** | Ability of lungs to stretch (filling term) | Obstructive ↑, Restrictive ↓ |
| **Elastance** | Ability of lungs to collapse from stretched position (expiration term) | Obstructive ↓, Restrictive ↑ |

### Surface Tension

**Law of Laplace:**
$$P = \frac{2T}{r}$$

Where:
- P = Pressure
- T = Surface tension
- r = Radius

**Clinical implications:**
- Large alveoli remain open due to high radius
- Small alveoli collapse, causing atelectasis

### Surfactant

- Produced by **Type II alveolar cells**
- Decreases surface tension → Decreases pressure → Increases compliance and reduces alveolar collapse

**Composition pathway:**
```
Choline + Diacylglycerol → Dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC)
```

**Lecithin:sphingomyelin ratio:**
- ≥ 2:1 in amniotic fluid = lung maturity

### Hysteresis

- Compliance (i.e., the slope of a pressure/volume curve) changes with inspiration and expiration
- Compliance is **lower during expiration** and at **extreme volumes** (very full or very empty lungs)

> **See Figure 2.4** for transpulmonary pressures by lung volume in health and disease.

**Transpulmonary Pressure:**
- Pressure across the lung that produces pulmonary ventilation
- Equal to the difference between pleural and alveolar pressure
- At a given lung volume:
  - **Higher** in restrictive disease
  - **Lower** in obstructive disease

## Dynamics: Flow, Resistance, and V/Q Matching

### Flow

**Ohm's Law:**
$$\Delta P = Q \times R$$

Where:
- P = Pressure
- Q = Flow
- R = Resistance

#### Flow Types

| Type | Relationship | Reynold's # | Location |
|------|-------------|-------------|----------|
| **Laminar** | ΔQ ∝ ΔP | < 2000 | Peripheral airway |
| **Turbulent** | ΔQ ∝ √ΔP | > 2000 | Central airway |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Reduce density of gas → favors laminar over turbulent flow → decrease work of breathing

### Breathing Cycle

#### At Rest
- Lung collapsing forces = Chest wall expanding forces
- Intraalveolar pressure = **0 cm H₂O** (equal to atmospheric pressure)
- Pleural pressure = **−5 cm H₂O** (negative)
- Transmural pressure = **+5 cm H₂O** (positive)
- Lung volume = FRC

#### Inspiration
1. Inspiratory muscles contract
2. Thoracic volume increases
3. Intraalveolar pressure < **0 cm H₂O** (lower than atmospheric)
4. Air flows in
5. Intrapleural pressure becomes more negative
6. Lung volume = FRC + TV

#### Expiration (Passive)
1. Elastic forces of lungs compress greater volume of air in alveoli
2. Alveolar pressure > atmospheric pressure
3. Air flows out
4. Intrapleural pressure returns to baseline (**−5 cm H₂O**)

#### Forced Expiration
- Similar to passive expiration, EXCEPT:
- Use of musculature (abdominal wall, internal intercostals)
- Results in **even more positive intrapleural pressure**
- Rapidly forces air out of the lungs

### Resistance

**Poiseuille's Law:**
$$R = \frac{8\eta l}{\pi r^4}$$

Where:
- R = Resistance to flow
- η = Viscosity of gas
- l = Length of airway
- r = Radius of airway

> **Note:** Medium-sized airways = Highest resistance (small-sized bronchi exist in parallel, so less resistance)

#### Factors Influencing Resistance

**1. Airway Radius Modification via Bronchial Smooth Muscle**

| Stimulation | Receptor | Effect | Example |
|-------------|----------|--------|---------|
| **Sympathetic** | β2 | Smooth muscle relaxation → airway dilation → ↓ resistance | β2-agonist inhalers |
| **Parasympathetic** | — | Smooth muscle contraction → airway constriction → ↑ resistance | Anticholinergic toxicity |

**2. Lung Volume**
- High volume = more traction holding airways open → decreased resistance (and vice versa)

### V/Q Matching

#### Calculating Ventilation

| Term | Formula |
|------|---------|
| Minute ventilation | Tidal volume × breaths/min |
| Alveolar ventilation | (Tidal volume − dead space) × breaths/min |

#### Dead Space

> **Definition:** Ventilation but no perfusion (see Figure 2.5)

| Type | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Anatomic dead space** | Volume of conducting airways (~150 mL) — areas that move air but do not participate in gas exchange |
| **Physiologic dead space** | Functional measurement of lung volume that does not participate in gas exchange |

**Calculating Physiologic Dead Space:**
$$V_D = V_T \times \frac{P_{ACO_2} - P_{ECO_2}}{P_{ACO_2}}$$

Where:
- VD = Physiologic dead space
- VT = Tidal volume
- PACO2 = PCO₂ of arterial blood = PCO₂ of alveolar gas
- PECO2 = PCO₂ of expired air

> **Clinical Pearl:** If physiologic dead space > predicted anatomic dead space → pathology present (e.g., **pulmonary embolism** — clot disrupts blood flow [Q = 0] → V/Q = ∞; **100% O₂ will help**)

#### Shunt

> **Definition:** Perfusion but no ventilation (see Figure 2.5)

- Example: Airway obstruction (V = 0) → V/Q = 0
- **100% O₂ does NOT help**

> **See Figure 2.5** for dead space and shunt.

#### V/Q Mismatch Physiology

| Gas | Curve | Clinical Implication |
|-----|-------|---------------------|
| **O₂** | Sigmoidal Hgb binding | Generally saturated in alveolar-capillary bed; hyperventilating does NOT help hypoxemia |
| **CO₂** | Linear Hgb binding | Increased ventilation CAN increase removal; hyperventilating CAN help compensate |

## Gas Exchange

### Oxygen

**Oxygen Transport:**
1. Dissolved in blood
2. **Bound to Hgb** (most important/majority)

#### Hemoglobin

- Structure: **2α2β subunits**
- Heme moiety with iron-containing porphyrin

**Key Definitions:**

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **O₂ capacity** | Maximum amount of O₂ that can be bound to Hgb; must be measured at **100%** saturation |
| **O₂ content** | Total O₂ carried in blood = Hgb-bound O₂ + dissolved O₂ |

$$O_2 \text{ content} = (1.34 \times [Hgb] \times \% \text{ Sat}) + (0.0031 \times PaO_2)$$

#### Oxygen–Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve

> **See Figure 2.6** for the O₂–Hgb dissociation curve.

**Curve Shape:** SIGMOIDAL

| Shift | Cause | Effect |
|-------|-------|--------|
| **Shift L** | ↓ PCO₂, ↓ 2,3-DPG, ↓ Exercise, ↓ Temperature, ↓ H⁺, Fetal Hgb, CO | **Increased O₂ affinity** — O₂ binds Hgb more tightly |
| **Shift R** | ↑ PCO₂, Altitude, ↑ 2,3-DPG, Exercise, ↑ Temperature, (CADET), ↑ H⁺ | **Lower O₂ affinity** — O₂ released from Hgb more easily |

> **Mnemonic (CADET):** CO₂, Acidosis, 2,3-DPG, Exercise, Temperature

### Carbon Dioxide

**CO₂ Transport:**
1. **HCO₃** formed by carbonic anhydrase (~**90%** of CO₂)
2. Dissolved CO₂
3. Carbaminohemoglobin (i.e., Hgb–CO₂)

**Hgb–CO₂ Dissociation Curve:** LINEAR

**Haldane Effect:**
- In lungs, Hgb is oxygenated → Hgb–CO₂ dissociation curve shifts **right + down** → CO₂ unloads

### Variations/Disruptions in Hemoglobin Function

#### Methemoglobinemia (MetHgb)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Mechanism** | Iron in Hgb converts from ferrous [Fe²⁺] to ferric [Fe³⁺] form → unable to bind O₂ |
| **Causative medications** | Dapsone, nitrates |
| **Treatment** | Methylene blue |

#### Carboxyhemoglobin (COHgb)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Mechanism** | Reaction between carbon monoxide and hemoglobin; CO releases more slowly than CO₂ from Hgb |
| **Symptoms** | Tiredness, dizziness, unconsciousness, death |
| **Treatment** | Non-rebreather, hyperbaric chamber |
| **Specialty** | Involves toxicology |

> **See Figure 2.6** for the oxygen–hemoglobin dissociation curve.

## Diagnostics in Pulmonology

### Chest X-Ray (CXR)

#### Types of CXR

| Type | Method | Indications |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **PA and Lateral CXR** | Radiation posterior → anterior; obtained with lateral images | Most common modality for lung imaging |
| **AP Portable CXR** | Radiation anterior → posterior | Used for unstable/hospitalized patients; less informative; subject to rotation; enlarges cardiac silhouette |
| **Lateral Decubitus CXR** | Patient placed lateral (on side) | Assessment of free-flowing vs. loculated pleural fluid; increasingly replaced by POCUS |

#### Approach to CXR Interpretation

1. **Verify** patient name and date
2. **Identify** CXR type
3. **Evaluate quality:**
   - **Rotation:** Clavicular symmetry, trachea midline
   - **Inspiration:** Count 8.5–11 rib spaces above diaphragm
   - **Penetration:** If thoracic spine seen through heart → film is overexposed
4. **Read systematically:**
   - Airway (trachea)
   - Bronchoalveolar markings
   - Cardiac silhouette (and mediastinum)
   - Diaphragm
   - Extras/Everything Else (lung parenchyma, MSK, lines/tubes)

> **See Figure 2.7** for normal CXR and depiction of CXR findings.

**CXR Patterns:**
- Linear
- Reticular
- Nodular
- Reticulonodular
