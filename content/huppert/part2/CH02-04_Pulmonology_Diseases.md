# Diseases

## Alveolar Hemorrhage

### Definition

Bleeding into the alveolar space due to disruption of the alveolar–capillary basement membrane.

### Clinical Presentation

Cough, fever, hemoptysis, or diffuse GGOs, plus other concerning features (e.g., extrapulmonary signs of vasculitis, known condition associated with DAH, declining hemoglobin without clear reason).

**Pearl:** Hemoptysis is only present in 50% of cases and absence does not predict hemorrhage severity. Consider alveolar hemorrhage in a patient with GGOs who is failing to progress as expected (e.g., patient with suspected cardiogenic pulmonary edema who is not improving with diuresis).

### Diagnostics

- **Labs:** CBC, BUN/Cr, UA, ESR, CRP. Consider rheumatologic workup including ANA, dsDNA, RNP, C3, C4, RF, CCP, ANCA, MPO, PR3, cryoglobulins, anti-GBM, RVVT, cardiolipin, B2-glycoprotein Ab
- **Imaging:** Noncontrast chest CT (consider contrast if suspect other causes of hemoptysis): Imaging shows patchy or diffuse GGOs
- **Bronchoscopy:** Serial lavage (gets progressively more hemorrhagic), rule out infection, check cell count with differential to evaluate for eosinophilia

### Management

- **Capillaritis:** Immunosuppression; for ANCA-associated vasculitis, typically give pulse-dose steroids first, then adjunctive therapies (cyclophosphamide, rituximab, etc.)
- **Bland hemorrhage or diffuse alveolar damage:** Treat underlying cause, supportive care

### General Principles

- **Obstructive = ↑compliance, ↓elastance** → problem emptying the lung
- ↑TLC, ↑RV → problem pushing air out → air trapping
- ↓↓FEV1; ↓FVC → FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.7

## Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

### Epidemiology

Most common in former smokers (although can affect persons in cities with air pollution; women may develop with less significant smoking history). Typical onset age >40 years.

### Definitions

- **COPD:** Persistent airflow limitation resulting from the consequences of chronic inflammation from smoking. Classic subtypes were chronic bronchitis and emphysema.
- **Chronic bronchitis:** Clinical diagnosis of chronic productive cough for 3 months/year for >2 years. Normal DLCO.
- **Emphysema:** Pathologic diagnosis describing permanent enlargement of airspaces and destruction of alveoli, causing loss of diffusing capacity and reduced elastic recoil. ↓DLCO.
  - **Centrilobular:** Tobacco activates PMNs, inhibits α1-antitrypsin, ↑oxidative stress; upper lung predominant.
  - **Panlobular:** Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency; lung bases with bilateral basilar bullae; can also have liver disease (PAS+).
- **Small airway disease:** Third characteristic feature of COPD; small bronchioles are narrowed and reduced in number.
- **Asthma–COPD overlap syndrome:** Some reversibility with bronchodilators.

### Pathogenesis

- **Airways:** Chronic inflammation, increased goblet cells and mucous glands; airway collapse due to loss of tethering from alveolar wall destruction
- **Lung parenchyma:** Affects structures distal to terminal bronchiole
- **Pulmonary vasculature:** Smooth muscle hypertrophy → chronic hypoxic vasoconstriction of small pulmonary arteries

### Clinical Presentation

Dyspnea, chronic cough, sputum production.

### Physical Exam

- **Early in disease:** Normal or only prolonged expiration or wheezing on forced exhalation
- **Increasing severity:** Hyperinflation (increased resonance to percussion), decreased breath sounds, wheezes/crackles

**Pearl:** Clubbing is NOT typical in COPD; suggests comorbidities such as lung cancer, ILD, or bronchiectasis.

### Diagnosis

- CXR may be normal (CXR is only 50% sensitive for detection of COPD)
- **PFTs:** ↓FEV1, FEV1/FVC <0.70, ↑TLC, ↑RV
- Consider testing for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency in young patients (<45 yr) or non/minimal smokers (patients may also have liver disease)
- Rule out other causes of subacute/chronic dyspnea (consider BNP, TTE, chest imaging including CTPE)

### Characterization

Characterize symptom burden and risk of exacerbation using the GOLD Group AD classification system, based on exacerbation history and symptom burden (mMRC, CAT questionnaires).

### Chronic Treatment

**Three things improve survival:**
1. Smoking cessation
2. Home supplemental oxygen (if PaO₂ <55 mmHg, cor pulmonale)
3. Lung volume reduction surgery

**Initial treatment regimen (general approach, must be tailored):**
1. Start with short-acting beta agonist (SABA) or short-acting muscarinic antagonist (SAMA) PRN
2. Add long-acting muscarinic antagonist (LAMA, e.g., Spiriva)
3. Add long-acting beta agonist (LABA)
4. Add inhaled corticosteroid (ICS)

**Treatment of refractory COPD:**
- Consider PDE4 inhibitor (roflumilast) if inflammation is significant, or chronic azithromycin to prevent recurrent exacerbations
- If failing triple therapy (LAMA/LABA/ICS) with eosinophilia, consider targeted therapies
- For select patients with apical predominant refractory disease, surgery may be an option
- Recommend pulmonary rehabilitation

### Treatment of Acute COPD Exacerbation

**Cardinal symptoms:** Increased dyspnea, sputum quality, sputum purulence.

**Etiology:** Viral/bacterial infection (70%), inhaler nonadherence, pollution/poor air quality, forest fires, pulmonary embolism, idiopathic.

**Management:**
- Bronchodilator: β2-agonist (albuterol) and anticholinergic (ipratropium)
- Systemic corticosteroids: Improve FEV1/SpO₂, shorten recovery time/length of stay; prednisone 40 mg PO × 5 days (non-inferior for preventing re-exacerbation compared to 14-day course; REDUCE trial, JAMA 2013)
- Supplemental O₂ (SpO₂ goal 88–92%); avoid hyperoxia
- **Ventilatory support:** Indicated for acute hypercapnic respiratory failure; benefit demonstrated for NIPPV; reassess in 1–2 hours; if not improving, consider intubation
- **Antibiotics:** Consider if 3/3 cardinal symptoms present, or 2/3 with one being sputum purulence, or if patient requires noninvasive mechanical ventilation; total course typically 5–7 days; commonly used: doxycycline, azithromycin

**Prevention:** Influenza vaccine annually; pneumonia vaccines: PCV13 (if not previously given) followed by PPSV23 ≥8 weeks later, then PPSV23 q5yr.

**Complications:** Compensatory polycythemia (Hct >55% men, >47% women); pulmonary hypertension; compensatory metabolic alkalosis (check BMP for elevated HCO₃).

## Asthma

### Pathogenesis

**Triad:**
1. Airway inflammation
2. Airway hyperresponsiveness
3. Reversible airflow obstruction

### Clinical Presentation

Shortness of breath, wheezing.

### Asthma Syndromes

| Syndrome | Features |
|----------|----------|
| Allergic asthma | +IgE |
| Exercise-induced bronchospasm | — |
| Occupational asthma | Farmers, factory workers, other exposures |
| Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease | "Samter's triad": Asthma, sinus inflammation with nasal polyps, aspirin/NSAID sensitivity; avoid aspirin/NSAIDs or consider aspirin desensitization |
| Reactive airways dysfunction syndrome | Acute airway hyperreactivity in response to inhaled irritant; NOT the same as nonspecific "reactive airways" |
| Allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA) | Chronic hypersensitivity to Aspergillus colonization in asthma or CF patients; recurrent exacerbations, peripheral eosinophilia, high IgE, +Aspergillus IgG/IgE, central bronchiectasis, upper lobe parenchymal opacities; Tx: steroids, consider antifungals |

### Differential Diagnosis

- Virus-induced bronchospasm
- **Chronic eosinophilic pneumonia (CEP):** Idiopathic; median age 30–40 yr; fever, dyspnea, productive cough, weight loss, peripheral eosinophilia; classic CXR = bilateral peripheral/pleural-based opacities; Tx: steroids
- **Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA/Churg-Strauss):** Chronic rhinosinusitis, asthma, pronounced peripheral eosinophilia; +pANCA; Tx: steroids; if refractory, consider cyclophosphamide
- **Vocal cord dysfunction:** Consider if "asthma" does not improve with treatment or dramatically improves with intubation; worsened by stress; diagnosed by direct laryngoscopy or characteristic flow–volume loop; common comorbidities: GERD, sinus disease, OSA, obesity

### Diagnosis

PFTs; spirometry before and after albuterol (increase in FEV1 ≥12% = "reversibility"); bronchoprovocation test (methacholine).

### Classification

**Asthma severity is characterized by impairment and risk of exacerbation requiring oral steroids:**

| Classification | Symptoms/SABA Use | Nighttime Awakening | Interference with Activity | Exacerbations Requiring Steroids | Initial Treatment |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|--------------------------------|----------------|
| Intermittent | <2 days/week | <2×/month | None | 0–1/year | Step 1 |
| Mild persistent | >2 days/week, but not daily | 3–4×/month | Minor | ≥2/year | Step 2 |
| Moderate persistent | Daily | >1×/week, but not nightly | Some | ≥2/year | Step 3 + PO steroid course |
| Severe persistent | Throughout the day | Often nightly | Extreme | ≥2/year | Step 4/5 + PO steroid course |

### Chronic Treatment

Initial controller therapy based on classification; step-up based on Assess, Adjust, Review Response (GINA Guidelines 2020).

### Treatment of Acute Asthma Exacerbation

**Clinical features:** Dyspnea, wheezing, increased home inhaler use; abnormal peak flow (<200 L/min consistent with severe obstruction); hypoxia.

**Etiology:** Viruses, allergens, occupational exposures, pollution, aspirin/NSAIDs, exercise, inadequate chronic treatment, medication noncompliance.

**Management:**
- **Assess severity:** HR, RR, O₂ saturation, pulsus paradoxus, accessory muscle use, inability to speak in full sentences
- **Major risk factors for fatality:** (1) Recent history of poorly controlled asthma; (2) Prior near-fatal asthma. Minor risk factors = allergies, aspirin-associated asthma
- **Provide therapies:** Supplemental O₂ to maintain SpO₂ >92%; short-acting beta2 agonists (e.g., albuterol nebulizers q4hr); ipratropium bromide; oral corticosteroids (total duration 5–7 days); if severe, consider IV steroids, IV magnesium, high-dose inhaled corticosteroids

## Asthma–COPD Overlap Syndrome

**Background:** Asthma and COPD are increasingly recognized as heterogeneous and overlapping conditions.

**Prototypical differences:** Typical age of onset, presence of atopy, smoking history/pack years, reversibility.

**Treatment:** Sporadic trials with various definitions; may be increasingly described in the future.

## Bronchiectasis

### Etiology

Irreversible pathologic dilation of bronchi or bronchioles due to infectious process with airway obstruction (tumor, TB, COPD), impaired drainage (CF, ciliary dysfunction), or abnormality in host antimicrobial defense (CVID); approximately 50% of cases are idiopathic.

### Clinical Presentation

Productive cough (excessive sputum), recurrent pneumonia.

### Diagnosis

PFTs with mild/moderate obstruction; high-resolution chest CT can definitively diagnose.

### Treatment

Airway clearance, antibiotic therapy for exacerbations, pulmonary rehabilitation.

---

## Cystic Fibrosis (CF)

### Etiology

Autosomal recessive mutation in CFTR gene.

### Diagnosis

Typically diagnosed in children; occasionally in adults. Diagnose by genetic testing (CFTR mutation present) or biochemical testing (sweat test).

### Comorbidities

Diabetes, infertility, osteoporosis, liver disease.

### Treatment

Airway clearance, antibiotic therapy, nutritional support including pancreatic enzyme replacement, diabetes management, psychosocial support.

### Complications

Bronchiectasis, ABPA, NTM, Burkholderia cepacia infection, sinusitis, male infertility, pancreatitis, intestinal intussusception.

## Restrictive Lung Diseases

### General Principles

- **Restrictive = ↓Compliance, ↑Elastance** → Problem getting air in
- Normal DLCO in neuromuscular disorders; ↓DLCO in ILD; ↑DLCO in obesity
- ↓TLC, ↓RV; increased radial traction keeps airways open
- FEV1↓, FVC↓↓ → FEV1/FVC ratio normal or increased

### Extrapulmonary Restrictive Disease (Normal DLCO)

- **Neuromuscular diseases:** Guillain-Barré, myasthenia gravis, poliomyelitis, post-polio, ALS; may cause weakening of respiratory muscles
- **Diaphragmatic disease**
- **Chest wall conditions:** Kyphoscoliosis; obesity hypoventilation syndrome ("Pickwickian syndrome," BMI >50); Tx: weight loss, NIPPV; consider respiratory stimulants (progestin, acetazolamide)

## Overview of Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)

### Definitions

Large group of diagnoses, most causing progressive scarring/fibrosis of the lung with restrictive physiology.

### Clinical Presentation

Chronic progressive dyspnea with associated dry cough; "Velcro-like crackles," wheezing, clubbing; may also have symptoms/signs of underlying pathology (e.g., connective tissue disease features).

### PFTs

Restrictive pattern with low DLCO.

### Etiologies/Classification

See Figure 2.13.

### Imaging/Pathology Pattern Correlates

| HRCT Pattern | Pathology | DDx |
|-------------|-----------|-----|
| UIP (honeycombing, traction bronchiectasis, subpleural basilar predominant) | UIP | Often idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) if other exposures excluded |
| NSIP (symmetric bilateral GGO, fine reticulations, traction bronchiectasis, subpleural sparing) | NSIP | Autoimmune, drug, XRT |

### Clinical History Clues

- **Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP) exposures:** Birds, down comforter/pillows, molds, sauna/indoor hot tub
- **Occupational exposures:** Hard metals, coal, silica
- **Smoking history**
- **Medications:** Nitrofurantoin, amiodarone
- **Radiation**
- **Extrapulmonary CTD symptoms:** Raynaud's, rash, arthritis
- **Family history of ILD, early graying of hair**

### Diagnosis

- PFTs: Restrictive pattern, low DLCO
- High-resolution chest CT (HRCT)
- Autoimmune serologies: Start with ANA, Jo1, SSA/SSB, RF, CCP ± ANCA
  - If positive: MCTD (U1RNP), SLE (SM, dsDNA), scleroderma (Topo1, RNA polymerase III)
  - If negative: Consider myositis panel if concern for myositis

### Treatment

Supplemental O₂, pulmonary rehab, transplant referral if indicated; IPF: nintedanib vs. pirfenidone; most others: immunosuppression.

## Additional Details About Specific ILD Diagnoses

### Idiopathic Interstitial Pneumonias (IIPs)

Broad classification within ILDs of unknown cause; most common is idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF).

### Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)

- **Etiology:** Unknown; may be related to mucin gene mutation or telomere length; older adults (>60 yr), former smoker, absence of underlying cause
- **Diagnostics:** HRCT shows UIP pattern (basilar predominant, peripherally distributed; honeycombing with traction bronchiectasis; lack of air trapping); pathology: UIP pattern
- **DDx:** Rule out hypersensitivity pneumonitis (exposures) and connective tissue disease (rheumatology ROS)
- **Treatment:** Nintedanib or pirfenidone (both slow progression; do not improve function or halt progression); immunosuppression is harmful (PANTHER trial, NEJM 2012); avoid intubation if possible; nintedanib (BID; side effects: nausea, GI; CI: CAD or anticoagulation); pirfenidone (TID; side effect: photosensitivity)

### Nonspecific Interstitial Pneumonia (NSIP)

Can be idiopathic, overlap with other IIP, or associated with HIV, connective tissue diseases, drugs, hypersensitivity pneumonitis; predominantly affects never-smoker, middle-aged women.

- **Imaging:** HRCT basilar predominant: reticular markings, subpleural sparing, GGO; classic: basilar GGO with subpleural sparing
- **Tx:** Mild disease may be self-limited; otherwise, initial treatment is steroids or other immunosuppressive adjuncts

### Cryptogenic Organizing Pneumonia (COP, formerly BOOP)

Rare form of ILD; bronchioles and alveoli become inflamed; most common in patients 40–50 yr; clinically mimics pneumonia; chest imaging often with recurrent, migratory, peripheral-predominant opacities; Tx: steroids; high risk of recurrence.

### Acute Interstitial Pneumonia (AIP, Hamman-Rich syndrome)

Acute onset, rapidly progressive; median age ~50 yr; presents with fever, cough, progressive dyspnea over 7–14 days; imaging mimics ARDS; Tx: supportive care, steroids; prognosis: 50% mortality.

### Desquamative Interstitial Pneumonia (DIP)

Most common in patients with heavy smoking history (>90%); median age 30–40 yr; CXR may be normal (20%); HRCT reveals GGO/centrilobular nodules, no peripheral reticulation; Tx: smoking cessation, ?steroids.

### Respiratory Bronchiolitis-Associated ILD (RB-ILD)

On clinical spectrum with DIP; median age 30–40 yr, typically smokers, M:F = 2:1; imaging similar to DIP; Tx: smoking cessation, ?steroids.

### Lymphoid Interstitial Pneumonia (LIP)

Usually associated with rheumatic disease, immunodeficiency, or infection (EBV); cysts common (>60%), not seen in NSIP; may progress to lymphoma (5%); Tx: depends on underlying cause.

### Pleuroparenchymal Fibroelastosis (PPFE)

Rare; idiopathic or associated with chemotherapy; HRCT shows upper lobe predominant pleural and subpleural lung parenchymal fibrosis.

### Exposure-Related ILD

- **Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP):** Exposure to bird feathers, mold; variable presentation (acute: 4–6 hours after exposure, self-limited with removal; chronic: insidious cough with constitutional symptoms and fibrosis); chronic HP HRCT: small centrilobular nodules, GGO, lobular areas of decreased attenuation and vascularity; Tx: detailed history, removal of exposures; partial lung function may return
- **Pneumoconiosis:** Requires exposure (silica, asbestos, coal, talc, beryllium, hard metal); imaging findings differ based on causative agent

### Drug/Radiation-Induced ILD

Nitrofurantoin, amiodarone, chemo, XRT, immunotherapy (PD-1 inhibitors).

### Connective Tissue Disease ILD (CTD-ILD)

Systemic sclerosis, RA, myositis, Sjogren's, MCTD.

### Sarcoidosis

- **Epidemiology:** Young to middle-age adults; more common in Black individuals
- **Etiology:** Unknown
- **Three classic features:** (1) Bilateral hilar adenopathy; (2) Pulmonary reticular opacities; (3) Skin, joint, and/or eye lesions
- **Other features:** Elevated ESR/CRP, hypercalciuria, elevated serum ACE (suggestive but variable utility)
- **Diagnosis:** No single definitive test; diagnosis relies on compatible clinical/radiographic features, exclusion of other differential diagnoses, noncaseating granulomas on pathology
- **Treatment:** Steroids as first-line

### Acute Eosinophilic Pneumonia (AEP)

Rare, cause unknown; age 20–40 yr; M:F = 2:1; nonproductive cough, dyspnea, fever in nearly all patients; usually symptoms <1 week; leukocytosis initially neutrophilic, may become eosinophilic; ESR/CRP, IgE elevated; CXR: subtle reticular or GGO, later diffuse GGO; BAL eos >25%; Tx: steroids.

### Chronic Eosinophilic Pneumonia (CEP)

Rare, cause unknown; age 30–50 yr; M:F = 1:2; association with asthma/atopy; chronic onset of dyspnea, weight loss, fever, productive cough; bilateral peripheral/pleural-based consolidations ("photographic negative" of pulmonary edema); BAL eos >25%; Tx: steroids.

### Pulmonary Alveolar Proteinosis (PAP)

Diffuse lung disease with accumulation of amorphous, PAS-positive material (surfactant and apoproteins) in distal airspaces; primary (impaired surfactant clearance) or secondary (dust exposure, hematologic dyscrasia, post-hematopoietic stem cell transplant); age 40–50 yr, M:F=2:1, affects smokers disproportionately; presents with dyspnea on exertion, cough, fatigue, weight loss, fever over weeks to months; CXR: alveolar opacities in "bat wing" pattern; HRCT: GGO with "crazy paving" pattern; Tx: whole lung lavage.

### Lymphangioleiomyomatosis (LAM)

Rare, multisystem due to mTOR pathway activation; affects young women; primary or associated with tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC-LAM); diffuse cystic lung disease; progressive dyspnea, spontaneous pneumothorax, pleural effusion, fatigue, chylothorax, chyloperitoneum; HRCT: thin-walled cysts; Tx: sirolimus, everolimus.

### Amyloidosis

May cause ILD; more common tracheobronchial infiltration, persistent pleural effusions, parenchymal nodules (amyloidomas).

## Diseases of the Pleura

### Pleural Effusion

Thoracentesis should be performed for new effusion to calculate Light's criteria.

**Light's Criteria (at least one for exudate):**
- Pleural protein/serum protein >0.5
- Pleural LDH/serum LDH >0.6
- Pleural LDH >⅔ upper limit of laboratory's reference range for serum LDH

**Transudative:** Increased hydrostatic pressure (CHF), decreased oncotic pressure (cirrhosis, nephrotic, hypoalbuminemia); pH 7.4–7.55.

**Exudative:** Increased permeability or decreased lymphatic drainage (infection, malignancy, CTD, CABG, PE); pH 7.3–7.45; <7.2 typical for empyema.

**Other useful features:** High amylase (pancreatitis, malignancy); milky fluid (chylothorax); bloody fluid (malignancy); lymphocytic predominance and/or ADA elevation (TB).

**Treatment:** Transudative: Diuretics, sodium restriction. Exudative: Treat underlying process; if complicated, involve pulmonology for therapies (indwelling pleural catheters, chest tubes).

### Pneumothorax

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Spontaneous – Primary** | Typically in tall lean men when dilated alveolar bleb ruptures |
| **Spontaneous – Secondary** | Complication of lung disease |
| **Traumatic – Iatrogenic** | e.g., central line, thoracentesis; always check post-procedure CXR; mediastinum shifts TOWARD pneumothorax |
| **Tension** | Accumulation of air with one-way valve collapses ipsilateral lung; mediastinum shifts AWAY from pneumothorax; causes: mechanical ventilation, CPR, trauma; medical emergency! Perform immediate needle thoracostomy (5th ICS, midaxillary), followed by chest tube |

## Diseases Involving the Vasculature

### Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

**Virchow's triad:** (1) Endothelial injury; (2) Venous stasis; (3) Hypercoagulability.

**Risk factors:** Older age, malignancy, prior VTE, hypercoagulable state (factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency), prolonged bed rest, CHF, obesity, orthopedic surgery, trauma, pregnancy, OCPs.

**Diagnosis:**
- **EKG:** Classic findings (rare): S1Q3T3, RV strain, new incomplete RBBB; most common: sinus tachycardia
- **CXR:** Classic findings (rare): Hampton's hump, Westermark's sign; most common: normal
- **Well's criteria, Geneva Score, PESI score** for clinical probability assessment
- **LOW pretest probability** → D-dimer (high sensitivity 95%, low specificity 50%; Christopher study, JAMA 2006)
- **HIGH pretest probability (or +D-dimer)** → CTPE (Stein et al., NEJM 2006)

**Approach:** Assess hemodynamic stability; if unstable, manage emergently; if stable, assess PE risk (low risk, intermediate-low, intermediate-high).

**Classifications and Management:**

| Classification | Key Features | Treatment |
|--------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Massive (high-risk)** | SBP <90 mmHg, SBP drop >40 mmHg, or shock >15 min despite IVF | Fibrinolytic (catheter-directed or reduced-dose systemic thrombolysis); contraindications: history of hemorrhagic stroke or stroke of unknown origin, ischemic stroke in previous 6 months, CNS neoplasm, major trauma/surgery/head injury in previous 3 weeks, bleeding diathesis, active bleeding |
| **Nonmassive – Intermediate-high risk** | No hypotension but evidence of abnormal RV function (echo or CT), elevated troponin/BNP; BOVA score >4 | Anticoagulation; multidisciplinary discussion about role for fibrinolytics |
| **Nonmassive – Intermediate-low risk** | No hypotension, no RV dysfunction, but positive sPESI (age >80, cancer, cardiopulmonary disease, HR >110, SBP <100, SpO₂ <90%) | Anticoagulation |
| **Low-risk** | Negative sPESI (age <80, no cancer, no cardiopulmonary disease, HR <110, SBP >100, SpO₂ >90%) | Consider outpatient management with DOAC |
