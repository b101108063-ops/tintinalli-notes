## 1. Dissociative Disorders

**Dissociative amnesia** is characterized by episodes of inability to remember salient information, with retrograde amnesia being most common. **Dissociative fugue** involves sudden unexpected purposeful travel away from one's usual environment along with amnesia for the period of fugue. Patients are unaware of their amnesia and do not recall the period of fugue, and they typically present with low anxiety despite confusion. Dissociative disorders are often associated with trauma and war exposure.

**Depersonalization** is a sense of disconnection from one's self, body, or actions. This is often vague and difficult for patients to describe. **Derealization** is a sense of disconnection from reality or one's surroundings, with patients describing a feeling of "unreality." Derealization is also often vague and difficult for patients to describe.

## 2. Impulse Control Disorders

**Intermittent explosive disorder** is characterized by recurrent aggressive outbursts disproportionate to the stressor. Treatment includes cognitive behavioral therapy and pharmacotherapy with anticonvulsants, antidepressants, or antipsychotics.

**Kleptomania** is characterized by an uncontrollable urge to steal objects not needed for personal use or monetary gain. Patients experience pleasure or relief when stealing but feel shame or guilt afterward. Treatment includes cognitive behavioral therapy, insight-oriented psychotherapy, and pharmacotherapy with antidepressants or opioid antagonists.

**Pyromania** involves multiple episodes of deliberate fire setting, often with tension or arousal before the act and gratification afterward. Patients often have a fascination with fires. Children with fire-setting behavior generally have a better prognosis. Treatment includes cognitive behavioral therapy and pharmacotherapy with antidepressants or anticonvulsants.

## 3. Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders

**Somatic symptom disorder** is characterized by somatic symptoms that are distressing or result in significant disruption of daily life, along with excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviors related to those symptoms. Treatment includes regularly scheduled visits with providers rather than PRN visits when symptoms spike, along with patient education and cognitive behavioral therapy.

**Conversion disorder (functional neurological symptom disorder)** is characterized by one or more symptoms of altered motor or sensory function that are inconsistent with recognized neurological or medical conditions. Patients are typically calm and unconcerned about their symptoms, sometimes described as "la belle indifférence." Conversion disorder is a diagnosis of exclusion. Treatment includes patient education, cognitive behavioral therapy, and physical therapy for motor deficits.

**Illness anxiety disorder** is characterized by preoccupation and anxiety about having a serious disease without somatic symptoms or with only mild symptoms. Treatment aims to improve the patient's ability to tolerate and cope with anxieties through regularly scheduled visits with providers, reassurance, and cognitive behavioral therapy.

**Factitious disorder** is characterized by consciously feigning or producing symptoms in the absence of external rewards. Treatment involves using only objective signs and data to decide on diagnostic and therapeutic procedures rather than relying on patient-reported symptoms.

**Munchausen syndrome** involves repeated episodes of simulated or feigned illness, with patients undergoing unnecessary medical procedures. Patients may exhibit peregrination (wandering from hospital to hospital). Symptoms typically stop when external rewards or benefits are obtained.

## 4. Eating Disorders

### Anorexia Nervosa

**Anorexia nervosa** is defined as an intense fear of gaining weight leading to significantly low body weight. Treatment includes psychotherapy such as family-based therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy, and interpersonal psychotherapy, as well as nutritional rehabilitation and weight restoration. Common criteria for inpatient admission include abnormal vitals, abnormal electrolytes or EKG findings, and BMI less than 14 kg/m². Note that bupropion is contraindicated in anorexia nervosa due to increased risk of seizures.

### Binge Eating Disorder

**Binge eating disorder** is characterized by binge eating episodes in which patients eat more than normal without a sense of control, sometimes without feeling full, often eating large amounts even when not hungry. It is associated with obesity but can occur in patients who are overweight or of normal weight. Treatment includes psychotherapy such as cognitive behavioral therapy, interpersonal therapy, and dialectical behavior therapy, as well as nutrition education and pharmacotherapy with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) or anticonvulsants such as topiramate.

## 5. Sleep Disorders

**Chronic insomnia** is characterized by difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep causing significant distress or impairment. First-line treatment is cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I), which is the gold standard and most effective treatment. Pharmacotherapy with benzodiazepines or benzodiazepine receptor agonists (Z-drugs) can be used effectively but is generally reserved as second-line due to risks. Side effects of benzodiazepines include daytime sedation, drowsiness, dizziness, lightheadedness, and cognitive impairment. Side effects of Z-drugs are similar to benzodiazepines and can also include complex sleep-related behaviors such as sleepwalking and sleep driving.

**Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)** is characterized by apnea during sleep lasting 20–30 seconds, which may cause hypoxia and sleep fragmentation. Etiologies include obesity, structural causes such as enlarged tonsils and nasal polyps, and neurological causes. The diagnosis is established with polysomnography (overnight sleep study). Treatment for mild to moderate disease (fewer than 20 apneic episodes per hour) includes weight loss and avoidance of alcohol. Treatment for severe disease (more than 20 apneic episodes per hour) is continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP).

**Narcolepsy** is characterized by rapid onset of REM sleep, causing excessive daytime sleepiness, involuntary sleep attacks, and cataplexy (sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by strong emotions). Treatment includes pharmacotherapy for sleepiness with modafinil or methylphenidate, and pharmacotherapy for cataplexy with venlafaxine or SSRIs.

**Idiopathic hypersomnia** is characterized by excessive daytime sleepiness and prolonged nocturnal sleep without cataplexy. Treatment includes stimulant medications and lifestyle modifications.

**Kleine-Levin syndrome** is a rare cause of recurrent hypersomnia characterized by episodes of hypersomnia, hyperphagia, hypersexuality, and aggression. It primarily affects young males.

### Parasomnias

**Parasomnias** are unwanted events or experiences that occur during sleep.

**Sleepwalking (somnambulism)** occurs during non-REM sleep. Patients do not remember the events. Treatment includes safety precautions and addressing underlying triggers.

**Sleep terrors** occur during non-REM sleep. Patients have episodes of sudden arousal with screaming but no memory of the event and return to sleep afterward. Treatment is reassurance.

**Nightmare disorder** occurs during REM sleep. Patients have recurrent frightening dreams that end upon awakening with vivid recall. Treatment includes imagery rehearsal therapy.

**REM sleep behavior disorder** is characterized by sleep talking, yelling, limb jerking, and dream enactment during REM sleep. Treatment includes ensuring the safety of the sleeping environment and considering melatonin or clonazepam.

## 6. Substance Use Disorders

Substance use disorders are named according to the type of substance involved in problematic use.

### Cocaine

Cocaine produces increased energy, increased confidence, anxiety or panic, agitation, tremors, insomnia, tachycardia, hypertension, and diaphoresis.

### Benzodiazepines

Benzodiazepines are used for anxiety, insomnia, muscle spasms, and seizure management. Side effects include daytime sedation, drowsiness, dizziness, and cognitive impairment. Overdose can cause respiratory depression, which may be reversed with flumazenil.

### Opioids

Opioids are used for pain management, cough suppression, and diarrhea management. Side effects include constipation, nausea, vomiting, and respiratory depression. Overdose management includes naloxone. Withdrawal is characterized by rhinorrhea, lacrimation, piloerection, yawning, and gastrointestinal distress.

### Alcohol

Alcohol withdrawal can cause tremors, anxiety, insomnia, tachycardia, hypertension, and fever. Severe withdrawal can progress to delirium tremens characterized by confusion, agitation, fever, and autonomic instability. Treatment includes benzodiazepines, which are the first-line therapy, and supportive care including fluids and electrolyte replacement.

### Caffeine

Caffeine can cause anxiety, insomnia, palpitations, tremor, and gastrointestinal distress. It is the most widely used psychoactive substance in the world.

### Nicotine

Nicotine produces alertness, mild euphoria, and increased heart rate. Withdrawal causes irritability, anxiety, difficulty concentrating, and increased appetite.

### Cannabis

Cannabis produces euphoria, relaxation, altered time perception, and increased appetite. It can cause impaired short-term memory, impaired coordination, and paranoia. Withdrawal is generally mild.

### Hallucinogens

Hallucinogens such as lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD) and psilocybin produce altered perception, hallucinations, and changed thought processes. They do not produce physical dependence or significant withdrawal.

### Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)

MAOIs such as phenelzine are used for atypical depression characterized by overeating, oversleeping, and phobias, often in younger patients. MAOIs carry a risk of hypertensive crisis when combined with tyramine-containing foods (aged cheeses, cured meats, fermented foods). Treatment of hypertensive crisis includes phentolamine or nitroprusside. MAOIs also carry a risk of serotonin syndrome when combined with SSRIs, SNRIs, linezolid, or antiemetics. Onset of serotonin syndrome is acute, typically within 24 hours of ingestion, and presents with autonomic dysfunction including hyperthermia, tachycardia, hypertension, and diaphoresis, as well as neuromuscular abnormalities and altered mental status.

## 7. Delirium

**Delirium** is an acute disturbance in attention and awareness with fluctuating course. It is characterized by an acute onset with a medical etiology. Risk factors include advanced age, underlying cognitive impairment, and polypharmacy. Treatment is aimed at identifying and treating the underlying cause while providing supportive care and managing agitation with low-dose antipsychotics if necessary.
