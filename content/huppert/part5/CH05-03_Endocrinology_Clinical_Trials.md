## 1. Diabetes Prevention: DPP Trial

**Citation**: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2002;346(6):393–403.

**Study design**: Multicenter, double-blind, randomized controlled trial that randomized 3234 middle-aged adults with impaired glucose tolerance to intensive lifestyle counseling versus metformin versus placebo.

**Result**: The intensive lifestyle counseling reduced the onset of diabetes by **58%** compared to placebo. Metformin treatment reduced the onset of diabetes by **31%**. These benefits persisted throughout the study's 15-year follow-up.

## 2. Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT)

**Citation**: *New England Journal of Medicine* 1993;329(14):977–986. Follow-up: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2000;342(6):381–389.

**Study design**: Multicenter randomized controlled trial that randomized 1441 patients with type 1 diabetes to intensive versus conventional glycemic control therapy.

**Result**: Patients who kept their blood glucose levels as close to normal as safely possible with intensive therapy had significantly lower rates of microvascular complications including retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy.

**Long-term follow-up**: After 30 years of follow-up, the group that had tightly controlled blood glucose during the study continued to show benefits in reducing microvascular complications and had improved overall survival compared to the conventional therapy group.

## 3. UK Prospective Diabetes Study (UKPDS)

**Citation**: *Lancet* 1998;352:837–853. Follow-up: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2008;359(15):1577–1589.

**Study design**: Multicenter randomized controlled trial that randomized 3867 patients with newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes to intensive versus lenient glycemic control therapy.

**Result**: A follow-up study 10 years later found that the intensive glucose control group had a lower risk of microvascular complications. Patients who underwent intensive glycemic control (fasting glucose less than 108 mg/dL) had better outcomes compared to those with standard targets.

---

## 4. Intensive Glucose Control in Critically Ill Patients: NICE-SUGAR Study

**Citation**: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2009;360(13):1283–1297.

**Study design**: Multicenter, non-blinded, randomized controlled trial that randomized 6104 patients with type 2 diabetes in intensive care units to intensive glucose control (target fasting glucose of 81–108 mg/dL) versus liberal glucose control (target glucose less than 180 mg/dL).

**Result**: Intensive glucose control led to more deaths than the conventional approach. The target glucose of less than 180 mg/dL was associated with lower mortality in critically ill patients, suggesting more conservative glucose targets are safer in this population.
