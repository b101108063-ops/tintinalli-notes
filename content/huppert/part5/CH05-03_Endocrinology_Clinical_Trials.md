## Diabetes

### DCCT Trial

*Diabetes Control and Complications Trial.*
N Engl J Med 1993; 329(14):977–986. Follow-up: N Engl J Med 2000; 342(6):381–389.

Multicenter randomized controlled trial that randomized 1441 patients with type 1 diabetes to intensive vs. conventional glycemic control therapy. Patients who kept blood glucose levels as close to normal as safely possible with intensive treatment had fewer diabetes-related health problems after 6.5 years (including diabetic retinopathy, kidney disease, and cardiovascular disease) compared to conventional treatment. Long-term follow-up demonstrated that these benefits persisted. After 30 years of follow-up, the group with tightly controlled blood glucoses had a 32% reduction in major cardiovascular events, suggesting that better control early in DM1 can prevent cardiovascular disease.

### UKPDS Trial

*UK Prospective Diabetes Study.*
Lancet 1998; 352:837–853. Follow-up: N Engl J Med 2008; 359(15):1577–1589.

Multicenter randomized controlled trial that randomized 3867 patients with newly diagnosed DM2 to intensive vs. lenient glycemic control therapy. A follow-up study 10 years later found that the intensive glucose control group had a lower risk of MIs and death from any cause. Patients who underwent intensive glycemic control (fasting glucose <108 mg/dL) had a 25% reduction in microvascular complications, but there was no effect on macrovascular disease or mortality between treatment groups.

### NiceSugar Study

*Intensive vs. Conventional Glucose Control in Critically Ill Patients.*
N Engl J Med 2009; 360(13):1283–1297.

Multicenter, non-blinded, randomized controlled trial that randomized 6104 ICU patients expected to stay ≥3 days to intensive glucose control (goal glucose 80–108 mg/dL) vs. liberal glucose control (<180 mg/dL). Intensive glucose control led to more deaths than the conventional approach, suggesting more permissive glucose control (<180 mg/dL) is preferred in the ICU setting.

## Diabetes Prevention

### DPP Trial

*Diabetes Prevention Program.*
N Engl J Med 2002; 346(6):393–403.

Multicenter, double-blind, randomized controlled trial that randomized 3234 middle-aged adults to intensive lifestyle counseling (dietary changes and 150 minutes of exercise per week for a weight loss goal of ≥7%) vs. usual care. Intensive lifestyle counseling reduced the onset of diabetes by 58% compared to usual care. Metformin treatment reduced the onset of diabetes by 31%. These benefits persisted through the study's 15-year follow-up.
