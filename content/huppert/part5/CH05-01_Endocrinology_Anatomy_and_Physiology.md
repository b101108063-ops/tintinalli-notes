## 1. Adrenal Gland

The **hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis** regulates the production of cortisol and androgens. ACTH activates the production of androgens, cortisol, and aldosterone in the adrenal gland. Aldosterone production is also regulated by the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).

The **adrenal gland** consists of the outer cortex and the inner medulla. The cortex is further divided into three layers: the glomerulosa, the fasciculata, and the reticularis. Each of these layers produces different hormones.

**CRH** (corticotropin-releasing hormone) from the hypothalamus stimulates ACTH release from the pituitary. **ACTH** then stimulates the adrenal cortex to produce cortisol and androgens. **VP** (vasopressin) also has ACTH-releasing activity. Negative feedback from cortisol inhibits both CRH and ACTH.

## 2. Adrenal Cortex Disorders

### Hyperaldosteronism

**Etiologies**:

| Type | Cause | Key Feature |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary** | Adrenal disorders | High aldosterone, independent of renin |
| **Secondary** | Renovascular disease, renal artery stenosis, malignant hypertension | High aldosterone, dependent on renin |

**Primary causes** include adrenal hyperplasia, adrenal adenoma (Conn's syndrome), and adrenal carcinoma (very rare).

**Diagnosis**: Laboratory findings notable for high aldosterone, which is independent of renin in primary hyperaldosteronism.

**Treatment**: Adenomas are treated with surgical resection. If no lesion is seen or if there is bilateral adrenal involvement, consider adrenal venous sampling to help localize the source. If no localization is found, the cause is likely hyperplasia or glucocorticoid-remediable aldosteronism (GRA).

### Cushing's Syndrome

**Etiologies**:

- **ACTH-dependent**: High ACTH stimulates excess cortisol production from the adrenal glands.
  - Pituitary ACTH production (Cushing's disease).
  - Ectopic ACTH production (e.g., from lung tumor, pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor).
- **ACTH-independent**: Low ACTH due to exogenous steroids or adrenal adenoma/tumor.

**Key testing approach**:

1. **Step 1: Confirm hypercortisolism**
   - 24-hour urine free cortisol (useful for shift workers or patients with suspected cyclic Cushing's).
   - 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test: If dexamethasone suppresses ACTH and cortisol is less than 1.8 mcg/dL, Cushing's syndrome is unlikely. An elevated morning cortisol (greater than 1.8 mcg/dL) suggests Cushing's syndrome.

2. **Step 2: Determine the etiology**
   - Measure ACTH.
   - **Low ACTH** = ACTH-independent Cushing's syndrome. Differential includes exogenous steroids versus adrenal adenoma/tumor. Perform CT abdomen/pelvis to evaluate the adrenal glands and surgically remove the adrenal tumor if found.
   - **High ACTH** = ACTH-dependent Cushing's syndrome. Evaluate for pituitary ACTH production (Cushing's disease) or ectopic ACTH production.

3. **High-dose (8 mg) dexamethasone suppression test** can help differentiate etiologies:
   - Pituitary source: High-dose dexamethasone causes ACTH and cortisol suppression (greater than 50% suppression).
   - Ectopic source: High-dose dexamethasone does NOT cause suppression (less than 50% suppression).

4. **Inferior petrosal sinus sampling** may be needed to confirm pituitary versus ectopic source.

5. **Imaging**: Obtain pituitary MRI. For suspected ectopic sources, consider CT chest/abdomen/pelvis or functional imaging (octreotide scan, MIBG SPECT, or DOTATATE PET/CT).

**Treatment**: Surgical resection of the pituitary adenoma, adrenal tumor, or ectopic ACTH-secreting tumor.

### Adrenal Insufficiency

**Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease)**: The adrenal glands themselves are dysfunctional. Consider adrenal imaging, adrenal autoantibodies (steroid autoantibodies are a marker for autoimmune adrenalitis), and evaluation for tuberculosis or other infiltrative diseases.

**Secondary adrenal insufficiency**: Low ACTH. Low-normal ACTH, normal plasma renin, and normal aldosterone suggest secondary adrenal insufficiency. Consider pituitary MRI and check other pituitary hormones.

**Treatment**: Primary adrenal insufficiency requires oral corticosteroid (hydrocortisone or prednisone) and mineralocorticoid (fludrocortisone) replacement. Secondary adrenal insufficiency only requires glucocorticoid replacement.

## 3. Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

### 21-Hydroxylase Deficiency

**Epidemiology**: Most common congenital adrenal hyperplasia, accounting for approximately 90% of cases.

**Pathophysiology**: The enzyme deficiency causes an inability to produce cortisol (and aldosterone in severe forms). This leads to increased ACTH drive and accumulation of androgen precursors.

**Classification**:

- **Classic CAH**: More severe form with prenatal virilization.
- **Non-classic CAH**: Milder form with later presentation.

**Clinical features in XX karyotype**: Virilizing features including ambiguous genitalia and normal internal female reproductive structures.

**Treatment**: Glucocorticoid replacement to suppress ACTH and reduce androgen excess. Mineralocorticoid replacement may also be needed. Surgical correction of female genitalia can be considered if abnormal.

### 17-Hydroxylase Deficiency

**Clinical features**: Hypertension and hypokalemia due to excess mineralocorticoid activity.

**In XX karyotype**: Phenotypic female with lack of secondary sexual development.

**In XY karyotype**: Complete male pseudohermaphroditism; characterized by external female phenotype with testes.

**Diagnosis**: High aldosterone, low cortisol, and low DHEA.

### 11-Hydroxylase Deficiency

**Clinical features**: Virilization and premature puberty.

**In XX karyotype**: Labial fusion.

**In XY karyotype**: Precocious puberty.

**Diagnosis**: Low aldosterone, low cortisol, and high DHEA.

## 4. Pheochromocytoma

**Definition**: Catecholamine-secreting tumor of the adrenal medulla (or paraganglioma if extra-adrenal).

**Epidemiology**: Can occur in childhood or later in life. Approximately 50% are hereditary (associated with MEN2, von Hippel-Lindau disease, and other syndromes).

**Clinical features**: The classic presentation is paroxysmal symptoms known as the "5 Ps": Pressure (hypertension), Pain (chest pain or headache), Palpitations, Pallor, and Perspiration. Paroxysms generally last less than an hour and may be precipitated by positional changes, stress, or certain medications.

**Diagnosis**:

- **Low suspicion**: 24-hour urine metanephrines and creatinine (more specific).
- **High suspicion**: Plasma free metanephrines (more sensitive).

**Treatment**: Surgical resection after appropriate alpha-blockade to prevent hypertensive crisis.

**Prognosis**: If malignant, typically poor prognosis.

## 5. Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal Axis

The **hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis** regulates the production of sex hormones. **GnRH** (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) from the hypothalamus stimulates the pituitary to secrete **LH** (luteinizing hormone) and **FSH** (follicle-stimulating hormone). ## 6. Pituitary Gland

The pituitary is a small gland positioned at the base of the brain that controls the function of most other endocrine glands. The pituitary secretes hormones that regulate thyroid function (TSH), adrenal function (ACTH), gonadal function (LH/FSH), growth (GH), and water balance (ADH). Certain hormones are secreted by the anterior lobe (adenohypophysis) whereas others are secreted by the posterior lobe (neurohypophysis).

## 7. Thyroid Gland

### Thyroid Hormone Synthesis

The thyroid gland produces thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3). Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) from the pituitary stimulates thyroid hormone production. The thyroid also secretes calcitonin from parafollicular C cells.

### Thyroid Axis

The hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis follows the same principles as other axes: TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) from the hypothalamus stimulates TSH release, TSH stimulates thyroid hormone secretion, and thyroid hormones provide negative feedback to the hypothalamus and pituitary.
