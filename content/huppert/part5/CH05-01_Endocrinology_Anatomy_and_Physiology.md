# Anatomy and Physiology

## Endocrine Axes

### Organization

There are five parallel endocrine axes in the hypothalamic-anterior pituitary regulatory system. This chapter organizes anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, and treatments by endocrine axis.

### The Five Axes

1. Thyroid hormone
2. Glucocorticoids
3. Sex steroids (estradiol and testosterone)
4. Growth hormone
5. Prolactin

---

## Pituitary

### Hormones Secreted by the Pituitary

The pituitary is a small gland positioned at the base of the brain. It controls the function of most other endocrine glands.

#### Anterior Pituitary

##### Hypopituitarism

**Etiologies:**
- **Primary:** Traumatic (head injury), neoplastic (adenoma), infiltrative/inflammatory (sarcoidosis), vascular (sickle cell disease, pregnancy-related), infections (histoplasmosis, TB), developmental/structural (dysplasia)
- **Secondary:** Hypothalamic dysfunction or stalk interruption

**Clinical features:** Depends on which hormones are lost and the extent and duration of deficiency.

**Hormone deficiencies:**
- **↓Growth hormone (GH):** Fatigue, increased fat mass, decreased muscle mass, growth retardation (children); measured by elevated IGF1 on oral glucose tolerance test (GH suppression test)
- **↓LH/FSH:** Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (infertility, amenorrhea, impotence)
- **↓TSH:** Hypothyroidism, growth retardation (children)
- **↓ACTH:** Secondary adrenal insufficiency (hypocortisolism; relative preservation of mineralocorticoid production)
- **↓Prolactin:** Inability to lactate

**Diagnosis:** Get pituitary MRI; typically low anterior pituitary hormones and low target-organ hormones (e.g., low TSH, low free T4).

**Treatment:** Replace deficient hormones; adrenal insufficiency and hypothyroidism are most important; patients need careful glucocorticoid dose adjustment during times of stress (e.g., acute illness).

##### Pituitary Tumors

**Approach:**
1. Is there mass effect?
2. Is the tumor causing hormone excesses or deficiencies?
3. Is the tumor likely to grow and cause problematic mass effect?

Obtain MRI brain and repeat over time as needed.

**Differential diagnosis:** Pituitary adenomas (can occur as part of MEN1 syndrome), microadenomas (<1 cm; incidentally found in ~10% of brain MRIs), macroadenomas (≥1 cm), Rathke cleft cysts, craniopharyngiomas, meningiomas, metastatic disease.

**Symptoms:** Can cause mass effect (headache, visual changes from optic chiasm compression → bitemporal hemianopia), hypopituitarism (decreased hormone levels), or be functional (increased hormone levels).

**Functional tumors causing increased hormone levels:**

| Hormone | Condition | Symptoms | Diagnosis | Treatment |
|---------|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Prolactin** | Hyperprolactinemia | Hypogonadism (amenorrhea, decreased libido, infertility, galactorrhea) | Elevated prolactin (>200–250 ng/mL strongly suggests prolactinoma) | If asymptomatic microadenoma: observation; if symptomatic or macroadenoma: dopamine agonists (bromocriptine/cabergoline); surgery NOT needed unless medical management inadequate |
| **Growth hormone** | Acromegaly | Large jaw/hands/feet/tongue, frontal bossing, cardiomegaly | Elevated IGF1 (confirm with oral glucose tolerance test), then pituitary MRI | Surgery (transsphenoidal resection), radiation; dopamine agonists (somatostatin analogues); pegvisomant (GH receptor antagonist) |
| **TSH** | — | See hyperthyroidism section | — | — |
| **ACTH** | Cushing's disease | Proximal muscle weakness, facial plethora, wasting of extremities with increased fat in abdomen/face, wide purplish striae, bruising, dorsocervical/supraclavicular fat pads, DM2, neuropsychiatric symptoms | Step 1: Establish hypercortisolism (late-night salivary cortisol, 24-hr urine free cortisol, overnight 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test); Step 2: Differentiate ACTH-dependent from ACTH-independent (measure ACTH); if ACTH low → adrenal CT; if ACTH high → pituitary MRI + petrosal sinus sampling | Surgery (transsphenoidal resection) |

**Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) Syndromes:**

| Type | Mutation | Clinical Manifestations |
|------|---------|----------------------|
| **1** | MENIN inactivation | Parathyroid hyperplasia, pancreatic islet cell neoplasia, pituitary adenoma |
| **2A** | RET proto-oncogene | Medullary thyroid carcinoma, pheochromocytoma, parathyroid hyperplasia |
| **2B** | RET proto-oncogene | Medullary thyroid carcinoma, pheochromocytoma, mucosal and GI neuromas |

#### Posterior Pituitary

##### ↓Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): Diabetes Insipidus (DI)

**Etiology:** ADH channels do not function to reabsorb water, either due to inadequate ADH (central) or lack of response to ADH at the kidneys (nephrogenic).

**Central DI:** Low posterior pituitary production of ADH. Etiologies: Idiopathic (~50% of cases in adults), acquired (trauma/surgery, neoplasms, infiltrative/infectious [Langerhans, sarcoidosis, TB], vascular, congenital malformations, genetic).

**Nephrogenic DI:** Normal ADH production but renal tubules do not respond appropriately. Etiologies: Medications (lithium, cisplatin), metabolic (hypercalcemia, hypokalemia), ureteral obstruction.

**Clinical features:** Polyuria (>3 liters urine output in 24 hours), polydipsia; high serum osms, low urine osms.

**Diagnosis:** Confirm polyuria with 24-hr urine volume (>3 L/day); urine typically hypotonic (<300 mosm/kg); exclude glucosuria. Differentiate primary polydipsia from DI: history, serum sodium, and plasma osms can help. Further testing: water deprivation test, hypertonic saline infusion test, plasma copeptin.

Differentiate central from nephrogenic DI: ADH (desmopressin) challenge; increase UOsm >50% = Central DI; stable = Nephrogenic DI.

**Treatment:**
- **Central DI:** Desmopressin (DDAVP), chlorpropamide (increases ADH secretion and enhances effect)
- **Nephrogenic DI:** No good treatments; sodium restriction and thiazide diuretics can be useful

##### ↑ADH: Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH (SIADH)

**Etiology:** Ectopic production of ADH by a tumor (e.g., small cell lung cancer), CNS trauma, pulmonary infection, medications (SSRIs, chlorpropamide, oxytocin, morphine, vincristine, desmopressin), postoperatively.

**Clinical features:** Low serum Na⁺ and osms due to free water retention; normal or high urine sodium and osms. Acute symptoms: Lethargy, somnolence, seizures, coma, death.

**Diagnosis:** Check serum/urine sodium and osms; SIADH is a diagnosis of exclusion.

**Treatment:** Fluid restriction is the mainstay of treatment.

## Thyroid

### The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid Axis

The enzyme 5′-iodinase converts the biologically inactive thyroxine (T4) to the active form triiodothyronine (T3). Conversion of T4 → T3 can be inhibited by environmental conditions (illness, starvation) or medications (PTU, propranolol). T4 can also be converted to reverse T3 (rT3), which is a biologically inactive metabolite.

### Hypothyroidism

**Etiologies:**
- **Primary:**
  - **Hashimoto's thyroiditis:** Most common cause in developed countries; autoimmune thyroid destruction; >90% of patients have positive TPO antibodies; patients can be hyperthyroid initially then hypothyroid; thyroid exam is firm and painless; no imaging required unless concern for nodule
  - **Subacute viral thyroiditis (de Quervain's):** Viral infection with very tender thyroid; ↑ESR, jaw pain; patients initially hyperthyroid, then hypothyroid; typically returns to euthyroid; treatment: NSAIDs, ASA for pain; self-limited over 2–4 months
  - **Reidel's thyroiditis:** Rare, chronic inflammatory disease with dense fibrosis replacing normal thyroid parenchyma; can cause neck tightness, dyspnea, hoarseness, cough; thyroid exam: firm, "hard as wood," fixed, nontender; often misdiagnosed as malignancy
  - **Iatrogenic:** Surgical destruction, post-radiation or radioactive iodine, medications (lithium, amiodarone)
  - **Iodine deficiency:** Most common cause worldwide
- **Central:** Hypothalamic or pituitary failure; TSH can be inappropriately normal but T3/T4 are low

**Clinical features:** Fatigue, weakness, lethargy, cold intolerance, heavy menstruation, weight gain, constipation, slow mentation, depression, carpal tunnel syndrome, dry skin, coarse hair, hoarseness, myxedema (non-pitting edema from increased glycosaminoglycans), bradycardia.

**Diagnosis:** ↓T4/T3, ↑TSH; +anti-TPO Ab in Hashimoto's thyroiditis.

**Subclinical hypothyroidism:** ↑TSH, normal free T4 and T3 with no or subtle symptoms; treatment controversial; often initiate if TSH >10 mU/L or between 5–10 mU/L if pregnant or attempting pregnancy.

**Treatment:** Levothyroxine daily; goal is to normalize TSH. "Start low and go slow" in older patients; initial dose 25–50 mcg/day; recheck TSH in 6 weeks and titrate. Supratherapeutic dosing risks cardiovascular complications.

**Myxedema coma:** Altered mental status, hypothermia, hypotension, hypoventilation, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia. Precipitated by infection, cold, stressor, untreated hypothyroidism. Check cortisol to rule out adrenal insufficiency. Treatment: Consult endocrinology; supportive care, IV T4 (careful with large IV bolus in CAD/CV risk), co-administer IV hydrocortisone until adrenal insufficiency is ruled out.

### Hyperthyroidism

"Thyrotoxicosis" (excess circulating thyroid hormone) is preferred over "hyperthyroidism," though hyperthyroidism is the most common cause of thyrotoxicosis.

**Etiologies:**
- **Graves' disease:** Most common cause of thyrotoxicosis (60–75%); common in women age 20–40 yr; autoimmune antibody stimulates TSH receptor → ↑T3, T4; thyroid: diffusely enlarged, nontender, may have bruit; exophthalmos, pretibial myxedema; diagnosis: thyroid ultrasound (enlarged, increased vascularity), thyroid-stimulating antibodies
- **Multinodular goiter/toxic adenoma:** Pituitary tumor secreting TSH and/or resistance to thyroid hormones; thyroid: bumpy, irregular, asymmetric; diagnosis: radioiodine uptake scan (patchy uptake)
- **Thyroiditis:** Thyrotoxic phase of thyroid inflammation; often transient hyperthyroidism for 2–6 weeks, then hypothyroid after 6–12 weeks; may require treatment for both phases; majority ultimately return to euthyroid; **Painful:** inflammatory (de Quervain), infection (post-URI), radiation; **Painless:** postpartum (+TPO), silent, drug-induced
- **Molar pregnancy:** Rare, due to HCG binding to TSH receptor; positive pregnancy test
- **Iatrogenic:** Use of levothyroxine (T4) or liothyronine (T3) for weight loss

**Clinical features:** Nervousness, insomnia, tremor, heat intolerance, diarrhea, palpitations, muscle weakness, brisk reflexes, weight loss, lid lag, arrhythmias, hypertension.

**Diagnosis:** ↓TSH, ↑T3 and/or T4 (often sufficient to order only TSH and free T4).

**Subclinical hyperthyroidism:** ↓TSH, normal free T4 and T3 with subtle symptoms; repeat labs in 3 months; consider treatment if TSH persistently <0.1 mU/L and patient is >65 yr or has cardiac risk factors or osteoporosis.

**Determine the cause:** TRAb for Graves' disease; radioiodine uptake scan if etiology unclear (contraindicated in pregnancy/breastfeeding):
- **High uptake:** Diffuse → Graves'; nodular/patchy → multinodular, toxic adenoma
- **Low uptake:** Thyroiditis, recent iodine load (contrast, amiodarone)

**Treatment:**
- **Beta blockers (propranolol, atenolol):** For acute symptom management regardless of etiology; propranolol also decreases T4 → T3 conversion
- **Methimazole, PTU:** Inhibit T4 synthesis at high doses; methimazole preferred (once-daily, less hepatotoxicity), but CI in first trimester of pregnancy; check baseline CBC with differential, LFTs, TSH; risk of agranulocytosis
- **Radioactive iodine (RAI):** Careful in Graves' (may worsen ophthalmopathy unless pretreated with steroids, so treat with methimazole first); CI in pregnancy
- **Surgery:** For obstructive goiter; side effects: permanent hypothyroidism, recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, hypocalcemia

**Thyroid storm:** True thyroid storm typically occurs in setting of untreated/poorly treated hyperthyroidism with a precipitating event (surgery, trauma, infection, DKA, MI, childbirth, iodine load, amiodarone). Symptoms: Tachycardia, fever, GI symptoms; CNS dysfunction is often a requirement. Treatment: Consult endocrinology; supportive care (IVF, cooling blankets, acetaminophen), propranolol, steroids, PTU, bile acid sequestrants. Only consider iodine after consulting endocrinology.

### Sick Euthyroid Syndrome

**Pathophysiology:** Illness causes caloric deprivation and an increased inflammatory state, which may lead to abnormal thyroid function tests.

**Diagnosis:** Check all TFTs; interpret carefully in hospitalized patients.

**Treatment:** Often no treatment needed; repeat TFTs outpatient once acute illness resolves. Administering levothyroxine is not helpful unless other signs/symptoms consistent with hypothyroidism. **Pearl:** If TSH undetectable or >20 mU/L, suggests true thyroid disease.

### Thyroid Nodule

**Etiologies:**
- **Benign:** Follicular epithelial cell adenomas (common; present in 5–10% of adults)
- **Malignant:** Papillary carcinomas (80–90%), follicular carcinomas (5–10%), medullary thyroid cancer (<10%), other (1–2%)

**Clinical features:** Palpable nodule, mass effect symptoms, or incidental imaging finding.

**Diagnosis:**
1. First check TSH
2. If TSH high or normal: Get thyroid ultrasound (to assess features, evaluate for other nodules/nodes); if concerning features, biopsy with FNA
3. If TSH low: Check FT4, T3, and thyroid uptake on ¹³¹iodine scan
   - If "hot" functioning nodule: Treat hyperthyroidism
   - If "cold" non-functioning nodule: Biopsy and surgically resect if needed

**Multinodular goiter:** Diagnose using same principles as single nodule; higher risk of malignancy; consider surgery if compressive symptoms.

## Adrenal Gland

### The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis

ACTH activates production of androgens, cortisol, and aldosterone. Aldosterone production is also regulated by RAAS. The adrenal cortex consists of three layers: glomerulosa, fasciculata, and reticularis.

### Hyperaldosteronism

**Etiologies:**
- **Primary (adrenal disorders):** Adrenal hyperplasia, adrenal adenoma (Conn's syndrome), adrenal carcinoma (very rare), glucocorticoid-remediable aldosteronism (GRA)
- **Secondary (extra-adrenal disorders):** Renovascular disease (renal artery stenosis, malignant hypertension), states with low effective arterial volume (CKD, CHF, cirrhosis), Bartter's or Gitelman's syndrome, renin-secreting tumor

**Clinical features:** Hypertension (~20% of cases; common cause of resistant hypertension); hypokalemia (10–40%; potassium normal in many patients); metabolic alkalosis; mild hypernatremia.

**Diagnosis:**
1. Check AM plasma renin and aldosterone
2. **Low renin, low aldosterone:** Non-aldosterone mineralocorticoid excess
3. **Low renin, high aldosterone, high aldo/renin ratio (>20):** Suspect primary; perform salt suppression test to confirm; adrenal CT or MRI; if unilateral, likely adenoma → surgical resection; if no lesion or bilateral, adrenal venous sampling; if no localization, likely hyperplasia or GRA
4. **High renin, high aldosterone, low aldosterone/renin ratio (<10):** Suspect secondary

**Pearl:** Hold aldosterone receptor antagonists and ACEi/ARBs 24–48 hours prior to blood draw.

### Cushing's Syndrome

**Definitions:** Cushing's syndrome = excess cortisol regardless of cause; Cushing's disease = Cushing's syndrome due to pituitary ACTH hypersecretion.

**Etiology:** Exogenous glucocorticoids is most common cause. Endogenous: pituitary adenoma secreting ACTH (most common; high ACTH; suppressed with high-dose dexamethasone); adrenal adenoma/tumor (low ACTH); ectopic ACTH production (high ACTH; not suppressed with high-dose dexamethasone).

**Clinical features:** Moon facies, buffalo hump, truncal obesity, muscle weakness with thin extremities, abdominal striae, hypertension, DM2, immune suppression, bruising, facial plethora, depression/anxiety, amenorrhea.

**Diagnosis:**
1. **Screen:** 1 mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test (cortisol <1.8 mcg/dL = exclude Cushing's); alternatively 24-hr urine free cortisol or late-night salivary cortisol
2. **Determine etiology:** Measure ACTH
   - **Low ACTH:** CT abd/pelvis to evaluate adrenal glands
   - **High ACTH:** High-dose (8 mg) dexamethasone suppression test or brain MRI
     - Suppressed (>50%): Pituitary adenoma → pituitary MRI + inferior petrosal sinus sampling
     - Not suppressed (<50%): Ectopic ACTH-producing tumor (usually lung) → CT C/A/P or octreotide/MIBG SPECT/DOTATATE PET

**Treatment:** Surgical resection; possible initiation of hydrocortisone replacement after surgery; etaconazole and metyrapone can be given if medical therapy preferred or needed prior to surgery.

### Adrenal Insufficiency (AI)

**Etiologies:**
- **Primary:** Autoimmune (antibody against adrenal enzymes — most common), vascular (hemorrhage, thrombosis, trauma), infectious (TB — adrenal calcification, CMV, histoplasmosis), medications (ketoconazole, rifampin, etomidate), deposition diseases (amyloid, sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis), metastatic disease (lung/breast)
- **Secondary:** Hypopituitarism; glucocorticoid therapy (consider if >3 weeks of ≥20 mg prednisone or equivalent, or long-term use of ≥10 mg prednisone or equivalent — suppression of HPA axis is most common cause)

**Clinical features:** Fatigue (most common), nausea/vomiting, lethargy, hypoglycemia, hypercalcemia, eosinophilia (rare), abdominal pain, anorexia. **Primary only:** Skin hyperpigmentation in areas of friction and/or sun exposure (MSH is a byproduct of ACTH synthesis), hyperkalemia, hyponatremia.

**Diagnosis:**
1. **Check early AM cortisol:** <3 mcg/dL is very concerning; >18 mcg/dL rules out AI
2. **Standard cosyntropin stimulation test with 250 mcg:** Normal response is cortisol >18 mcg/dL 60 minutes after administration; can be performed at any time of day
3. **Determine etiology:** Check plasma ACTH, plasma renin, serum aldosterone
   - **High ACTH, high plasma renin, low aldosterone:** Primary adrenal insufficiency → adrenal imaging, adrenal autoantibodies
   - **Low-normal ACTH, normal plasma renin, normal aldosterone:** Secondary adrenal insufficiency → pituitary MRI, check other pituitary hormones

**Treatment:**
- **Primary:** Oral corticosteroid (hydrocortisone or prednisone) and mineralocorticoid (fludrocortisone)
- **Secondary:** Oral corticosteroid only; no mineralocorticoid needed
- **Stress-dose steroids:** Increase dose 2–3× for minor stressors; for major stressors, often need 10× dosing

### Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia

**21-hydroxylase deficiency:** Most common CAH (90% of cases); autosomal recessive; inability to produce cortisol (and aldosterone in salt-wasting forms) → low cortisol, low/normal aldosterone, high ACTH, high DHEA.

- **Classic CAH:** More severe; **Virilizing form:** XX karyotype: ambiguous genitalia, normal ovaries/uterus; XY karyotype: precocious puberty; **Salt-wasting form:** Hypotension, hyperkalemia, emesis/dehydration in first 2–4 weeks
- **Non-classic CAH:** More mild; presents later with signs of androgen excess; some patients asymptomatic

**Diagnosis:** High levels of 17-OH progesterone.

**Treatment:** Steroids and mineralocorticoids (negative feedback loop); surgical correction of female genitalia if abnormal.

**17-hydroxylase deficiency:** Hypertension, hypokalemia; XX karyotype: phenotypic female with lack of secondary sexual development; XY karyotype: complete male pseudohermaphroditism; high aldosterone, low cortisol, low DHEA.

**11-hydroxylase deficiency:** Hypertension, hypokalemia; XX karyotype: labial fusion; XY karyotype: precocious puberty; low aldosterone, low cortisol, high DHEA.

### Pheochromocytoma

**Definition:** Catecholamine-producing tumor derived from sympathetic/parasympathetic nervous system; rare (0.1% of patients with hypertension); can occur in childhood or later.

**Etiology:** 50% hereditary (MEN2, von Hippel-Lindau disease, neurofibromatosis type 2, somatic RET mutations).

**Clinical features:** Paroxysmal spells: Pressure (hypertension), Pain (chest pain, headache), Perspiration, Palpitations (tachycardia), Pallor (vasoconstriction); spells last <1 hour; may be precipitated by positional changes, medications, surgery.

**Diagnosis:** Low suspicion: 24-hr urine metanephrines and creatinine (more specific); High suspicion: fasting plasma free metanephrines (more sensitive); if elevated, obtain adrenal imaging with CT or MRI with contrast.

**Treatment:** Start alpha blockade (phenoxybenzamine) first, titrate BP <130/90 mmHg; then add beta blocker for reflex tachycardia if needed (never start before adequate alpha-blockade due to hypertensive crisis risk); target HR 60–80 bpm; surgical resection after adequate alpha blockade and volume expansion (typically weeks).

### Adrenal Masses

**Incidentally discovered adrenal mass:** Common (≥2% in general population; more common with increasing age).

**Differential diagnosis:**
- **Non-functioning mass:** Benign adenoma (most common), hemorrhage, lipoma, cyst, malignancy (metastasis from another site more common than primary adrenal malignancy)
- **Functioning mass:** Pheochromocytoma, adenoma or carcinoma producing catecholamine, aldosterone, or cortisol

**Diagnosis:** Consider screening for excess hormones (pheochromocytoma, hyperaldosteronism, cortisol); identify patients at risk for malignancy (>4 cm adrenal mass, known malignancy); imaging characteristics (density <10 HU on CT suggests adrenal adenoma). Almost never biopsy an adrenal mass.

**Adrenocortical carcinoma:** Rare; associated with excessive adrenal hormone production and local mass effect; typically poor prognosis.
