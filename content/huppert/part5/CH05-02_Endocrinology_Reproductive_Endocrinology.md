## 1. Diabetes Mellitus

### Definitions

| Criterion | Value |
| :--- | :--- |
| **HgA1c** | ≥ **6.5%** |
| **Fasting glucose** | ≥ **126 mg/dL** |

### Classification and Treatment Approach

**Type 1 Diabetes (DM1)** represents approximately 5–10% of diabetes cases. It is caused by autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells. The most common autoantibody is GAD65. If there is high suspicion for DM1, also consider others including IA2, insulin autoantibodies, and zinc transporter (ZnT8) antibodies. If autoantibodies are absent, consider maturity-onset diabetes of the young (MODY) versus acquired causes (pancreatitis, neoplasia, cystic fibrosis, hemochromatosis). Minority of patients with DM2 (5–10%) can also have positive islet cell antibodies.

**Type 2 Diabetes (DM2)** represents approximately 90–95% of diabetes cases. Screen for DM2 starting at age 45 years every 3 years. Consider earlier screening if risk factors for DM2 are present (obesity, first-degree relatives with diabetes, etc.). Patients with DM2 can also be asymptomatic.

### Management of DM1

Insulin is the mainstay of therapy; oral agents are not effective. Can consider continuous glucose monitor and insulin pump.

### Management of DM2

Generally, the goal is HgA1c less than 7.0%, especially early in the disease course. Guidelines recommend individualized HgA1c goals with decreasing life expectancy and increasing comorbidity (e.g., may increase target to less than 8.0% in older patients).

**Recommended management steps**:

1. **Step 1**: Discuss and optimize non-pharmacologic management (diet, exercise, weight loss).
2. **Step 2**: Add metformin. Titrate up slowly to allow for adjustment to side effects, up to maximum dose of metformin 1000 mg twice daily.
3. **Step 3**: Add additional agents if needed based on medical comorbidities (cardiovascular risk factors, CKD, heart failure, obesity).

**Common considerations for add-on therapy**:

- Established or high-risk cardiovascular disease: GLP-1 receptor agonists (e.g., liraglutide, semaglutide) or SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) are preferred.
- Heart failure: SGLT2 inhibitors are preferred.
- CKD: SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 receptor agonists.

### Insulin Types

| Type | Examples | Onset/Duration |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rapid-onset** | Aspart, lispro, glulisine | Rapid |
| **Short-acting** | Regular | Shorter |
| **Long-acting** | Glargine, detemir, degludec | Extended |

### Somogyi Effect vs. Dawn Phenomenon

**Somogyi effect**: Low overnight blood sugar causes rebound and morning blood sugar is high. Treatment adjustment involves decreasing evening (PM) insulin.

**Dawn phenomenon**: Early morning hormone surge (growth hormone, cortisol) causes morning hyperglycemia. Treatment adjustment involves increasing PM insulin.

## 3. Clinical Trials in Diabetes

### Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT)

**Citation**: *New England Journal of Medicine* 1993;329(14):977–986. Follow-up: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2000;342(6):381–389.

**Study design**: Multicenter randomized controlled trial that randomized 1441 patients with type 1 diabetes to intensive versus conventional glycemic control therapy.

**Result**: Patients who kept their blood glucose levels as close to normal as safely possible with intensive therapy had significantly lower rates of microvascular complications (retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy).

**Long-term follow-up**: After 30 years of follow-up, the group that had tightly controlled blood glucose during the study continued to show benefits in reducing microvascular complications and had improved overall survival.

### UK Prospective Diabetes Study (UKPDS)

**Citation**: *Lancet* 1998;352:837–853. Follow-up: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2008;359(15):1577–1589.

**Study design**: Multicenter randomized controlled trial that randomized 3867 patients with newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes to intensive versus lenient glycemic control therapy.

**Result**: A follow-up study 10 years later found that the intensive glucose control group had a lower risk of microvascular complications. Patients who underwent intensive glycemic control (fasting glucose less than 108 mg/dL) had better outcomes.

### NiceSugar Study

**Citation**: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2009;360(13):1283–1297.

**Study design**: Multicenter, non-blinded, randomized controlled trial that randomized 6104 patients with type 2 diabetes to intensive glucose control (target fasting glucose of 81–108 mg/dL) versus liberal glucose control (target glucose less than 180 mg/dL).

**Result**: Intensive glucose control led to more deaths than the conventional approach, suggesting more conservative glucose targets may be safer in critically ill patients.

### Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP)

**Citation**: *New England Journal of Medicine* 2002;346(6):393–403.

**Study design**: Multicenter, double-blind, randomized controlled trial that randomized 3234 middle-aged adults with impaired glucose tolerance to intensive lifestyle counseling versus metformin versus placebo.

**Result**: The intensive lifestyle counseling reduced the onset of diabetes by 58% compared to placebo. Metformin treatment reduced the onset of diabetes by 31%. These benefits persisted throughout the study's 15-year follow-up.

## 4. Urinary System Anatomy

**Organs**: The organs of the urinary system include the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra. The function of the urinary system is to filter the blood and create urine as a waste product.

**Kidney anatomy**: The kidney has an outer region called the renal cortex and an inner region called the medulla. The renal columns are connective tissue extensions that radiate from the cortex to the medulla. The papillae are bundles of collecting ducts that carry urine made by the nephron to the minor calyces. The renal hilum is the entry and exit site for the blood vessels, nerves, lymphatics, and ureter.

## 5. Nephron Physiology

**The nephron** is the functional unit of the kidney that uses four mechanisms to process blood: filtration, reabsorption, secretion, and excretion.

**Process**: First, blood flows through Bowman's capsule, and fluid and solutes are filtered out to form ultrafiltrate. The ultrafiltrate then passes through the proximal convoluted tubule, the loop of Henle, the distal convoluted tubule, and the collecting duct, where reabsorption and secretion occur.
