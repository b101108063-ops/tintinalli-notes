# Reproductive Endocrinology

## The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal Axis

GnRH pulse elicits pulses of LH/FSH. LH acts on Leydig cells → testosterone; FSH acts on Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis.

---

## Females

### Menstrual Cycle

- **Follicular phase (day 0–13):** From onset of menses to the LH surge; follicle develops; estradiol inhibits FSH and LH and promotes endometrial healing; rising estradiol causes menstruation to end.
- **Ovulation (day 14):** Dominant follicle secretes estradiol; rising estradiol positively feeds back to increase LH/FSH; LH surge causes ovulation.
- **Luteal phase (day 15–28):** Corpus luteum produces estrogen; if not fertilized, it involutes and progesterone declines; if fertilized, zygote secretes bHCG, which sustains the corpus luteum.

---

### Amenorrhea

#### Primary Amenorrhea

**Definition:** Absent menses at age 15 years.

**Etiologies:**
- **Genetic (50%):** Turner's syndrome (XO karyotype) — webbed neck, coarctation of aorta; diagnosis: high FSH, check karyotype
- **Hormonal (35%):** Low sex hormones due to excessive exercise, anorexia, craniopharyngioma; diagnosis: low FSH, consider brain MRI
- **Anatomic/structural (15%):** Müllerian agenesis, transverse vaginal septum, imperforate hymen; diagnosis: transvaginal ultrasound

#### Secondary Amenorrhea

**Definition:** Absent menses for >3 months in women with previously regular cycles or >6 months if irregular.

**Etiologies:**
- **Pregnancy** (always rule out first!)
- **Ovarian:** PCOS (↑FSH), ovarian failure
- **Hypothalamic:** Stress, exercise, systemic illness, weight loss → functional hypothalamic amenorrhea
- **Pituitary:** Prolactinoma (↑prolactin), empty sella syndrome
- **Endocrine:** Hypo/hyperthyroidism, diabetes, obesity
- **Uterine:** Asherman's syndrome post D&C

**Diagnosis:** Urine pregnancy test, FSH, LH, prolactin, estradiol, TSH; abnormal findings guide management; if normal, progesterone challenge can assess estrogen status.

### Hyperandrogenism Syndromes

#### Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS)

**Symptoms:** Weight gain, male pattern baldness, acne, oligomenorrhea; risk endometrial cancer due to unopposed estrogen.

**Criteria:** Need two of the following: (1) Oligomenorrhea/anovulation; (2) Clinical/biochemical evidence of hyperandrogenism (hirsutism, acne); (3) Polycystic ovaries on pelvic ultrasound.

**Diagnosis:** Diagnosis of exclusion (rule out thyroid dysfunction, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, hyperprolactinemia); labs/imaging not required unless needed to satisfy criteria or rule out other diagnoses. If PCOS diagnosed, also screen for diabetes, HLD, HTN, and OSA.

**Treatment:** Oral contraceptive pills; weight loss; metformin if diabetes or impaired oral glucose tolerance testing.

#### Other Causes of Hirsutism/Virilization

- **Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (e.g., 21-hydroxylase deficiency):** Accumulation of 17-OH progesterone → DHEA; if DHEA >7.0 mg/dL, get CT abd/pelvis to evaluate adrenal glands
- **Adrenal neoplasm:** Rapid hair growth, amenorrhea, virilization
- **Ovarian neoplasms:** Sertoli-Leydig cell tumors secrete testosterone; rapid-onset acne, hirsutism, amenorrhea; if testosterone >150 ng/dL, obtain pelvic ultrasound

---

### Infertility

**Definition:** Failure to conceive after 12 months of unprotected sex (~2×/week) in women <35 yr and after 6 months in women ≥35 yr. For women in their 20s to low 30s, one menstrual cycle ≈ 25% chance of pregnancy.

**Etiologies:**
- **Issues with ovulation:** PCOS, anovulation; diagnosis: day 21 progesterone, FSH (check ovulatory reserve; high FSH suggests less reserve), anti-Müllerian hormone, pelvic ultrasound
- **Blocked transport of egg:** PID, tuberculosis, fibroids, adhesions; diagnosis: hysterosalpingogram
- **Idiopathic**
- **Semen analysis:** Volume, motility, morphology; ddx azoospermia (no sperm): cystic fibrosis, XXY Klinefelter's

**Diagnosis:** Both partners should be evaluated concurrently; females: evaluate ovulatory function (mid-luteal progesterone) and anatomic abnormalities (hysterosalpingogram); males: semen analysis.

**Treatment:** Clomiphene (SERM — inhibits estrogen binding at hypothalamus/pituitary so releases FSH/LH): increases ovulation 2–3 fold; metformin can improve fertility in PCOS. **Assisted reproductive technologies:** Intrauterine insemination, IVF (use GnRH agonist leuprolide to shut down LH; give FSH to target release of 10 eggs; evaluate with pelvic ultrasound and estradiol levels; give LH to cause ovulation; harvest eggs ~36 hours later; can freeze as eggs or embryos).

## Males

### Physiology

GnRH pulse elicits pulses of LH/FSH; LH acts on Leydig cells → testosterone; FSH acts on Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis; testosterone decreases with age.

### Hypogonadism

**Primary hypogonadism:** Testicular failure causing decreased testosterone and sperm production. Ddx: Klinefelter's syndrome (XXY), mumps orchitis, sequelae of radiation therapy, testicular trauma or torsion.

**Secondary hypogonadism:** Insufficient GnRH production or deficient LH/FSH production. Ddx: Pituitary tumor/infiltrative disease, prolactin excess, Kallmann's syndrome (associated with anosmia), OSA, obesity, idiopathic.

**Iatrogenic:** Exogenous testosterone (body building), chronic opiates, steroids.

**Clinical features:** Decreased morning and spontaneous erections, decreased libido, gynecomastia, decreased axillary or genital hair, decreased mood/energy, problems with sleep and memory.

**Diagnosis:** General screening not recommended; in men with specific signs and symptoms, two early AM testosterone levels (fasting if possible) below reference range is indicative; in obesity, free testosterone can be measured (obesity lowers SHBG). If low, check LH/FSH: elevated → primary hypogonadism; low/normal → secondary hypogonadism (further workup: prolactin, hemochromatosis screening, consider pituitary imaging).

**Treatment:** Testosterone replacement; if overweight, weight loss also increases testosterone levels.

### Gynecomastia

**Etiology:** Excess estrogen or increased estrogen/androgen ratio.

**Clinical features:** Increase in breast size; usually bilateral; can be benign in adolescents, patients with chronic medical conditions (cirrhosis, malnutrition, CKD), obesity (pseudogynecomastia), or associated with certain medications (anabolic steroids, spironolactone). Concerning features: unilateral, nontender, fixed breast masses.

**Diagnosis:** Review history and medications; if features concerning for malignancy (unilateral, palpable mass), order mammogram; if no clear cause, also consider testicular exam, bHCG, estradiol, testosterone, LH, and prolactin.

## Endocrine Pancreas

### Diabetes Mellitus

**Definition:**
- HgA1c ≥6.5%
- Fasting glucose ≥126 mg/dL × 2 readings
- Random glucose >200 mg/dL × 2 (or × 1 if acute metabolic decompensation)

**Classifications:**
- **Type 1 (DM1):** Destruction of islet cells causing absolute insulin deficiency
- **Type 2 (DM2):** Insulin resistance, abnormal insulin secretion, excessive hepatic glucose production, abnormal fat metabolism
- **Prediabetes (pre-DM2):** HgA1c 5.7–6.4% or impaired fasting glucose (100–125 mg/dL); recommend diet/exercise, add metformin in certain cases
- **MODY:** Autosomal dominant forms due to defects in genes regulating insulin secretion
- **Gestational diabetes (GDM):** Hyperglycemia during second or third trimester without prior diabetes diagnosis

### Clinical Features of Type 1 vs. Type 2 Diabetes

| Feature | Type 1 Diabetes (DM1) | Type 2 Diabetes (DM2) |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Epidemiology** | 5–10% of cases; typical onset <20 yr | 90–95% of cases; typical adult onset |
| **Pathophysiology** | Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells → insulin deficiency | Insulin resistance, impaired secretion, abnormal fat metabolism, excessive hepatic glucose production |
| **Genetic associations** | Some predisposition; HLA-DQ/DR | Stronger genetic concordance than DM1; no HLA association |
| **Screening/diagnosis** | No screening; send diagnostics if hyperglycemia symptoms; check islet cell autoantibodies (GAD65 most common) | Screen starting at age 45 yr every 3 yr; consider earlier if risk factors (obesity, first-degree relatives, GDM, PCOS, HTN, HLD) |
| **Clinical features** | Polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue, weight loss, blurred vision, fungal infections, neuropathy | Can also be asymptomatic |
| **Treatment** | Lifelong insulin; 0.5 units/kg → ½ long-acting, ½ pre-meal; consider continuous subcutaneous insulin infusions | Pre-DM2 (HgA1c 5.7–6.4%): diet, exercise, consider metformin if age <60 yr, BMI >35, or GDM; DM2 (HgA1c >6.5%): First line metformin + weight loss/diet; second line: SGLT2i or GLP1-RA for most patients |

### Acute Complications

DKA and HHS — see Critical Care Chapter 3.

### Chronic Complications of Diabetes

| Microvascular (Glycemic control reduces/slows progression) | Macrovascular (Glycemic control does not always reduce) |
|------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Nephropathy (glomerular hyperfiltration → thick GBM → albuminuria) | Coronary artery disease |
| Retinopathy (cataracts, retinopathy, glaucoma) | Peripheral vascular disease |
| Neuropathy (peripheral and autonomic: impotence, overflow incontinence, neurogenic bladder, gastroparesis) | Stroke/TIA |

### Medication Classes for Diabetes

| Class | Examples | Expected ↓ HgA1c (%) | Mechanism | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------|---------|-------------------|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| **Alpha-glucosidase inhibitor** | Acarbose, miglitol | 0.5–0.8 | Blocks breakdown of carbohydrates in intestine | Weight neutral | GI side effects, TID dosing |
| **Biguanides** | Metformin | 1.0–2.0 | Decreases hepatic glucose production; decreases insulin resistance | Weight neutral to weight loss, no hypoglycemia, low cost, potential ASCVD benefit | GI side effects common, contraindicated with GFR <30, B12 deficiency, lactic acidosis risk |
| **DPP4i** | Linagliptin, saxagliptin, sitagliptin | 0.5–0.8 | Increases insulin synthesis/release from pancreatic beta cells | Weight neutral | Possible increased heart failure risk with saxagliptin, joint pain |
| **GLP1 RA** | Dulaglutide, exenatide, liraglutide, semaglutide | 0.5–1.5 | Enhances glucose-dependent insulin secretion; slows gastric emptying | Reduction in MACE, weight loss, no hypoglycemia, benefit in NASH | Most require injection, GI side effects common |
| **SGLT2i** | Canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, ertugliflozin | 0.5–0.7 | Reduces renal tubular glucose reabsorption | Reduced CV mortality, improved renal outcomes, no hypoglycemia, weight loss | UTIs, vulvovaginal candidiasis, bone fractures, euglycemic DKA |
| **Sulfonylureas (SU)** | Chlorpropamide, glyburide, glipizide | 1.0–2.0 | Stimulates pancreatic beta cells to release insulin | Rapidly effective | Hypoglycemia, weight gain, concern in patients with CAD |
| **Thiazolidinediones (TZD)** | Pioglitazone | 0.5–1.4 | Decreases insulin resistance in muscle, fat, liver | Improved lipid profile, potential ASCVD benefit (if no heart failure), benefit in NASH | Fluid retention, heart failure, weight gain, bone fractures, potential bladder cancer risk |
| **Insulin** | Rapid: Aspart, lispro, glulisine; Short: Regular; Intermed: NPH; Long: Detemir, glargine, degludec; Concentrated: U500 | 1.5–3.5 | Helps cells break down glucose | Rapidly effective, no dose limit | Injections required, hypoglycemia risk, weight gain |
