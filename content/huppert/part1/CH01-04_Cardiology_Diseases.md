## 1. Ischemic Heart Disease

### Stable Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)
* **Definition**: Asymptomatic CAD or stable angina (occurring reliably with exertion/emotional stress, relieved by rest/nitroglycerin; due to fixed atherosclerosis).
* **Diagnosis**: Stress testing; CCTA (computed tomography angiography) vs. invasive coronary angiogram.
* **Workup**: HgA1c, lipid panel, ECG, TTE to assess LV function.
* **Treatment – Risk Factors**:
  * Smoking cessation, exercise, weight loss, diet.
  * Control HTN (goal $\mathbf{< 140/\text{90 mmHg}}$), start ACEi (or ARB).
  * Treat DM (preferably with SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 agonists).
  * Treat HLD: start statin (goal LDL $< \mathbf{70\text{ mg/dL}}$); consider adding ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitor, or icosapent ethyl based on specific criteria.
  * Aspirin and Beta blocker (BB), particularly if prior ACS event.
* **Treatment – Antianginal**:
  * **Beta blocker (BB)**: Increases coronary blood supply and decreases myocardial demand.
  * **Calcium channel blocker (CCB)**: Similar mechanism as BB, used as a second agent.
  * **Nitrates**: Vasodilation of coronary arteries; venodilation decreases preload/wall stress/O2 demand.
  * **Ranolazine**: Inhibits late inward sodium current in ischemic myocytes.
* **Revascularization**: Consider for symptomatic patients despite optimal medical therapy or for physiologically significant lesions. 
  * *Exceptions with mortality benefit*: Significant left main disease ($> \mathbf{50\%}$ stenosis), significant 3-vessel CAD ($> \mathbf{70\%}$ stenosis), or HFrEF.

### Myocardial Infarction (MI)
* **Type I MI**: Spontaneous MI (ACS from a thrombus in a coronary artery).
  * **NSTEMI**: Subendocardial ischemia. Criteria requires rise/fall in troponin AND one of the following: symptoms of acute myocardial ischemia, new ischemic ECG changes, pathologic Q waves, new wall motion abnormality, or coronary thrombus on angiography.
  * **STEMI**: Transmural ischemia.
* **Type II MI**: MI due to ischemic imbalance (demand ischemia). Treat the underlying hemodynamic cause.
* **Coronary Vasospasm**: Transient coronary vasospasm causing angina at rest; often in young women who smoke and have migraines/Raynaud's. Diagnosed by transient ST elevation on ECG during chest pain. Treated with smoking cessation, CCB, long-acting nitrates; avoid non-selective beta-blockers.

### Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) Management
* **Initial Therapy**: Aspirin (325 mg PO $\times$1 chewed, then 81 mg daily), Anticoagulation (e.g., Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SQ q12h or heparin drip), P2Y12 inhibitor (Clopidogrel, Ticagrelor, or Prasugrel), Statin (Atorvastatin 80mg or Rosuvastatin 40 mg), Beta blocker (goal HR $\mathbf{\text{60 bpm}}$), ACEi (if hypertensive/normal renal function), and pain control (Sublingual nitroglycerin).
* **Revascularization**: STEMI requires immediate PCI (door to balloon $< 90$ min); NSTEMI is less urgent (within 24–48 hr) unless intermediate to high risk.
* **Post-MI Management**: Lifelong Aspirin and Atorvastatin 80 mg; P2Y12 inhibitor for 1 yr; Beta blocker; ACEi/ARB; Spironolactone/Eplerenone if EF $< \mathbf{40\%}$ and DM/HF symptoms; TTE; lifestyle modifications; cardiac rehabilitation; consider ICD if EF $< \mathbf{35\%}$ post-MI despite 40 days of medical therapy.

## 2. Approach to Bradycardia (HR < 60 bpm)

* **Unstable**: Prepare for transcutaneous pacing. Give Atropine 0.5 mg IV q3–5 min (max 3 mg); use Dopamine or Epinephrine if pacing/atropine are not effective; transvenous pacing.
* **Stable**: Observe, get ECG to rule out high-degree AV block, treat underlying etiology.

### Bradycardic Rhythms
* **Sinus Bradycardia**: Caused by increased vagal tone (sleep, athletes, vasovagal episodes) or pathologic/extrinsic causes (medications, ischemia, infiltrative diseases, hypothermia). Treat underlying problem or give glucagon (BB overdose) / $\text{CaCl}_2$ (CCB overdose) if unstable.
* **AV Blocks**:
  * **First degree**: Prolonged PR ($> 200$ ms); usually asymptomatic, no treatment necessary.
  * **Second degree (Mobitz I / Wenckebach)**: Lengthening PR until dropped beat. Improves with atropine/exercise; no treatment needed unless symptomatic.
  * **Second degree (Mobitz II)**: No change in PR length but sudden dropped beat. Can progress to complete block; treatment is a pacemaker.
  * **Third degree (Complete)**: No AV conduction. Treatment is a pacemaker.

## 3. Approach to Narrow QRS Complex Tachycardia (HR > 100 bpm)

* **Unstable**: Treat underlying cause (if sinus tachycardia) or perform synchronized cardioversion.
* **Stable**: Identify the rhythm based on regularity, onset, atrial rate, P-wave morphology, RP intervals, and response to vagal stimulation/adenosine.

### Regular Atrial Rhythms
* **Sinus Tachycardia (ST)**: Treat underlying etiology.
* **AV Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia (AVNRT)**: Regular, narrow QRS ($120–\mathbf{220\text{ bpm}}$), no P waves. Acute treatment: Vagal maneuvers, IV adenosine. Chronic: Ablation vs. nodal blocking agents.
* **AV Reentrant Tachycardia (AVRT)**: Associated with WPW syndrome. Can be Orthodromic (narrow QRS) or Antidromic (wide QRS). Treatment: Vagal maneuvers, IV adenosine, nodal agents.
* **Atrial Tachycardia (AT)**: Impulse generated outside the SA node. Short-term treatment: BB or CCB. Long-term: BB, CCB, ablation.
* **Atrial Flutter**: Saw-toothed flutter waves, atrial rate $\sim\mathbf{300\text{ bpm}}$. Treatment: Rate control (metoprolol, diltiazem), consider cardioversion, anticoagulation, ablation.

### Irregular Atrial Rhythms
* **Premature Atrial Complexes (PACs)**: Benign, no treatment required unless symptomatic.
* **Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia (MAT)**: $\ge 3$ P waves with differing morphologies; associated with COPD. Treatment: CCB or BB, treat underlying disease.
* **Atrial Fibrillation (Afib)**: Irregularly irregular rhythm, no P waves.
  * **Unstable**: Emergent synchronized cardioversion.
  * **Stable (RVR)**: Rate control (BB, CCB, Amiodarone, Digoxin), consider cardioversion.
  * **Chronic Treatment**: Reduce stroke risk (anticoagulation with Warfarin or DOAC based on CHA2DS2-VASc), rate control, rhythm control (cardioversion, antiarrhythmic drugs, or ablation procedures).

## 4. Wide QRS Complex Tachycardia

* **Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)**: 
  * **Unstable**: Assume VT and proceed with DCCV or ACLS.
  * **Stable**: Differentiate from SVT with aberrancy. Monomorphic (single QRS morphology) or Polymorphic (varies beat to beat).
  * **Torsades de pointes**: VT with varying QRS amplitude in the setting of long QTc; treated with IV Magnesium 4 g, repolating K, and keeping HR $\mathbf{\text{100 bpm}}$.
  * **Treatment**: IV amiodarone, lidocaine; prepare for synchronized cardioversion. Long-term management includes ICD, antiarrhythmics, ablation.
* **SVT with Aberrancy**: Conducts down a slow system (e.g., BBB). Treated with Adenosine.
* **Ventricular Fibrillation (Vfib)**: Irregular, wide QRS. Treated with non-synchronized defibrillation followed by amiodarone.

## 5. Heart Failure (HF)

* **Classification**: HFrEF (EF $< \mathbf{40\%}$), HFpEF (EF $> \mathbf{50\%}$), HFmrEF (EF $40–\mathbf{50\%}$).
* **Symptoms**: Dyspnea, decreased exercise capacity, orthopnea, edema. **Signs of congestion**: S3, crackles, JVD. **Signs of low output**: Cool extremities, narrow pulse pressure, low SBP.
* **Workup**: TTE, CXR, Natriuretic peptides (BNP, NT-proBNP), ECG, Troponin, Sodium.
* **HFrEF Treatment (Mortality Benefit)**: ACEi, ARB, ARNI (Sacubitril/Valsartan), Beta blockers, MRAs (Spironolactone/Eplerenone), SGLT2 inhibitors.
* **Advanced Therapies**: ICD, CRT, CardioMEMS, LVAD, Heart transplant.

## 6. Pulmonary Hypertension

* **Diagnosis**: mPAP $> \mathbf{20\text{ mmHg}}$ on Right Heart Catheterization.
* **WHO Classification**:
  * **Group 1 (Arterial)**: Pre-capillary; treated with pulmonary vasodilators.
  * **Group 2 (Venous)**: Post-capillary (due to left heart failure); treat underlying HF.
  * **Group 3 (Respiratory)**: Due to lung disease/hypoxia; treat underlying lung disease, oxygen.
  * **Group 4 (Thromboembolic)**: CTEPH; anticoagulation, surgery.
  * **Group 5 (Other)**: Sarcoid, metabolic disease; directed at underlying disease.

## 7. Valvular Heart Disease

* **Aortic Stenosis (AS)**: Harsh crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur. Severe AS requires valve replacement (SAVR vs. TAVR).
* **Aortic Regurgitation (AR)**: Diastolic blowing decrescendo murmur. Treated with surgical AVR.
* **Mitral Stenosis (MS)**: Opening snap, diastolic rumble. Medical management with diuretics and rate control; definitive management with percutaneous balloon valvuloplasty or surgery.
* **Mitral Regurgitation (MR)**: Holosystolic murmur radiating to axilla. Treated with surgical repair/replacement or MitraClip.
* **Tricuspid Regurgitation (TR)**: Louder with inspiration. Often no treatment, or bioprosthetic TV.

## 8. Myocardial and Pericardial Diseases

* **Myocarditis**: Inflammation due to infection or autoimmune conditions. Treated with supportive care.
* **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)**: Crescendo-decrescendo systolic ejection murmur (louder with Valsalva/standing). Treat by avoiding dehydration/diuretics; give BB or CCB. ICD for sudden death prevention.
* **Acute Pericarditis**: Pleuritic/positional chest pain, friction rub, diffuse ST elevations. Treated with Aspirin, NSAIDs, colchicine.
* **Cardiac Tamponade**: Beck's triad (hypotension, elevated JVP, muffled heart sounds). Treated with IVF and urgent pericardiocentesis.

## 9. Diseases of the Vasculature

* **Hypertensive Emergency**: Elevated SBP with end-organ damage. Reduce MAP by **25%** in 1-2 hours via IV Nitroprusside or Nicardipine.
* **Aortic Dissection**: Abrupt onset tearing pain with pulse/BP asymmetry. Diagnosed by CTA. Type A requires emergent surgery; Type B managed medically (IV BB, Nitroprusside).
* **Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)**: Screen men 65-75 yr who have ever smoked via ultrasound. Repair if $> \mathbf{5.5\text{ cm}}$ (men) or $> \mathbf{5\text{ cm}}$ (women).
* **Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD)**: Claudication, diagnosed by ABI $< 1.0$. Treated with smoking cessation, exercise, Aspirin, Cilostazol; consider PCI or surgery.
* **Acute Arterial Occlusion**: Six Ps (Pain, Pallor, Poikilothermic, Paralysis, Paresthesia, Pulselessness). Treated with immediate IV heparin and vascular procedure/surgery.
