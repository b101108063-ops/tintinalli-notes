## 1. Ischemic Heart Disease

### Stable Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

#### Definition

Stable CAD refers to either asymptomatic coronary artery disease or stable angina—chest pain that occurs reliably with exertion or emotional stress and is relieved by rest or nitroglycerin. The underlying pathology is fixed atherosclerosis.

#### Diagnosis

- Stress testing
- Coronary CT angiography (CCTA) or invasive coronary angiogram

#### Baseline Workup

- Hemoglobin A1c (HgA1c)
- Lipid panel
- ECG
- Transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE) to assess left ventricular function

#### Treatment — Risk Factor Modification

| Risk Factor | Management |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Lifestyle** | Smoking cessation, exercise, weight loss, heart-healthy diet |
| **Hypertension** | Goal $< 140/90$ mmHg; initiate ACE inhibitor (or ARB if intolerant) |
| **Diabetes** | Preferentially treat with SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 agonists |
| **Hyperlipidemia** | Start statin (LDL goal $< 70$ mg/dL); consider ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitor, or icosapent ethyl based on specific criteria |
| **Antiplatelet therapy** | Aspirin |
| **Beta-blocker therapy** | Particularly if prior ACS event |

#### Treatment — Anti-Anginal Therapy

| Medication | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Beta blocker (BB)** | Increases coronary blood supply and decreases myocardial demand |
| **Calcium channel blocker (CCB)** | Similar mechanism to BB; used as a second agent |
| **Nitrates** | Coronary vasodilation; venodilation decreases preload, wall stress, and O₂ demand |
| **Ranolazine** | Inhibits late inward sodium current in ischemic myocytes |

#### Revascularization

Consider revascularization for symptomatic patients despite optimal medical therapy or for those with physiologically significant lesions.

| Exception | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- |
| Left main disease $> 50\%$ stenosis | Mortality benefit from revascularization |
| Three-vessel CAD $> 70\%$ stenosis | Mortality benefit from revascularization |
| HFrEF with significant CAD | Mortality benefit from revascularization |

### Myocardial Infarction (MI)

#### Type 1 MI (Spontaneous MI)

Type 1 MI is ACS caused by a thrombus forming in a coronary artery.

| Subtype | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **NSTEMI** | Subendocardial ischemia (non-ST elevation MI) |
| **STEMI** | Transmural ischemia (ST elevation MI) |

**NSTEMI diagnostic criteria** requires a rise/fall in troponin AND at least one of the following:

- Symptoms of acute myocardial ischemia
- New ischemic ECG changes
- Pathologic Q waves
- New wall motion abnormality
- Coronary thrombus on angiography

#### Type 2 MI

Type 2 MI results from an ischemic imbalance—myocardial injury due to conditions that cause demand ischemia (e.g., tachycardia, anemia, hypoxia). Treatment should focus on the underlying hemodynamic cause.

#### Coronary Vasospasm

Coronary vasospasm causes transient coronary vasoconstriction resulting in angina at rest. This condition is often seen in young women who smoke and have a history of migraines or Raynaud's phenomenon.

- **Diagnosis**: Transient ST elevation on ECG during chest pain
- **Treatment**: Smoking cessation, calcium channel blocker, long-acting nitrates
- **Avoid**: Non-selective beta-blockers

### Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) Management

#### Initial Therapy

| Component | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Antiplatelet** | Aspirin 325 mg PO once (chewed), then 81 mg daily |
| **Anticoagulation** | Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SQ q12h or heparin drip |
| **P2Y12 inhibitor** | Clopidogrel, ticagrelor, or prasugrel |
| **Statin** | Atorvastatin 80 mg or rosuvastatin 40 mg |
| **Beta blocker** | Goal heart rate $< 60$ bpm |
| **ACE inhibitor** | If hypertensive or with normal renal function |
| **Nitroglycerin** | Sublingual for pain control |

#### Revascularization

| Presentation | Timeline |
| :--- | :--- |
| **STEMI** | Immediate PCI required (door-to-balloon time $< 90$ minutes) |
| **NSTEMI** | Less urgent; PCI within 24–48 hours unless intermediate to high risk |

#### Post-MI Management

- Lifelong aspirin and atorvastatin 80 mg
- P2Y12 inhibitor for 1 year
- Beta blocker
- ACE inhibitor or ARB
- **If EF $< 40\%$ and diabetes or HF symptoms**: Add spironolactone or eplerenone
- Transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE)
- Lifestyle modifications
- Cardiac rehabilitation
- **If EF $< 35\%$** post-MI despite 40 days of optimal medical therapy: Consider ICD placement

## 2. Approach to Bradycardia (Heart Rate < 60 bpm)

### Initial Management

| Clinical Scenario | Management |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Unstable patient** | Prepare for transcutaneous pacing. Administer atropine 0.5 mg IV every 3–5 minutes (maximum 3 mg). If pacing or atropine is ineffective, consider dopamine or epinephrine. Prepare for transvenous pacing. |
| **Stable patient** | Observe, obtain ECG to rule out high-degree AV block, and treat the underlying etiology. |

### Bradycardic Rhythms

#### Sinus Bradycardia

Sinus bradycardia is caused by increased vagal tone (common during sleep, in trained athletes, and during vasovagal episodes) or by pathologic or extrinsic factors (medications, ischemia, infiltrative diseases, hypothermia).

- **Treatment**: Treat the underlying problem.
- **Overdose reversal**:
  - Beta-blocker overdose: Give glucagon.
  - Calcium channel blocker overdose: Give calcium chloride ($\text{CaCl}_2$).

#### AV Blocks

| Block | ECG Findings | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **First-degree AV block** | Prolonged PR interval $> 200$ ms | Usually asymptomatic; no treatment necessary |
| **Second-degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach)** | Progressive PR lengthening until a beat is dropped | Improves with atropine or exercise; no treatment needed unless symptomatic |
| **Second-degree Mobitz II** | Fixed PR interval with sudden dropped beat (no PR prolongation) | Can progress to complete heart block; treatment is pacemaker placement |
| **Third-degree (complete) heart block** | No AV conduction (P waves and QRS complexes are independent) | Permanent pacemaker placement |

## 3. Approach to Narrow QRS Complex Tachycardia (Heart Rate > 100 bpm)

### Initial Management

| Clinical Scenario | Management |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Unstable patient** | Treat the underlying cause (if sinus tachycardia) or perform synchronized cardioversion. |
| **Stable patient** | Identify the rhythm based on regularity, onset, atrial rate, P-wave morphology, RP intervals, and response to vagal stimulation or adenosine. |

### Regular Atrial Rhythms

| Rhythm | Characteristics | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sinus tachycardia (ST)** | Normal sinus rhythm with rate $> 100$ bpm | Treat underlying etiology |
| **AV nodal reentrant tachycardia (AVNRT)** | Regular, narrow-complex tachycardia (120–220 bpm); no P waves visible | Acute: vagal maneuvers, IV adenosine. Chronic: ablation or nodal blocking agents |
| **AV reentrant tachycardia (AVRT)** | Associated with WPW syndrome; orthodromic (narrow QRS) or antidromic (wide QRS) | Vagal maneuvers, IV adenosine; avoid nodal agents if antidromic |
| **Atrial tachycardia (AT)** | Impulse generated outside the SA node; may be focal or multifocal | Short-term: BB or CCB. Long-term: BB, CCB, or ablation |
| **Atrial flutter** | Saw-toothed flutter waves; atrial rate approximately 300 bpm | Rate control (metoprolol, diltiazem); consider cardioversion, anticoagulation, or ablation |

### Irregular Atrial Rhythms

| Rhythm | Characteristics | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Premature atrial complexes (PACs)** | Benign premature beats from the atrium | No treatment required unless symptomatic |
| **Multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAT)** | $\ge 3$ distinct P-wave morphologies; associated with COPD | CCB or BB; treat underlying disease |
| **Atrial fibrillation (Afib)** | Irregularly irregular rhythm; no P waves | See below |

#### Atrial Fibrillation (Afib) Management

| Clinical Scenario | Management |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Unstable patient** | Emergent synchronized cardioversion |
| **Stable patient with rapid ventricular response (RVR)** | Rate control (BB, CCB, amiodarone, digoxin); consider cardioversion |
| **Chronic management** | Stroke risk reduction (anticoagulation with warfarin or DOAC based on CHA₂DS₂-VASc score); rate control; rhythm control (cardioversion, antiarrhythmic drugs, or ablation) |

## 4. Wide QRS Complex Tachycardia

### Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

| Clinical Scenario | Management |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Unstable patient** | Assume VT and proceed with DCCV or ACLS protocols |
| **Stable patient** | Differentiate from SVT with aberrancy; can be monomorphic (single QRS morphology) or polymorphic (varies beat to beat) |

#### Torsades de Pointes

Torsades de pointes is a specific form of polymorphic VT characterized by QRS complexes that vary in amplitude (appearing to twist around the baseline). It occurs in the setting of a prolonged QTc interval.

- **Treatment**: IV magnesium 4 g, repolarizing potassium, and maintaining heart rate $> 100$ bpm.

#### VT Treatment

- Acute: IV amiodarone, lidocaine; prepare for synchronized cardioversion
- Long-term: ICD placement, antiarrhythmic medications, ablation

### SVT with Aberrancy

SVT with aberrancy occurs when a supraventricular impulse conducts down a slow system such as a bundle branch block. Treatment is the same as for other narrow-complex tachycardias, including adenosine.

### Ventricular Fibrillation (Vfib)

Ventricular fibrillation is an irregular, wide-complex rhythm with chaotic electrical activity.

- **Treatment**: Non-synchronized defibrillation followed by amiodarone

## 5. Heart Failure (HF)

### Classification

| Type | Ejection Fraction |
| :--- | :--- |
| **HFrEF** (Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction) | EF $< 40\%$ |
| **HFpEF** (Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction) | EF $> 50\%$ |
| **HFmrEF** (Heart Failure with Mildly Reduced Ejection Fraction) | EF $40$–$50\%$ |

### Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms**:

- Dyspnea
- Decreased exercise capacity
- Orthopnea
- Peripheral edema

**Signs of congestion**:

- S3 heart sound
- Pulmonary crackles
- Elevated jugular venous pressure (JVD)

**Signs of low cardiac output**:

- Cool extremities
- Narrow pulse pressure
- Low systolic blood pressure

### Workup

- Transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE)
- Chest X-ray
- Natriuretic peptides (BNP, NT-proBNP)
- ECG
- Troponin
- Serum sodium

### HFrEF Treatment with Mortality Benefit

Pharmacotherapy with proven mortality benefit in HFrEF includes:

- ACE inhibitors (or ARBs, or ARNI such as sacubitril/valsartan)
- Beta blockers
- Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (spironolactone or eplerenone)
- SGLT2 inhibitors

### Advanced Therapies

- Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD)
- Cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT)
- CardioMEMS
- Left ventricular assist device (LVAD)
- Heart transplantation

## 6. Pulmonary Hypertension

### Definition

Pulmonary hypertension is defined as a mean pulmonary artery pressure (mPAP) $> 20$ mmHg on right heart catheterization.

### WHO Classification

| Group | Name | Mechanism | Management |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Group 1** | Arterial PH | Pre-capillary pulmonary arterial hypertension | Pulmonary vasodilators |
| **Group 2** | Venous PH | Post-capillary pulmonary hypertension due to left heart failure | Treat underlying heart failure |
| **Group 3** | Respiratory PH | Due to lung disease or hypoxia | Treat underlying lung disease; supplemental oxygen |
| **Group 4** | Thromboembolic PH | Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) | Anticoagulation; surgical pulmonary thromboendarterectomy |
| **Group 5** | Other PH | Sarcoidosis, metabolic disease, hematologic disorders | Directed at underlying disease |

## 7. Valvular Heart Disease

| Valve Lesion | Murmur Characteristics | Management |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Aortic Stenosis (AS)** | Harsh crescendo-decrescendo systolic ejection murmur | Severe AS requires valve replacement (SAVR or TAVR) |
| **Aortic Regurgitation (AR)** | Diastolic blowing decrescendo murmur | Surgical AVR |
| **Mitral Stenosis (MS)** | Opening snap followed by diastolic rumble | Medical: diuretics and rate control. Definitive: percutaneous balloon valvuloplasty or surgery |
| **Mitral Regurgitation (MR)** | Holosystolic murmur radiating to the axilla | Surgical repair or replacement; MitraClip for select patients |
| **Tricuspid Regurgitation (TR)** | Murmur louder with inspiration | Often no treatment; bioprosthetic tricuspid valve replacement for select patients |

## 8. Myocardial and Pericardial Diseases

### Myocarditis

Myocarditis is inflammation of the myocardium, most commonly due to viral infection or autoimmune conditions.

- **Treatment**: Supportive care

### Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)

HCM is characterized by a crescendo-decrescendo systolic ejection murmur that becomes louder with Valsalva maneuver or standing (decreased preload).

- **Management**:
  - Avoid dehydration and diuretics
  - Beta-blocker or calcium channel blocker therapy
  - ICD placement for sudden death prevention in high-risk patients

### Acute Pericarditis

Acute pericarditis presents with pleuritic or positional chest pain, a pericardial friction rub, and diffuse ST elevations on ECG.

- **Treatment**: Aspirin, NSAIDs, and colchicine

### Cardiac Tamponade

Cardiac tamponade is characterized by Beck's triad:

- Hypotension
- Elevated jugular venous pressure
- Muffled heart sounds

- **Treatment**: IV fluids and urgent pericardiocentesis

## 9. Diseases of the Vasculature

### Hypertensive Emergency

A hypertensive emergency is defined as an elevated systolic blood pressure with evidence of end-organ damage.

- **Management**: Reduce mean arterial pressure by 25% within 1–2 hours using IV nitroprusside or nicardipine.

### Aortic Dissection

Aortic dissection presents with abrupt onset tearing chest pain radiating to the back, often with pulse or blood pressure asymmetry between limbs.

- **Diagnosis**: CTA of the chest and abdomen
- **Type A dissection**: Requires emergent surgical repair
- **Type B dissection**: Managed medically with IV beta-blockers and nitroprusside

### Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)

- **Screening**: Men aged 65–75 years who have ever smoked should be screened via ultrasound
- **Repair threshold**: $> 5.5$ cm in men or $> 5$ cm in women

### Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD)

PAD presents with claudication and is diagnosed by an ankle-brachial index (ABI) $< 1.0$.

- **Management**: Smoking cessation, exercise therapy, aspirin, cilostazol; consider PCI or surgical revascularization for refractory symptoms

### Acute Arterial Occlusion

Acute arterial occlusion is characterized by the six Ps:

| Sign | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pain** | Severe, sudden onset |
| **Pallor** | Pale extremity |
| **Poikilothermia** | Cool extremity |
| **Paralysis** | Motor deficit |
| **Paresthesia** | Sensory deficit |
| **Pulselessness** | Absent distal pulse |

- **Treatment**: Immediate IV heparin and urgent vascular procedure or surgical revascularization
