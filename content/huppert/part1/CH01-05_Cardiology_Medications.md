## 1. Common Medications in Cardiology

### Calcium Channel Blockers (CCB)

| Category & Examples | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Dihydropyridine (DHP)**: amlodipine, clevidipine, nicardipine, nifedipine, nimodipine | Blocks voltage-dependent L-type $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ channels, acting primarily on vascular smooth muscle, leading to decreased muscle contractility | Hypertension; angina (including Prinzmetal variant angina); Raynaud's phenomenon. *Nimodipine*: Prophylaxis against vasospasm in subarachnoid hemorrhage | Peripheral edema, flushing, dizziness |
| **Non-DHP**: diltiazem, verapamil | Blocks voltage-dependent L-type $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ channels, acting primarily on the heart | Hypertension; angina; atrial fibrillation or flutter rate control | Cardiac depression, AV block, hyperprolactinemia, constipation, gingival hyperplasia |

### RAAS (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System) Inhibitors

| Class & Examples | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ACE inhibitors (ACEi)**: captopril, enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril | Inhibition of ACE lowers angiotensin II levels (reducing GFR via efferent arteriole relaxation); prevents inactivation of bradykinin | Hypertension; heart failure; left ventricular hypertrophy; diabetic nephropathy | Cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema. **Teratogen — contraindicated in pregnancy** |
| **Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARB)**: losartan, candesartan, valsartan | Blocks the binding of angiotensin II to its receptor; does not increase bradykinin levels | Same indications as ACEi; often used second-line when patients develop an ACEi-related cough | Similar to ACEi, but cough and angioedema are much less common |

### Vasodilators & Nitrates

| Medication | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hydralazine** | Relaxes vascular smooth muscle via cGMP pathway; arteriolar dilatation exceeds venodilation | Acute severe hypertension; combination therapy with organic nitrates for heart failure | Compensatory tachycardia (contraindicated in CAD), headache, SLE-like syndrome |
| **Nitroprusside** | Direct vasodilation; arteriolar dilatation exceeds venodilation | Hypertensive emergency; acute decompensated heart failure (afterload reduction) | Cyanide toxicity (particularly in renal failure) |
| **Organic Nitrates**: nitroglycerin, isosorbide dinitrate, isosorbide mononitrate | Vasodilation via increased nitric oxide production; venodilation predominates over arteriolar dilatation | Angina; acute coronary syndrome | Reflex tachycardia, hypotension, flushing, headache. **Contraindication**: Right ventricular MI |

### Other Cardiac Medications

| Medication | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ranolazine** | Inhibits late-phase inward $\text{Na}^{+}$ current in ischemic cardiomyocytes, reducing wall tension and oxygen consumption | Refractory angina | Dizziness, headache, nausea, QT prolongation |
| **Milrinone** | Selective PDE-3 inhibitor; increases inotropy and chronotropy via enhanced $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ influx; relaxes vascular smooth muscle | Acute, decompensated heart failure | Hypotension, arrhythmia |
| **Digoxin** (Cardiac Glycoside) | Inhibits $\text{Na}^{+}/\text{K}^{+}$ ATPase, which indirectly inhibits the $\text{Na}^{+}/\text{Ca}^{2+}$ exchanger, increasing inotropy; slows heart rate via vagal nerve stimulation | Heart failure; atrial fibrillation rate control | Cholinergic effects, hyperkalemia. Toxicity is particularly concerning in renal failure |

### Antiarrhythmic Drugs (Vaughan-Williams Classification)

| Class & Examples | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Class I** ($\text{Na}^{+}$ channel blockers) | Slows or blocks phase 0 depolarization in partially depolarized cells | — | — |
| &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;**IA**: quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide | Moderately slows phase 0; prolongs action potential and refractoriness | Atrial and ventricular arrhythmias | QT prolongation, SLE-like syndrome (procainamide) |
| &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;**IB**: lidocaine, mexiletine | Preferentially binds to depolarized tissue; shortens action potential | Post-MI ventricular arrhythmia; digitalis-induced arrhythmia | CNS effects |
| &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;**IC**: flecainide, propafenone | Markedly slows phase 0; no effect on action potential duration | Supraventricular tachycardia (including atrial fibrillation) | Pro-arrhythmic (especially post-MI) |
| **Class II** ($\beta$ blockers): metoprolol, propranolol, esmolol, atenolol, carvedilol | Decreases SA node and AV nodal activity; decreases slope 4 of abnormal pacemaker cells | Rate control for atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, or other SVTs | Bradycardia, masked signs of hypoglycemia, impotence, dyslipidemia |
| **Class III** ($\text{K}^{+}$ channel blockers): amiodarone, ibutilide, dofetilide, sotalol | Increases duration of action potential and effective refractory period | Atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, ventricular tachycardia | Prolonged QT interval. *Amiodarone*: Pulmonary, hepatic, and thyroid toxicity |
| **Class IV** ($\text{Ca}^{2+}$ channel blockers): verapamil, diltiazem | Decreases conduction velocity and increases effective refractory period at the AV node | Rate control in atrial fibrillation and SVTs conducted by the AV node | Edema, constipation, flushing, bradycardia, AV block |

## 2. Procedural & Surgical Interventions

### Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI)

PCI serves both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. It allows for identification of coronary abnormalities (diagnostic angiogram) and stent placement to improve coronary perfusion (therapeutic intervention).

### Coronary Artery Bypass Graft (CABG)

CABG involves using a graft (typically a saphenous vein or internal mammary artery) to bypass a stenotic coronary artery.

**Main indications**:

- Triple-vessel coronary artery disease
- Severe left main artery stenosis
- Severe proximal LAD stenosis with concurrent circumflex stenosis or EF $< 50\%$

### Valve Replacement

- **Surgical aortic valve replacement (SAVR)**: Traditional open surgical approach for aortic stenosis.
- **Transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR)**: Less invasive, catheter-based approach for aortic stenosis, suitable for select patients.

### Radiofrequency Ablation

Radiofrequency ablation uses catheter-delivered energy to selectively disrupt electrical signals causing arrhythmias. It is a curative procedure for many supraventricular tachycardias.

### Heart Transplantation

Heart transplantation is the definitive treatment for end-stage heart failure.

**Most common indications**:

- Non-ischemic cardiomyopathy (approximately 59% of transplants)
- Ischemic cardiomyopathy (approximately 40% of transplants)

## 3. Key Clinical Trials and Publications in Cardiology

### Cardiovascular Disease Risk Prevention

#### FOURIER (*N Engl J Med 2017*)

- **Study design**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of 27,564 patients with atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease and LDL $\ge 70$ mg/dL on statin therapy. Patients were randomized to evolocumab (PCSK9 inhibitor, 140 mg every 2 weeks or 420 mg monthly) versus placebo.
- **Result**: Evolocumab significantly reduced the risk of the composite primary endpoint (cardiovascular death, myocardial infarction, stroke, hospitalization for unstable angina, or coronary revascularization) compared to placebo (9.8% vs. 11.3%).

### Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

#### FAME-2 (*N Engl J Med 2012*)

- **Study design**: 888 patients with stable CAD considered for PCI underwent fractional flow reserve (FFR) testing. Those with $\ge 1$ functionally significant lesion were randomized to FFR-guided PCI plus best medical therapy versus best medical therapy alone.
- **Result**: FFR-guided PCI reduced the composite endpoint of death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, and urgent re-catheterization compared to medical therapy alone.

#### SYNTAX (*N Engl J Med 2009*)

- **Study design**: 1,800 patients with previously untreated three-vessel or left main CAD were randomized to CABG versus PCI.
- **Result**: Major adverse cardiac or cerebrovascular events at 12 months were significantly higher in the PCI group (17.8% vs. 12.4% for CABG; $P = 0.002$). CABG remains the standard of care for these patients.

#### ISCHEMIA (*N Engl J Med 2020*)

- **Study design**: 5,179 patients with moderate to severe stable CAD were randomized to a conservative strategy (medical management) versus an early interventional strategy (revascularization).
- **Result**: No significant difference in ischemic cardiovascular events or death over a median follow-up of 3.2 years.

#### ORBITA (*The Lancet 2017*)

- **Study design**: 230 patients with stable CAD and ischemic symptoms were randomized to PCI of flow-limiting lesions versus a placebo (sham) procedure.
- **Result**: No difference in exercise time or symptom scores at 6 weeks follow-up.

### Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) and Post-ACS Care

#### CAPRICORN (*The Lancet 2001*)

- **Study design**: 1,959 hemodynamically stable post-myocardial infarction patients with LVEF $\le 40\%$ who were already on an ACE inhibitor were randomized to carvedilol versus placebo.
- **Result**: Carvedilol decreased all-cause mortality (12% vs. 15%).
