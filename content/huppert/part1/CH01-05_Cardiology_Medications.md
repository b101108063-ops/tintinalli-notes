## 1. Common Medications in Cardiology

### Calcium Channel Blockers (CCB)
| Category & Examples | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Dihydropyridine (DHP)**:<br>amlodipine, clevidipine, nicardipine, nifedipine, nimodipine | Block voltage-dependent L-type $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ channels $\rightarrow$ ↓muscle contractility; acts primarily on **vascular smooth muscle**. | Hypertension, Angina (including Prinzmetal), Raynaud's.<br>*Nimodipine*: Prophylaxis against vasospasm in SAH. | Peripheral edema, flushing, dizziness. |
| **Non-DHP**:<br>diltiazem, verapamil | Block voltage-dependent L-type $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ channels; acts primarily on the **heart**. | Hypertension, Angina, Afib/flutter rate control. | Cardiac depression, AV block, hyperprolactinemia, constipation, gingival hyperplasia. |

### RAAS Inhibitors
| Class & Examples | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ACE inhibitors (ACEi)**:<br>captopril, enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril | Inhibition of ACE lowers angiotensin II (reducing GFR via efferent arteriole relaxation); prevents inactivation of bradykinin. | Hypertension, Heart failure, LV hypertrophy, Diabetic nephropathy. | Cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema. **Teratogen** (contraindicated in pregnancy). |
| **Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARB)**:<br>losartan, candesartan, valsartan | Blocks binding of angiotensin II; does not increase bradykinin levels. | Same as ACEi. Often used second-line if patient develops an ACEi cough. | Same as ACEi, but cough/angioedema are much more rare. |

### Vasodilators & Nitrates
| Medication | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hydralazine** | Relaxes smooth muscle via cGMP; Arteriole dilatation $>$ venodilation. | Acute severe hypertension, combination therapy with organic nitrates for HF. | Compensatory tachycardia (contraindicated in CAD), headache, SLE-like syndrome. |
| **Nitroprusside** | Vasodilation via direct action; Arteriole dilatation $>$ venodilation. | Hypertensive emergency, ADHF (afterload reduction). | Cyanide toxicity (careful in renal failure). |
| **Organic Nitrates**:<br>nitroglycerin, isosorbide dinitrate, isosorbide mononitrate | Vasodilation via increased nitric oxide; Venodilation $>>$ arteriole dilatation. | Angina, ACS. | Reflex tachycardia, hypotension, flushing, headache.<br>**Contraindication:** Right ventricular MI. |

### Other Cardiac Medications
| Medication | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ranolazine** | Inhibits late phase $\text{Na}^{+}$ current in ischemic cardiomyocytes $\rightarrow$ ↓wall tension & O2 consumption. | Refractory angina. | Dizziness, headache, nausea, QT prolongation. |
| **Milrinone** | Selective PDE-3 inhibitor; increases inotropy/chronotropy via $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ influx; relaxes vascular smooth muscle. | Acute, decompensated HF. | Hypotension, arrhythmia. |
| **Digoxin** (Glycoside) | Inhibits $\text{Na}^{+}/\text{K}^{+}$ ATPase (indirectly inhibits $\text{Na}^{+}/\text{Ca}^{2+}$ exchanger) $\rightarrow$ ↑inotropy; slows HR via vagal nerve. | Heart failure, Afib rate control. | Cholinergic effects, hyperkalemia. Toxicity (particularly in renal failure). |

### Antiarrhythmics (Vaughan Williams Classification)
| Class & Examples | Mechanism | Typical Uses | Side Effects |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Class I** ($\text{Na}^{+}$ blockers)<br>**IA**: quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide<br>**IB**: lidocaine, mexiletine<br>**IC**: flecainide, propafenone | Slows/blocks phase 0 conduction in depolarized cells. | **IA**: Atrial/ventricular arrhythmias.<br>**IB**: Post-MI ventricular arrhythmia, digitalis-induced arrhythmia.<br>**IC**: SVT (including Afib). | **IA**: QT prolongation, SLE-like syndrome.<br>**IB**: CNS effects.<br>**IC**: Pro-arrhythmic (especially post-MI). |
| **Class II** ($\beta$ blockers)<br>metoprolol, propranolol, esmolol, atenolol, carvedilol | Decreases SA $<$ AV nodal activity; decreases slope 4 of abnormal pacemaker cells. | Rate control for Afib/flutter or other SVTs. | Bradycardia, masked signs of hypoglycemia, impotence, dyslipidemia. |
| **Class III** ($\text{K}^{+}$ blockers)<br>amiodarone (shows class I-IV properties), ibutilide, dofetilide, sotalol | Increases duration of action potential / effective refractory period. | Afib/flutter, VT. | Prolonged QT.<br>*Amiodarone*: Pulmonary, hepatic, and thyroid toxicity. |
| **Class IV** ($\text{Ca}^{2+}$ blockers)<br>verapamil, diltiazem | Decrease conduction velocity and increase effective refractory period. | Rate control in Afib, SVTs conducted by the AV node. | Edema, constipation, flushing, bradycardia, AV block. |

## 2. Procedural & Surgical Interventions

* **Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI)**: Diagnostic (identify coronary abnormalities) and therapeutic (stent placement for better coronary perfusion).
* **Coronary Artery Bypass Graft (CABG)**: 
  * Main indications: Triple-vessel coronary disease, severe left main artery stenosis, or severe proximal LAD stenosis with concurrent circumflex stenosis or EF $< \mathbf{50\%}$.
* **Valve Replacement**: e.g., surgical aortic valve replacement (SAVR) or transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR) for aortic stenosis.
* **Radiofrequency Ablation**: Disrupts electrical signals causing arrhythmia.
* **Heart Transplantation**: Most common indications are non-ischemic cardiomyopathy ($\sim\mathbf{59\%}$) and ischemic cardiomyopathy ($\sim\mathbf{40\%}$).

## 3. Key Clinical Trials and Publications in Cardiology

### Cardiovascular Disease Risk Prevention
* **FOURIER** (*N Engl J Med 2017*): Evaluated **evolocumab** (PCSK9 inhibitor) vs. placebo in patients with atherosclerotic CVD and LDL $\ge \mathbf{70\text{ mg/dL}}$ already on a statin. 
  * *Result*: Evolocumab significantly reduced the risk of the composite primary end point (cardiovascular death, MI, stroke, hospitalization for unstable angina, or coronary revascularization) compared to placebo ($\mathbf{9.8\%}$ vs. $\mathbf{11.3\%}$).

### Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)
* **FAME-2** (*N Engl J Med 2012*): Compared FFR-guided PCI plus best medical therapy vs. best medical therapy alone in stable CAD.
  * *Result*: FFR-guided PCI reduced the composite endpoint of death, nonfatal MI, and urgent re-catheterization.
* **SYNTAX** (*N Engl J Med 2009*): Randomized patients with three-vessel or left main CAD to CABG vs. PCI. 
  * *Result*: Major adverse cardiac/cerebrovascular events at 12 months were significantly higher in the PCI group ($\mathbf{17.8\%}$ vs. $\mathbf{12.4\%}$). CABG remains the standard of care for these patients.
* **ISCHEMIA** (*N Engl J Med 2020*): Randomized moderate to severe stable CAD patients to conservative strategy (medical management) vs. early interventional strategy (revascularization).
  * *Result*: No significant difference in ischemic cardiovascular events or death over a median of 3.2 years.
* **ORBITA** (*The Lancet 2017*): Randomized stable CAD patients with ischemic symptoms to PCI vs. a placebo procedure.
  * *Result*: No difference in exercise time or symptom scores at 6 weeks follow-up.

### Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) and Post-ACS Care
* **CAPRICORN** (*The Lancet 2001*): Evaluated **carvedilol** vs. placebo in hemodynamically stable post-MI patients with LVEF $\le \mathbf{40\%}$ who were already on an ACE inhibitor.
  * *Result*: Carvedilol decreased all-cause mortality ($\mathbf{12\%}$ vs. $\mathbf{15\%}$).
