## 1. Approach to Chest Pain

When evaluating a patient with chest pain, a structured approach is essential to rapidly identify or rule out life-threatening conditions.

### Initial Evaluation

#### History

A thorough history should assess:

- Character and quality of the pain
- Provoking and relieving factors
- Duration and frequency of symptoms

#### Physical Examination

A targeted examination should include:

- Vital signs, with blood pressure measured in both arms and legs
- Cardiac and chest wall examination
- Pulmonary examination
- Abdominal examination

### "Cannot-Miss" Diagnoses (The Big 5)

The following five life-threatening causes of chest pain must always be considered first:

1. **Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)**
2. **Pulmonary Embolism (PE)**
3. **Aortic Dissection**
4. **Tension Pneumothorax**
5. **Esophageal Rupture**

### Etiologies of Chest Pain by Organ System

| System | Diagnosis | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cardiac** | **Angina** | Supply/demand mismatch (e.g., ACS, stable angina, vasospasm, SCAD, MINOCA). "Typical" features include substernal location, exertional provocation, and relief with rest or nitroglycerin. |
| | **Pericarditis** | Pleuritic, sharp, retrosternal pain relieved by sitting forward; associated with a pericardial friction rub. |
| | **Advanced HF / PAH** | Pressure sensation, exertional dyspnea, and syncope (particularly in advanced pulmonary arterial hypertension). |
| **Vascular** | **Aortic dissection** | Sudden onset tearing pain radiating to the back; unequal pulses or discordant blood pressures; often associated with hypertension or connective tissue disorders. |
| | **Pulmonary embolism** | Sudden onset, pleuritic pain associated with dyspnea. |
| **Pulmonary** | **Pneumonia/Pleuritis** | Lateral to midline pain; brief duration; associated with dyspnea, cough, and fever. |
| | **Tracheobronchitis** | Midline burning discomfort with coughing. |
| | **Pneumothorax** | Sudden pleuritic pain with dyspnea and decreased breath sounds. |
| **Gastrointestinal** | **Peptic ulcer disease** | Burning epigastric pain relieved with food or antacids. |
| | **GERD** | Burning epigastric pain worsened when lying flat after meals. |
| | **Esophageal spasm** | Retrosternal pressure or tightness mimicking angina; often relieved by nitroglycerin. |
| | **Biliary / Pancreatitis** | Right upper quadrant pain (biliary) or prolonged epigastric or back pain (pancreatitis). |
| **Neuromuscular** | **Costochondritis** | Sudden or fleeting pain reproduced with palpation of the chest wall. |
| | **Rib fracture** | Reproducible pain associated with injury; worsens with movement. |
| | **Herpes zoster** | Dermatomal burning pain with a vesicular rash. |
| **Psychological** | **Panic disorder** | Chest tightness, palpitations, and dyspnea. |

### Initial Diagnostic Workup

#### ECG

The ECG should be examined for:

- ST changes (suggesting ACS, pericarditis, or demand ischemia)
- Right ventricular strain pattern (suggesting PE)
- Q waves (suggesting prior ischemia)

#### Chest X-Ray (CXR)

The chest X-ray may reveal:

- Pneumonia
- Rib fractures
- Pneumothorax
- A widened mediastinum (suggesting aortic dissection)

#### D-dimer

- Use an age-adjusted cutoff for patients over 50 years: age $\times$ 10 equals the upper limit of normal.
- A normal D-dimer result can rule out PE in low- or intermediate-probability scenarios.
- A normal result can also rule out aortic dissection in low-risk patients.

#### CT Imaging

| Study | Indication |
| :--- | :--- |
| **CTA (arterial contrast)** | Used when aortic dissection is the primary concern |
| **CTPA (pulmonary angiography)** | Used when pulmonary embolism is the primary concern |

## 2. Approach to Syncope

### Definition

Syncope is defined as a transient loss of consciousness due to low blood pressure resulting in inadequate cerebral perfusion.

### Syncope Mimickers (Rule Out First)

- Stroke
- Seizure
- Transient ischemic attack (TIA)
- Atypical migraine
- Mechanical fall
- Generalized weakness

### Types of Syncope

#### Cardiogenic Syncope

Cardiogenic syncope is characterized by sudden loss of consciousness *without* a prodrome.

- **Key diagnostic step**: Obtain an ECG and initiate telemetry monitoring.
- **Differential diagnosis**:
  - Outflow obstruction (e.g., aortic stenosis, hypertrophic obstructive cardiomyopathy [HOCM])
  - Arrhythmias

#### Neurocardiac (Reflex) Syncope

Neurocardiac syncope is characterized by the "3 Ps":

- **Prodrome** (e.g., dizziness, nausea, diaphoresis preceding the event)
- **Positional** (triggered by standing or changes in posture)
- **Provocation** (triggered by a specific situation such as coughing, urination, or carotid pressure)

- **Diagnostic test**: Tilt-table test can be helpful in confirming the diagnosis.
- **Differential diagnosis**:
  - Vasovagal syncope
  - Situational syncope (post-tussive, micturition)
  - Carotid sinus hypersensitivity

#### Orthostatic Syncope

Orthostatic syncope is triggered by a change in position, particularly when moving from supine to standing.

- **Abnormal orthostatic vital signs**:
  - Systolic blood pressure decreases $\ge 20$ mmHg upon standing
  - Diastolic blood pressure decreases $> 10$ mmHg upon standing
- **Differential diagnosis**:
  - Volume depletion
  - Autonomic insufficiency (medications, neurogenic causes, elderly patients)
  - Adrenal insufficiency

### Syncope Workup

The evaluation of syncope should include:

1. Thorough history (including witnessed accounts when available)
2. Orthostatic vital signs
3. Careful cardiac and neurologic examinations
4. ECG to look for arrhythmias, conduction disease, WPW (short PR, delta wave), long QT, or Brugada pattern (V1: RSR' pattern with downsloping ST elevation)

#### Cardiac Workup

If cardiogenic syncope is suspected, pursue continuous telemetry monitoring and consider a transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE) to determine whether the heart is structurally normal or abnormal.

| Heart Structure | Consider |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Structurally normal heart** | Channelopathies (long/short QT, Brugada), conduction system abnormalities, ventricular tachycardia/ventricular fibrillation, electrolyte disturbances |
| **Structurally abnormal heart** | Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), arrhythmogenic right ventricular dysplasia (ARVD), infiltrative cardiomyopathies (sarcoid, amyloid), congenital conditions, valvular pathology, severe pulmonary arterial hypertension |

## 3. Stable Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

### Definition

Stable CAD refers to either asymptomatic coronary artery disease or stable angina—exertional or emotional chest pain that is reliably relieved with rest or nitroglycerin. The underlying cause is fixed atherosclerosis.

### Diagnosis

- Stress testing
- Coronary CT angiography (CCTA) or invasive coronary angiogram

### Baseline Workup

- Hemoglobin A1c (HgA1c)
- Lipid panel
- ECG
- Transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE) to assess left ventricular function

### Treatment and Risk Factor Modification

#### Lifestyle Modifications

- Smoking cessation
- Regular exercise
- Weight loss
- Heart-healthy diet

#### Hypertension Management

- Blood pressure goal: $< 140/90$ mmHg
- First-line therapy: ACE inhibitor (or ARB if intolerant)

#### Diabetes Management

- Preferred agents: SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 agonists

#### Hyperlipidemia Management

- Initiate statin therapy (e.g., atorvastatin, rosuvastatin)
- LDL goal: $< 70$ mg/dL
- **If LDL is not at goal**: Consider adding ezetimibe.
- **If LDL is still not at goal or with family history**: Consider adding a PCSK9 inhibitor.
- **If fasting triglycerides $> 135$ mg/dL** in patients with known CAD or diabetes: Consider adding icosapent ethyl.

#### Medications for Cardiovascular Risk Reduction

- Aspirin
- Beta blockers, particularly in patients with a prior ACS event

### Anti-Anginal Symptom Therapy

| Medication | Mechanism of Action | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Beta blocker (BB)** | Increases coronary blood supply (by decreasing heart rate and increasing diastolic filling time) and decreases myocardial demand (by decreasing contractility and wall stress) | First-line therapy |
| **Calcium channel blocker (CCB)** | Similar mechanism to beta blockers; used as a second agent when needed | Alternative or adjunct to BB |
| **Nitrates** | Coronary vasodilation and venodilation, which decreases preload, wall stress, and oxygen demand | Options include sublingual/transdermal nitroglycerin (as needed), isosorbide dinitrate (oral BID or TID), or isosorbide 5-mononitrate (oral daily or BID) |
| **Ranolazine** | Inhibits late inward sodium current in ischemic myocytes | Used for refractory angina |
