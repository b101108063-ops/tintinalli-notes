## 1. Electrocardiography (ECG)

It is important to approach an electrocardiogram (ECG) using the same system every time.

### Rate & Rhythm

- **Rate Definitions**: Bradycardic (<**60 bpm**), normal (60–**100 bpm**), tachycardic (≥**100 bpm**).
- **Rate Assessment**: Use the 300-150-100-75-60-50 Rule, or count the number of QRS complexes × 6 for a 10-second strip.
- **Rhythm Regularity**: If not 1:1, determine if P > QRS (AV block) or if P < QRS (accelerated junctional or ventricular rhythm).
- **Sinus Rhythms**: P before every QRS and QRS after every P. Atrial activity initiating at the sinus node shows P upright in II, down in avR, and sinusoidal in V1.
- **Nonsinus Rhythms**:
  - **Abnormal atrial rhythms**: Premature atrial contractions (PACs), ectopic atrial rhythm (<**100 bpm**), atrial tachycardia (>**100 bpm**), atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, AVNRT, AVRT, paced atrial rhythms.
  - **Junctional rhythms**: Junctional escape rhythm (<**60 bpm**), accelerated junctional rhythm (60–**100 bpm**), junctional tachycardia (>**100 bpm**).
  - **Ventricular rhythms**: Premature ventricular beats (PVCs), idioventricular (escape) rhythm (<**50 bpm**), accelerated idioventricular rhythm (50–**100 bpm**), ventricular tachycardia (>**100 bpm**), ventricular fibrillation, paced ventricular rhythms.

### Axis Deviation

| Axis | ECG Findings | Differential Diagnosis (Ddx) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal axis** | +I, +II | |
| **Left axis deviation** | +I, -II | LVH, LBBB, LAFB, inferior MI, chronic lung disease, hyperkalemia. |
| **Right axis deviation**| -I, +aVF | RVH, LPFB, lateral wall MI, PE, chronic lung disease, arm lead reversal, dextrocardia, ostium secundum ASD. |
| **Extreme "NW" axis** | -I, -aVF, +aVR | Ventricular rhythm, pacing, hyperkalemia, lead misplacement. |

### Intervals & Conduction Blocks

- **Interval Measurement**: Big box = 0.2 seconds, small box = 0.04 seconds.
- **PR Interval**: Normal is 0.12–0.2 seconds (3–5 small boxes). 
  - *Long PR Ddx*: First-degree AV block, high vagal tone, conduction delay, AV nodal agents, hypokalemia. 
  - *Short PR Ddx*: WPW, high catecholamine state.
- **QRS Complex**: Normal is <0.12 seconds (<3 small boxes). 
  - *Long QRS Ddx*: 1) Ventricular rhythm; 2) Bundle branch block; 3) Intraventricular conduction delay (IVCD); 4) Medications (e.g., TCAs, antiarrhythmics); 5) WPW; 6) Paced rhythms.
- **Bundle Branch Blocks (BBB)**: QRS may be narrow (incomplete block) or wide (complete block).
  - **LAFB (Left Anterior Fascicular Block)**: LAD (-45° to -90°) with no other cause, qR in I/avL, rS in II/III/avF.
  - **LPFB (Left Posterior Fascicular Block)**: RAD with no other cause, rS in I/avL, qR in II/III/avF.
  - **RBBB**: V1 rSR' (QRS terminal forces POSITIVE), wide slurred S in lateral leads (I, avL, V5, V6).
  - **LBBB**: V1 down (QRS terminal forces NEGATIVE), tall R in lateral leads, deep S in V1–V3. New LBBB may suggest ischemia.
- **Bifascicular blocks**: Wide QRS due to two unhealthy/slow conduction tracts; always abnormal and indicative of a diseased conduction system, triggering workup for underlying heart disease. Includes RBBB + LAFB, or RBBB + LPFB.
- **Trifascicular block**: Wide QRS due to three unhealthy/slow tracts; always abnormal; should trigger workup for underlying disease and consideration of ambulatory ECG monitoring. Patients alternate between RBBB and LBBB, or have any bifascicular block + AV block.
- **QT Interval**: Varies with HR; ballpark normal is less than half the RR interval. Corrected QT (QTc) = QT / √(RR): 0.33–0.47 seconds. If prolonged, risk of torsades.
  - *Long QT Ddx*: Hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, medications, long QT syndrome.
  - *Short QT Ddx*: Hypercalcemia, short QT syndrome, digoxin toxicity.

### Hypertrophy & Atrial Abnormalities

- **RA enlargement**: II-P tall and peaked >**2.5 mm**; V1–V2 prominent initial positivity of P >**1.5 mm**.
- **LA enlargement**: II-P wide, double peaked >120 ms; V1 more negative.
- **RV Hypertrophy (RVH)**: Right axis deviation required; early R wave progression; V1 with tall R.
- **LV Hypertrophy (LVH)**: Cannot call LVH in the setting of LBBB.
  - *Framingham*: R in avL >**11 mm** in men or >**9 mm** in women.
  - *Sokolow Lyon*: S in V1 + R in V5 or V6 > **35 mm**.
  - *Cornell*: S in V3 + R avL >**28 mm** in men, >**20 mm** in women.
  - *Romhilt-Estes LVH Point Score*: 5 points consistent with LVH, 4 points is considered probable.

### Ischemia & Infarction

- **R wave progression**: Normal transition occurs V3–V4.
- **Q waves**: Pathologic if >**25%** R wave height, **1 mm** in width. Evidence of prior MI if ≥2 pathologic Q waves in contiguous leads. S1Q3T3 pattern suggests RV strain (e.g., due to a pulmonary embolism).
- **ST segment Elevations**: Consider ischemia; location can help localize the lesion. STEMI requires new **1 mm** ST elevation in two consecutive leads (in V1–V3 criteria is **1.5 mm** for women and **2 mm** for men), measured from the J point. 
- **Modified Sgarbossa Criteria (for LBBB)**: ANY of the following meets criteria for an acute MI:
  1. Concordant ST elevation >**1 mm**.
  2. ST depression >**1 mm** V1-V3.
  3. ST elevation to S wave amplitude ratio >0.25.
- **ST segment Depressions**: NSTEMI typically >**0.5 mm** downsloping or horizontal ST depressions.
- **T waves**: Inversions can indicate ischemia/infarction (especially if >**2 mm**, symmetric, and across precordium). Normal in BBB (T wave should be opposite the terminal forces of the QRS).

## 2. Echocardiography

- **Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE)**: Most common type; transducer placed on chest/abdomen to noninvasively assess cardiac function, heart valves, and contraction.
- **Transesophageal echocardiography (TEE)**: Transducer passed into esophagus for better visualization of cardiac structures. Indicated for evaluating embolic source, endocarditis, prosthetic/native valve dysfunction, and aortic dissection/aneurysm.
- **Point-of-care (POC) echocardiography**: Bedside assessment to identify causes of acute cardiac failure or hemodynamic instability. Highly dependent upon user skill.

## 3. Cardiac Stress Testing

- **Indications**: Assessment for new CAD in patients with chest pain ruled out for ACS. Helpful for patients with an INTERMEDIATE pre-test probability of CAD.
- **Chest Pain Quality**: Features concerning for CAD include substernal location, provoked by exertion/emotion, and improved by rest/nitroglycerin.
  - *Typical anginal*: All 3 features present.
  - *Atypical*: 2 features present.
  - *Nonanginal*: 0–1 features present.
- **Contraindications**: Recent MI, active ACS, decompensated heart failure, uncontrolled arrhythmias; severe hypertension.
- **Instructions**: Take all medications as usual EXCEPT beta blockers; no caffeine prior to vasodilator testing.

### Pre-Test Probability of CAD

| Age (yr) | Typical Angina | Atypical Angina | Nonanginal Chest Pain | Asymptomatic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **30–39** | Intermediate | Very low | Very low | Stress testing is generally not indicated |
| **40–49** | Intermediate | Low | Very low | |
| **50–59** | Intermediate | Intermediate | Low | |
| **60–69** | High | Intermediate | Intermediate | |
| **>70** | High | Intermediate | Intermediate | |

### Types of Stress Tests

- **Stressors**: 
  - *Exercise (Treadmill/Bike)*: Assesses exercise tolerance.
  - *Vasodilator (regadenoson)*: Contraindicated in WPW, COPD, sinus node dysfunction/AV block, baseline hypotension.
  - *Beta-1 agonist (dobutamine)*: Contraindicated in frequent ventricular arrhythmias, afib with RVR, LV outflow tract obstruction, severe hypertension (SBP >**180 mmHg**).
- **Detectors**:
  - *ECG only*: Cannot localize ischemia; contraindicated if baseline abnormal ECG.
  - *Echo*: Localizes ischemia and assesses valvular hemodynamics; highest specificity.
  - *Nuclear (PET/SPECT)*: Localizes ischemia; highest sensitivity, but involves radiation exposure.

## 4. Advanced Imaging & Catheterization

### Myocardial Viability & CT Angiography

- **Myocardial Viability Study**: Identifies "hibernating" myocardium that could regain function post-revascularization via MRI or FDG-PET.
- **Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)**: Direct anatomic and physiologic (CT-FFR) assessment. Noninvasive ECG-gated CT requiring a low heart rate (<**70 bpm**). Allows clinicians to rule out significant left main CAD in stable angina and start medical therapy without invasive testing.

### Left Heart Catheterization (Coronary Angiogram)

- **Procedure**: Thin catheter placed in an artery (radial or femoral), advanced to the ascending aorta, and contrast injected to visualize coronary arteries.
- **Indications**: Diagnostic and therapeutic (PCI) for CAD (symptoms, ACS, HF, VT, post-arrest) and determining Aortic Stenosis severity.
- **Percutaneous Coronary Interventions (PCI)**: Drug-eluting stents (DES) are the standard of care with lower restenosis rates than bare metal stents. Requires lifelong aspirin and dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) for 6 months (or 1 yr if ACS).
- **Complications**:
  - *Immediate*: Stroke (~**0.05%** at 30 days), vascular access complication (hematoma, retroperitoneal bleed), contrast-associated AKI (Cr peaks in 3–5 days), dissection, MI, in-stent thrombosis.
  - *Longer-term*: In-stent restenosis (gradual onset anginal symptoms months to years after PCI).

### Right Heart Catheterization

- **Procedure**: Insertion of a catheter into the right heart and pulmonary arteries via the IJ or brachial vein to measure RA, RV, PA, wedge pressures, and cardiac output.
- **Indications**: Diagnosis of pulmonary hypertension and assessment of hemodynamics in heart failure.
