## 1. Electrocardiography (ECG)

A consistent, systematic approach to ECG interpretation is essential for avoiding missed diagnoses.

### Rate & Rhythm

#### Heart Rate Definitions

| Classification | Heart Rate |
| :--- | :--- |
| Bradycardic | $< 60$ bpm |
| Normal | 60–100 bpm |
| Tachycardic | $\ge 100$ bpm |

#### Rate Assessment Methods

- The 300–150–100–75–60–50 Rule provides a rapid estimation of heart rate.
- Alternatively, count the number of QRS complexes in a 10-second strip and multiply by 6.

#### Rhythm Regularity

When the relationship between P waves and QRS complexes is not 1:1:

- **P > QRS**: Suggests an AV block.
- **P < QRS**: Suggests an accelerated junctional rhythm or ventricular rhythm.

#### Sinus Rhythms

Sinus rhythms are defined by a P wave preceding every QRS complex and a QRS following every P wave. Atrial activity originating from the sinus node produces a P wave that is upright in lead II, down in lead aVR, and sinusoidal in lead V1.

#### Nonsinus Rhythms

##### Abnormal Atrial Rhythms

| Rhythm | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- |
| Premature atrial contractions (PACs) | Premature ectopic beats from the atrium |
| Ectopic atrial rhythm | Atrial rhythm $< 100$ bpm |
| Atrial tachycardia | Atrial rhythm $> 100$ bpm |
| Atrial fibrillation | Irregularly irregular rhythm with no organized atrial activity |
| Atrial flutter | Sawtooth flutter waves at approximately 300 bpm |
| AVNRT | AV nodal reentrant tachycardia |
| AVRT | AV reentrant tachycardia |
| Paced atrial rhythms | Atrial pacing from an implanted device |

##### Junctional Rhythms

| Rhythm | Heart Rate |
| :--- | :--- |
| Junctional escape rhythm | $< 60$ bpm |
| Accelerated junctional rhythm | 60–100 bpm |
| Junctional tachycardia | $> 100$ bpm |

##### Ventricular Rhythms

| Rhythm | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- |
| Premature ventricular beats (PVCs) | Premature ectopic ventricular beats |
| Idioventricular (escape) rhythm | Ventricular rhythm $< 50$ bpm |
| Accelerated idioventricular rhythm | Ventricular rhythm 50–100 bpm |
| Ventricular tachycardia | Wide-complex tachycardia $> 100$ bpm |
| Ventricular fibrillation | Chaotic, irregular wide-complex rhythm |
| Paced ventricular rhythms | Ventricular pacing from an implanted device |

### Axis Deviation

| Axis | ECG Findings | Differential Diagnosis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal axis** | Positive in lead I, positive in lead II | — |
| **Left axis deviation** | Positive in lead I, negative in lead II | LVH, LBBB, LAFB, inferior MI, chronic lung disease, hyperkalemia |
| **Right axis deviation** | Negative in lead I, positive in aVF | RVH, LPFB, lateral wall MI, PE, chronic lung disease, arm lead reversal, dextrocardia, ostium secundum ASD |
| **Extreme "NW" axis** | Negative in lead I, negative in aVF, positive in aVR | Ventricular rhythm, pacing, hyperkalemia, lead misplacement |

### Intervals & Conduction Blocks

#### Interval Measurement

- One big box on ECG paper equals 0.2 seconds.
- One small box equals 0.04 seconds.

#### PR Interval

- **Normal PR interval**: 0.12–0.2 seconds (3–5 small boxes).
- **Prolonged PR interval — differential diagnosis**:
  - First-degree AV block
  - High vagal tone
  - Conduction delay
  - AV nodal blocking agents
  - Hypokalemia
- **Short PR interval — differential diagnosis**:
  - Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome
  - High catecholamine state

#### QRS Complex

- **Normal QRS duration**: $< 0.12$ seconds ($< 3$ small boxes).
- **Prolonged QRS — differential diagnosis**:
  - Ventricular rhythm
  - Bundle branch block
  - Intraventricular conduction delay (IVCD)
  - Medications (e.g., TCAs, antiarrhythmics)
  - WPW syndrome
  - Paced rhythms

#### Bundle Branch Blocks

Bundle branch blocks result in a widened QRS complex. The block may be incomplete (narrow QRS) or complete (wide QRS).

| Block | ECG Findings |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB)** | Left axis deviation ($-45^\circ$ to $-90^\circ$) with no other cause; qR pattern in leads I and aVL; rS pattern in leads II, III, and aVF |
| **Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB)** | Right axis deviation with no other cause; rS pattern in leads I and aVL; qR pattern in leads II, III, and aVF |
| **Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)** | V1 shows rSR' pattern (terminal QRS forces are POSITIVE); wide, slurred S wave in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5, V6) |
| **Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)** | V1 shows a negative deflection (terminal QRS forces are NEGATIVE); tall R wave in lateral leads; deep S wave in V1–V3. New LBBB may suggest myocardial ischemia |

#### Bifascicular Blocks

Bifascicular blocks produce a widened QRS due to two unhealthy or slow conduction tracts. These findings are always abnormal and indicate a diseased conduction system, warranting investigation for underlying heart disease. The most common patterns are:

- RBBB with LAFB
- RBBB with LPFB

#### Trifascicular Block

Trifascicular block produces a widened QRS due to three unhealthy or slow conduction tracts. This is always abnormal and should trigger a workup for underlying disease and consideration of ambulatory ECG monitoring. Patients typically alternate between RBBB and LBBB, or have any bifascicular block combined with AV block.

#### QT Interval

The QT interval varies with heart rate; a general rule is that the QT interval should be less than half of the RR interval.

- **Corrected QT (QTc)**: $\text{QTc} = \text{QT} / \sqrt{\text{RR}}$
- **Normal QTc**: 0.33–0.47 seconds
- **Prolonged QTc**: Increases the risk of torsades de pointes.
  - *Causes*: Hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, medications, long QT syndrome.
- **Short QTc**:
  - *Causes*: Hypercalcemia, short QT syndrome, digoxin toxicity.

### Hypertrophy & Atrial Abnormalities

#### Right Atrial (RA) Enlargement

- In lead II: Tall, peaked P wave $> 2.5$ mm.
- In leads V1–V2: Prominent initial positivity of the P wave $> 1.5$ mm.

#### Left Atrial (LA) Enlargement

- In lead II: Wide, bifid (double-peaked) P wave $> 120$ ms in duration.
- In lead V1: More deeply negative P wave deflection.

#### Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH)

- Requires right axis deviation on ECG.
- Early R wave progression across the precordium.
- Tall R wave in lead V1.

#### Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)

LVH cannot be diagnosed in the presence of LBBB due to the confounding effect on the QRS complex.

| Criteria | Measurement |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Framingham criteria** | R wave in aVL $> 11$ mm (men) or $> 9$ mm (women) |
| **Sokolow Lyon criteria** | S wave in V1 plus R wave in V5 or V6 $> 35$ mm |
| **Cornell criteria** | S wave in V3 plus R wave in aVL $> 28$ mm (men), $> 20$ mm (women) |
| **Romhilt-Estes Point Score** | A score of 5 points is consistent with LVH; a score of 4 points is considered probable |

### Ischemia & Infarction

#### R Wave Progression

Normal R wave transition occurs in leads V3–V4.

#### Q Waves

- **Pathologic Q waves** are defined as Q waves that are $> 25\%$ of the R wave height, or $> 1$ mm in width.
- Evidence of prior myocardial infarction requires $\ge 2$ pathologic Q waves in contiguous leads.
- The S1Q3T3 pattern (prominent S wave in lead I, Q wave in lead III, and inverted T wave in lead III) suggests right ventricular strain, such as from a pulmonary embolism.

#### ST Segment Elevations

ST segment elevations suggest myocardial ischemia. The location of elevation can help localize the culprit lesion.

- **STEMI criteria**: New ST elevation $\ge 1$ mm in two consecutive leads (with the exception of V1–V3, where the threshold is $1.5$ mm for women and $2$ mm for men), measured from the J point.

#### Modified Sgarbossa Criteria (for LBBB)

In patients with LBBB, any of the following criteria meets the ECG definition of acute myocardial infarction:

1. Concordant ST elevation $> 1$ mm.
2. ST depression $> 1$ mm in leads V1–V3.
3. ST elevation to S wave amplitude ratio $> 0.25$.

#### ST Segment Depressions

- **NSTEMI** typically presents with $> 0.5$ mm downsloping or horizontal ST depressions.

#### T Waves

T-wave inversions can indicate ischemia or infarction, particularly when:

- The inversion is $> 2$ mm in depth.
- The inversion is symmetric.
- The changes span the precordium.

**Note**: T-wave inversions are a normal finding in the presence of bundle branch block (the T wave should be opposite to the terminal forces of the QRS).

## 2. Echocardiography

### Types of Echocardiography

| Modality | Description | Typical Uses |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE)** | Most common type; transducer placed on the chest or abdomen to noninvasively assess cardiac function, valves, and wall motion | General assessment of cardiac structure and function |
| **Transesophageal echocardiography (TEE)** | Transducer passed into the esophagus for superior visualization of cardiac structures | Evaluation of embolic source, endocarditis, prosthetic or native valve dysfunction, aortic dissection or aneurysm |
| **Point-of-care (POC) echocardiography** | Bedside assessment to rapidly identify causes of acute cardiac failure or hemodynamic instability | Emergency and critical care settings; highly dependent on operator skill |

### IVC Assessment for Estimating Right Atrial (RA) Pressure

| IVC Diameter | Compressibility | Estimated RA Pressure |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $> 2.1$ cm | $< 50\%$ collapsible | $\sim 15$ mmHg |
| $< 2.1$ cm | $< 50\%$ collapsible | $\sim 8$ mmHg |
| $> 2.1$ cm | $> 50\%$ collapsible | $\sim 8$ mmHg |
| $< 2.1$ cm | $> 50\%$ collapsible | $\sim 3$ mmHg |

## 3. Cardiac Stress Testing

### Indications

Stress testing is used to assess for new coronary artery disease (CAD) in patients with chest pain that has been ruled out for acute coronary syndrome (ACS). This test is most helpful in patients with an **intermediate pre-test probability** of CAD.

### Chest Pain Characterization

Features concerning for CAD include:

- Substernal location
- Provoked by exertion or emotional stress
- Improved by rest or nitroglycerin

| Classification | Features Present |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Typical (classic) angina** | All 3 features |
| **Atypical angina** | 2 of 3 features |
| **Nonanginal chest pain** | 0–1 features |

### Contraindications to Stress Testing

- Recent myocardial infarction
- Active acute coronary syndrome
- Decompensated heart failure
- Uncontrolled arrhythmias
- Severe hypertension

### Pre-Test Instructions

- Take all medications as usual **except** beta blockers.
- Avoid caffeine prior to vasodilator stress testing.

### Pre-Test Probability of CAD

| Age (years) | Typical Angina | Atypical Angina | Nonanginal Chest Pain | Asymptomatic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **30–39** | Intermediate | Very low | Very low | Stress testing is generally not indicated |
| **40–49** | Intermediate | Low | Very low | — |
| **50–59** | Intermediate | Intermediate | Low | — |
| **60–69** | High | Intermediate | Intermediate | — |
| **>70** | High | Intermediate | Intermediate | — |

### Types of Stress Tests

#### Stressors

| Stressor | Description | Contraindications |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Exercise (Treadmill/Bike)** | Assesses exercise tolerance and functional capacity | — |
| **Vasodilator (Regadenoson)** | Causes coronary vasodilation to unmask perfusion defects | WPW, COPD, sinus node dysfunction or AV block, baseline hypotension |
| **Beta-1 agonist (Dobutamine)** | Increases heart rate and contractility | Frequent ventricular arrhythmias, atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response (RVR), left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) obstruction, severe hypertension (SBP $> 180$ mmHg) |

#### Detection Modalities

| Modality | Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ECG only** | Simple, inexpensive | Cannot localize ischemia; contraindicated if baseline ECG is abnormal |
| **Echocardiography** | Localizes ischemia and assesses valvular hemodynamics | Operator-dependent; highest specificity |
| **Nuclear (PET/SPECT)** | Localizes ischemia | Highest sensitivity; involves radiation exposure |

## 4. Advanced Imaging & Catheterization

### Myocardial Viability & CT Angiography

#### Myocardial Viability Study

This study identifies "hibernating" myocardium—regions of viable but underperfused heart muscle that may recover function after revascularization. Imaging modalities include cardiac MRI and FDG-PET.

#### Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)

CCTA provides direct anatomic and physiologic (via CT-FFR) assessment of coronary arteries. It is a noninvasive, ECG-gated CT study that requires a low heart rate ($< 70$ bpm) for optimal image quality. CCTA allows clinicians to rule out significant left main coronary artery disease in stable angina and to initiate medical therapy without invasive testing.

### Left Heart Catheterization (Coronary Angiogram)

#### Procedure

A thin catheter is placed in an artery (radial or femoral approach), advanced to the ascending aorta, and contrast is injected to visualize the coronary arteries.

#### Indications

- Diagnostic and therapeutic (PCI) for CAD presenting with symptoms, ACS, heart failure, ventricular tachycardia, or post-cardiac arrest.
- Determining the severity of aortic stenosis.

#### Percutaneous Coronary Interventions (PCI)

- Drug-eluting stents (DES) are the standard of care due to lower restenosis rates compared to bare metal stents.
- PCI requires lifelong aspirin and dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) for a minimum of 6 months (or 12 months if ACS).

#### Complications

| Timing | Complication |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Immediate** | Stroke ($\sim 0.05\%$ at 30 days), vascular access complication (hematoma, retroperitoneal bleed), contrast-associated AKI (creatinine peaks in 3–5 days), coronary dissection, MI, in-stent thrombosis |
| **Longer-term** | In-stent restenosis (gradual onset of anginal symptoms months to years after PCI) |

### Right Heart Catheterization

#### Procedure

A catheter is inserted into the right heart and pulmonary arteries via the internal jugular or brachial vein to measure right atrial, right ventricular, pulmonary artery, wedge pressures, and cardiac output.

#### Indications

- Diagnosis of pulmonary hypertension.
- Assessment of hemodynamics in heart failure.
